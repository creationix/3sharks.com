What measures are citizens and the government of Arkansas taking to lessen water pollution in Arkansas, and how does water pollution in Arkansas compare to that of the rest of the United States and the world?

 

            Often times cities in Arkansas have trouble finding clean drinking water for their citizen’s to use.  This is because pollutants such as excreta, bathing and wash water, and kitchen wastes from residential communities and sludge and harmful chemicals from industrial and commercial establishments are not treated properly before being put back into the lakes and rivers of Arkansas.  Though most cities in Arkansas are willing to improve their water supply, many of the small communities are not able to finance the needed facilities.  Water is used everyday by just about everyone.  Water is used to flush toilets, wash hands, cook with, wash with, and drink.  When harmful water is taken into the body, sickness and even disease can be the result.

            Some cities in Arkansas can afford and are proposing anti- pollution efforts such as new treatment facilities to improve the cities’ drinking water.  One example of this is Caddo Valley, Arkansas.  The city of Arkadelphia provided Caddo Valley with public drinking water from the Ouachita River.  Caddo Valley proposed that a new water treatment facility be built so that the

 

city could provide their own water from the Caddo River.  Previously, the businesses and residents of Caddo Valley had to use three package wastewater treatment plants and septic tanks for other water.  The locations of the three area package plant discharges are shown in figure one  (Wasteload Allocation Study; Caddo Valley, Arkansas).  This new treatment facility improved the cities drinking water greatly and lessened the chance for sickness and disease.  This instance at Caddo Valley is not uncommon for small towns in Arkansas. 

Treatment facilities such as the one at Caddo Valley are operated by an aerator, which is defined in the Americana Encyclopedia v. 1 as “an apparatus by means of which oxygen in the air is introduced into water, and dissolved in it, for the purpose of destroying bacteria” (Americana Encyclopedia v.1).  Not only does this aeration process oxidize organic matter in water, but it also removes harmful gases, such as sulfur, that causes unpleasant odors and tastes.  Aeration is one of a combination of methods that include chlorinating and filtration that are used to purify municipal water supplies. 

            Several events that took place in during the late 1960’s and the early 1970’s brought the seriousness of the world’s water pollution problem to the public’s attention.  These events and the major increase of uses for water throughout the world lead to the development of nine standards of water quality that must be satisfied.  These standards are stated in the Americana Encyclopedia v.1 as:

“(1) preservation of rivers in their natural state;

 (2) potability of the water supply;

 (3) preservation and enhancement of fish and wildlife;

 (4) safety for agricultural use;

 

 (5) safety for recreational use, including swimming;

 (6) accomodation to a great variety of industrial purposes;  

 (7) freedom from nuisance;

 (8) generation of power for public utilities;

 (9) dilution and transport of wastes.”

 

            Many towns in Arkansas are using several different methods to keep from polluting their water supply.  There are five common methods that are used to do this.

            The first of these methods is called the sanitary system.  Created in the 19th century, this system collects the wastewaters from the household commercial, industrial, and institutional establishments and conducts them through a sanitary sewerage, or wastewater collection system to a treatment site.”  The sanitary system was an excellent way of keeping harmful elements from reaching the water supply.  As stated in the Encyclopedia Americana, “These collection systems include plumbing systems, connections from plumbing systems into the collection system, sewers, manholes, and pumping stations.”  All of these systems are part of the process of protecting and keeping clean water.

            A second method of sanitation is natural decomposition.  According to the Encyclopedia Americana, “Human body wastes are naturally decomposed and stabilized by microorganisms, either in the ground or in the topsoil or receiving waters. This process was utilized by using the wastewater on agricultural lands.” 

           

 

 

A third method for protecting our water supply is called primary treatment.  According to the Encyclopedia Americana, in this process “the wastewater is conveyed to a central point, screened to remove large objects and grit.  Chambers to remove grit and primary sedimentation

sands are used to remove the suspended settleable solids.”  This is a long process, but is very effective.  “It reduces the concentration of suspended solids by about 60% but removes very little of the other constituents of the wastewater.” 

            A fourth method for treating wastewater is the Activated-Sludge Process.  According to the Encyclopedia Americana, “ In this process, heavy concentrations of aerobic microorganisms, called biological floc or activated sludge, are suspended in the liquid by agitation that is provided by air bubbled into the tank or by mechanical aerations.  Most of the biologically active sludge is then returned to the aeration tank to treat the incoming water.”  The Activated-Sludge Process may seem long, but it is just another effective manner of keeping harmful pollutants out of our supply.  A fifth method of protecting our water supply is referred to as the sand filter.  “Sand filters are beds of fine sand, usually three feet deep, through which the wastewater slowly seeps.  As it seeps through the sand, the organic matter is decompressed and stabilized be the microorganisms in the wastewater.”  This sounds like it would take hours to do, but it is really an effective way of cleaning the water.

            A sixth method of cleaning wastewater is the contact bed.  “The bed consists of many layers of stone, slate, or other inert material.  It operates on a fill-and-draw basis, and the organic matter delivered during the fill period is decomposed by the microorganisms on the bed.”  So the end result is decomposed matter which can later be turned into other matter.

           

           

Many nations of the world are currently facing water shortages.  These shortages are a result of pollution, droughts, inefficient use of water, overpopulation, or a combination of these

problems.  Fortunately, Arkansas does not suffer from thse problems as other states and countries do.  The Arkansas River is actually one of the cleanest rivers in the country.  According to the Encyclopedia Americana, only ten percent of the river is polluted.  This is compared to the lower Colorado River with fifty- percent pollution and the Mohawk River in New York with ninety percent.  Also, Lake Ouachita in Arkansas is the second cleanest lake in America. 

                By the year 2000, the world-wide demand for irrigation is expected to be twice what it was in 1975.  During this same time, industry is expected to increase twenty-fold and water needs in at least thirty countries may exceed the reliable supply.  The twenty-first century is quickly approaching and with many environmental problems to consider, water should be our first priority.
