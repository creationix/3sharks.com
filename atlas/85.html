“What Are the Causes and How Has Pollution Affected the Rivers and Streams of Arkansas?”


Pollution in the rivers and streams in Arkansas is a major factor in the well-being of aquatic life and it also has effects on the life downstream. The major cause of pollution in Arkansas’ rivers and streams is agricultural runoff. Since farm animals such as cattle, pigs, and fowl produce considerable amounts of waste that can constitute a significant source of water pollution (Bergman & Renwick, p. 350), measures must be taken to regulate and prevent problems before further damage is done. The effects of the pollution of farm animals are not only a state problem, but they are also a problem for the nation. The downstream flow causes the pollution to stream into the Mississippi River eventually dumping into the Gulf of Mexico. The effects and regulations are to be discussed in further detail later. To understand the effects of the pollution, the pollutants that are in the water must first be explained.

Pollutants

Pollution from the waste of cattle, swine, and poultry can amount to significant amounts of pollution in rivers. The major cause of pollution in Arkansas’ rivers and streams is agricultural runoff. Animal waste associated with the production of livestock (poultry, cattle, and swine) contribute significantly to non-point source contamination of surface water in northwest Arkansas (Davis, Nelson, & Vendrell, 2000). In fact, environmental activists have said that the rivers in the northwestern part of Arkansas are already polluted. “Of particular concern is the White River, which feeds into Beaver Lake, the source of drinking water for much of northwest Arkansas, including the towns of Fayetteville, Springdale, and Rogers,” (Kopsky, 1998). Benton County Daily Record stated Bill Keith as saying most people would agree that the nutrients in northwest Arkansas may not be a problem now, but they are on the verge of being a problem (Whiteley, 1998). Tests have been conducted by the United States Geological Survey revealing that the Ozark farmland streams in this region contain more nutrients from animal waste than most of the other streams in the nation. The animal wastes contain nutrients high in nitrogen (Associated Press, 1998). The effects of nitrogen in the rivers are discussed later. Other pollutants in waters in the state are coliform bacteria including fecal coliforms and also fecal

streptococci (Alexander, 1979). Fecal matter in the water is the waste of animals. The coliform bacteria is bacteria whose presence in waste water is an indication of pollution and potentially dangerous contamination. Fecal Streptococci is one of the most dangerous contaminants found in the rivers in Arkansas. Streptococci is a bacterium that can destroy red blood cells and cause various diseases in human beings, including scarlet fever and tonsillitis. Two rivers in Arkansas that have been tested are Hill Creek and Cypress Creek. Studies of these rivers show that the predominant sources of pollution is livestock or poultry waste. These pollutants can cause problems toward humans and life in the streams. If correct measures are not taken to eliminate or lessen the amount of pollutants in the water, the effects could be detrimental.

Effects

Big corporate giants such as Tyson and Cargill could be blamed for the increase of agricultural pollution. Tyson, a big chicken producer, and Cargill, a big pork producer, are both big corporations that raise fowl and swine and then sell their rendered products for consumers to buy. Although, the number of hog farms has declined by 50 percent since the 1980’s, the number of swine in each corporate owned farm has increased by 253 percent. There are approximately 400 of these huge operations (consisting of 1,500-6,000 swine in each) in Arkansas today. There are also over 4,500 contracted poultry operations in the state (Kopsky, 1998).

The pollution problem is created from the waste of these animals, most of these huge operations build manure lagoons where all the farms waste is collected. The problem is the karst

Being so highly porous, this limestone has a very high risk of collapsing. When this happens, all of the nutrients are released into the groundwater making the groundwater vulnerable to become contaminated.

The more serious long term pollution is taking place in Arkansas’ rivers and streams. Of particular concern in the White River which feeds into Beaver Lake, the source of drinking water for much of northwest Arkansas (Kopsky, 1998). These manure lagoons contain contaminants such as diseased organisms, residual hormones, antibiotic residues, pesticide residues, and other organic matters that make it unsuitable for the use of fertilizer (Fram,1998). The manure also contains high volumes of potash and phosphate.

High levels of phosphate in water can cause algae blooms. Algae are a group of aquatic plant-like organisms. They grow very quickly, especially when they have a good source of nutrients (such as phosphate). Algae tends to cloud the water, making it much harder for vegetation under the water to get sunlight. When these submerged plants are unable to reproduce, the habitat for aquatic life is reduced. Eventually these algae die and begin to decompose. During the decomposition, oxygen levels are lowered. The lowered oxygen levels make it difficult for aquatic organisms to survive (Fram, 1998). Besides killing fish and vegetation, this algae also has a great effect on Arkansas’ recreational waters. The dark green scum make the water extremely unattractive, giving it a bad odor. This has caused many problems for many guides, resort owners. Laws and Regulations

Many big businesses control the livestock and poultry operations in Arkansas. Individual workers work their farms for the big companies also. One problem with having big corporations is that they tend to escape the Arkansas Department of Pollution Control and Ecology with violations made on the environment. In the early 1990’s, the department allowed Cargill to double the size of their operation in Mena even though they have had several violations against them (Kopsky, 1998). The department had issued 90 citations for over 20 violations including overflowing sewage lagoons, to not dispensing of the wastewater correctly.

One contract farmer for Tyson food company was cited for a major violation, but the fine was dropped. The farmer had been dumping exceedingly large amounts of animal waste from approximately 6,000 hogs into Mineral Springs Creek. He had an irrigation line running from the sewage lagoon to the creek. As the water ran through the line it would spill over onto the grass and kill it, due to the high content of fecal matter. The fine was dropped because he told the department “the leak came from another line used for recycling waste water and had not reached the creek.” (Kopsky, 1998)

The department has publicly admitted that over half of the state hog operations have had numerous violations. Most of these violations were very dangerous such as leaks and overfilled lagoons. The Public Policy Panel, a public interest group support center in Little Rock, found that the department only did inspections once every three years, when it should be done once every year (Kopsky, 1998). When the department actually does inspect, enforcement is rare and fines are light. The department also claimed that they did not have enough staff to make yearly inspections. Since there seemed to be so many problems concerning the laws of the hog operations, the state of Arkansas came up with Regulation number five. It was the stiffest regulation in Arkansas history (Kopsky, 1998). This regulation has to deal with the requirements hog farmers must have to open a farm. It is required that “no confined animal operation using a liquid waste disposal system shall be constructed or operate unless the owner has first obtained a permit from the department” (Department of Pollution Control and Ecology [DPCE], 1992). Before submission of an application, the farmer must first put together a statement telling everyone his plan to operate a confined livestock or fowl facility. He must submit this to his local newspaper and let this circulate for no less than two weeks (DPCE, 1992). When the copy runs, he must also state that if anyone in the community has any problems or questions with having the farm nearby, he must write his objections to the department heading this project. This person must also have the education and training to accurately control waste and odor. Then, they must provide a permit application to the department (DPCE, 1992). If this information on the permit changes, the applicant must inform the department of the change. The Act goes on to state that “waste containment structures shall not be constructed within 1,320 feet of the nearest existing occupied dwelling for confined animal operations in excess of a certain number of animals” (DPCE, 1992). There are also land application requirements for these farmers. Waste and wastewater has to be evenly distributed over sites. They are not allowed to distribute when the ground is saturated, frozen, covered with ice or snow, or when there is over “0.25 inches applied on slopes with a grade of more than 15 percent” (DPCE, 1992). Of course, they may not operate near public water sources. The applicant must also always keep sufficient records of their farms and what they do with the animal waste, and they must submit annual reports to the department (DPCE, 1992). Prevention

Although there are laws to regulate agricultural pollution in Arkansas, more prevention from allowing these toxic substances from going into our rivers and streams is now being taken into action. Farmers can take many measures in order to prevent pollution. One method would be to avoid spreading manure on frozen ground (Gegner, 1999). Since the ground in Arkansas is not frozen for long periods of time, this problem can be easily avoided. This will prevent any unnecessary runoff when the frozen ground melts. Also, the equipment that spreads the manure, either a spreader or spraying unit, needs to be properly adjusted. This will make sure no excess fertilizer will be sprayed or spread onto the ground. Another system of waste management is pasteurizing hogs. When hogs are pasteurized, they move around on their own without being in a confined space and they are able to evenly distribute their manure without the aid of the operators, enabling the hogs to distribute the manure themselves. Now, with proper rotations and selecting stable, non-erodible land, hazards of pollution into streams and waterways are reduced (Gegner, 1999). The proper soil testing methods would be used for this. The soil then will not contain an excess amount of manure that will erode easily. If the tests reveal an imbalance, farmers can either apply lime or other fertilizers to assume proper soil balance, or they could even restrict application levels (Kuepper, 1998). Prevention can help more to reduce pollution caused by runoff of agricultural waste than laws and regulations can. Proper techniques to prevent pollution should be taken to help keep Arkansas’, and other states, rivers and streams natural and beautiful.

The pollution in the rivers and streams of Arkansas is very serious and damaging to one’s health and safe environment. As the pollution is pinpointed, one can see the effects it has on human life, as well as aquatic life. The effects are likewise are very serious.
