Research Question:  How has shape note singing diffused across the United States and how has it affected the ways in which our communities are developing?

 

Music plays an important role in everyday life.  It can effect how communities and individual families communicate with one another and utterly effect how the United State, and the communities within it, grow.  In the past, one of most influential genres has been the spiritual folk hymn.  But, due to the fact that many Americans could not read music, rapid advancements of new and different types of spiritual folk hymns across America were not possible.  So, a method of reading music was introduced to a vast majority of the Americans, called shape note singing. Although, the precursors of shape note singing began many centuries ago, it was not until shape note singing was incorporated into the rich history and culture of American spirituals did its diffusion in the United States really become important.   

     Shape-notes are just like ordinary western musical notation, except that the note heads have different shapes to indicate their location on the staff as well as a musical syllable. 

(Willard, 1998:  http://)

But today’s recognized system of shape-notes is quite different from its precursors.  The first system ever devised using syllables to denote pitches was in the eleventh century, by the monk Guido of Arezzo.  He constructed a hexachord system of six syllables consisting of ut, re, mi, fa, sol, and la.  For many centuries this was the norm.  Guido trained young men to sing by using this hexachord system.  But, it was not until the sixteenth century that singers from all across England began to use a shorter version, which consisted of just using the four syllables fa, sol, la, and mi.  At the same time, many different countries across Europe expanded on Guido’s hexachord system, which became a seven-syllable system (Italian:  do re me fa sol la si).  Soon many British and European societies became accustomed to the use of these four and seven syllable systems.  But due to centrifugal forces, many colonists began to move to North America for religious refuge, as a result relocation diffusion began to occur.  Soon after the colonies rooted and began to grow, an irredentist society formed with in many of these colonies.   As a result, political and social reforms were not easily welcomed.

(Law, 1997:  http://) But, in the mid-seventeenth century, Massachusetts, Virginia, and Pennsylvania became the three most successful colonies within North America for these areas became cultural hearths.  These areas contained a vast number of people in which ways of life, distinctive technologies, and cultural traits could develop.  Within these societies, strong religious communities became a way of life.  In the religious communities, congregational singing was a cultural trait that had developed among the Protestant churches.  Through congregational singing many people could come together and sing for the sole purpose of praising God.  ( Getis,  55-64)

So, as congregational singing became more prevalent in the churches, the spiritual folk hymn was born.  This genre of music began within the Caucasian community and was based on many verses from Psalms.  But, during this time many people could not read music, and the only way in which a congregation could learn music was through oral repetition.   As a result, one of the technological advancements that occurred was in the development of singing schools.

                These singing schools helped teach many individuals how to sing, but unfortunately, it was at a very slow rate. For it took time to teach people how to recognize different types of notes, key signatures, and relating that to actually notes on a staff.  But, in 1790 a man from Philadelphia named John Connolly developed four distinct geometrical note shapes to represent different syllables, of the four-note syllable system (which was later to be called shaped notes).  He decided to use a triangle for the syllable “Fa”, an oval for the syllable “Sol”, a square for the syllable “La”, and a diamond for the syllable “Mi”.

 

 

(Willard, 1998:  http://)

With this new system, there was no need to worry about different key signatures and the shapes ingeniously indicated where the different pitches were, therefore the intervals were always the same between different shapes.  As a result, Connolly began to teach his family and different parts of his community a new way to read music without ever learning how to read Western musical notation.  This allowed people to ignore all previous musical teaching methods and learn how to read music at faster rate of speed.  (Law, 1997:  http://)

 But, thanks to the “Better School Movement” and individuals such as Lowell Mason, shape notes were given barriers to diffusion.  In this “Better School Movement” Mason allow and encouraged many European styled musicians to systematically remove shape notes from being taught across the colonies.  Fortunately in 1801, Connolly sold the shape note system to William Little and William Smith, who produced the first shape-note songbooks, called the Easy Instructor.  In the Easy Instructor, the rich historical and oral traditions that had been passed down from generation to generation had been published.  This was the first example of mass productions of spiritual folk hymns across America.   Unfortunately soon after, civil war broke out between the north and the south and a new barrier emerged.  As a result the friction of distance became so great the singing school movement ceased to exist and the new focus for attention was on the division between these two regions.   ( Sabol, 1998 :  http://)

Luckily around the mid 1800’s, a non-denominational singing organization developed called the Sacred Harp.  This organization developed within the small farming communities of the west and south.  The formation of this group had occurred due to the fact the modernization, a massive diffusing barrier, had not reached the smaller farming communities of the west and south.  This group usually came together prior to or directly after a harvest.  The Sacred Harp focused on the musical event and emphasized community participation, not performance.  Since the Sacred Harp focused on community development, time honored traditions began to form.  At each yearly harvest, distant communities would come together and form huge camp meeting and sing songs in the memory of others. At these camp meetings, the singers referred to, as "the class” would not sit in a formal sectional choral, but in a hollow square facing one another, each part on equal sides of the square. The "arranging committee" or coordinators allow different singers to lead various songs to “the class”. The singers are arranged around the leader.  The melody, which is the tenor section, faces the leader.  The altos are directly opposite of the tenor section while the basses are to the right and the sopranos (trebles) are to the left.  The use of instruments is totally unacceptable.  Therefore, someone with a good sense of relative pitch gives the pitch.  The pitch is usually given 1 to 2 whole steps lower than the written pitch to enables people to sing in a comfort zone.  The leader sings the tenor (lead) part if possible and determines the tempo by beating of the hand up and down with 2 or 3 beats per measure, but with slow 4/4 songs occasionally in a cross pattern with 4 beats per measure.  The singers sing with volume, intensity, and enthusiasm, which transcends down to each and every person in the room. The soprano and tenor parts are identical and are sung in unison but usually an octave apart.  The focus is on community rather than choral singing, so the use of dynamics is of less concern and the blending of voices is not observed.  The beauty and moving moments in Sacred Harp music is demonstrated through the feeling of total community involvement. (Willard, 1998:  http://)

Each song is sung first by singing the syllables of the four shape notes (fa, sol, la, mi), followed by singing of the words. This practice conforms to and honors the singing school tradition and assists in learning the tune.  But the main purpose of the formation of the Sacred Harp was to create powerful music together in a friendly, accepting atmosphere, while singing the spiritually meaningful words in remembrance of the deceased, sick, and shut-in singers from across the community.   (Sabol, 1998:  http://)  With the development of the Sacred Harp, many communities grew a close nit society in which communication barriers ceased to exist. 

But, soon the twentieth century came and modernization utterly destroyed the close nit societies.  The nuclear family was disappearing due to the urbanization of the surrounding countryside.  Also, the harvesting season was no longer a focal point for community development.  As a result, the initial appeal for community development changed from remembering the deceased or sick and shut in singers of the community to the quick hustle and bustle of city life. 

          As modernization began to slowly diffuse across the country, social change slowly broke down the racial barriers that had kept many individuals from communicating socially and religiously.  As a result, many of the white and black communities began to intermingle and exchange ideas about religion and community development.   As time progressed, the different societies began to relish the community aspects that were developed through shape note singing, as a result communication remained apparent between the black and white communities.   As time progressed, communication between the white and black communities strengthened and the time honored tradition of the Sacred Harp extended its realm to include many black historical folk spirituals. As many new publications of Sacred Harp emerged such as Colored Sacred Harp of 1934, the spiritual folk hymns began to express and exemplify themes of going home to the promise land, the defeat of satin, and gaining ground against sin, which demonstrated an existence of a historical oral tradition. (Dyen, 95-132)

Initially one might wonder where the fascination of singing shape-notes might come from.  Gaylon L. Powell said it was “one of four aspects: the unique sound, the music is for everyone to sing and not for performance, the meaningful poetry, and the strong community atmosphere. (Powell, 1996:  http://)”

 One feature for a new singer is the explosive sound.  Many people are flabbergasted when faced with this new type of music, for many times it does not resemble one’s religious affiliations.  Many find the sound to be a joyful noise but at the same time it is sung in reverence of those who have passed away.  Its unique harmony, known as dispersed harmony, may demonstrate a new since of unfamiliarity, but can leave one feeling calm and reassured. “The energy that the singers use to sing can also be appealing; with this much energy, it shows that the singers love and enjoy the music they are singing. (Powell, 1996:  http://)”

The new singer will also find comfort in the performance, for it allows everyone to participate even for the untrained singer.  If you can’t carry a tune no one will care for everyone is encouraged to sing. “The whole setting is very much like that found in Ephesians 5:19, ‘Speaking to yourselves in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing and making melody in your heart to the Lord.’ This text did not even mention the quality of the voice. (Powell, 1996:  http://)”

Many individuals are attracted to the text, for most of the songs resemble sermon and contain a meaning lesson.  Attending an all-day singing can be an overwhelming experience for many individuals who are non-religiously affiliated, for they can come to appreciate the singers spiritual ideals and move over their personal dedication.   Many people who experience Sacred Harp have never been able to duplicate its unique textual poetry.  Caleb Jarvis Taylor said it best by:

 “I find Him in singing, I find Him in prayer;
In sweet meditation He always is near,
My constant companion, O may we ne'er part;
All glory to Jesus, He dwells in my heart (Powell, 1996:  http://).”

 

 

 

The sense of community that is offered to many individuals is probably the most overwhelming influence.  Many people observe that the individuals singing are more than just friends they are family. “Dick Dunagan, of Beloit, Wisconsin, said that being welcomed to join in on the singing had a distinct feeling of ‘coming home.’ Alix Baillie, of Takoma Park, Maryland, had this observation, "The way we sing to each other, feed each other, welcome each other into our homes, accept all comers unconditionally--that's real Christianity, and 'real' most other religions as well. It is extraordinary and inexpressibly beautiful. Along with the sense of community with singers across the square is the sense of community with singers who have gone before--those recently lost, and those long-dead."

 (Powell, 1996:  http://)” Carol Gray, a music teacher of the Clinton School District, expressed that music plays an important role in community development.  She said that community development was the most apparent within the worship services for it offered a time for the congregation to sing songs that reflected on their common heritage.  Gray also expressed the overwhelming worth one feels when demonstrating the importance of music and the feeling of respect and connection between her peers. (Gray, 2000)

Community Development also played an important role in the diffusion of shape note singing to Arkansas.  Old Davidson Camp ground near Arkadelphia is an active shape notes music campsite along with Jeffrees Camp in Crossett started by Noland Jeffrees. These camps, which are active today, played an important role in the social development of these communities.  They brought families from all across Arkansas to a place were they could communicate and participate freely and openly about the joys and sorry within their lives.  However, today, many of these communities have dissolved the use of shape note singing because community development has taken a back seat to the busy life of today’s youth.    In Dardanelle the Gately Hill Baptist Church still sings by shape notes, but that is because the majority of the church members consist of the old generational shape note singers.  Although, since the centennial in 1976 many of the faithful followers have looked for a comeback, but unfortunately not much hope has been shown.  As a result, the lack of community involvement and commitment will utterly lead to the death of shape note singing.  (Williard, 1998:  http://)

Shape note singing has influenced the musical development and growth of the vast majority of communities within the United States.  Through the process of diffusion, one can see the importance of the singing school movement, and the formation of shaped notes.  One can also observe how shaped notes have influenced the communication of different societies and brought the spiritual folk hymn into a mainstream genre.   But regrettably, due to the barrier of modernization shape note singing began to digress into an unknown only to be revived for a short period of time but utterly slow down to a unfortunate slow death.
