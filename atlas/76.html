Why are earthquakes in the East more destructive than those in the West? How would an earthquake in Arkansas compare?


Earthquakes are one of the Earth’s most fascinating phenomenons. Thousands occur each day all over the world. Even though people may not feel them every time they occur, they still happen. To have a better understanding of earthquakes, an explanation about the plate tectonics theory would help. Alfred Wegener argues that many years ago, the continents of the Earth were part of one large continent called Pangea (Bergman and Renwick, 91). This is shown in Illustration I. Illustration I shows the plates of the world combined into one continent and the movement of the plates to form separate continents. Today, one huge continent does not exist because the plates moved apart from each other, forming separate continents. Illustration II shows the separated continents and labels the Earth’s plates. Pangea may have occurred only one point in time, but it shows how strong the force of convection is within the Earth. The force had to be strong enough to move the plates together and then apart again.

These plates still move today by the force of convection. Convection occurs when a gas or liquid is heated, expands, and moves closer to the cooler portion of the gas or liquid (Bergman and Renwick 47). The Earth’s core is very hot. The surface’s temperature is considerably cooler, so the heat rises up. This is due to the Earth’s need to equalize or find balance (Cossman). Illustration III shows the core of the Earth in relation to the crust or surface layer. The mantle is a section in between the crust and the core made of rocks (Roberts, 56). Convection is a result when the Earth was originally formed and a result of radioactivity (Cossman). Convection causes the movement of the plates, which produce earthquakes.

An explanation of how earthquakes occur will help create a better understanding of all this. Convection constantly pushes around the plates of the Earth. There are three different types of plate movement. One type is when two plates move apart from each other (Lauber, 50). This is called a divergent plate boundary (Bergman and Renwick, 96). A second type is when two plates meet (Lauber, 51). This type of movement is called a convergent plate boundary (Bergman and Renwick, 96). A third type of movement is when two plates move past each other (Lauber, 51). This is called a transform plate boundary (Bergman and Renwick, 96). Subduction occurs when plates move and one goes on top of another, which usually forms mountains (Cossman). Diversion occurs when plates pull apart from each other and usually forms rift valleys (Cossman). The movement of plates is shown in Illustration IV. The place where the Earth’s plates move is called the focus (Bergman and Renwick, 91). The area on the surface above the focus is called the epicenter (Hodgson, 16). Illustration V shows the focus and epicenter. Hanging wall refers to the rock above the fault, and footwall refers to the rock below the fault (Yeats, 25). Faults are explained a little later. The depth of the focus beneath the surface is the focal depth (Yeats, 25).

The movement of plates causes stress on rocks (Bergman and Renwick, 97). When the rocks are stressed far enough and fracture, it is called a fault (Bergman and Renwick, 97). Earthquakes also occur on faults (Yeats, 24). If the rock above the fault moves up or down, it is called a dip-slip fault (Yeats, 25). An example of a dip-slip fault is the reverse fault in the San Fernando Valley in California (Yeats, 27). If the rock moves parallel, it is called a strike-slip fault (Yeats, 25). An example of a strike-slip fault is the San Andreas Fault in California. “There are two kinds of strike-slip fault, right-lateral and left-lateral” (Yeats, 25). A right-lateral strike-slip fault is when objects tend to move to the right during an earthquake (Yeats, 25). A left-lateral strike-slip fault is when objects tend to move to the left during an earthquake (Yeats, 25). A normal fault is a type of dip-slip fault where the rock above the fault moves down with respect to the rock below the fault (Yeats, 25). When the rock above the fault moves up with respect to the rock below the fault, it is called a reverse fault (Yeats, 25). Researchers have named “reverse faults that do not reach the surface…blind faults” (Yeats, 25). Illustration VI shows the plate movement during a normal fault, reverse fault, blind reverse fault, right lateral strike-slip fault, and left lateral strike-slip fault.

While the plates are being pushed around, vibrations are sent up, which are called earthquakes (http://pubs.usgs.gov/). According to Roberts, “the Earth’s rocks are elastic, being strong and rigid” (102). When they are struck by motion, they vibrate. Some vibrations are of low frequency while others are high enough to be heard (http://pubs.usgs.gov/). “When the rocks vibrate the motions become waves” (Roberts, 102), and there are four types of waves. The first type is a compression wave. Compression waves travel fast through the Earth and are called primary or “P” waves (http://pubs.usgs.gov/). The second type is a transverse or shear wave. It arrives later (http://pubs.usgs.gov/). The P wave is noticed as a “sharp thud or blast-like shock” (http://pubs.usgs.gov/). It causes particles to vibrate in lines (Roberts, 103). An S wave is noticed as “a swaying or rolling motion” (http://pubs.usgs.gov/). It causes particles to vibrate from side to side (Roberts, 103). The third type is an “L” or Love wave. L waves are much longer than the other two waves. They travel on the surface of the Earth and move sideways in orbits (Roberts, 106). A fourth type of wave is the Rayleigh wave. The Rayleigh wave is the most elaborate of all and causes particles to move in vertical, circular paths (Roberts, 108). Three of the types of waves are described in Illustration VII.

The method for classifying earthquakes is the Richter Scale (Golden, 46). It is used to compare the strength of earthquakes. The magnitude of an earthquake is known as the size or strength of an earthquake’s waves (Golden, 46) and the amount of energy released (Roberts, 148). The quakes are given a numeric value, for example a “magnitude 3 quake is usually barely felt, magnitude 5 causes minor damage, and 6 or higher may lead to widespread destruction” (Golden, 46). “Each whole number represents a tenfold increase in wave size over the previous number” (Golden, 47). For example, the waves of a magnitude 7 quake are ten times that of a magnitude 6 quake (Golden, 47).

The quality of soil around an earthquake can have a large impact on the damage done. When people think about the causes of deaths during an earthquake, they tend to think of the shaking that causes buildings to fall down and the destruction of other man -made objects. It is important to realize, however, that soil plays a large role in the destruction caused by earthquakes. The characteristics of the soil on top of the ground and the severity of the quake determine the amount of damage caused by vibrations. “The important geologic factors at a[n] [earthquake] site are the (1) softness of the rock or soil near the surface and (2) thickness of the sediments above hard bedrock” (http://geopubs). Lower density sediments, like sand, allow earthquake vibrations to pass through more easily and slowly. The slower the waves move, the more intense the earthquake. Higher density sediments, like bedrock, make earthquake vibrations travel faster with less intensity (Yeats, 145). “The influence of the underlying soil on the local amplification of earthquake shaking is called the site effect” (http://usgs.gov/).

The location of the epicenter and the soil around it determines how destructive an earthquake will be. Earthquake intensity is the amount of damage an earthquake causes at a specific location (Yeats, 35). Earthquake intensity is determined by a post-earthquake survey of the area where damage occurred, and people are asked questions about what they felt (Yeats, 35). The intensities of the earthquakes are “influenced by the characteristics of the ground” (Yeats, 38). One example of this is liquefaction, which is a “physical process that takes place during some earthquakes that may lead to ground failure” (http://www.es.ucsc.edu). When the ground has a high content of fine grain sands and silts, the soil acts like a liquid rather than a solid (http://www.es.ucsc.edu). The pores between the sand and silt may loose strength and turn to liquid if shaken by an earthquake (Yeats, 147). This liquid soil is not able to support soil or buildings on top of it (http://www.es.ucsc.edu). Therefore, “buildings on solid rock tend to fare better (and thus are subjected to lower intensities) then buildings on thick soft soil” (Yeats, 38).

The Eastern United States is in the middle of a plate (http://explorezone.com/), while the Western United States is at the very edge of a plate. The proximity of the plate to the edge, would appear to cause the West have more damaging earthquakes than the East. However, this is untrue because soils play a very important role in how much damage is done. Meaning, if an earthquake were to occur in the East and West at the same exact time with the same exact magnitude, the earthquake in the East would cause more destruction. Experts predict that an earthquake in the East would be more damaging because of the soft sediment (http://explorezone.com). This would cause liquefaction to be greater in the East due to buildings in low line areas around rivers and bays (http://explorezone.com). The underlying land on the West is mainly bedrock. Due to the fact that less sediment covers the bedrock, the land is not damaged as much as the East, which is covered by lots of sediment. Differences in the sediments covering California alone prove this. According to the USGS website, “shaking levels double from the edge of the Los Angeles Basin, where the sediments are thin, to the middle of the basin, where sediments reach a thickness of more than 6 kilometers (4 miles)” (http://geopubs). The East, as a whole would be comparable to the center of the basin. The West, as a whole, would be comparable to the edges of the basin. This difference in the soil types is what would make the Eastern United States have more damaging earthquakes than the Western United States.

What is the probability of an earthquake occurring in Arkansas? Most people probably relate earthquakes to California or maybe Japan, but not Arkansas. Do not be so quick to judge. Arkansas is one of six states located along the New Madrid seismic zone (Jackson, 17). During the winter of 1811-1812, three of the largest earthquakes in North American history occurred along this region, all of which had magnitudes greater than 8.0 on the Richter scale (Stewart and Knox, 19). Since the installation of seismic instruments along the New Madrid region, it has become known that earthquakes happen all the time in the mid-south. Out of the total 150 earthquakes per year, many are too small to be felt by people (www.ceri.memphis.edu/public/). The probability of an earthquake with a magnitude of 8.0 occurring in Arkansas in the next 15 years is less than 1% (www.ceri.memphis.edu/public/). An earthquake with the magnitude 6.0 occurring within the next 15 years has a probability of 63% (www.ceri.memphis.edu/public/). “The possibility of a great earthquake happening soon in the New Madrid seismic zone is small. Scientists believe that catastrophic events like those of the 1811-12 happen in the New Madrid region every 550-1200 years” (www.ceri.memphis.edu/public/).

The type of soil located within a particular region determines the amount of damage caused by an earthquake, and Arkansas contains mainly sedimentary rocks with also some igneous rocks. Sedimentary rock is classified as being either clastic (rocks made up of grains of sand, silt, and clay) or chemical (rocks made up of shell fragments, saline water deposits, and other materials deposited from solution). Illustration VIII, which is a cross section of a portion of Arkansas, shows that bedrock can be found almost a half-mile below the surface. Therefore, earthquakes in Arkansas would be comparable to those in the East.

In conclusion, earthquakes are greatly influenced by the soil surrounding the epicenter of a region. The differences in the soil types of the East and West United States vary greatly. The East contains bedrock covered by large amounts of sediment, whereas, the West is primarily bedrock. Therefore, earthquakes in the East would tend to be more devastating than those in the West. Surprisingly, Arkansas, which is a state not known for its earthquake activity, has many that occur but are never felt. The probability of an earthquake occurring in Arkansas is small, but due to the soil coverage, the earthquake could be quite devastating.
