Prepared:  October 7, 2000

 

  How Soon Will the Grand Prairie Be Without Water?

 

 

The Grand Prairie area of Arkansas faces a tremendous problem.  Residents are running out of water.  For years, farmers in this region have relied on rice as their chief source of income.  The increased amount of land being used for rice has resulted in the need for more fresh water, and therefore more irrigation.  This need has been met by two aquifers located below the town of Stuttgart (See Appendix B for location). Unfortunately, this water supply is almost gone.

The freshwater that humans use to survive exists on Earth in many forms.  They obtain this water from lakes, streams, rivers, precipitation, and groundwater.  Groundwater is found below the earth’s surface.  Rocks below earth’s surface are either permeable or impermeable.  Permeable rock layers are able to conduct water.  When these permeable layers store groundwater in useable amounts, they are called aquifers.  Aquifers can be confined or unconfined.  The Alluvial is an example of an unconfined aquifer.  This means that there is a permeable layer of rock above the aquifer, and an impermeable layer below it.  Underneath this impermeable layer lies the Sparta aquifer.  The Sparta is a confined aquifer because it has another impermeable layer of rock located below it.  This explains why the Alluvial is rechargeable and the Sparta is not.  Water is able to soak down through the soil to the Alluvial. The Sparta cannot be recharged because impermeable layers of rock surround it (Christopherson 1994: 254). The large amount of clay in the Grand Prairie area is responsible for the formation of the two aquifers that lie beneath the surface.  Both of these aquifers have been critically depleted.

The top aquifer is the Mississippi River Alluvial.  Man-made wells reach this aquifer at depths of 135 to 210 feet (Bennett 1999: 2).  A large portion of the Alluvial is considered critical due to the fact that it has a saturated thickness of less than 40 feet (“Importance” 1998: 4). The Sparta aquifer lies below the Alluvial.  It is between 300 and 600 feet thick.  Wells must be dug anywhere from 600 to 1000 feet deep in order to reach this aquifer.  Farmers rely on the Alluvial aquifer to supply approximately 90 percent of their irrigation water.  Since the Alluvial is quickly being depleted, wells are now being sunk into the Sparta aquifer (Bennett 1999: 2). 

The Grand Prairie’s clay cap makes it a prime location for the growing of rice (UACE 1999: 12).  There are currently 5.2 million acres of irrigated cropland in Eastern Arkansas.  These crops require 2.4 trillion gallons of water for irrigation.  Approximately 2.0 trillion of those gallons are from ground water (“Importance” 1998: 1).  Wells that tap into the aquifer are beginning to pump sand (Bennett 1999: 1).  Farmers began irrigating the area in the 1940’s.  Soybean production increased the rate of irrigation when international trade resulted in greater demand for the crop in the 1970’s.  Farmers needed more and more water to grow the soybeans.  It has reached the point where there just is not enough water to support the large number of crops.  Farmers have long depended on runoff from other farms to supply their crops with water.  Landowners are now beginning to catch their own runoff (Bennett 1999: 2-4).  This runoff will only provide 20 percent of the water necessary to irrigate crops (Bennett 1999: 5).  Appendix A shows the increase in groundwater use throughout the years.  These numbers cannot continue to grow because there simply is not enough water left.  The Alluvial aquifer will no longer be able to supply water to the Grand Prairie by the year 2015 (UACE 2000: 12).

This loss will have dire effects on the economy of the Grand Prairie.  Rice production will drop by 23 percent.  Farmers will be forced to switch to growing soybeans, which require less water to survive (UACE 2000: 12).  If nothing is done to correct the problem, the Grand Prairie will lose an astonishing 67,980 acres of rice and 62,792 acres of soybeans by the year 2015 (Bennett 1999: 5).  This could present a problem, considering the fact that 42 percent of the United States’ rice is produced in Arkansas.  There will be an 18 to 20 billion dollar impact on Arkansas’ economy (“Importance” 1998: 1).  The people of the Grand Prairie also depend on the aquifers for drinking water.  The Sparta aquifer is a source of clean, pure water.  The Sparta faces two problems.  Not only will it be completely dry in 15 years, now salt water that is located beneath the aquifer is bleeding up into the freshwater.  The water will soon be undrinkable (“Bumpers” 1999: 2).  Another consequence of the loss of water is the resulting effect on waterfowl and aquaculture.    According to the US Army Corps of Engineers, Stuttgart is both the rice and duck capitol of the world.  Rice supplies these waterfowl with food needed for their survival.  About 17,400 acres of rice are flooded every winter in order to feed the ducks (UACE 2000: 12).  As for aquaculture, more than 80 percent of the United States’ commercially raised fish bait is produced in Arkansas.  There are 58,000 acres of land devoted to aquaculture.  The loss of this water will have a 600 million dollars impact on the state’s economy. 

The Grand Prairie Demonstration Project is now being developed to help solve this problem.  This project will increase irrigation efficiency and on-farm storage, make better use of groundwater, and import water from the Arkansas and White Rivers (“Importance” 1998: 3).  It will cover a total of 394,475 acres.  Under this project, only seven percent of the total water supply will be provided by groundwater in order to allow recharging of the Alluvial.  More acres of on-farm storage reservoirs will also be added.  Canals, channels, pipelines, and a pumping station on the White River will be created in order to import water to farms (“Importance” 1998: 5).  The US Geological Survey (USGS), the Army Corps of Engineers, the Natural Resources Conservation Service (NRCS), the Arkansas Soil and Water Conservation Commission, Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, and the Arkansas Heritage Commission are all working together to make this project work.  The White River Irrigation District (WRID), which was formed by farmers’ petitions and court order, will own and operate the project.  The main factor in this project will be the White River.  Canals, pipes, and streams will be used to deliver water from the river to farms.  Pumping will stay within certain limits, so there will not be a negative effect on the river (“Washington” 1999: 2).  State law places restrictions upon diverting water from the White River when its water flow is at low levels.  The project will divert a maximum of two percent of the flow of the White River.  Less than one-half of one percent of the flow will be diverted during waterfowl season.  The project will withdraw very little water from the river during the months that the river normally floods.  Studies conducted by the Arkansas Natural Heritage Commission and the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission have shown that the changes that will result from the project will be similar to natural changes (UACE 2000: 11).   Land will have to be obtained from farmers to be used in the project.  The WRID does not favor invoking their power of imminent domain, which would mean taking land from the farmers without their consent. The White River Irrigation District is also in charge of getting a majority of landowners to sign petitions stating that they want to move ahead with the project.  February 29, 2000 was set as a deadline for signatures (“Washington” 1999: 2).  A majority of signatures from the 1012 landowners in Arkansas, Lonoke, Monroe, and Prairie counties was obtained to meet this deadline (“Grand” 2000: 1).

The 300 million dollar project is scheduled to begin construction in the fall of 2000 and to be completed in six years. The 30-year bonds used for finance will be paid off in 36 years (UACE 200: 6).  The government will provide 65 percent of the cost; which will leave at most, 35 percent of the project to be funded by landowners (“Bumpers” 1999: 3).  This 81.6 million dollars will come from an annual assessment of irrigated land and the sale of water (Bennett 1999: 6).  The annual assessment will cost farmers between one and three dollars per irrigated acre, depending on how much their land will benefit from the project (UACE 2000: 2).  Water will cost farmers approximately 28 dollars per acre-foot (Bennett 1999: 6).  The WRID will be responsible for transporting this water to the farms.  Farmers will then buy the water to use as they see fit (UACE 2000: 2). 

The US Army Corps of Engineers has determined that the project will not have negative effects on the environment.  A team of experts from the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service, Natural Resources Conservation Service, Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, Arkansas Natural Heritage Commission, and Arkansas Soil and Water Conservation Commission concluded that the environment would not be harmed by this project (UACE 2000: 12).  The greatest impact on the river will be a one-foot decrease at its lowest point.  This should not affect animal habitats.  There was a concern that mussels from the White River would be introduced into streams and tributaries, thereby harming the native mussels.  Surveys concluded that the project would not be the cause of a problem since this introduction would occur eventually with or without the

project (UACE 2000: 14-15).  The project will actually benefit the environment in many ways.  A plan is being developed to supply ducks with a habitat that will be flooded annually within the project’s boundaries.  This will help to ensure that people will be able to continue relying on these ducks as a source of income.  Sadly, only 560 acres of natural Grand Prairie land remain.  The Grand Prairie project will plant and protect native vegetation.  The new channels that will be built will provide more habitats for fish.  One of the goals of the project is to protect the environment along with the rice crops (“Importance” 1998: 4).

The US Army Corps of Engineers took many alternative plans into consideration before deciding on this project.  The first alternative was to take no action at all.  This suggestion was quickly discarded.  The second alternative was to build more on-farm reservoirs.  This would not work because there would be no source to supply the reservoirs with water.  Simply conserving water would result in only 31 percent of useable land.  Other alternatives included importing and conserving water without additional storage, and combining conservation, storage, and importation with and without additional storage (UACE 2000: 4).  The Grand Prairie Area Demonstration Project includes the best aspects of all of these alternatives.

 Opposition from farmers came along with the alternative measures.  Many landowners were afraid of losing their land and being charged too much money for water under this plan.  This opposition began to decrease due to recent education efforts that were made by the WRID.  Farmers will not lose their property rights.  In fact, their rights to runoff, streams, and reservoirs are not threatened.  Farmers do not have to buy the water at all if they do not wish to, and they are free to continue to use their own wells (Bennett 1999: 6).

The Grand Prairie Demonstration Project is a necessity for the people of the Grand Prairie.  This project will help to stop, and hopefully reverse the damage that is being done to the Alluvial and Sparta aquifers.  Rice farming is extremely important to the economy of the Grand Prairie Area.  Officials are beginning to realize that they are facing a serious problem.  The Grand Prairie Demonstration Project seems to be the best solution.  The future of the Grand Prairie depends on the success of this project.
