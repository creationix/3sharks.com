Why is Arkansas considered part of tornado alley?

A7: Physical Geography: Tornado alley

A7: Physical Geography: Tornado alley

“Why is Arkansas considered part of tornado alley?”

By Jennifer Lee, Shannon Stephens, Aaron LaMaster (TAZ)

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2619

Prepared: March 3, 2001


Imagine if you will. It is a dark stormy night. The rain outside is increasing and the wind begins to howl. All you can hear is thunder, and all you can see is lightning. The news keeps flashing radar updates on the radio and says that there is a possible tornado. You think as you sit in your bathtub in your little house in Conway Arkansas, “Could I possibly be in danger of being hit by a tornado?” You can be the judge of that after you examine the following information on tornadoes and tornado alley.

First of all, one needs to know exactly what a tornado is. What climatic features cause a tornado? What geographical features cause a tornado? Then, one should have sufficient information on what tornado alley actually is. What climatic and geographical features influence this alley? Finally, with this information a person should be able to draw their own conclusions as to whether or not they are in danger on a night as described above.

What is a tornado? This question seems simple enough, but actually has many complex factors involved. A tornado is a vertically spinning cell of wind that can reach speeds of nearly 300 miles per hour (Clayborne, 2000). Many factors, climatically and geographically, cause a tornado.

Climatically, tornadoes have potential to be much more common than they are. The United States climate is very good for tornadoes. Tornadoes love warm, humid air along low-pressure fronts that are frequently found over the central United States (University of Michigan, 2000). These clashing fronts give a perfect spawning ground for tornadoes. Tornadoes are formed before thunderstorms when a change in wind direction and increase in wind speed with increasing altitude creates an invisible, horizontal spinning effect in the lower atmosphere. These winds are then hit by warm updrafts causing the horizontal winds to move vertically in a funnel like structure (USA Today, 2001). These updrafts are caused by convection of warm air as it makes its way north. The lower, warm air is less dense than the higher, cooler air, thus the warm air rushes up when it meets a cold front due to convection (USA Today, 2001). HERE INSERT A PICTURE OF UPDRAFT!

Convective caps and atmospheric layers work together to influence tornado formation. Strong upper level troughs moving across the plains cause convective caps. A convective cap acts like a cap over a body of air. This cap prevents convection from the warmer air (USA Today, 2001). At the surface, southerly winds move warm, humid air from the Gulf into the plains. In the middle layers of the atmosphere, the winds veer to the southwest and transport hot, dry air from the southwest. This forms a layer of hot, dry air in which the temperature often increases with height. At the high layers of the atmosphere, westerly winds transport cool, moist air from the Pacific Ocean which go up over the convective cap. This becomes the perfect setting for severe weather by having warm, humid air near the surface and hot, dry air above it. Then, adding cool, moist air at the higher levels just intensifies the chance for severe weather (Geoffery, 2000). (PUT U.S. AND WINDS PHOTO HERE). (X-SEC OF ATMOSPHERE HERE AS WELL). From this data one can see that the United States is the perfect spawning ground for tornadoes.

Geographically, the United States is very supportive of tornadoes as well. The way the U.S. is positioned gives us clashing of different humidities due to mountain ranges, deserts, and oceans on both sides. The oceans cause moist, cool air to blow in from the west. This air is then forced up over the Rockies where it decreases in temperature and moisture. After rising over the mountains it settles on the warmer air that is blowing up from the south due to the Gulf of Mexico and its warm climate. Then, hot, dry air that is caused by the deserts and plains blows in from the southwest (Jennings, 1970). All of these different air masses have different characteristics due to the different geographical features they blow over before they meet. This difference is where the entire tornado formation recipe comes from. (SHOW A MAP HERE OF THE U.S. AND ITS DIFFERENT GEOGRAPHIES).

Now, put these factors together over a particular part of the United States and you can begin to see the path of tornado alley. Tornado alley is defined as the area of the United States in which tornadoes are most frequent. It encompasses the great lowland areas of the Mississippi, the Ohio, and lower Missouri River Valleys. They are most frequent between the Rocky Mountains and the Appalachians (Davies, 2001). Taking into consideration the formation of tornadoes, as discussed above, you know that tornadoes love warm, humid air. Also, tornadoes need low-level winds that shift frequently, and grow stronger just above the ground. Large storms also grow best when there is no competition from other storms (Rotter, 1997). Most often, these requirements are met in the central United States. (SHOW MAP OF TORNADO ALLEY ON UNITED STATES).

In the central U.S., all of these different air masses collide. As these air masses collide, they have no other option but to slide on top or bottom of one another (Jennings, 1970). When this happens you can see the possible formation of convective caps as described above. When these convective caps restrict the convection of the warm air, a lot of pressure is built up. Then, when the convective cap is broken, all hell breaks loose. Typically this creates enough force to cause a severe thunderstorm or a tornado over the central United States in the region known as tornado alley (USA Today, 2001).

With this information one should know why tornadoes form over tornado alley, but what makes them use tornado alley as a travel route? A geographic look at the features of the United States reveals that actually tornado alley is somewhat like a funnel for tornadoes. A cross section of the United States shows that the Rockies in the west and the Appalachians in the east form high boundaries on the sides. Then, by looking at the cross section one can see that the gradual slope of the land between the two mountain ranges comes to a valley at the Mississippi River (Davis, 1992). This acts as a boundary line for a tornado to travel down once it is formed from the colliding air masses.

Another reason tornadoes travel down this path is that tornadoes like to travel the path of least resistance (Rotter, 1997). Obviously this path occurs between the two mountain ranges. The tornado has nothing blocking its path, therefore it just moves along with the front as the front makes its way through tornado alley. (SHOW X_SEC OF US HERE).

Now to answer the question, “Why is Arkansas considered part of tornado alley?” One obvious reason is that Arkansas falls in the central United States. Remember from above that the central U.S. is where all the air masses meet and influence tornado activity.

Another reason Arkansas is considered part of tornado alley is because of its proximity to the Mississippi River. Remember from the picture above that the Mississippi River is the lowest part tornado alley, thus tornadoes tend to use this route as an efficient path to travel. Also consider that Arkansas doesn’t offer many obstacles for the tornadoes to run into such as mountain ranges or other geographical features, and tornadoes like to travel the path of least resistance.

So, in conclusion, due to climatic factors present over Arkansas, as well as the geographical position of Arkansas, Arkansas is definitely in the path of tornado alley! So if you are in your bathtub in Conway Arkansas wondering if a tornado is going to hit your house, this information should help you make your decision. Our advice is hold on tight; it could be a wild ride!
