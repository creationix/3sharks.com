P E D I G O G Y

What is Arkansas Atlas?


The Arkansas Atlas is a Web-based collection of UCA student research projects related specifically to Arkansas physical and cultural geography. However it is much more than a collection of information. It is a high technology device being used to enhance our student’s engagement with learning, increase their level of effort, and publicize UCA’s student accomplishments on the World Wide Web, according to Arkansas Atlas creator Ron Cossman, a lecturer in the Geography department and Resident Master of Hughes Residential College.

The rough draft or “beta” version is on a student’s web site while final touches are made before it is place on the Geography, Political Science and Sociology web site. It may currently be viewed at: http://www.3sharks.com/atlas.

The Atlas serves several purposes. First, it enables students to put additional effort and pride into their class work. “Simply knowing that their projects will be read by others, perhaps even their grandmother, students take more responsibility for their projects,” Cossman said. Anecdotal evidence from the students themselves confirms that they approach this learning project much more seriously, given the higher visibility and stakes involved. The experiences from the Arkansas Atlas project will be used as the basis for an application to the National Science Foundation for a special grant to further explore the impact of technology in the classroom on educational outcomes.

The second purpose of the electronic Atlas is to serve as a springboard for similar student research in other disciplines. “There is no reason that students in an Arkansas history class could not take some of the information in the Atlas, expand upon it, and publish additional research. Our Geographic Information Systems (GIS) class could develop electronic maps to supplement some of the projects. The combinations are endless,” Cossman said.

The third purpose of the Atlas is to support writing across the curriculum. Initially students do not expect that they will be writing in a geography course. When they discover the breadth of geographic topics, they easily develop an affinity for a topic on which they can write about. Current educational research documents the fact that students who are interested in their course material will engage it at a higher level.

The fourth purpose of the Atlas is to establish a unique body of information about Arkansas and the region. “The variety of topics that the students have developed as research papers is amazing. Topics range from the risk of earthquakes and the history of volcanoes in Arkansas to the spatial diffusion of music in Arkansas and where social control districts are located on UCA’s campus,” Cossman said.

The final purpose of the Atlas is to be a very visible, high technology recruitment tool. “Marketed properly, the Atlas could be the crown jewel of UCA’s freshman recruitment program. We can point to it and say, ‘Look what our freshman are involved in, and capable of doing, on the Web. Don’t you want to be a part of that freshman class?’” said Cossman. Marketing on the Web is also virtually cost free, making it an attractive tool to market UCA to computer savvy would-be students.

Cossman plans three more major improvements to the Atlas before the end of the semester. First, a section entitle Pedagogy will be added. This will include a description of how the Atlas was incorporated into a freshman Principles of Geography class, a copy of the class syllabus and on-line copies of forms used in class including student permission to publish their research forms. The second addition will be two disclaimers. The first is a warning that the work is student research and not peer reviewed or tested. A second warning will also be posted on how to properly cite the material contained on the web site. “Plagiarism was a major issue among my students, but not in the way that you would think. The ‘C’ students were concerned that they properly cite all their material and not be guilty of plagiarism. It was my ‘A’ students who were concerned that other people would plagiarize them!” Cossman said.

The third addition will be the completion of up-loading all the maps, audio clips and video clips that are part of some of the Atlas projects. “I continually emphasized to my students that this was not to be a static paper report. Exploit the full capabilities of the Web by including audio, video, animation, anything the web can handle. Make your report come alive.” Cossman said.
