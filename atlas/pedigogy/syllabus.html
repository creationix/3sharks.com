I. Course Description and Objectives

The rationale for this course derives from the fact that geography is everywhere. Yet people remain generally ignorant of geography for two reasons. First, they assume that memorizing all the state capitols is the same as learning geography. It is not. Rote memorization simply means that we now have a skeleton of facts on which we can now begin to place the muscles, organs and all the other cool stuff. The second reason that people often mis-specify geography is that they are not looking for it. They have not been sensitized to the geographies of; politics, war, religion, land forms, culture, gender relations or economics. Once you have successfully completed this course, you will be able to literally see geography everywhere.

The purpose of Geography 1305 is to offer the student a more integrated (holistic) view of the world in which we live, as well as an understanding of the underlying processes. The course is concerned with the evolution of the regional variation on earth, especially as exemplified by humans and their interaction with the earth. The physical environment, history, and technological changes all affect this variation and will be combined with factual descriptions of the present to give us a better understanding of the world today.

This particular section of Geography 1305 rocks. It offers students a unique opportunity, that of performing research to answer a geographic question of their choice and publishing those findings on UCA’s World Wide Web server. Group research projects in this class will be added to the Arkansas Atlas project, a proposed web-based atlas of geographic information about Arkansas. Students in this will become published authors, and, in the process, contribute to the geographic knowledge about their state, region and world.

Principles of Geography is a basic survey course that should help all students, regardless of their majors, to understand the increasingly complex world and its problems, and probable future. The course objectives are:

1. To teach the student to “think geographically.” This means to take into account fundamental issues such as: assumptions, scale, context and meaning.

2. To enable the student to be able to more intelligently discuss and understand basic interrelationships between societies and their environments;

3. To gain a better understanding of the physical processes that affect and change our planet, as well as how they affect and are affected by human’s actions;

4. To enable the student to better understand non-western cultures; and

5. To show how interdependent the United States is with the rest of the world: physically, economically and culturally.

V. Plagiarism/Academic Dishonesty

Plagiarism is the act of using someone else’s words, pictures, ideas, or procedures without proper acknowledgment, or to present them as if they originated with you. In science and especially in academics, plagiarism is unacceptable. In an exam, for example, copying from someone else’s test booklet and handing it in as if it were your own work is plagiarism. In some instances it is difficult to document whether plagiarism has occurred. In other situations, particularly a learning situation, it is possible that students who do not know the protocols of academic expression can inadvertently plagiarize. In some cultures, direct use of another person’s works bring great honor to the quoted person. In this university, plagiarism constitutes a form of cheating, and will not be tolerated. If you are unsure whether to cite someone else’s work as you write an assignment, come talk to me about it.

University regulations regarding academic misconduct, as set forth in the UCA Student Handbook and other University documents and publications, will be strictly enforced. Penalties for plagiarism or cheating range from failing the assignment to being expelled from the University.

The Arkansas Atlas project places an additional burden upon the author in this class. First, all material that is not original (i.e., yours) must be cited. However the Arkansas Atlas projects are also teaching tools. Their purpose is to help people learn more about geographic concepts and Arkansas’ geography. As such, they will view your Arkansas Atlas projects as the starting point and, probably, want to learn more. Information that you might consider “common knowledge” or easily obtained from a reference book, such as the constitution of Arkansas, is not so common to a reader from Belize. Therefore, also cite material as a means of directing the reader to source documents and other information. Do not assume that they are familiar with our region, culture, government or economy. When in doubt, ask about citations. What is “common knowledge” here, is not necessarily that around the globe.

VII. Helpful Hints

A few hints (borrowed from Dr. Jeff Allender’s geography syllabus) for success in this class (and life in general). Stay up-to-date on the readings. Now only will it prepare you for the exams, but you will follow the lectures and discussion much better than if you are coming in unprepared. In will allow you to contribute to the class discussions and to intelligently analyze the ideas and concepts we will discuss later.

As most of the exam questions will come from the class discussion and lectures (being reinforced by the readings), taking good, efficient notes is essential. Study in your Atlas groups and go over your notes regularly to make sure that you are getting the main ideas. In class, look for the main ideas and concepts. In the book they are highlighted by subheads. I attempt to verbally identify major breaks and new concepts in my lecture. Also refer to the outline that I put on the board for each lecture. If, at the end of class you have taken more than a page to a page and one half of notes for every 30 minutes of class, then you are focusing on the examples, not the larger concepts. On the other hand, if you are taking less than half a page per 30 minutes of class, you either already know the material and this is simply review, or you are not catching the major concepts. Critical listening, and note taking are acquired skills, but they are essential in life for the purpose of seeing the big picture and making the connections to everyday life.

Finally, if you are having trouble in class or are not doing well on the exams, please see me immediately. I work on campus. I live on campus. If my 18 office hours per week are not convenient, we can make arrangements for another time. Working on a problem early in the semester is often successful, whereas waiting until the last week is simply too late. You will note from the calendar that fully half of the total points in this class will be awarded by midterm. If you are flunking at that point, it may be mathematically impossible for you to pull it out at the end. Put the work in at the front end of the semester.

Geography Code of Conduct


In the Department of Geography, Political Science and Sociology, instructors strive to create an atmosphere of mutual trust and respect in which learning, debate, and intellectual growth can thrive. Creating this atmosphere requires that instructors and students work to achieve a classroom in which learning is not disrupted. At the most basic level this means that everyone attend class, be prepared with readings and assignments completed, and that students pay attention. This means no conversations with friends, reading the newspaper, coming late, or leaving early. Such behavior is disruptive to the instructor and to your fellow classmates.


These basics of classroom etiquette are an important means of building and showing mutual respect. Inevitably, however, disagreements will arise. Sometimes these disagreements will be about content, sometimes about grades or course procedures, and sometimes they will be about the treatment of participants in the class. In order to facilitate the resolution of these disagreements, the following guidelines should be followed by everyone:


C All interactions must be guided by mutual respect and trust.


C If you are bothered by some aspect of the class, identify what it is that is bothering you and center the discussion on that issue.


C Address issues that concern you early. Problems are easier to resolve before they fester.


C Consider whether it is best to address your concerns in class or in a separate appointment with the instructor. Remember, behavior that disrupts your follow classmates is not acceptable.


C Abusive speech or behavior will not be tolerated in any interaction between students or between student and instructor. If an instructor feels that your speech or behavior is abusive, you will be asked to leave the room. If you believe an instructor has become abusive, you may leave the room and talk with the department chairperson. Debate and discussion can continue when all parties proceed with mutual respect.


C If mutual respect cannot be restored, either you or the instructor may take the issue to the department chairperson or the Campus Ombudsman Office.

Guide to citing sources for Geography 1305


In the atlas and reaction paper assignments for this class you are asked to draw upon multiple sources to help support your atlas chapter or argument. Standards of academic honesty require that when you quote directly from a text you must use quotation marks and cite the source. When you paraphrase or use one or more of another=s ideas you must indicate the source and give the exact page or pages from which the ideas or paraphrasing were taken. Failure to do so constitutes plagiarism. The following is a guide to citing sources in this class.


Quotation:

AFamine is usually due to a breakdown in the economic and political institutions which ensure food security the distribution of surplus to regions suffering shortages@ (Hartmann 1995:178).


Paraphrase:

Famine is usually caused by the breakdown of economic and political institutions (Hartmann 1995:178).

Note the period comes after the citation.


A variation:

Hartmann (1995:178) argues that famine is usually caused by the breakdown of economic and political institutions.


Works Cited:

At the end of your chapter or essay you need to provide a list of works cited. An entry for an articles might look similar to this:


Hartmann, Betsy. 1995. AThe Malthusian orthodoxy.@ Chapter 2 in Reproductive Rights and Wrongs: The Global Politics of Population Control. Revised Edition. Boston: South End Press.


When citing two or more books or articles written by the same author in the same year, use a letter after the date. For example, two articles by Gould might be labeled 1993a and 1993b and the bibliographic entries would read in part:


Gould, Peter, 1993a. ATransmission Break: a geography of the condom@ ... [etc.]

Gould, Peter, 1993b. AHow things spread, hierarchical jumps, and spatial contagion@ ... [etc.]


When citing an article that is available in both printed and electronic form, give both locations in the citation. Note the Aor@ in this entry.


Beck, Roy. April 1994. AThe Ordeal of Immigration in Wausau,@ The Atlantic Monthly, 273(4):84-97, or: http://www.theatlantic.com/election/connection/immigrant/beckf.htm.


When citing a document that is only available in electronic form, give the full Internet address.


IPUMS, 1998, AIPUMS-98 User=s Guide,@ http://www.ipums.umn.edu/~ipums98/intro.html.


Remember, the purpose is to identify ideas or information that are not your own, and to provide the reader with enough information that they can locate the original source document. If in doubt, ask!



Geography 1305: Principles of Geography

An Overview of the Arkansas Atlas Assignments


Purpose:

The purpose of this series of assignments is two-fold. First, the assignment’s purpose is to gather sufficient information about Conway and Arkansas so that an atlas of the physical and human geography of the area can be published. The second purpose is to provide you with an interactive geography experience, to allow you to collect information about processes that affect you on a daily basis.


Nonexclusive List of Topics


A. PHYSICAL GEOGRAPHY (Assignment #1)

1 Soils, underlying structures and their history

2 Earthquakes

3 Erosion (as it shapes the land, constraints to development)

4 Rivers and streams (location, usages, source, pollutants, etc.)

5 Groundwater (source, location, usage, pollutants)

6 Air (temperature, wind speed and direction, pollutants)

7 Climate (temperature, moisture, seasons, determinants)

8 Ecosystems: Tie the systems together

9 Pollution: (sources, effects)

10 Human Impacts: Plants and Animals (exotics, hunting, farming)

11 Human Impacts: Agriculture (location, crops, markets, inputs, outputs)

12 Human Impacts: Development (type, location, inputs, products, outputs)

B. HUMAN GEOGRAPHY (Assignment #2)

1 Demographics (population, birth rates, death rates, growth rates, etc.)

2 Cultural Attributes (ethnicity, race, religion, language, gender)

3 Spatial Behavior (urban hierarchy, nodes and links)

4 Political Geography (history of the state/nation, have the boundaries changed over time, who was here first, what/who are the city/county named after, political affiliation of the city/counties/state, political culture)

5 Economic Geography (will WalMart take over the world, how has the order of certain goods changed, why are jobs leaving the U.S., what kind of jobs are coming to the state, to Conway?

6 Natural Resource Geography (why is rice king, what happened to cotton, what minerals do we mine, are were a “resource endowed” state?)

7 Urban Geography (will Conway’s downtown survive, why is growth on the fringe).

8 Regional Geography (what role does Conway play for Little Rock?)

9 The Geography of Music (where did gospel, blues, folk music come from, how did it get here, what is the “native” music).

10 Socially Constructed Geographies (why do we have gated communities?).


C. GEOGRAPHIC INFORMATION SYSTEMS (Note whether this is Assignment #1 or #2)

1. Availability of Maps (digital, vector/raster, satellite, Conway, Faulkner, Arkansas, etc.)

2. Mapping the Atlas (using Intergraph’s MGE or ESRI’s ArcView to map the data that are supplied)


The Atlas Assignments:

Each student will review the list of proposed topics. Consider your interests, your background and experience, maybe even skim the text if you are unfamiliar with some of the topics (see Web page listings in some chapters). The point of this assignment is that you choose to spend more time with a topic that you like, or a topic that you would like to learn more about. I will consider alternative topics that are not on the list IF three or more students propose them and IF you talk to me before bids are due. Each student will bid on one topic (from physical or GIS) for the first Atlas project, and one alternative topic. During the second half of the semester you will bid on a topic from the list of either human geography topics or GIS. The reason for the alternative topic is that I will not allow an Atlas group to be smaller than three members, and if your primary topic has no other bidders, then I will assign you to your alternative topic.


Bids:

Once you have selected your topic (and alternative), each student will make a typed bid for a topic, and make a case for researching that topic (your interest in the topic, your special experience/skills/training, why you in particular would do the best research job for that topic, and a “rough” idea of where you would go for information and what you would collect). Warning: Each bid MUST include: your name, your topic, the number from the list (e.g. “C.2 Mapping the Atlas”) and the same for your alternative bid. Based on bids I will assign individuals into teams for each topic. Following assignment to a team, each team will elect a coordinator and coin a team name (bonus points for creativity). Group members will also fill out and sign a “Group Contract” that outlines their responsibilities to the group.

There are three separate stages of Atlas project development. The first is a rough or draft proposed research agenda. This includes; 1. the research question(s), 2. proposed sources of information, proposed resources to be used, and 3. plan of action (who is responsible for what, when, etc.). The purpose of the outline is to help you focus your research question, tackle a reasonable topic, and provide ideas for resources. It is also designed to head off future problems. These drafts will be commented upon and returned in class. At that time a format for the final submission will be distributed in class. The second stage of the Atlas project development is the draft paper. The draft must be reviewed by the University Writing Center (Irby 118) prior to submission in class. Reservations are not required, but highly recommended. This should be done a few days in advance since the reviewer may make comments and suggestions for improvements that you want to incorporate. Please note: No draft Atlas projects will be accepted without a Writing Center review slip attached to the document. Drafts are just that, the work completed to date. I do not expect a completed paper. By the same token, you should have most of your research done and some of your text and references. There will be detailed comments, criticisms, and suggestions on your drafts. The third and final stage of the Atlas project is the final submission. As to participation, note that, as part of the submission, each member of the team will grade the contribution of other members of the team. These grading sheets are confidential and yes, if a group member has contributed zero to the project you may grade them as a zero (for 20% of their grade, the weight of the Atlas project).

In approaching these research projects keep in the mind the purposes of this activity. The first purpose, is to allow you to delve into a research topic that interests you. The discipline of geography is broad enough to encompass a number of interesting issues and problems. So, select a topic that you would like to learn more about.

The second purpose is to produce research that is interesting, informative and publishable, with the end result of collecting these research assignments into a publishable atlas. You must approach your assignment from the perspective that you are writing a chapter in an geographic atlas about Conway and Arkansas. In very general terms you must poise a geographically related question and then answer it, and in the process tell an interesting story. In other words, tell an informative story that any layperson could understand and follow.

You must demonstrate some creativity in your research sources. No more than half the material used may be from Internet sites. Other sources include (but are not limited to): government publications, annuals, atlases, books, journals, magazines, guides, newspapers (Nexis is an acceptable source), UCA library archives and interviews. Sources that are prohibited in the Atlas are encyclopedias and dictionaries. You may use them as background information, but you may not cite them as a source in your Atlas. This is college and we have moved beyond encyclopedias. I encourage students to interview people working in their respective fields to gather the latest information relating to their topic (e.g., faculty, instructors, local and state officials, etc).

Warning: All material that is not original must be properly cited (see the statement in the syllabus about plagiarism). Citing material serves two purposes. First, it identifies primary (original) versus secondary information. Work that is not cited is assumed to be your own original work. Second, it enables readers to locate the original source of information.


Format

The final assignments will be submitted in both digital and hard copy form. All digital submissions must be in Microsoft Word and/or Excel and/or ArcView (a GIS format). The format is 12 point, Times New Roman font, paper margins of one inch on all sides, and double spaced. Additional details about the assignment will be announced in class.
