How does agriculture affect Arkansas’ water system?

                Arkansas’ primary industry doesn’t seem like an industry at all; it’s agriculture.  Agriculture plays an important role in the lives and health of the state’s aquifers ( the primary sources of water), rivers, lakes and streams.  Not only is over usage a problem, but erosion and contaminated runoff from cattle, poultry and agricultural chemicals also affect our state’s water.  In the following pages, will examine, in more detail, the affects of each on Arkansas’ water system.

                Ground water is an important resource in Arkansas.  It consists of water that fills the voids, or open spaces, in rock and mineral particles in soil or bedrock.  If ground water is present it is of no use if it is not accessible to humans.  The accessibility is determined by the porosity and permeability.  The porosity is the measure of the volume of voids (or the pore space) in the body.  The permeability is the measure of how easily the water can flow from pore to pore within the body.  The porosity depends on the number of the voids and their size, as well as the permeability of the body.  The bodies that are porous and permeable enough to provide significant amounts of ground water to wells and springs are called aquifers.  The amount of water pumped out of these aquifers, over a sustained period of time, is measured in gallons per minute or GPM.  The aquifers that usually have the greatest yield are made of highly porous and permeable sandstone, unconsolidated layers of sand and gravel, and beds or zones of highly fractured rock of any type. () 

                Most of the ground water used in Arkansas is for agricultural use in the Coastal Plain and the wider parts of the Arkansas River Valley.  Every year between 3,000 and 4,000 new wells for domestic use, community supplies, crop irrigation, and commercial purposes are drilled throughout the state.  During an average year about 4.7 billion gallons of water is taken from Arkansas wells.  About 4.3 billion gallons is used for irrigation alone.

            Because of this distribution total withdrawal during the growing season is much higher.  The Quaternary deposits of sand and gravel in the Mississippi River Embayment provides a large percentage of the ground water used in Arkansas.  This aquifer is referred to as the Mississippi River Valley alluvial aquifer.  An alluvial aquifer is one that is surrounded by clay, sand, and/or gravel.  The irrigation wells ranging from 100 to 200 feet deep usually yield between 1,000 and 2,000 gallons per minute.  However, much of the water taken from Quaternary aquifers cannot be used for human consumption.  This is due to the high iron content of the water.  Most of the water used for domestic and municipal needs come from the deeper Tertiary and Cretaceous aquifers that lie under the Gulf Coastal Plain.  Here is a breakdown of Arkansas’ major aquifers and their main uses:

The Cockfield aquifer is the only source of serviceable ground water for the communities in southeastern Arkansas ( ). The portion of the aquifer that lies in Arkansas ranges from 300 to 400 feet in depth and provides between 10 and 300 gallons per minute.  Below the Cockfield aquifer lies the Sparta aquifer.  The top of the Sparta aquifer is between 200 and 500 feet deep.  However, in some wells, water has been drawn from in excess of 1000 feet.  Some of these wells may yield up to 1,200 gallons per minute. The Sparta aquifer and the Mississippi River Valley alluvial aquifer are the most productive aquifers in Arkansas.  The extensive pumping of these two aquifers has caused a decline in the water levels throughout the Gulf Coastal Plan region in the southern and eastern parts of the state.  It has also resulted in lower well yields and the deterioration of the chemical quality of water in much of the region.

The Wilcox aquifer and the Nacatoch aquifer are deeper than the Sparta aquifer and the Mississippi River Valley alluvial aquifer.  However, their water is too saline for most uses, therefore they are mainly used for public supplies. 

            While agriculture does affect Arkansas’ water in many  ways, it’s important not to overlook the effects of erosion, runoff and the physical reshaping of agricultural lands on our water systems.  In the highlands of Arkansas, agriculture isn’t to blame for most of the erosion of topsoil.  The highest percentage of direct runoff occurs from erosion of roadsides and road banks, but in the eastern lowlands, back swamps and delta areas it is a different story.

                  It is these areas that are most affected by agricultural erosion.  Here agriculture’s effects don’t manifest themselves openly, but rather hide behind masks of deforestation and wet land drainage.  Over the last one hundred and fifty years, the vast majority of Arkansas’ lowland forests have been eliminated and its natural wet lands drained.  A vast network of ditches have turned the back swamps into dry, flat crop lands.  Most were initially cleared to prevent constantly wet conditions which hindered successful farming and development.  (Cullom 1992: 15)  The land has been sliced into a grid of large main outlet ditches connected to intermediate lateral ditches which, in turn, are connected to smaller ditches that collect runoff at the local farm level.  (Cullom 1992:15-16)  The above  illustration shows the progression and extent of the drainage project

          While erosion’s affects are visible on the large scale in the form of wash outs and loss of soil, it’s important not to lose sight of what runoff does to the environment and the organisms in it, but also the way it influences future agricultural success.  For millions of years, the Mississippi and Arkansas Rivers flooded upstream, and natural erosion carried the rich silt to the lowlands of eastern and southern Arkansas. Natural erosion is what made the areas so fertile to begin with.   After human intervention in the form of dams, dikes and levees upstream the natural cycle of  flooding was interrupted.  Arkansas lowland farmers are now dependent on agricultural fertilizers and chemicals to replace the vital nutrients that are lost to erosion every season and not replaced by the regular flooding cycle.  (Cossman 1999)

The continuing erosion of the lowlands degrades the soil resource to a point where long-term production is reduced.  (ASCS 1986:39)  Erosion carries harmful pesticides and valuable nutrients into the water system and also distributes 239 million tons of dust into the air annually.  (Baite 1983:44)  Dissolved minerals and sedimentation also affect marine life in the water systems that the runoff reaches.  Often salt dissolves into runoff water, harming fish and making extra, more costly treatment necessary.  (Baite 1983:47) Excess sedimentation causes bodies of water to become more shallow, sand bars to form, reduces the size of bodies of water, hinders photosynthesis in marine bacteria and affects the “respiration, growth and spawning of fish and shellfish.”  (Baite 1983:47)  “Siltation is the most common pollutant affecting surveyed rivers and streams.”  ( )  Siltation alters aquatic habitats which can cause death to fish and can fill spaces where insects live.  The loss of insects affects fish and other wildlife that eat them. ( ) When the insects, which are a major source of food for the fish, are gone, soon  species of fish and other aquatic life disappear.

Runoff may also carry harmful materials into the water.  Often, agricultural runoff carries herbicides, pesticides, fertilizers and bacteria from animal wastes.  Not only is this harmful for the environment, but also costly to treat and clean.  Farmers also lose money in the sense that they lose a portion of the chemical that they paid to have put on their field. 

Understandably, erosion carries away many of the valuable nutrients that farmers spend millions of dollars on every season.  With this in mind, it is important to realize that crop planting and cultivation techniques are vital in preventing erosion.  In Sandra Baite’s Soil Erosion:  Crisis in America’s Cropland? it is suggested that farmers use techniques such as contour cropping, the staggering of different crops across one large field, delayed tillage and direct tillage of the spring crop over the remains of the fall harvest.  Similar techniques are also suggested by the Arkansas Soil Conservation Service.  These suggestions imply that a large responsibility of preventing erosion is placed on the farmer’s shoulders.  His planting, tilling and soil conservation techniques make all the difference. 

                Erosion is a problem, to some degree, in all parts of the U.S.  What is often unnoticed by most Americans, though, is the harmful contaminants contained within the water runoff and soil that is eroded.  Major contributors to contaminated erosion in Arkansas are the cattle and poultry farming industries.  Arkansas is home to one of the nation’s most thriving poultry industries, as well as a strong cattle industry.  A drive through Arkansas is superabundant with cattle pastures and chicken houses. Both industries have serious affects on the surrounding rivers, lakes and streams.  However, the poultry industry seems to affect Arkansas’ water more than cattle farming.  

                In Faulkner county alone, there were 55,000 head of cattle in 1998. ( ).  Cattle farms have to have a continuous supply of water.  In Arkansas this supply mostly comes from streams, springs or man made ponds.  Ponds are usually dug in a low lying area that takes advantage of run off and ground water. Cattle affect the water in a number of ways, the most obvious being their consumption of it.  A normal size steer will drink approximately 4.2 gallons of water a day.  A Jersey milk producing cow will drink on average twelve gallons a day, and a milk producing Holstein will drink twenty-three gallons a day (Willrich, Smith 1990:111).  This information proves the importance of fresh water.

                One pollution worry cattle farmers have to be concerned with is the proper disposal of dead animal carcasses.  Because of the environmental concerns regarding the disposal of cattle that have died during normal operation, burial methods have been enacted.  Complete burial is required, with properly sited and constructed burial pits.  Burial plots should meet the following requirements:  be located at least 400 ft. from wells or domestic water, be located 100 ft. from any surface water, the bottom of the pit must be 4 ft. above seasonal high water, the pit must allow 3 ft. of earth cover, pit sites should be staggered.  Another common method of disposal is burning, here again, the burning site in relation to nearby water must be observed (Willrich, Smith 1990:156).  This method produces carbon-dioxide emissions, which are green house gasses that are harmful to the environment, just as burning any living or once living thing will do. 

                The primary means of water pollution via cattle farming is through the runoff of nutrients found in animal manure.  Arkansas does not have the feedlot waste pollution problem that many other states are experiencing, because Arkansas’s method of cattle farming operation is mostly range and pasture systems.  Manure is primarily distributed along the most traveled routes in the pasture.  “The pattern of excreta deposition follows the patterns of movement, lounging, feeding and watering of the cattle.” (Sandage 1998: 3)   Therefore, water source, shade and mineral/supplement feeder locations collect the great majority of the manure and urine, concentrating nutrients in a very small portion of the total area. This concentration of minerals,  particularly phosphorous [from cattle excretions], can further intensify the potential for phosphorous runoff into water sources on those pastures that already contain high levels of phosphorous. (Sandage 1998:3) Increased levels of nutrients (phosphates and nitrates from waste)  in streams, rivers and lakes cause increased algae growth.  When the algae die their decomposition is aided by oxygen-consuming bacteria.  As these bacteria’s numbers increase, the level of oxygen for fish and other aquatic life decreases.  As the cycle progresses, aquatic life begins to die.  “Diversity of plant and animal life continues to decrease until the lake is suitable only for bacteria that do not require oxygen.”  (Getis, Getis, Fellman 1998:151) The following illustration, taken from Introduction to Geography by Arthur and Judith Getis and Jerome Fellmann, c. 1998, illustrates the cycle described above.

                Poultry farming is Arkansas’ leading live animal agricultural industry.  In 1998, Faulkner county had 104,000 broilers, ( The predominant area of the poultry industry is north central and north west Arkansas.  Poultry farming has its own set of needs and problems.  The huge amount of water, approximately 150 gallons a day for one chicken house, that is needed to sustain and cool the chickens is piped in and allowed to flow through nipple basins.  The sprayers used to cool the chickens is piped in as a mist and allowed to hit giant fans.  The majority of water used for poultry production in Arkansas does not come directly from the waterways, as cattle production does.  Most water is supplied to the chicken house from the city water supply, which may come from an underground aquifer, a lake or a river, depending upon the area of the state.

                For poultry farmers, waste and carcass disposal is often the biggest problem.  The massive amounts of birds that are housed together create a large amount of waste. This waste, or litter, is often spread, as fertilizer, on cattle grazing pastures to encourage the growth of feed grasses.  When too much liter and manure accumulate in a given area, we see the same results as we do with runoff from cattle manure.  (Walker 1999) The following two photos compare the difference between a stream that has not been contaminated with excess nutrients and one that has.  Note the abundance of algae in the contaminated stream.

                Cattle and poultry farming, however, is not the only agricultural source of pollution.  The chemicals used in agriculture are also a major cause of contamination in our freshwater supply.  In fact agriculture is the main overall cause of pollution in groundwater, lakes and streams.  There are two main types of chemicals that contaminate our water:  fertilizers and biocides.

Photo taken from “Soil Quality Concerns: Pesticides,” USDA Nat. Res. Cons. Serv.

                The agricultural use of fertilizers and manure increases the availability of plant-essential elements, and thus increases the total yield and/or quality of crops ( ).  Pollution occurs when nitrates and phosphates have been used in fertilizers. . . (Getis, Getis, Fellman 1998:150).  Nitrates being salts of nitric acid, and phosphates being salts of nitric acid used in the fertilizers.  These fertilizers put excess nutrients in the water causing it to overload with nutrients and become oxygen deficient.

                 Biocides are divided into categories according to the target organisms they control.  Pesticides are synthetic organic chemicals used to control unwanted or harmful pests, such as insects that feed on crops (National Soil Survey Center 1998:1).  Herbicides are used to control weeds in fields and lawns.  Among the chemicals classified as pesticides are insecticides, fungicides, nematicides and rodenticides ( ). The biocides become harmful when soils do not chemically, biologically, or photochemically degrade them into harmless materials (National Soil Survey Center 1998:2).  Runoff from farms where such biocides have been applied contaminates both ground and surface waters (Getis, Getis, Fellman 1998:152).  Depending on the solubility of the soil the pesticides can soak through the soil and contaminate aquifers, underground bodies of flowing water that makes up 99% of our freshwater supplies ( ).  An EPA survey found biocides in about 10% of all community water systems, with 1% containing potentially unsafe concentrations (Getis, Getis, Fellman, 1998:152).  

                What is being done to prevent and protect our water from pollution?  Steps are being taken on many levels to protect our water from contamination.  Many farmers have voluntarily adopted best management practices and other measures that will help protect water from contamination.  Standards and regulations have also been made on products to help control the contamination of water supplies.  However, these things may not be enough.  People need to be better educated on the problems of water pollution so more laws like the Clean Water Act can be passed. 

                  The research has examined the various ways in which agriculture affect Arkansas’ water, and concluded that harmful runoff, siltation, and over usage are all problems.  It has discussed the specific causes of each and preventative measures need to stop, or at least slow, their occurence.  And yet, through all of the discussion, one thing becomes clear: human action or lack thereof is generally responsible for the detrimental effects on Arkansas’ water and now human intervention will play a vital role in saving it.
