Arkansas Blues: Delta Blues at the Crossroads

 

History of the Blues

In the world’s marketplace for ideas, America is searching for a unique cultural identity, for few things can be called uniquely ours. We look to Paris and Milan for our fashions. We developed the first mass marketed car, but German and Italian designers create better vehicles. The microwave, an American invention, was reverse engineered by the Japanese and now they are the ones who sell it. Even the atomic bomb was developed with the help of a recent foreign emigrant, Albert Einstein. With that said however, this nation’s greatest asset is its ability to take diffuse ideas and mold them into something that is ours.

The origins of the blues music are shrouded in the mists of time, but there are some generally accepted ideas as to where it came from and how it developed. One idea says that the blues were actually a form of African music that transmitted the traditions and sufferings African slaves experienced. These songs covered a wide range of subjects from work to love to spiritual music (Carney, 55). Another idea is that the blues came from black sharecroppers who lived together in large communities. These communities did not quite have the same conditions that slaves faced, but living was pretty hard (Carney, 56). Also, in these communities, different African tribal groups were thrown together and these songs were one way to communicate traditions through music to their children.

According to George Carney, the new world instruments were unfamiliar to the slaves, but they were able to express their tribal music through vocal chants and the rhythmic banging of work implements (Carney, 57). After the Civil War, the musicians had freedom to travel where they wanted. In order to do this, they needed easily transportable instruments. As a result, early blues musicians primarily used the guitar and harmonica. Both of these instruments are inexpensive and easy to carry which allowed the musicians to be fairly mobile with their songs.

Robert Johnson, Blues man Extraordinaire

In the early part of the 1900’s, the premier blues musician was a man by the name of Robert Johnson. While dying at the young age of 27, Robert Johnson’s impact on blues culture and the development of guitar styles has been substantial. An interesting point is that Johnson only recorded 29 songs, but these songs have been more closely analyzed than any other blues composer (Blue Flame).

Robert Johnson was born illegitimately to Julia Dodds and Noah Johnson in 1911. When he was either three or four, he moved to Memphis with Julia and her husband Charles. In 1930, at the age of 19, he got married, but soon after lost his wife to labor in childbirth. It was at this point that Johnson struck out as a wandering blues man (Blue Flame).

What happened next is part of the myth and legend surrounding Robert. Apparently, Robert’s skill at playing the guitar was almost non-existent. He was so bad that other blues artist refused to play with him. Then, mysteriously, his disappeared for a brief while. When he returned, his skill was much greater than it should have been. Robert often boasted of having gone to the crossroads to make a deal with the devil. He had sold his soul to the devil for skill at the guitar.

It is a little difficult to tell where reality stops and myth begins. The question of where his sudden spurt of talent came from is one idea that allows the myth to continue. Also, Robert talked about Hellhounds being on his trail and the fact that the Devil himself was after him. A large part of his music deals with this subject and good verses evil. Most likely, the myth was the product of jealousy of other artists and some shrewd marketing. The myth started when other blues musicians speculated on where Robert’s sudden talent had come from (Blue Flame). Probably to keep the mystique alive and to practice stuff that would not be copied until he had an opportunity to perform it, Robert practiced in graveyards in the middle of the night.

The career of Robert was cut short by the unfortunate difficulty of being caught in bed with another man’s wife. In revenge, the jealous husband laced a bottle of whiskey with strychnine. Johnson died in Greenwood, Mississippi with only a few recordings under his belt but what he started has many lingering effects that are felt in the music scene today. Some of the most famous names in music look back to Robert as their inspiration: Muddy Waters, Eric Clapton, B.B. King, and Richards from the Rolling Stones to name a few (Blue Flame).

 

Diffusion of the Blues

“A discerning collection of blues records can act as vinyl stratigraphy of North America’s black population, reflection the development of a people and of how it responded to the environmental changes that were thrust upon it (Carney, 55).” Blues music has its roots in the struggles of African slaves who yearned for freedom and a better life. After emancipation, blacks were nominally free, but still tied to the land in a sharecropping system. In other words, the names had been changed, but the conditions remained the same. The process of diffusion was quite slow at first. Blues flowed up from the plantations along major communication routes, such as railways and the Mississippi river (Carney, 57). The major reason diffusion was slow was that the artist had to be physically present. According to George Lee, it took, W. C. Handy, to promote and produce the blues in recordings, introducing the rest of America to the Blues (Lee, 132). W. C. Handy eventually collaborated with Harry Pace, a small town songwriter, (Lee, 134) and started the Handy and Pace Company in 1917. In that same year, the company moved to New York and created a sensation on Broadway. Perhaps Handy’s greatest idea was to have a woman front the Blues. The style became a sensation and every company wanted a piece of the action. Blues music, in the mainstream, took off (Lee, 143). Handy played major role in the diffusing the blues, he took it from the fields and the “Negro breakdowns on Saturday night ” and commercialized it and brought it to rest of the world.

“If you’ve ever been mistreated – then you know what I’m talkin’ about… (Eddie Boyd, quoted in Finn, pg. 92).” The blues style is simple with 12 repeated measures and a repeatable theme, but it speaks to the soul. Blues music is not always about sadness, but is more appropriately described as life as seen through the eyes of a man who had to struggle to survive.
