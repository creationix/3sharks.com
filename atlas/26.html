B7: Urban Geography

“Growth and Expansion in Conway”

By: Leslie Griffin, Reneta Porter, and Elizabeth Tennyson

The Population Readiness Response Team

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2519

May 4, 1999

 

What changes and plans are being made to handle growth rates in Conway?  Will there be enough space to accommodate the growth that Conway is experiencing?

 

 

                The city of Conway has become one of the fastest growing cities in the state of Arkansas.  During this decade Conway has grown at an annualized rate of 6.9%. (Attachment #1, Growth Plan)  A large part of the population growth is due to the number of families moving into Conway.  For Conway to handle all of the growth that it is experiencing, many preparations must be made.

                One of the first changes that need to be made is the availability of housing.  There are a large number of new lots being cleared and new homes being built upon them. (see attachment #2, Photos)  A little over $41 million more than spent in 1997, has been paid for the construction of new units.  To help fill this “healthy” growth rise, 711 acres were annexed to the city in 1998. ( Conway continues to grow...)  Included as attachment number 3 are some photos of Conway’s housing sites under construction.  These sites can be seen throughout Conway, and not all of these areas are being used for houses, some of these sites are for apartment complexes as well. (see attachment #2, Photos) Most of the people moving into the area are married couples.  Married-couple households make up 62% of all family households in Conway. ( Faulkner County Summary Statistics)  Along with married couples also comes children, who are enrolled into the local schools.

                There has been a 27% increase in student enrollment in the Conway schools.  One major concern in the growth of the schools, is the amount of space available to handle all of the new students.  A total of 155 students, for grades kindergarten through twelfth, have moved into the Conway school district between the 1998 and the 1999 school years.  Kindergarten through fifth grades, the numbers have only increased by 3 during this time frame.  Like the kindergarten through fifth grade division, the eleventh and twelfth grades only saw a few new faces with 9 new students.  Sixth, seventh, and eighth grades saw the largest increase with 79 new pupils and right behind them were the ninth and tenth grades with an increase of 65. (see attachment #3, History of Enrollment) Many construction projects are being planned and thought out.  One such plan involves the purchase of six acres of land and another six acres within the year.  The first six acres were purchased in 1998, while the second six were scheduled for purchase in February, 1999. ( Growth has Conway school...)  This land site will be used for the construction of a new elementary school, located on South Donaghey. (see attachment #4, Construction plan for school) Five million dollars has been set aside to build the new school.  Part of this is the approximate $389,000 that has been designated as the proposed budget. (Proposed Budget for New Elementary)

                Other accommodations for the student growth in the Conway area are additions to the present schools (Jim Stone Elementary) and the remodeling of older school buildings (Ellen Smith Elementary).  Jim Stone Elementary is already underway with plans to add four new classrooms.  The students will also enjoy a new auto shop building as well as the classrooms.  As seen in the fifth attachment, the additions will be made near the north end, close to College Avenue. (Attachment #5, ) The funding for the new accommodations will be approximately $125,000, which will be covered by the state vocational funding.  ( Growth has Conway school...)  This is a crowded area due to the expansion plans of the University, which will also involve extensive construction work.

                With all of the people coming into Conway, the traffic has grown as well.  This new increased traffic will require road improvements and road extensions.  Two such projects are the extension of Hogan Lane to Old Morrillton Highway and the expansion of Prince Street to three lanes. ( Conway continues to grow...)  To help decrease traffic congestion, the idea of bike routes has been put on the table.  These bike routes would connect many areas of the city to one another, hopefully encouraging people to bike instead of drive.  This new traffic increase has also helped to open the doorway for businesses. ( Conway continues to grow...)

                The majority of new businesses has been chain franchises.  Many franchise chains have decided to have a location in Conway, making it an ideal stop for travelers headed to or from Little Rock.  The downside to this particular increase is that Conway is now losing smaller, locally owned business, which are largely located in the downtown area.  Luckily, Conway is still at a lower rate of unemployment than the state average (4.7% vs. 5.1%).  ( Faulkner Grows)

                With this population burst in Conway, additions are having to be made to the police and fire departments.  The police have set up a five year plan that will divide the town into three sections.  Each section will have two substations containing eight to ten officers each. (Interview with Police Chief) The police also hope to increase community involvement by encouraging more neighborhood crime watch programs.  To pay for these, there will be an increase in revenues for electrical power and sales tax. ( Conway continues to grow...)

                The only concern or drawback should be on the accommodation of so many people moving into the area, though the city of Conway seems prepared for the explosion.   Richard Magee sums up Conway’s growth by saying, “if Conway’s population continues to rise, it could become the state’s second largest city”.  ( Conway continues to grow...)  This growth explosion of Conway began in the early 1990's, is still continuing today, and is predicted to continue into the future millennium.
