How has the implementation of rules and regulations set forth by the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission helped to rebuild and stabilize the white-tailed deer population in the state?

            The white-tailed deer have inhabited this region for roughly fifteen to twenty million years (Cartwright, Patterson, Peterson, Poarch, and Sutton: 1996a). They were allowed to exist without pressure from humans for almost the entirety of this time.  The Indian tribes that formerly inhabited this area hunted the white-tailed deer only when necessary for survival and did not have a drastic impact on the overall population of the species (Cartwright, Patterson, Peterson, Poarch, and Suton: 1987b).  However, when white men started settling in this area, they often times killed the white-tailed deer in absurdly large numbers to sell for profit or for specific cuts of meat.  This did not have an adverse affect immediately, but as the human population increased, the white-tailed deer became increasingly scarce (Donaldson, Holder, Hunter: 1951).  "By 1930 the white-tailed deer population of Arkansas had been reduced to only 500 animals" (Cartwright 1996a: 10).  To protect the white-tailed deer and other wild game in our state, The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission was created in 1915.  This leads us to the purpose of this paper, which is to find out how the introduction of rules and regulations set forth by The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission has affected the white-tailed deer population in the state. 

            When The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission was formed in 1915, the commission's intended effects were not immediately felt in the population of wild game in the state.  This inability to stop or curb the population decrease, especially in the case of the white tailed deer, caused the general public to become concerned.  This concern prompted immediate action, and government officials promptly passed an act that defined a 61-day open season for deer.  This season ran from November 11 to January 10 and allowed the harvest of two deer per person (Cartwright 1987b).  However, the newly formed commission still faced several challenges before things would start to improve for them.  The first challenge they had to overcome was the problem of lack of funding.  They could afford only eighty game wardens to cover the entire state, and these men did not even have the power to make arrests (Cartwright 1996a).  The second obstacle that The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission had to hurdle was the great flood of 1927.  This huge flood covered a large portion of the vast delta of eastern Arkansas, pushing the remaining deer onto isolated patches of high ground.  These deer herds were devastated by local hunters who took advantage of the situation (Cartwright 1987b).  After such a rough beginning it would have been easy for The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission to have just given up, but the struggle to save the white-tailed deer was about to take a drastic change. 

            It was stated earlier in this paper that in 1930 the white-tailed deer population in Arkansas had dwindled to 500 animals.  The majority of these remaining white-tailed deer were located in two remote regions of Arkansas.  They were the Sylamore Creek area of the Ozark Mountains and the vast bottomlands of the White and Arkansas rivers (Donaldson 1951).  To keep these precious few remaining white-tailed deer from being wastefully killed, and therefore making them virtually extinct in Arkansas, The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission decided to create refuges for them.  In 1926 and 1927 the commission bought, with funds raised by taxes and the sale of hunting licensees, enough land to go along with land grants from the government so that they could create several state and federal game refuges (Morrison 1980).  These refuges were conveniently located in the remote and somewhat hard to reach regions that were mentioned earlier.  This allowed the white-tailed deer to remain in a relatively safe place while their populations grew.  It became apparent that these steps alone would not bring the full-scale turnaround of the white-tailed deer population.  At this point in time, The Arkansas Game and Fish began another phase of their plan to replenish "The Natural State" with white-tailed deer.

            "The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission also started a deer restocking program, at first buying white-tailed deer from private herds and later trapping them on refuges for relocating in unoccupied parts of their former range" (Cartwright 1987b).  This statement by Cartwright gives an excellent overview of how The Arkansas Game and Fish reestablished white-tailed deer herds throughout their previous vast domain.  The continued support and compliance of the general public and the government, as well as the excellent habitat of this region allowed for the deer population to grow at an exponential rate (Donaldson 1951).  A chart showing this characteristic may be viewed at the back of this paper.  “By 1946, the statewide deer population had grown to an estimated 31,617 with deer present in 71 of the state's 75 counties” (Cartwright 1987b: 8).  Censuses such as this one were carried out by pilots in small planes counting the growing herds of the state.  These censuses showed that by the 1980's the white-tailed deer had made a complete recovery, and in some cases had become too frequent (Cartwright 1996a).  This topic of overpopulation will be discussed later.  Because of several factors, including the diverse areas of human population and physical geography of the state of Arkansas, the density of deer populations varies from region to region.

            As stated above, the diversity in the physical features of Arkansas plays a pivotal role in where large populations of deer will reside.  White-tailed deer can thrive in an environment that produces plentiful food such as acorns, wild grass seed, and agricultural products such as corn, soybeans, and wheat ( Hirth 1977).  Along with this abundance of food, they must also be afforded the right habitat.  This habitat consists of  forest edge, open woodlands, brush land, and second growth hardwoods (Morrison 1980).  Therefore, the regions of the state that most closely fit these characteristics are going to have the largest deer populations.  Arkansas can be divided geographically into four major sections.  They are the Ouachita Mountains, the Ozark Mountains, the Delta Region, and the West Gulf Coastal Plain.  The Ouachita and Ozark Mountain ranges cover much of the western, northern, and central sections of the state.  The Delta Region covers nearly all of the eastern section of the state. The fourth region is the West Gulf Coastal Plain, which covers a good deal of the southern section of the state (Cartwright 1996a).  The Arkansas Game and Fish must treat each of these regions differently.  They each have their own obstacles to overcome so that a constant population of deer may be maintained. 

            The four regions of Arkansas can be further subdivided into regional hunting zones.  These zones are created based on deer population, densities, habitat quality and distribution, human population densities, physical features, and sociological  and political influences.  Since 1987, the state has been subdivided into twenty zones (Cartwright 1987b).  The overall goal of each hunting zone is to maintain a population of deer, which can easily be sustained by the region's resources(Gavin 1984).  To reach this goal The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission must accommodate the deer season, to the deer population.  An excellent example of  the commission customizing the hunting season occurs in the southern regions of the state, near hunting zones 12,14,15, and 19 (Deer Zones Map).  In these zones, there is a substantially longer season, coupled with  a more liberal limit on the deer you are allowed to harvest.  The longer season and larger limits are in response to the overpopulation, which is occurring in that part of the state.  The Gulf Coastal Plain Region of southern Arkansas accounts for nearly sixty percent of the statewide deer harvest each year (Cartwright 1987b). 

            A factor that The Arkansas Game and Fish has to consider when setting its annual rules and regulations is urbanization.  The fact that our cities and towns are expanding at a rapid rate has a large impact on the deer population in that area.  When people build houses and roads through the habitat of the white-tailed deer, there will be encounters with them.  This is the reason that so many deer are killed each year in automobile accidents (Hirth 1977).  Humans also must take into consideration the fact that once they take their habitat, the deer must find some place to go.  This often times ends up with the situation of people having deer eating the grass out of their yards, or rummaging through their trash.

            There are several categories of deer hunting that are provided in Arkansas.   They are modern gun, muzzle loading, and archery season.  Each regional hunting zone has specific dates during which each are allowed.  When The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission sets the limits and dates for the next hunting season,  they must consider the population of the deer herd.  "Biologists monitor deer herds based on knowledge of population changes and production" (Cartwright 1987b:12).  They can make an estimate of how many deer can be taken without hurting next year's crop.  A good estimate is that 30-40 percent of the deer herd may be harvested without harming the overall population of the herd (Cartwright 1987b). 

            As you can see, The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission has taken gigantic steps since its founding over eighty years ago.  The commission has brought the white-tailed deer population of Arkansas back from the brink of extinction to a thriving existence all over the state.  The challenges that face this organization will undoubtedly change in the future, just as they have in the past.  The experience and knowledge that they have gained thus far will definitely allow the commission to flourish in the future.
