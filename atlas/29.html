What Impact Does Human Intervention Have on the Whitetail Deer Population of North Central Arkansas?

A10: Physical Geography: Animals

.Human Impacts on Animals.

By: Laura Davis, and Clay Johns (Team Whitetail)

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2518

Prepared: February 18, 1999

 

What Impact Does Human Intervention Have on the Whitetail Deer Population of North Central

Arkansas?

 

Human impact on the population of a given species generally takes either of two forms:  ways in which a species. population is reduced or enhanced, or ways in which a population.s habitat is encroached upon or enhanced.  In examining the impact of man.s actions upon whitetail deer in north central Arkansas, it becomes necessary to consider the species from: 1. a biological perspective, 2.  a historical perspective, and 3.  a management perspective.  The term .wildlife management. is now  preferred to the older .game management,. (Stout, 1999, p.1) and even this terminology reflects a change in perspective.

Human intervention on the whitetail deer population takes various forms, including sharing a habitat, cutting or otherwise changing a forest dwelling, food plots and brush piles created with the purpose of increasing deer numbers, etc.  It is impossible to consider this topic without also considering the issue of hunting. Of the many environmental impacts mankind has on our earth, few are more likely to arouse controversy in contemporary dialogue.  We now turn our attention to the consideration of whitetail deer from a biological perspective.

Whitetail Deer: A Biological Perspective

Whitetail deer are at home in a variety of habitats.  Originally woodland animals, they now prosper in such diverse locations as the broken cover of the Midwestern plains, the foothills of the Rockies, and farm and ranch lands.  They live in the arid parts of southwestern Texas and northern Mexico where drought prevails and temperatures can exceed 100 degrees F.  In such locations, they survive eating such food as guajillo, blackbrush, and prickly pear.  They also live in the northern U.S. and Canada in sub-zero temperatures.  In these locations, they eat cedar, ground hemlock and maple (Biggs, 1994, p. 42).  The topography of north-central Arkansas provides a rugged terrain with secluded ridges and deep draws, broken by farmland, pastures and creek bottoms that provide a suitable habitat for deer (Bowman, Nov.12, 1996, Section C).

The most serious threat to whitetails everywhere is expanding human populations and thus the inevitable destruction of natural habitats.  This forces deer to move to less productive habitats.  Such encroachment has limited or doomed many other animal species, but the whitetail deer has been able to move, adjust, and prosper more successfully than any large wild animal (Biggs, 1994, p.43) except the coyote (Davis, 1998).  In fact, as humans have spread, they have actually created habitat for deer.  According to Jay Kirkpatrick, director of science and conservation biology at Zoo Montana in Billings, .Urbanization provides a deer with everything it could ever want . . . ornamental shrubs, gardens and lawns to eat, half acres of woods here and there to bed down in . . . the only predators are cars.. (Nelson, 1994, p.36).

The whitetail deer is said to be one of the most adaptable animals on earth.  One of its adaptations is a relatively high reproductive rate for an animal of its size (Young, 1995, p.3).  Does can breed as early as six months in some areas (Nelson, p.36), although typically, they don.t reach sexual maturity until one year of age and begin having young at age two (Donaldson, 1951, p.10).  They then bear young every year, often giving birth to twins and even triplets.  Over the course of an average 10-year lifespan, one doe and her offspring could produce 100 fawns (Nelson, p.37).  Even when compared to other deer, the whitetail.s reproductive rate is high (Young, p.3).

 Deer on poor diets have a lower pregnancy rate and delayed sexual maturity.  However, the birth rate of even poorly-nourished white-tail deer is high enough that births will more than replace the parents.  In one study of birth rates in poorly nourished does, a herd.s population increased by nearly 80%.  The reproduction rate can range from 1.15 fawns per poorly nourished doe to 1.85 fawns per well-nourished doe  (Young, p.3).  Clearly, even when subjected to inadequate nourishment, a deer population will continue to expand.  However, the condition of individuals in the herd will suffer.

The whitetail deer of the Arkansas Ozarks are more dependent on acorns for survival than are whitetail in other parts of the country.  The soil of this mountainous area produces lower nutritional quality browse (Treat, 1991, p.2).  When there is insufficient food, a natural .controlling influence,. in the form of fawn sex ratio, neonatal mortality (death of newborns), and winter mortality will occur (Young, p. 4).  In years of poor acorn yields, studies have shown that the whitetail deer of Arkansas have higher mortality rates due to otherwise non-fatal conditions, such as parasites and being chased by dogs (Treat, p.5).  The acorn yield is affected by many variables, ranging from drought to harvesting of oak timber to the age of trees and the tree.s exposure to sunlight (Treat, p. 9).  However, as Treat points out in her thesis dealing with whitetail deer and their habitat in Arkansas, .too much clearing is harmful because it severely restricts the number of acorn producing trees; too little clearing is harmful because insufficient sunlight inhibits acorn production and new plant growth on the forest floor..  (Treat, p.10). Since these variables are not always within man.s control, whether or not deer get insufficient food is not totally the result of manmade influences.

In expanding populations, death from malnutrition is more likely, and it is a slow, painful process.  Young.s article quotes John Ozoga in Whitetail Winter:

 .Fat depletion and physical weakening progress with nearly undetectable signs, until it.s too late for recovery.  In the final stages . . . a deer.s coat roughens, its hip bones show, and hollows appear in its flanks.  The starving animal spends most of its time bedded down in a curled head to tail position to minimize body surface exposure.  It adopts a lethargic, uncaring attitude, no longer bounding away, flag waving, (authors. note - flag refers to the deer.s tail) as danger nears.  Small deer, especially, stand humpbacked, their front legs spread slightly, back legs close together, holding their heads up at a 45-degree angle (below horizontal) . .  Thereafter, a deer so stressed could no longer handle the metabolic stress of feeding.. (Young, pp. 4-5).

Biologically, one would expect a deer.s high reproductive rates to be balanced by high mortality rates from some of the previously mentioned factors, resulting in reduced populations.  However, looking at the broad historical perspective of whitetail deer populations in Arkansas, this does not prove to be the case.

Whitetail Deer: A Historical Perspective

Arkansas had large numbers of whitetail deer in its early history. Indians made use of most of the deer, from its meat to its bones and skin. Later, settlers used the deer for clothing and food.  While there are no exact numbers of the deer population when white settlers first came, it is generally agreed that it wasn.t until the turn of the century that Arkansas. deer herd suffered a major decline.  As man encroached into the deer.s habitat, building roads, railroads, and settlements, land was cleared of deer habitat.  Lumbering, grazing, uncontrolled burning of woodlands and the use of dogs were all contributing factors  (Cartwright, 1986, p.3). An additional factor was market hunting, which was legal until 1915.  Deer that foraged in gardens and croplands were killed, including does and fawns, and in many cases, only the hams were used, as the rest was left to buzzards (Donaldson, p.13).

Old-timers have told stories dating prior to 1900 when hunters would compete to see who could kill the most deer.  It is said that two good hunters might kill from 50-60 deer in a 10 to 14-day period.  When the hunt was over, the hunter sometimes took the hams, but the rest was left to rot.  After deer became scarce, there was sometimes even competition to see who could kill the last deer in a given area  (Donaldson, p.15). 

The numbers of whitetail declined to the point that sightings were a newsworthy event.  At the turn of the century, sightings were so rare that newspapers would report on the front page that a deer was seen in the community (Stout, p.1).  By 1930, an estimated 500 animals were all that remained of the once massive herd, and these were scattered in isolated spots (Cartwright, p.3).

 The Arkansas Game and Fish Commission was formed in 1915 (Cartwright, p.3), and in 1916 it placed a 61-day open season on deer.  Other laws followed, but the deer population continued dropping (Donaldson, p.15) until it reached the lows of the early 1930's. 

Statistics kept by the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission of .Arkansas Reported Deer Harvest. date back to 1938, the first recorded year.  In 1938, 203 deer were harvested (Appendix -Arkansas Game and Fish Table).  It must be kept in mind that these are reported deer harvested.  It is almost certain that the actual numbers taken were somewhat higher, due to illegal hunting, failure to report, etc., but this can be seen as the most reliable indicator available.

The turning point dated from the establishment of Arkansas. first game refuges in 1926-27.  These refuges, along with better law enforcement, began to show a positive effect in the numbers of whitetail deer.  In some counties, deer had been completely exterminated, and to even be able to establish game refuges, deer had to be stocked there  (Donaldson, p.16).  More than 40 state and federal refuges were established and more than 3,000 deer were stocked  (Crawford, p.3).  This was done either by obtaining deer from other states, or trapping native animals from other areas in Arkansas.  Those trapped came from overpopulated areas and were moved into suitable unoccupied range. Sometimes, individuals and organizations released a few deer into the state  (Donaldson, p. 16).

In 1942, the number of deer harvested in Arkansas climbed to 1,000, reached 4,122 by 1950, 15,000 by 1960, 26,017 by 1970, 45,202 by 1980, and had reached 152,460 by 1996, the last year for which figures are available (Appendix - AG&F Table).

Many processes took place during those years to cause the dramatic population changes.  The public began to develop more favorable attitudes toward all types of conservation (Donaldson, p.16).  In a General Election of the state on Nov. 7, 1944, Arkansas Constitutional Amendment 35 established independence of the Arkansas State Game and Fish Commission.  The Commission saw as its goal the   .. . .control, management, restoration, conservation and regulation of the birds, fish and wildlife resources of the State . . . .  The Commission exercises exclusive power over the issuance of licenses, bag limits, and the manner of taking game and fish, and also divides the state into zones.  Additionally, it oversees purchases and acquisitions of property for the purpose of carrying out its missions  (Arkansas Constitutional Amendment #35).

As of today, the Commission has acquired approximately 325,000 acres and leases another 300,000 acres from corporate private landowners.  It works cooperatively with the U.S. Forest Service, U.S. Army Corps of Engineers, U.S. National Park Service, as well as other organizations and private landowners .to enhance wildlife habitat on an additional 3.3 million acres.. (Arkansas Game and Fish, Harris, p.1).

 As statistical evidence shows, the deer population in Arkansas has grown substantially from the dismal lows reached in the early part of the century.  Just as man.s intervention has had an effect on the deer population, so do the increased numbers of deer have an impact on other animals, people, and the environment, and it is to this topic that we now turn.

Interactions of Deer With Humans

.There are dramatic ecological changes as deer populations grow,. according to William Porter, a wildlife biologist at Syracuse University (Budiansky, 1994, p.85).  In Wisconsin, for example, rare orchids and lilies are disappearing, and hardwoods and hemlocks have failed to reproduce for 50 years.  Deer have earned the name .rats with antlers. from some corners.  Deer are now present in places where they haven.t been for more than 100 years, (Budiansky, p.85) and there are nearly two times more deer in the lower 48 states than there were a decade ago.  In fact, their numbers may be up as much as 5,000 percent since the turn of the century  (Nelson, p.36). 

What does this mean for Arkansas?  As early as the 1940's, wildlife managers in Arkansas recognized deer management problems due to .bucks only. hunting and overprotection.  In the state, federal and state refuges became overpopulated with deer, resulting in deer losses due to disease and starvation, as well as range destruction.  (Cartwright, p.3). 

At that time, overpopulation wasn.t yet a problem outside of preserves and refuges.  In 1946, the statewide deer population had grown to 31,617, and by 1954 had reached 150,000.  Bucks-only hunting was required until the early 1970's.  This approach resulted in a healthy, growing deer herd, as based on body size and antler development.  Numbers were well below what the habitat could support (Cartwright, p.3).

However, in the late 1960's and 1970's, the deer herd began to exceed what the habitat could support.  The number of deer that a particular habitat can support without deterioration of the habitat or the deer is known as .carrying capacity..  This varies according to the geographical and biological features of a region.  For example, in the Ozark hills of north Arkansas, it may take 60 acres to support one deer year-round, where in south Arkansas, 60 acres may support six deer annually.  In Arkansas, it became apparent that the carrying capacity was being exceeded as evidenced by deterioration in antler development, reduced reproduction, increased natural mortality, an increase in parasites and disease, increased damage to crops, and deterioration of habitat  (Cartwright, p.3 ).  However, rather than these factors having a natural controlling effect on the population of deer herds, the numbers of deer remain high, while the health and quality of the animals remains low.  In the meantime, they are continuing to deplete habitat through overbrowsing  (Young p.7).

Studies done on plants and trees under carefully controlled conditions in other states indicate that there are significant reductions in tree diversity and size, and that bark stripping by deer makes trees susceptible to disease.  Understory vegetation and seedlings are affected by the loss of the taller trees above them.  Seedlings are unable to make the transition to saplings.  When there is insufficient regeneration of tree species, other animal species regenerate less successfully.  Overbrowsing by deer is cited as the major cause of unsuccessful regeneration  (Young, pp. 7-8).  A study by William McShea of the Smithsonian Institution on the effects of increased deer populations in years when there is a small acorn crop show reductions in the numbers of squirrels, chipmunks, flying squirrels, wood rats, and mice due to competition for food.  He also speculates that the destruction of low-lying plants in the understory eliminates habitat for the insects that birds feed on, reducing bird populations, as well (Budiansky, p.86).

In the mid 1980's, the deer population in Arkansas was estimated at 400,000 to 500,000  (Cartwright, p.3).  As with the rest of the nation, this many deer in Arkansas were bound to affect humans, as well as have consequences for the species itself.  For one thing, as the increased numbers of deer seek living space and food in areas populated by humans, there will be increasing interaction on many levels (Young, p.8).  Problems cited from deer overpopulation range from damage to crops and landscape plants, (Young, p.8) to damage to golf courses (Budiansky, p.85).  These occurrences have a significant economic impact, as well (Young, p.9).  Deer populations also contribute to the spread of Lyme disease, which is carried by deer ticks (Budiansky, p.86).  More important are highway accidents involving deer.  It seems that in north central Arkansas, nearly everyone has either been involved in an accident with a deer, or knows someone who has, and the consequences are potentially disastrous. 

In August 1997, a twin-engine plane taking off at the North Little Rock Municipal Airport runway hit a deer, forcing the plane to make an emergency landing in Jonesboro without the use of its landing gear, which had been damaged.  The deer died in the accident.  There had been two previous accidents within recent months at the airport (Waller, Aug. 29, 1997, Section 1-B).

Whatever one.s feelings about the beauty of deer and love for wildlife and nature, there are clearly negative consequences of increased deer populations in Arkansas and elsewhere.  The following section will examine the concept of managing deer populations to optimize coexistence between deer and other species, including man.

Managing Deer Populations

The .wildlife management. approach to the problem has become popular among biologists, landowners, and hunters in recent years.  While it may be incomprehensible to animal rights groups, some of whom view deer management as an issue of human convenience (Budiansky, p.86), ecologists advocate the necessity of management as a means of preserving other species and habitats.  In Arkansas, under the oversight of the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission and Cooperative Extension Service, management takes various forms.

Wildlife management has .gone through a philosophical and technical evolution since the turn of this century,. according to Rebecca Stout, Extension Specialist-Wildlife with the University of Arkansas Cooperative Extension Service (1999, p.1).  A photo caption on page 17  in David Donaldson.s 1951 book, Arkansas. Deer Herd expresses the long held view, .The legal hunter should receive the benefit of all surplus deer.. (1951, p. 17).

Gathering information from various sources, it appears that from initially having the purpose of increasing deer herds to benefit hunters (hence, the previous name of .game management.), wildlife management now includes the viewpoints of conservationists, biologists, ecologists, and the general public.  While there are undoubtedly hunters who still see hunting as the primary beneficiary of wildlife management, biologists view hunting as just one of several tools for maintaining the proper balance of deer due to the necessity of humans sharing habitat with them.

In Arkansas, following the major restoration program begun in the early part of the century, game management  took the form of strict enforcement of hunting regulations, restricted hunting seasons, and .bucks only. hunting seasons (Cartwright, p.3).  According to Rebecca Stout, Ph.D. with the University of Arkansas Cooperative Extension Service, .once deer populations were established, then conservative hunting regulations and takings allowed the .surplus. deer to be harvested, much like running a livestock operation.. (1999, p.1).

As previously stated, it became apparent as early as the 1940's that the potential to exceed carrying capacity could occur, which indeed took place in the 1960's and 1970's.  In the 70's, losses in Arkansas. deer herd due to disease were very heavy.  Research on south Arkansas. deer herd began to show imbalances in herd sex ratios, meaning there were too many does for the number of bucks, as well as poor physical condition of individuals, as determined by low body weights and low quality antlers.  Additionally, there was poor herd age structure, meaning very few adult bucks over 2.5 years of age.  These problems were due to the .bucks only. hunting seasons that had prevailed for years (Cartwright, p.4).

It came to be seen that heavy hunting pressure on bucks and too many deer for habitat conditions were the main cause of management problems.  The solution was seen as reducing  hunting pressure on bucks, allowing more to reach adult age and size, and harvesting does (Cartwright, p. 4).

In the Ozarks of north-central Arkansas, an area of traditionally lower deer population densities, as recently as 1986, deer populations were below carrying capacity, and it was necessary to continue restrictions on legal either-sex hunting to allow populations to increase.  This area was also prone to more illegal hunting, which further reduced numbers of deer (Cartwright, p. 4). 

Hunters had become so used to the .bucks only. rules that changes proposed by biologists were, and still are, difficult for many hunters to accept.  Again, according to Stout, .it has taken some time to change hunters. philosophies that shooting does is a necessary part. of game management  (1999, p.1).

Arkansas divides its deer herd into 20 zones.  Zones are based on deer population densities, habitat quality and distribution, human population densities, physiographic features, and sociological and political influences.  A statewide deer management plan was developed and adopted in 1981 by the Game and Fish Commission.  This plan includes management objectives which .can only be accomplished by legal either-sex deer harvest in many areas of the state..  (Cartwright, p. 4).  According to 1997 data, the most recent available, 2,332 deer were harvested in Van Buren County and 3,467 from Cleburne counties.  This compares to as many as 8,217 in Union county, and ranges from four to six thousand in each of several south-central Arkansas counties.  Deer taken totaled 167,305, a state record of 10% more than the previous year, and were composed of 63% bucks and 37% does. The 1997 deer season was the 81st in Arkansas (Appendix - AG&F Map).

Regardless of one.s feelings about hunting, it cannot be argued that the ecological impact of overpopulation has repercussions for all of us.  William Porter estimates that without hunting, deer populations would be five times what they are presently (Budiansky, p.86).  Nonlethal alternatives to hunting have been tried in some areas, but the costs of trapping and removal, assuming someone can be found to take the animals, run $600 a head.  It would be necessary to treat at least 80% of the entire female population with contraceptive surgical implants or immunizations to be effective, and this would cost from $500 to $1,000 per head (Budiansky, p.86).

Moral issues aside, with such figures, it becomes clear that involving hunters in wildlife management is a necessity.  Many hunters follow a code of ethics, as set down by tradition and by such organizations as the .Boone and Crockett Club,. founded in 1887 by Theodore Roosevelt.  Its goals include not only .to promote manly sport with the rifle,. but .to work for the preservation of the wild animal life of this country,. among others.  With its well known .Rules of Fair Chase. (defined as .the ethical, sportsmanlike and lawful pursuit and taking of any free-ranging wild game animal in a manner that does not give the hunter an improper or unfair advantage over such game animals,. and which lists such methods),  Boone and Crockett promotes hunting sportsmanship, ethics, and laws (Boone & Crockett, p.4).  Many hunters view themselves as .hunter-conservationists. or guardians of not only wildlife, but habitat.

 Aside from official, state-sanctioned laws and practices, private landowners, hunters, and individuals have an impact on the habitat and numbers of whitetail deer.  It is a growing trend for hunter-landowners and non-hunting landowners in Arkansas to have an impact on the physical habitat of whitetail deer through habitat improvement practices.  This takes the form of supplementing deer diets through food plots, which are planted in crops designed to enhance deer health, and creating or improving existing habitat.  Some landowners plant trees that provide shelter, a canopy, browse, and mast (nuts accumulated on the forest floor - Webster, 1995).  Selective logging is also done to enhance habitat.  Sometimes, blown down trees are left to make bedding sites thicker (Amenrud).  The Arkansas Extension Service provides landowners with information on how to construct brush piles for deer and other wildlife to use as resting and escape cover (Bratton, p.1).

 As far as our research can determine, it was as recent as the early 1980's that biologists believed there to be no significant impact on deer herd size or quality of individuals from .farming. for whitetails.  When, in 1983, Dr. Gary Schwarz, of the now famed Tecomate Ranch in South Texas approached wildlife managers and range scientists with his idea of intensively managing his ranch to produce trophy whitetails through the right combination of plants in the proper size, shape, and distribution of food plots, he was told that it could never work.  Biologists at the time were convinced that supplemental feeding of deer was a waste of time and money  (Schwarz, Nov./Dec. 1998, p.72).  Results on the Tecomate Ranch have proven otherwise, and wildlife management as it pertains to deer has reached nationwide proportions.

Dr. Schwarz.s ranch developed their own special formula seed mix called .LabLab. which works successfully in Texas.  Other companies have developed and are marketing their own mixes, including special clover mixes.  One term used to describe such plantings are .greenfields,. which refers to special plantings designed to lure deer and turkeys on their daily feeding rounds  (Fegely, p.1).  In Arkansas, the Cooperative Extension Service makes information about pasture, hay, winter cover crops, and legumes available to interested landowners, and provides federal funds to cost-share the implementation of it (Davis).  They provide information about varieties that work well in specific areas, as well as providing seeding rates and dates.  They also supply information from seed companies that have wild game food lists and ordering information, (Deauman - Table, Kaufman-Seeds - .Wild Game Food List.) but it is up to individual landowners to develop a  program on their own land.

 In some states, county deer management cooperatives have been formed with the stated goal of improving .the health of the deer herd through biologically sound harvest management,. as in Kent County, Maryland.  Record-keeping and the sharing of data is an essential element of such a program (Knauss, Jan. 30, 1998).  Dooly County, Georgia was the first in the nation to be chosen to try quality deer management on a countywide scale (Thurmond, 1998, p. 80).  In Arkansas, information about food plots and ground cover is provided by the Cooperative Extension Service, and books on management are available for purchase and in libraries through the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, but the actual development of a management plan lies with the initiative of individual landowners, and is therefore rather scattered.  Still, there is good effort to make the public aware of opportunities in wildlife management.  For example, on Feb. 13, 1999, a Wildlife Habitat Improvement Workshop was held in Fayetteville, featuring nationally known speakers in the field, and addressing methods of managing pastures, grasslands, and forests, establishing food plots and regenerating native food plants, as well as habitat improvement through prescribed burning, tree cutting, tree thinning, etc.  (Extension Service flyers).

It is interesting to note that in spite of such efforts, according to Rebecca Stout, PhD., there is debate among wildlife biologists about the value of food plots.  She states, .Unless food plots are carefully planned to provide a year-round food supply, they may do little to improve deer populations, health, and survivability.  Planting a fall food plot might congregate the deer so that hunters have a better chance of harvesting one, but the most critical time for survival for deer tends to be about February or March following a harsh winter.  To have even a marginal impact on the deer population, food plots need to be planted so that it provides food for deer at that critical time.  Therefore, basing changes in the deer population and its health on food plots may also be erroneous..  (Stout, pp. 1-2).  However, research on Texas. Tecomate Ranch does seem to indicate that both populations and individuals are enhanced through wildlife management practices (Schwarz, p.156).  Additionally, according to Dr. Wiley Johnson, Agronomist and Director of Forage Research at the Whitetail Institute of North America in Pintlala, AL, proper nutrition determines antler growth as well as healthy fawn production.  He concurs with Dr. Stout that food plots that become dormant in late winter and spring are not as effective as the year-round availability of the proper nutrient mix (Fegely, p.3).

Using information  available in Arkansas and from other sources, the Longfield Deer Camp on Culpepper Mountain in Van Buren County began a program of selective harvesting, record-keeping, food plots, and mineral supplementation.  .Every year that we.ve harvested doe, we have seen more deer each year beyond that.  This year, in the last day of the season, I sat on my stand and saw 12 deer around me, for the first time in 18 years.  Three were mature bucks, and one was a young buck,. said Mark Davis, D.D.S., who began the program. (Dr. Davis is herein quoted in his capacity as a nationally published author in hunting magazines, as well as his knowledge of hunting and wildlife management.)  Although he could have legally taken any of these deer, as a wildlife manager, Dr. Davis chose not to take any of them.  Dr. Davis states that in the same way that young calves are not slaughtered, it is unwise to harvest young deer.  .If you harvest mature deer, you still have a thriving population.  Food plots are crucial for deer, particularly after the rut, when male deer have depleted their fat and muscle mass.  If there were no food plots or browse for them to feed on at the end of the rut, near the end of December, there would be nothing for them to eat.  By that time, much of the acorns and browse have been consumed.  Although deer in Arkansas could get by, they will not prosper..  It is interesting to note that in this camp, the food plots are winter hardy and support the deer through the crucial periods cited by researchers.  Additionally, these plots support the deer throughout the year, and not just during the fall hunting season.  Dr. Davis states that not only do food plots help deer, but all other creatures in the food chain benefit from a sound and complete nutritional diet.

The Longfield Deer Camp has kept records for the past five years.  .We.ve noticed that as we.ve taken does, the population has not decreased.  There are definitely more sightings..  The camp has aged the deer harvested for the past two years, and is working constantly to educate themselves.  Practicing game management has required members to hunt differently than they have for all of their lives (Davis).

It is undeniable that the area of north-central Arkansas has seen a dramatic increase in its whitetail deer population.  According to Mike Cartwright, deer and elk coordinator for the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, .The Ozarks are really coming on strong as far as deer numbers..  Hunters and residents of the area provide evidence of this fact in numbers of deer harvested, (Bowman, Nov. 12/ 1996, Section C) in sightings of animals, in damage to gardens and shrubbery by foraging deer, and in vehicle accidents with deer.  While numbers of deer in north central Arkansas are not usually considered so excessive as to pose an acute problem, it is only through the efforts of landowners and hunters, in cooperation with biologists and our state agencies, that a proper balance is being achieved.  This balance is necessary to allow the continued prosperity of all interacting species.
