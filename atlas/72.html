INTRODUCTION

As we travel down the congested roads filled with construction and notice the development along the highway, we are forced to ask ourselves the question, “Is Arkansas growing?” If it is, then how and why is it growing, and is it a fast growth or moderate growth? To frame this question and give perspective, many different elements should be represented. The topics in which we will be discussing will include the present and past population growth of Arkansas. This will give us a good start as to how people have settled into Arkansas and how the population is growing. The next area of focus will be the current and past status of the Central Arkansas Counties and the reason why we picked them. The final area will be the economic status of Arkansas compared to the nation and why companies should move, start, or expand in Arkansas. So the question is, will the south rise again?

POPULATION GROWTH OF ARKANSAS

Arkansas had a population of 2.523 million residents in July, 1997 (http://www.census.gov/population/estimates/county/co-97-1_15.txt). The newest county population estimates recently released by the Bureau of the Census show continued population growth of Arkansas with a total growth of 172,195 (a growth rate of 7.3 percent) between 1990 and 1997 (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/research/popest.html). In fact, the Institute for Economic Advancement states that the growth is more than double the 64,000 (a growth rate of 3.0 percent) growth that had occurred during the 1980-1990 years. The Institute for Economic Advancement states that the state will keep continuing its population increases—from 2,350,725 in 1990 to 2,547,800 people in 2010 (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/research/projann.html).

PAST POPULATON GROWTH OF ARKANSAS

To set the current population growth of Arkansas into context, we looked at the state’s past growth. All of the statistics in this section are from Gunderson (1986).

From 1960 to 1970 Arkansas’s population increased by 7.7%, and during the 1970’s the rate of growth accelerated to 18.9%. In the 1950’s, the population declined everywhere in Arkansas except in the Urban Region. In the Urban Region, the population increased from 1950 to 1980. From 1960 to 1980 the population increased in the Ozark, Arkansas River Valley, Northwest, West, and Southwest regions (see Figures 1 and 2). In the Northeast, Southeast, and South regions, the population continued to decline from 1960 to 1970 but rose in the 1970’s.

Arkansas’s rural population patterns are similar to those for the total population except in the Northeast, Southeast, and South regions (see Figs. 3 and 4). The rural population of the Northeast Region declined from 1960 to 1970, then it grew 1.1% from 1970 to 1980. The rural population of the South Region rose 4.5% overall during the 1970’s, though it dropped in four of the counties in this region.

The farm population in Arkansas fell 47.6% during the 1960’s and it fell another 38% during the 1970’s (see Figs. 5 and 6). Perry County had the only increase in farm population during the 1960’s, but it lost 37.2% of its farm population from 1970 to 1980. The largest rates of decline in farm population were in the Northeast, Southeast, and South regions.

The decline in Arkansas’s rural population was exceeded only in Mississippi during the 1960’s. Rural population increased in all of Arkansas’s neighboring states during the 1970’s. Texas and Oklahoma’s population grew the most rapidly-27.3% and 20.9%, respectively, from 1970 to 1980. Louisiana had the regional low at 6.8%.

Total population grew in all of these states from 1960 to 1980, but at different rates. Arkansas’s total population growth rates appeared to be the median within the southeastern United States. In the 1960’s Mississippi had the slowest rate of growth (1.8%) and Texas had the most rapid (16.9%). During the 1970’s Texas’ total population still grew more rapidly than any of these states: 27.1%. Missouri had the slowest growth rate in the 1970’s; and the rates for Arkansas, Tennessee and Oklahoma were similar to each other.

From 1975 to 1980, 25 counties had net out-migrations of population. Seventeen of these counties were in the Northeast and Southeast regions. Phillips County had a net out-migration of 3,326 people. Mississippi County lost 2,642 people, and St. Francis County lost 2,217. Drew and Lonoke were the only counties in the Southeast Region with net in-migrations.

Four counties in the South Region had net out-migrations. In the six other regions, only Perry, Newton, Pike, and Lafayette counties had net out-migrations. Benton and Washington counties, both in the Northwest Region, had the largest net in-migrations: 11,670 and 9,265 people, respectively.

CURRENT AND PAST STATUS OF THE COUNTIES OF FOCUS

This portion of the report deals with the current status of population, personal and family economics, and poverty in central Arkansas. The six counties we chose to focus on are: Faulkner, Lonoke, Monroe, Praire, Pulaski and Saline (see Fig. 7). The Arkansas State Highway and Transportation Department, Planning Division, divides the state into development and planning districts with certain counties in each district (Arkansas State Highway and Transportation Department, Planning Division). We used this division of counties to determine which counties to study. We also chose the central Arkansas area because that is where everyone in the group lives

Though the labor force statistics begin at age 16, we felt that this was not an accurate age to use, therefore we decided that 18 years of age was a better starting point for labor force numbers and 64 years of age was a better ending point. The current labor force range according to the Bureau of Census, U.S. Department of Commerce starts at age 16 and ends at infinity when the person dies (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

The poverty status was determined for all persons except those institutionalized, in military group quarters, in college dormitories, and other unrelated individuals under the age of 15. These statistics were based on the definition given by the Social Security Administration in 1964, then modified by Federal interagency committees in 1969 and 1980, prescribed by the Office of Management and Budget (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

Faulkner County currently has a total population of 60,006 (Figure 8). It is 55.9 percent rural and 44.1 percent urban. Over 68 percent of the county's population falls in the 18 to 64 year old category which is the prime age category for employment. Roughly 47 percent of Faulkner county residents are employed and over 59 percent of the persons of age to work are employed. Unfortunately, Faulkner County is still at or near 7 percent unemployment (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

In 1991, Faulkner County had a per capita income of $13,699 a year, which was a 47 % increase over the 1983 per capita income of $7,267. In 1996, Faulkner County enjoyed a mean yearly household income of $28,064. Currently, Faulkner county has 10 percent of its families under the poverty level and 13.8 percent poverty overall (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

Lonoke County currently has a total population of 39,268 (Figure 8). It is 60 percent rural and 40 percent urban. Slightly fewer than 60 percent of the county's population falls in the 18 to 64 year old category, which is the prime age category for employment. Roughly 44 percent of Lonoke county residents are employed and just under 60 percent of the persons of age to work are employed. Lonoke County is still at or near 6.4 percent unemployment (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

In 1991, Lonoke County has a per capita income of $12,725 a year, this is a 35% jump in per capita income from 1983 when it was a mere $8,354. In 1996, Lonoke County ended the year with mean yearly household income of $28,885. Unfortunately, Lonoke county currently has 11.4 percent of its families under the poverty level and 14.9 percent poverty overall (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

Monroe County currently has a total population of 60,006 (Figure 8). It is 55.9 percent rural and 44.1 percent urban. Over 68 percent of the county's population falls in the 18 to 64 year old category which is the prime age category for employment. Roughly 47 percent of Monroe county residents are employed and over 59 percent of the persons of age to work are employed. Unfortunately, Monroe County is still at or near 7 percent unemployment (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

In 1991, Monroe County has a per capita income of $13,699 a year, this is a 33% increase from the 1983 per capita income level of $7,180. In 1996, Monroe County ended with a mean yearly household income of $28,064. Currently, Monroe County has 10 percent of its families under the poverty level and 13.8 percent poverty overall (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

Pulaski County currently has a total population of 349,660 (Figure 8). It is 89 percent urban and 11 percent rural. These large numbers for both population and population concentration should come as no surprise considering Pulaski county is home to the state's capitol and largest city, Little Rock. Over sixty percent of the county's population falls in the 18 to 64 year old category, which is the prime age category for employment. Consequently, roughly 50 percent of Pulaski county residents are employed and over 80 percent of the persons of age to work are employed. Pulaski County is still hovering at or near 5 percent unemployment (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

In 1991, Pulaski County has a per capita income of $16,665 a year, this is a 38% increase from 1983 when the per capita income was at $10,368. In 1996, Pulaski County enjoyed a robust mean yearly household income of $34,770. Needless to say Pulaski County currently has a low percent of families under the poverty level at 10.5 percent. The average poverty level for a family of four in Pulaski County is $12,678 (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

Saline County currently has a total population of 64,183 (Figure 8). It is 51.4 percent rural and 48.6 percent urban. Over 61 percent of the county's population falls in the 18 to 64 year old category which is the prime age category for employment. Roughly 46 percent of Saline county residents are employed and over 61 percent of the persons of age to work are employed. Saline County is at or near 5.1 percent unemployment (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

In 1991, Saline County has a per capita income of $13,445 a year, this is a 47% increase since the 1983 level of $7,171. In 1996, Saline County ended with mean yearly household income of $32,344. Currently, Saline county has 6.9 percent of its families under the poverty level and 9.3 percent poverty overall (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/csdc/cntyprofiles).

ECONOMIC STATUS OF ARKANSAS

When we discuss growth, economic development is extremely paramount. The population demographics of Arkansas consist of things like income and poverty rates. The population when dealing with income is comprised of three levels, poor, near poor, and non-poor. The percentages of these are 17%, 26.2%, and 56.6% respectively. The percentages when compared to the United States as a whole only change slightly to 15.4%, 20%, and 64.7% respectively. These numbers indicate that Arkansas is on course with the nation. The poverty rates of Arkansas are broken into three categories; children under age 19, 24%, adults ages 19-64, 12.4%, and 24.4% for the elderly of ages 65 and older, where as the United State’s are 22.9%, 11.8%, and 15.7% (http://www.kff.org/state/ar.html).


PAST GROWTH RATES FOR THE COUNTES OF FOCUS

Arkansas’ population increased 7.7 percent between 1960 and 1970, and the state increased 10.0 percent between 1970 and 1975 (Monroe/Arkansas Department of Local Services, 1977). However, not all the counties we focused on contributed to the state’s growth. Faulkner, Lonoke, Pulaski, and Saline increased in population, but Monroe and Prairie had a decrease in growth. The decrease or increase of population was due natural increase—more births over deaths—and by migration. All the statistics that are discussed can be seen in Tables ?-?.

Faulkner County experienced an increase of 8.9 percent between 1960 and 1970 and an increase of 6.0 percent between 1970 and 1975 (Foulkner/Arkansas Department of Local Services, 1977). [NOTE: Increase statistics for Faulkner County in the years between 1960 and 1970 and between 1970 and 1975 could not be found.]

Lonoke County experienced a moderate increase of 6.9 percent during 1960 and 1970 (Lonoke/Arkansas Department of Local Services, 1977). It continued to increase the next five years by 17.7 percent to 30,900 population. The Arkansas Department of Local Services contributed the majority of its growth to migration (1977). The out-migration was -4.0 between 1960 and 1970, and the in-migration became more important between 1970 and 1974 with a rate of 13.6 percent.

Pulaski and Saline County were two of Arkansas’ fastest growing counties. Pulaski’s total population increased 18.2 percent between 1960 and 1970, and it increased by 12.9 percent during the next five years to a population of 324,200 (Pulaski/Arkansas Department of Local Services, 1977). Pulaski’s growth was many due to natural increase. Migration also played a role to the increase, and the Arkansas Department of Local Services said that between 1960 and 1970, the in-migration rate was 2.8 percent. This rate continued to increase through 1975 by 6.7 percent. Saline County’s total population increase was even higher that Pulaski’s. Saline increased by 24.7 percent between 1960 and 1970 and by 19.2 percent between 1970 and 1975 to a population of 43,000. However, unlike Pulaski, most of Saline’s growth was due to migration. Between 1960 and 1970, Saline’s in-migration rate was 12.7 percent. It was even higher between 1970 and 1975 with a rate of 15.0 percent.

Unlike the previous counties mentioned, Monroe County’s population decreased. The population decreased by 9.6 percent between 1960 and 1970 (Monroe/Arkansas Department of Local Services,1977). The county continued to decrease the next five years by 2.7 percent to a population of 15,200. The Arkansas Department of Local Services suggested that the decrease was due to the out-migration rate being 21.9 percent (1977). However, during 1970 and 1975 the out-migration became less important to the decrease with a rate of 5.1 percent.

Prairie County also experienced a population decrease. Between 1960 and 1970, the total population decreased 2.5 percent. By 1975, the county decreased even further with a rate of 3.1 percent to a population of 9,900. Much of the county’s population lost was due to migration. The out-migration rate between 1960 and 1970 was 9.8 percent, and the rate was 4.8 percent during 1970 and 1975.

ECONOMIC CONDITIONS IN ARKANSAS

Over the last two years, Arkansas has experienced significant job loss from plant closings in the apparel industries(http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/fcast/apr98.htlm). The manufacturing employment of goods in Arkansas is stabilizing due from strong demand for parts and consumer goods. With Arkansas’s comparatively low corporate tax burden and progressive tax incentives, the state has managed to maintain its economic growth and development. Arkansas attracts companies looking to locate or expand due to it’s geographical location, efficient transportation to important markets, excellent labor relations, a strong infrastructure, services for established industries, quality of life and the diverse landscape and natural resources (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/fcast/apr98.htlm).

ECONOMIC FORECAST SUMMATION IN ARKANSAS

Research has shown that the state’s manufacturing base and economy should continue to grow and benefit from a fundamentally sound domestic U.S. economy. The restrictions on a tight labor market should lessen due to the low cost of capital and retail trade should hold steady on a moderate growth trend. The state’s employed residents should see total wage and salary income gains during 1998 and exceed the previous two years. However, the Arkansas outlook remains somewhat cautious for 1999. This outlook is due to such factors as serious problems with the Asian economics, the Y2K computer problem which could pose a mixed economic impact picture for the state, personal income fluctuating between 4.5% and 5.5%, and wage gains slowing slightly due to a higher employment growth and a continuation of the tight labor market. Inflation will move up slightly from unusually low levels to about 3% and the state revenue collections should remain stable for the next three years. In order for the state’s economy to continue to reflect growth, there has to be a continuous commitment to increase concessions to companies that have ongoing high wage, high-skill jobs to expand and locate in Arkansas (http://www.aiea.ualr.edu/research/fcast/apr98.html).

CONCLUSION

The question, “Will the south rise again?” was proposed in the introduction. Well, as far as the state of Arkansas is concerned it will—just not by tomorrow. Arkansas’ growth may not be rapid or Phoenix-like, however it is at a steady pace. The areas which we determined best illustrated growth are the state’s population and economy. Arkansas’ population grew 7.3 percent between 1990 and 1997. The best determiner of a region’s economic growth has to be in its individual per capita growth and ability to bring in new businesses, to create further economic growth. In both of these categories, Arkansas has grown right at or slightly above the national average. In fact, according to Survey of Current Business, Arkansas, as a state, has placed in the top quarter for individual per capita growth since third quarter of 1997 (Survey of Current Business). As for the six Central Arkansas counties we focused on, Faulkner, Lonoke, Monroe, Praire, Pulaski and Saline, their growth have been overall constant but not that impressive. This is because the counties are so diverse. For instance, Pulaski County has a population of 349,660 with an individual per capita of $22,356, while Monroe County with a population of 60,006 and an individual per capita of $13,440. So will the South rise again? Right now, no—but, with Arkansas’ steadily increasing growth rate, it may be on its way.
