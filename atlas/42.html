What impact does the Faulkner County Levee district No.1 have on the city of Conway?

B6: Natural Resource Geography

“Faulkner County Levee No. 1”

By Jermey Hutchins, Sam Hall, and Clayton Blair

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2519

Prepared: April 29, 2000

What impact does the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 have on the city of Conway?

 

            The Arkansas River rises from the eastern slope of the Rocky Mountains, and flows southeast through Kansas, Oklahoma, and Arkansas before entering the Mississippi River. The Arkansas River is the largest tributary of the Mississippi-Missouri River system (Arkansas River).  The volume of water that is carried by the Arkansas River is determined by several variables, including water run off from rain and melting snow and ice.  The amount of water received from this run off varies greatly throughout the year. This variance in the volume of water can lead to the sudden rising and flooding of the river. Because of this unpredictable behavior levees and other forms of flood protection were often implemented in the more densely populated regions where the flooding was most severe (Arkansas River).  One of these regions is located just west of the city of Conway, near the small farming community of Lolly.  At this site it was decided that a levee should be built to protect the fertile farmland and surrounding community (Reconnaissance).  A levee is an embankment constructed of earth that runs parallel to the body of water that it is restraining.  Conway and the area surrounding it have been and are currently experiencing a rapid growth in population, and therefore have experienced rapid development of land and expansion of the city.  The western section of Conway is where a majority of this development and expansion has occurred (Faulkner County).  As this trend of western expansion towards the Arkansas River continues, it will eventually extend to the regions that were frequently flooded by the river before the levee was constructed.  This leads us to the purpose of this paper, which is to find out what impact the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 has on the city of Conway.

            The Arkansas General Assembly founded the Faulkner County Levee District in 1905 with the passage of Act 20 (Arkansas River).  The Faulkner County Levee District was built approximately eight miles southwest of Conway on the Faulkner County side of the Arkansas River.  The Faulkner County Levee District was comprised of several levees that were not interconnected, and it originated with the purpose of constructing and maintaining levees, locks, dams, and culverts to protect the lands in the district from the overflows of the Arkansas River (Arkansas River).  The system of levees that were constructed in 1905 protected the Faulkner County Levee District for 22 years before it was overcome by the floodwaters of the Arkansas River.  This flood, which occurred in 1927, broke through two of the levee districts main levees and quickly filled the surrounding 8,000 acres of land with water from 5 to 15 feet deep.  Almost all of the crops that were planted, and several homes were destroyed in the flood (Patterson).  Traces of the tremendous flood can still be seen in the levee district in the form of gullies and washouts. 

The next big event for The Faulkner County Levee District occurred when the federal government approved the Flood Control Act of 1936.  This called for the reconstruction of The Faulkner County Levee District by adding new levee sections to several existing sections of levee (Renaissance).  The levee was raised to a height of one foot above the 1927 flood level, and the top, or crown, of the levee was extended to a width of eight feet.  The levee was also lengthened to 6.7 miles.  The reconstruction project cost the federal government $99,511, and was completed by the Army Corps of Engineers in 1938.  The new levee was named Faulkner County Levee District Levee No.1.  In 1945 and 1949 several additional setback levees were constructed because of Arkansas River bank caving (Reconnaissance).  Over the next fifty years no major advancements were made to The Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1.  The Faulkner County Levee District oversaw the minor repairs (Arkansas State water plan).

In 1990 the waters of the Arkansas River rose to within two feet of the top of the levee in many places, and within one foot of the top of the levee at one point (Faulkner County).  The next year, 1991, the Federal Emergency Management Association (FEMA) reevaluated its Flood Insurance Rate Maps (FIRM), and found that the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 did not meet one of their requirements to be considered a landward flood protection structure (Hall,98).  The requirement that was not met dealt was the lack of an adequate freeboard of three feet. Freeboard is the measurement from the water level to the top of the levee.  Because the freeboard did not meet their specifications, FEMA disregarded the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 (Hall,96).  This means that when FEMA constructed the FIRM maps in 1991, which show the flood plain of a region, they acted as if there was not a levee protecting the area.  The 1991 FIRM maps did not have an immediate effect on city officials; however, as the city of Conway began to expand west towards the Arkansas River it became an issue. 

In 1995 Congress appropriated $474,000 to complete a reconnaissance study by the Army Corps of Engineers on Levee No. 1.  After completion, the Corps of Engineers recommended raising the entire levee to an elevation of 2.1 feet above the 100-year flood elevation for the Arkansas River (Hall,96).  The cost of this renovation would cost approximately $300,000, but the Corps of Engineers required design criteria based on the probability of failure analysis and did not meet the three-foot freeboard required by FEMA. To meet the three foot freeboard required by FEMA it would cost an estimated $560,000.  The majority of the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 already met the Corps of Engineers criteria (2.1 feet above 100-year river flood), with about 1.1 mile of the levee needing to be raised one foot.  To conform to the FEMA requirements of a freeboard of three feet about 1.1 mile would need to be raised two feet and an additional one-half mile would have to be raised one foot (Hall,96). 

In April of 1999 reconstruction of the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 was completed.  It was decided that the criteria set forth by the Army Corps of Engineers would be followed (Faulkner County).  Therefore, the levee was raised to an elevation of 2.1 feet above the level of the 100-year flood level.  It was also widened at the top from eight to ten feet across.  These modifications that were made to the levee cost approximately $326,577 (Faulkner County).  Even with these recent improvements to Levee No.1 FEMA has not revised the FIRM maps and classified the levee as being a landward flood protection structure.  FEMA has relaxed its policy on freeboard to a certain extent, as long as the levee is structurally sound.  The Faulkner County Levee District Levee No. 1 meets this requirement, and the city of Conway is currently awaiting a response from FEMA on their current attempt to change the FIRM maps (Hall,96).

The Faulkner County Levee District Levee No.1 will play an important part in the future development of Conway.  If FEMA does not change their status on the levee then development will not be allowed to continue to expand west towards the Arkansas River.  The reason for this is that no building within the city limits of Conway is allowed to have their floor level below the flood plain of  285 feet (above sea level) in elevation without permission from the city council (Hall,96).  Without a reevaluation of the flood plain it is expected that flood plain development would either continue to be restricted or very expensive.  This most dramatically affects the areas located outside the western and southwestern portions of Conway.  This includes areas surrounding Tucker Creek, Tupelo Bayou, and western portions of Arkansas State Highway 60( Faulkner County).  If Levee No.1 were to gain status by the proper authorities as a landward flood protection structure, then the western edge of Conway will continue to expand until it reaches the Arkansas River.  The new flood plain elevations would be closer to 279 feet (above sea level) in elevation near the sections of Tucker Creek, and 272 feet (above sea level) in elevation near section of Tupelo Bayou (Memorandum).  This would allow the development of land that was once thought suitable only for agriculture.  You could live extremely close to the Arkansas River and not have to worry about living in the flood plain. 

As you can see, the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No.1 has made huge strides since its original construction over 95 years ago.  The Faulkner County Levee District was founded to save a small community and its surrounding farmland, and is now playing a vital role in the expansion of a rapidly growing city.  There are still some uncertainties ahead pertaining to the matter of what impact the Faulkner County Levee District Levee No.1 has and will have in the future.  Whether Levee No.1 serves to inhibit growth in the future, or if the levee serves as a catalyst for the rapid expansion of what was once considered to be a worthless flood plain, there is no doubt that it has a substantial impact on the city of Conway.
