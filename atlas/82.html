The Impact of Hispanic Migration to Arkansas

What are the social, cultural and economic effects of Hispanic migration into Arkansas?



The United States is currently undergoing unprecedented demographic changes. Due to immigration, which is the number one cause of population change in the U.S.-not births and deaths, white people will be in the minority by the middle of this century (Tilove 1998:1). This is based on the current trend of immigration and would be different if immigration were sharply curtailed; however, minority populations would still have greatly increased by the year 2050 because immigrants tend to have higher birth rates than Americans (Tilove 1998:2). Although the number of Asian immigrants is an important factor in considering these changes, Hispanics are the fastest growing minority in America. There are already more Hispanic children than there are of other minorities, and by the year 2005 Hispanics will be the largest minority group (Vobejda 1998:1). By 2050, the ethnic make-up of the U.S. will be 51% non-Hispanic white, 26% Hispanic, 14% black, and 8% Asian (Tilove 1998:2). It is important to note that “Hispanic” does not refer to race. According to the Census Bureau, a Hispanic is someone from Mexico, Puerto Rico, Cuba, Central America, or South America (Green 1998:1). That includes a diverse range of races, cultural backgrounds, and languages, although Spanish is the major one. In the 1990 census about half of Hispanics considered themselves racially white, while about 40% classified themselves as racially “other” (Tilove 1998:2). There are also African-American Hispanics (Cossman 1998:2).

Arkansas has seen a very significant increase in its Hispanic population in recent years. It leads the U.S. in Hispanic population growth rate, with a 127% increase since 1990 and raw numbers that exceeded predictions for 2005 in 1998! (Green 1998:1). About 25,000 Hispanics came to Arkansas between 1990 and 1997, most of them going to the northwestern part of the state (Green 1998:1, 2). About 65% of immigrants come because they already have immediate family here (Editorial 1998:1). Arkansas is seeing mainly secondary migration where Hispanics who were already in the U.S., in places like California or Texas, are coming to Arkansas. This accounts for the fact that only about 10% of Hispanics in Arkansas are illegal immigrants (Cossman 2001:3). Most Hispanics in Arkansas come from either Mexico or Central America (Green 1998:1). Figures from the 200 census show an even greater increase in Arkansas’ Hispanic population, with county numbers ranging from 62 up to 13,469 (http://argis.ualr.edu/00_hpop_cty.pdf) and percent change ranging from 46.9 percent to 1,420.3 percent (http://argis.ualr.edu/00_hpop_chg.pdf). By looking more closely at economics and language, we can get a better idea of what effects these increases may have on Arkansas in the future.

It has been a commonly held view that immigrants take jobs away from Americans, but is this really the case here in Arkansas? Hispanics are certainly coming here because of economic opportunities, but are they doing it at the expense of Arkansans who are already in the working force? As noted earlier, the highest concentration of Hispanic immigration is found in northwest Arkansas, and there are economic reasons behind this. The main draw is the poultry industry, as demonstrated by the Tyson Corporation’s various efforts to improve its burgeoning Hispanic workforce with English language and other programs (Kelley and Cossman 2000:10).

Another economic factor, besides just jobs, is the Hispanics’ impact on health care. That is one important service that everyone needs, obviously, and, as Hispanics become increasingly important to the work force, their health becomes increasingly important (Kelly and Cossman 2000:9). Health professionals and patients are becoming quite frustrated as more Hispanics seek health care it since it takes 2 to 3 times longer to help them “primarily because of translation issues, their lack of familiarity with modern medical practice, terminology, and individual medical histories” (Kelly and Cossman 2000:7).

Communication is a very important part of society, and language barriers are huge barriers to the diffusion of menti-facts, which are ideas, beliefs, philosophies, etc., and socio-facts, which are formal and informal social institutions. In order for enclaves, minorities on the fringes of society such as newly arriving immigrants, to become integrated, it is very important for such diffusion of ideas to occur. So, the assimilation of Hispanic immigrants hinges on their either learning English or having vital information available to them in Spanish.

This issue brings strong reactions at times since encountering Spanish all over causes many Americans to feel as if the U.S. were being transformed into a foreign land (Tilove 1998:1). Arkansas is conscious of its rising Hispanic population. Publications for Spanish speakers are available: El Hispano has been published in Little Rock since 1995 and earlier this year, El Latino, a new Spanish weekly published by the company that publishes Arkansas Times, made its debut on Arkansas newsstands; newer Wal-Mart stores carry People magazine in Spanish; and immigrants find “The Hispanic Newcomer’s Guide to Pulaski County” useful (Kelly and Cossman 2000:7). The Arkansas legislature has also made recent attempts to encourage more Arkansas students to learn Spanish. A bill was introduced and passed by the state Senate that would make it mandatory for all Arkansas high schools to offer at least two years of Spanish. Senator Jodie Mahony, who introduced this bill, was promoting it on the basis that Arkansas would benefit economically because of its proximity to Spanish speaking countries and the increasing number of Spanish speakers within the state if more Arkansans could speak Spanish (Osher 2001:1). This bill did not pass the House, however. Opponents claimed that it would hurt programs of other foreign languages and take control away from local school districts (“House Education Committee kills bill to favor teaching Spanish in schools” 2001:1).

The issue of Spanish in the public school systems is hotly debated all over the country, wherever significant numbers of Spanish speakers live. It is obvious that improvements in teaching Hispanic children must be made since their national achievement test scores are below average, and they are more likely to drop out of school than their white peers (Zehr 2000:1). The purpose of English as a second language (ESL) programs is help children improve their language proficiency to the point that they can attend all the required classes, which are taught in English, without falling behind (Stewart 2001:3). “Much of the research says that it takes three to four years for English competency to develop to such a level that students can learn the content in English,” said Emily de la Cruz, an education professor at Portland State University (AP 2001:1). Until they have the English skills, though, students learn mainly in their native language (Breslau 2001:2). The quicker student move out of this bilingual instruction the better, but some kids stay in it for six or seven years (Editorial 2001:1). Critics say that this is way too long and that this approach allows Spanish-speaking children to stay in their comfort zone (AP 2001:1). Although reforms limiting the time a student could remain in a bilingual program have been proposed, opponents feel that children would learn English much more quickly if they were totally immersed in English. Such immersion programs have already started in California, which has resulted in better English reading comprehension test scores among English learners (Breslau 2001:3).

Educators in Arkansas have also found that English immersion is most advantageous. The Springdale school district has students in its ESL program who speak Vietnamese and Laotian at home. Since none of the teachers speak these languages fluently, those children are totally immersed in English at school, and, according to Patricia Garcia, the ESL director there, they learn English more quickly than the students who speak Spanish (Branam 2001:1). St. Edward’s School, a Catholic private school in Little Rock that has had a drastic increase in new Hispanic students in recent years, teaches their ESL students in an English immersion environment in which they learn the language quickly (Gillespie 2001:1). The Rogers school district is making plans to start a special intensive English school for kids who are either new arrivals in the U.S. and have had no exposure to English or ones who have already lived here for a time but need special help. This program would be for students in grades 6-12, but they are already talking about creating one for younger children, too (Magsam 2001b:1).

Just how successful have schools been at integrating students and their parents into their new hometowns? One Arkansas elementary teacher has made some interesting observations. Beth Adame, of Dardanelle, said that the younger the students are, the more open they are to acculturation, or the exchange of cultural ideas and practices. She noted, though, that older children, teenagers, and adults are more prone to let cultural differences result in misunderstandings, racial slurs, and even violence. She said “the Mexican culture is so different from this culture that they often tend to clash.” On the bright side, though, she has seen “many Mexicans embrace this culture and try their best to assimilate” (interview with Beth Adame 2001). School administrators in northwest Arkansas also want to help Hispanics become more integrated by getting them more involved in their children’s schools (Magsam 2001a:1). Such parent-school participation is a very important socio-fact and can help a lot with community unity. The Rogers and Springdale school districts are trying to hire more translators so that they can communicate more easily with the parents who do not yet speak very much English. One school has even started a parent center with adult English classes and parenting courses in Spanish (Magsam 2001b:1). It seems like they really want to help the newcomers become part of their communities.

The influx of Hispanics into Arkansas has had a measurable impact on almost every aspect of the state’s society. On the economic front, Hispanics have come to Arkansas to take advantage of the numerous jobs in the poultry and other industries. In turn, health care facilities and other providers of social services have been forced to accommodate their needs. The language barrier has also been a notable source of impact with the immigration of Hispanics, as those same social and health care services have had to solve the problem of communication before even considering their capacity to provide their services. In addition, public schools have had to accommodate the educational needs of Hispanic children. By looking at these two pivotal areas of society, along with the latest census numbers regarding their population, we can see that Hispanics’ influence on Arkansas can only increase in the future.
