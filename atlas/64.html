“Watch out for that fuel spill!”

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2519

                                   Are Fuel Spills a Problem on Greers Ferry Lake?

     In the 1960's when engineers flooded the Greers Ferry/Heber Springs area to form the Greers Ferry Lake for economical purposes  no one thought that this beautiful place of new recreation would one day be harming its own habitat.  All of the excitement was over how tourism was going to build up this area’s economy and how this lake would bring in so many new job opportunities.  Most importantly though, this lake was going to be a sure source of drinking water that could be shared by all of the surrounding communities. (See map of Little Red River that was dammed to form Greers Ferry Lake)

      As we look at Greers Ferry Lake today though, we see so many things no one anticipated.

On a busy day on Greers Ferry Lake boats swarm in and out of marinas in search of the “lake-goer necessities” such as gas, food, and something cold to drink.  With the mad rush to meet the needs of the customer, no one has time to fill up a boat properly to make sure there are no fuel spills.  In turn, by the end of the day, the water around the marina takes on the characteristics of a minute oil slick.  These overflow valves that are supposed to tell you when a boat is full only create a mess on the boat and in the water spilling anywhere from a teaspoon to a half a cup of gasoline every time a boat fills up.

     If you look closely at a map of the Greers Ferry Lake, you will see that the lake is produced from its major source, the Little Red River.  Now, if you look even closer you will see the several different in-takes for a particular community’s drinking water (See map). These communities are: all of Cleburne county, Van Buren county, Guy, Greenbrier, and Conway.  Conway alone has over 35,000 people in it.  The map just goes to show how many different environments would be affected by a fuel spill.  Pollution is something created by us that destroys the environment we live in.  You would think we would take more steps to protect our natural resources, but nowhere in the “Boating Laws” pamphlet for Greers Ferry Lake does it state any stipulations placed on fueling up a boat. However, we are precautioned in the Greers Ferry Lake Map distributed by the Corps of Engineers to “Keep the area beautiful for other visitors.  Avoid damaging trees and plants, extinguish all fires and use trash cans to dispose of refuse” (Map 1).  How can we “keep the area beautiful” when we are steadily destroying it with our own foolishness?  The question is not whether or not fuel spills are a problem on Greers Ferry Lake, but rather how do we prevent it from happening?  How can we as a society spare what is left of our depleting surroundings?  With eight marinas steadily spilling gas into the lake, things are not getting any better for our environment.

     Fuel spills have many affects on the environment.  Vegetation, aquatic life, and human population are areas of the environment that are affected by these spills.  This water pollution costs our society greatly because we have to pay for the destroyed or stunted vegetation, polluted water supplies, loss of fish and wildlife, and some of the same types of health and materials damage that are caused by air pollution.   The standard uses of lake water and rivers consist of aquatic life support, fish consumption, swimming and diving, boating, drinking water supply, and agricultural use.  Once these waters are contaminated, these standard uses are no longer safe.  The funny thing is that no one has ever even studied our lake to see if we are in fuel spill danger.   

     Vegetation is greatly affected by spills causing the plants and many crops to die, and also harming the soil in which they grow.  Not only is future plant life affected by polluting the soil, but also the current life.  “Soil samples are taken from wells to determine the presence of any contaminants in the ground” (Kander 88).  Even chemicals that are intentionally applied annually to streets damage the roadside vegetation and can accumulate in streams, lakes and aquifers, potentially elevating sodium levels in drinking water.  “Recently the Coast Guard has taken a step toward environmental conservation by using the latest technology to clean up contaminated soil” (Kander 88).   Not only is this pollution destroying the quality of soil, but also is harming marine habitats.  

     Another section of the environment affected by fuel spills is the aquatic life.  Many states and governments reported recently that fair or poor water quality impacts aquatic life in over 4.4 million lake acres.  The Environmental Protection Agency encourages the states to designate fish consumption as a separate use in their water bodies to promote consistency in future reporting.  The Environmental Protection Agency ranks the pollutants and processes by the extent of their impacts on aquatic life and human activities rather than actual loads of spills in lakes.  This can be good considering the marine life that lives around the marina, such as grass carp.  You will notice that around a marina the fish are feed by tourists and a lot of times along with this fish food the carp are taking in the gas water.  Now, try to prove that drinking gas is not harmful to a fish.  This approach targets the pollutants and processes causing the most harm to aquatic life and public use of our waters rather than the most abundant pollutants in our lakes, reservoirs, and ponds.  When the vegetation and aquatic life are contaminated, then the human population is greatly affected as well.  Humans depend on plant life to feed them and provide oxygen.  When this oxygen is polluted, then humans are breathing in toxins affecting their health.  According to Banse, “When gasoline spills, about sixty percent of the rainbow slick evaporates into the atmosphere, joining the other airborne toxins that we ultimately breathe in.  The remaining forty percent dirties the water and wreaks havoc on the plants and animals that live there” (Banse 24).  Human’s irresponsibility and carelessness with their water supplies affect not only the aquatic life and vegetation, but also human life.

     This carelessness affects their health, recreation, and even puts a dent in their finances.  “Spilling even a single drop of gasoline on the water can land you big trouble with the Coast Guard.  Under current law, all gasoline or motor spills must be reported, and a boater who causes one can be fined or jailed.  This harsh mandate includes not only fuel overflow, but also the inadvertent pumping overboard of contaminated bilge water” (Banse 24).  A big spill could cut about $5,000 out of a person’s cash flow, which for some people could really crunch into their finance plan.  Humans do not realize the damage they cause themselves and their environment; therefore, they continue to be careless causing their lives to be affected in many ways.  There are many prevention steps we can take to decrease our carelessness and increase the life of our environment.  It is time to stop harming and to start nourishing our surroundings.

     Obviously fuel spills have a devastating affect on our environment.  Fortunately, there are many precautions and preventative steps that can be taken to eliminate this threat.  These steps range from using above-ground tanks to using more efficient shut-off valves on gas lines.

     Steps toward a fuel free environment begin with above-ground storage tanks because underground storage of fuel has been proven to be hazardous to our environment.  Many people today prefer to use underground storage tanks because they have a larger storage capacity than above-ground tanks.  According to Joe Floyd III, “The total above-ground tank storage capacity exceeds 1320 gallons” (Floyd 23).  This compared to the capacity of the underground storage tanks which exceeds 42,000 gallons is not very much (Floyd 23).  The second reason that most people prefer underground fuel storage tanks is due to price.  The above-ground tanks cost around $38,000 each, while the underground tanks cost considerably less.  According to John and Pauline Smith, owners of Smith’s Hill Creek Marina on Greers Ferry Lake, the above-ground tanks were required to be put in by the Corps of Engineers.  This new regulation cost in the ballpark of around $38,000 for two above-ground tanks.  The marina needed two tanks for the two different types of gasoline they sell. (Interview 1999).  This cost does not include the set up of the storage tanks or the cost of new gas hoses, so in the long run this new regulation will cost a marina owner well over $38,000 for the total renovation.  You can see why some marina owners would be hesitant to get rid of the tanks that they have got.

     Underground storage tanks do have a major disadvantage as far as environmental safety goes.  They tend to leak the gasoline additive methyl tert-butyl ether (MTBE).  Methyl tert-butyl ether “is a fuel oxygenate which enhances the octane in gasoline and decreases carbon monoxide emission by increasing burning efficiencies” (Reuter 1).  While high octane gasoline may make your boat or car run more economically efficient, it is also the leading reason why the EPA stresses having aboveground tanks.  Your high octane, cleaner, and better equipped gasoline is ultimately harming our environment by leaking out of containers and polluting our water sources.  This is very hazardous to our health considering the fact that MTBE is very soluble in water and therefore basically impossible to biodegrade and get rid of.

     MTBE’s are not only released into groundwater by leaking storage tanks, it is very possible that direct contamination of groundwater comes from “storm water flow and precipitation which washes through the urban atmosphere” (Reuter 1).  Since we cannot control the storm water and precipitation, we have to reduce the percent of MTBE’s in our waters by taking a few extra precautions aboveground.  This might mean that your boat may not be as economic when it comes to gas mileage, but really, what is more important–your boat or the environment that you only get a shot to protect once because when it is gone that is the end.

     Another preventative step that must be taken is the testing of the sources of our drinking waters.  By regular testing of our groundwater we can monitor exactly how much of the methyl tert-butyl ether is being deposited into our drinking water.  The Santa Clara Valley Water District in California is taking measures to test for leaking underground storage tanks.  According to their statistics, the number of reported cases of open fuel leaks contaminating groundwater was 648 cases (Haake 1).   These are cases that are still leaking at this moment.  They are still contaminating our environment.  If these frequent testings were better publicized they could help the general public to see the impact fuel leaks have on our environment and help us to set regulations to set in motion a higher standard for our water conditions.  Greers Ferry Lake contains several marinas, making it very possible for MTBE’s to get leaked into our water system.  This is why lake officials should  regularly test  their lakes to keep them free of MTBE’s and safe for the public.

     With MTBE’s becoming such a problem on lakes across the nation, several states have banned the use of personal water craft and other two-cycle engines from their waters.  This is due to the large amount of fuel that was getting injected into the lakes by these types of vehicles.  If a person wants to take steps to ensure that they do not lose their water recreation rights, then

 “there are a number of enviro-friendly steps boaters can take while still using their current motors” (Banse 24).  Taking these steps will help the boater to work with instead of against the environment.

     According to Rudow, “there are some preventative measures you can take to ensure a spill-free fueling every time you top-off” (Rudow 86).  One measure is to buy a Guzzler, which costs about $40.  A Guzzler is an environmental-friendly spill stop device.  It is “a plastic container with suction cups on the back that stick to your boat’s gel coat like a leech.  Just wet the suction cups and press the Guzzler against the hull.  A hole between the suction cups fits over your fuel tank vent, which is an escape route for most spilled fuel.  Once the Guzzler has caught the spilled fuel it can be removed from the boat, so that you can pour the fuel right back into your tank.  There is nothing to dispose of” (Rudow 86).

     There is also a liquid detergent available that will eat small spills off the top of the water.  While this is a help to the environment, it is not a long-term solution to an on-going problem. 

 Most marina owners are not going to be willing to foot the bill to clean up your mess.  Therefore, you should carry some type of liquid detergent on your boat at all times, because even dish washing liquid will clean up a very small spill.  This is an inexpensive device that can help purify our small spill problem.  Notice the use of the term “small” in the last sentence.  A large amount of dishwashing liquid could wind up doing as much harm to the water as a fuel spill.  However, if a person wishes to help the environment and save money at the same time then they can simply not fill their tanks all the way up.  This will eliminate the overflowing of fuel into the lake and also save a person a little money by not having to purchase a spill-stop device. 

      It is encouraging to know that there is something that we can do to stop the devastating affects of fuel spills on our environment.  Greers Ferry Lake furnishes water for communities big and small, including Conway, so don’t think that this problem won’t affect you.  Arkansas’s water sources are depleting rapidly and we must do everything in our power to save this one.  More cooperation and fewer fuel spills is our only hope for saving our environment as we know it.  Let’s all do each other a favor and get involved in the war against fuel spills!
