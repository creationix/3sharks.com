Why Do Earthquakes Occur in Arkansas When This Area Is Not Along A Continental Plate?

 

            The earth’s surface is made up of about twelve large plates that crash into, pull away from or slide past each other on a regular basis. (http:// tgl.geology.mvohio.edu)  Earthquakes are caused by the sudden release of energy at the intersections of these plates. (http://www.eb.comilso)  Earthquakes occur along faults when the tension against these plates becomes so great that the movement of the earth releases pressure.  (Getis 1998: 57)  The New Madrid Fault is a failed rift, which means it is a break in the North American Plate. (http://tgl.geology.mvohio.edu)  Scientists refer to the area where strong earthquakes occur in the central Mississippi Valley as the New Madrid seismic zone. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov) (See map A)

 

            The New Madrid area is one of the most active areas of earthquake activity in the United States with more earthquakes occurring here than in any other part of the United States east of the Rocky mountains. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  This fault system runs as far north as the juncture of the Ohio and Mississippi Rivers and as far south as eastern Arkansas. (http://tgl.geology.mvohio.edu)  The geological structures that are related to the causes of these earthquakes that lie deeply buried by thick layers of sediment. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  Consequently, very few clues regarding the causes of earthquakes in the New Madrid seismic zone are found at the Earth’s surface. (http.//quake.wr.usgs.gov)

 

            To unmask these hidden geological structures that are related to earthquakes, scientists use different techniques such as mapping the variations of the Earth’s magnetic fields. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  A magnetic map of the central Mississippi Valley made by USGS geophysicists shows a “series of faults beneath the continental crust in a weak spot known as the Reelfoot Rift.” (http://www.ceri.memphis.edu) (See Map B)  A rift structure is created by the Earth’s crust being pulled apart resulting in an ocean basin.  The Reelfoot Rift is referred to as a failed rift due to the fact that the Earth’s crust did not separate enough to create an ocean basin. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  The New Madrid region is unique in itself because it is not associated with any of these ocean ridges. (Pennick 1976: 138)  Despite the fact that no ocean basins were formed in this area, the Earth’s crust was disrupted enough to form major faults that contribute to the hundreds of earthquakes that occur in the Central United States each year. (http://quake.ualr.edu)

 

            In contrast to the western United States, the causes of earthquakes in the central and eastern United States are just beginning to be understood. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  Among scientists, a common agreement towards the cause of these earthquakes is in response to the presence of large bodies of igneous rock found within the Reelfoot Rift.  Many scientists believe these New Madrid earthquakes are produced “when rocks fractured because of the enormous weight of alluvial deposits.”(Bolt 1988:13)  These fractures are, perhaps, correlated to the dense rock thrust upward within this region. (Bolt 1988:13)

 

            These large bodies of igneous rock strongly influence the location of earthquakes within the New Madrid seismic zone and show up as areas of high magnetic intensity on the magnetic map of the region. (http://wr.usgs.gov.quake) (See map C)  This shaded relief magnetic map shows areas of high magnetic intensity, or large igneous bodies, such as “hills”. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  These hills are outlined in black.  Scientists uniformly agree that “the dispersed pattern of earthquakes associated with igneous rock bodies may be an important clue to the causes of earthquakes in the New Madrid seismic zone. (http://quake.wr.usgs.giv)

Though earthquakes are mostly associated with the state of California, scientists are beginning to come more concerned about other areas of the United States with an emphasis on the New Madrid area. (http.//tgl.geology.mvohio.edu)  According to seismologist Emil Mateker, “the  number of shocks, the area affected, and the severity of the sequence surpasses any record earthquake in the history of the North American Continent.” (Penick 1976:139)  Earthquakes in the New Madrid seismic zone affect much larger areas than earthquakes of similar magnitudes in the western United States.(http.//quake.wr.usgs.gov)  For example,  the San Francisco, California earthquake of 1906, with a magnitude of 78, was felt 350 miles away whereas the New Madrid earthquake of 1811, with a magnitude of 8.0, was felt over 1,000 miles away.(http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  This earthquake of 1811-1812 is the largest earthquake ever known to have occurred in Arkansas.(McFarland 1984:1)  The earthquakes of the New Madrid area may affect a much larger area, but are less frequent than those in the western United States.(http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  These damaging earthquakes occur less frequently in the central United States, but when they do occur, the damage can be far greater.(http://www.ceri.memphis.edu) 

 

            Earthquakes will continually shake the Mississippi Valley in the future.  Consequently, scientists are constantly striving to better understand the causes of these earthquakes to protect the residents of the Central United States from loss of life and property in future earthquakes.  The knowledge scientists have gained is being used to “increase public awareness to the region’s hidden seismic hazards. It is only through “better understanding of earthquake hazards and public education” will the people of the New Madrid seismic zone be prepared for the next big earthquake.(http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)

(http.//quake.wr.usgs.gov)  “Scientists estimate that the probability of a magnitude 6 to 7 earthquake occurring in the seismic zone within the next 50 years is higher than 90%. (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov)  “The probability of a large earthquake hitting the New Madrid area by the year 2010 is almost 100%. (http://tgl.geology.mvohio.edu)
