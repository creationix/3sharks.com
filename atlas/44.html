PALEO-ENVIRONMENTS IN THE ARKANSAS REGION

PALEO-ENVIRONMENTS IN THE

Paleo-Environments In the

Arkansas Region

 

By Noah’s ARKansas.

(A.K.A.  Matt Snowden)

 

 

What evidence has been found in the Arkansas region to support the hypothesis of pre-historic life therein?

            Absu.  Chaos.  Darkness.  In the beginning, there was only water.  Deep beneath the primeval sea, solidity struggled for breath.  The earth beneath the waters arose, as rocks and red magma pushed and boiled their way upward.  The sea churned with liquid fire as the land began to rise.  Surging  ever upward; reaching, seeking and searching.  Then there was dry land.  Order.  Structure.  Earth.

            For millenia the Earth changed her face.  Mountains arose from the seafloor, and lakes remained where once there were oceans.  The new land shifted to find a place to rest, though it never would.  Like giants in a forgotten dance, the continents were formed.

            Life and land were both children of the first ocean.  And soon after the land calmed itself a bit, the life was sure to follow.  With their relationship tenuous at first, the fragile new land and the fragile young life had their casualties.

            As the rocks began to cool and settle, falling between the cracks of the world, creatures were caught—an immortal reminder of what once was.  Ancient water, loaded now with minerals from the Earth, seeped into the trapped life forms and melted away the soft parts of their form.  Only the hard parts remained.  As the rock cooled and hardened, the bones of the creatures fused with the rock. 

            Whether in sea or on land, the Earth claimed her victims.  Aeons passed, and faces shifted still, moving sea to sky, but the imprints remained.  This is our fingerprint to the past.  Our best way to see what has come before.         

            Found within the rocky crags and cliffs of Arkansas’ mountains, which once stood as the southern coast of what is now North America, are thousands of fossils that stand as a testimony to the life that once flourished in this area millions of years ago.  The Arkansas region can be divided into four physiographic provinces, each of which correspond with a particular geologic province.  This region contains many surface rocks, which range in age from Ordovician (that is 438-505 million years ago).  It has been suggested that many of the rock formations and layers date as far back as the Cambrian (505-570 million years ago).  Indeed, the oldest formation in the Ouchita Mountains, known as Collier Shale, may possibly be Cambrian but is more likely Ordovician.  (No fossils have been found in this particular formation.)  The only systems that are not represented at the surface are the Jurassic (144-208 million years ago) , the Triassic (208-245 million years ago) and the Permian (245-286 million years ago).  (http://www.state.ar.us/agc/geology&.htm)                

Paleozoic rocks can be found in the northwest half of the state, and occur in three different provinces:  (1) the Ouchita Mountains, with their intensely folded and faulted cherts, sandstones, shales and slates; (2) the Ozark Plateau, with its subly tilted high-carbonate rocks; and (3) the Arkansas River Valley, with its folds and faults of sandstone and shale.  Ouchita rocks differ so greatly from those found of the same age in the Ozarks that a totally separate nomenclature is necessary.  (Stround and Hanson.) 

            Mesozoic and Cenozoic rocks form the base that underlies the Gulf Coastal Plain which, in stark contrast to the other provinces, is characterized by low physiographic relief.  Gently tilted fromations, much younger than the episode of late Paleozoic (245-570 million years ago) mountain building, reflect only the broad sinking of the Gulf region to the South.  (Stroud and Hanson.)

            All of the rocks found in Arkansas discussed thus far are sedimentary in origin and therefore have a high potential for fossiliferousness.  On the other hand, the Arkansas region does possess a few igneous rocks (rocks formed by the cooling of molten earth), proof of the areas brutal past, but these rocks only comprise less than one percent of the surface rocks in this region.  Igneous rocks are derived from once-molten rock, and therefore prevents greatly their chance of containing any fossils.  (Rhodes.)

            Perhaps the most important fossils found in the Arkansas region are those fossils discovered by J.B.  Friday on his farm in Locksburg (southwest AR) in 1972.  The fossils were foot bones of an early Cretaceous (66-144 million year old) therapod, a raptor-type creature, much like those found in Jurassic Park.  Though recent discoveries of more complete skeletons have brought new data to light, the Arkansaurus Fridayi, as it came to be known, is most definitely unique to this region—different from other Ornithomimids, but still a member of that family.  (http://rockhoundingAR.com/dino.html)

            Geology and Paleontology tell us that the Arkansas region was a very active and interesting region millions of years ago.  With mountains not so tall, but jagged and dark, and rocky crags and canyons, one can definitely see that Arkansas was never a quiet place (Geologically speaking, of course).  Arkansas has developed from total water immersion to shelves of limestone, sandstone and shale mountains in the northwest to moist lowlands in the southeast.  Rocky crags, twisting rivers and mountain lakes are today’s heritage from the Time Before.
