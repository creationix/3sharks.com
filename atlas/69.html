PHSICAL CHARACTERISTICS

            The Arkansas River begins in the Rocky Mountains of Colorado near Leadville, CO (see Figure 1).  It drops 10,000 feet in 125 miles and continues to travels through Kansas, Oklahoma, and Arkansas (see Figs. 2-4).  The river ends at the Mississippi River after traveling 1450 miles, and it is the largest contributor of the Mississippi River (http:comptons2.aol.com/encyclopedia/ARTICLES/00276_A.html

HISTORY (in the state of Arkansas)

            The Arkansas River was once a major waterway for steamboats.  Three of the first steamboats were the Comet, the Eagle, and the Robert Thompson (US Army Corps of Engineers, Little Rock Districk, 1975).  In March 1820, the Comet became the first steamboat to navigate the Arkansas River.  It left New Orleans on March 23, and arrived at the Arkansas Post on March 31.  The Eagle was another steamboat to navigate the River, and it arrived in Little Rock in 1822.  Slowly, more steamboats traveled the Arkansas River going further and further upstream.  The Robert Thompson went all the way to Fort Smith in 1822, and by 1833, steamboats were as far north as the military post, Fort Gibson, in the heart of Indian Territory. 

            Steamboats were a major way of transporting goods on the Arkansas River, but their success depended on nature.  Impassable sandbars and floods were big problems.  In 1872, one hundred seventeen steamboats were lost in the Arkansas River (McClellan-Kerr Arkansas River Navigation System, 1998).  Because of the unpredictable nature, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers were called to action in 1824 to improve the river and its harbors.

THE PROJECT FOR THE ARKANSAS RIVER

            The project for the river was authorized by Congress in the River and Harbor Act of July 24, 1946 (Clay, 1979).  However, construction did not begin until 1957.  Development for the project by the US Army Corps of Engineers was for better navigation, additional flood control, and other purposes.  The project for the river was the largest civil works project of the Corps of Engineers at this time. (McClellan-Kerr Arkansas River navigation System, 1998).

            In 1970, a 445-mile long waterway with seventeen locks and dams was completed (see Fig. 5).  One year later, Congress designated this waterway as the McClellan-Kerr Arkansas River Navigation System in honor of two of the waterway’s most persistent and persuasive supporters—the late US Senators John L. McClellan of Arkansas and Robert S. Kerr of Oklahoma (http://www.tulsaweb.com/port/history.htm).  The total cost of the project was $1.2 billion;  $600 million was invested in features of the project in Arkansas, and another $600 million was also invested in Oklahoma (McClellan-Kerr Arkansas River navigation System, 1998).

THE McCLELLAN-KERR ARKANSAS RIVER NAVIGATION SYSTEM

            The 445-mile navigation channel begins at the confluence of the White River and the Mississippi River.  It proceeds 10 miles upstream on the White River to the man-made Arkansas Post Canal and flows nine miles through the canal to the Arkansas River.  It is important to note that the McClellan-Kerr and Arkansas River are not always flowing as the same river.  The downstream protion of the Arkansas River is the major component of the 445-mile long system.  The McClellan-Kerr and the Arkansas River are the same at the point after the Arkansas Post Canal, traveling 377 miles through Arkansas into Oklahoma.  The McClellan-Kerr leaves the Arkansas River at Muskogee, Oklahoma, and follows the Verdigris River north for the last 50 miles.  The navigation system ends its 445-mile journey at Tulsa’s Port of Catoosa.  Figure 7 traces the navigation of the McClella-Kerr, and Table 1 lists the river ports and terminals by river mile.

ENVIRONMENTAL EFFECTS

            Bolton (1995) discussed many environmental effects of the McClellan-Kerr System.  One major visual change was a new stability with respect to the width of the river and the creation of semi-permanent edges as it stayed within a relatively fixed channel.  Unlike the system’s channel, the natural river had been a changeable thing that widened and narrowed frequently as it flooded its bottom land and then receded.  The natural river had even occasionally meandered into new courses creating oxbows—a crescent-shaped lake contained in an abandoned meander of a river—and then cutting them off.

            Bolton (1995) indicated that the biggest environmental gain associated with the McClellan-Kerr System was the reduction of turbidity—the opaque or muddy quality of the river.  The sediment load of the Arkansas River had ranged between 62 ad 97 millions tons in 1965.  However, after 1965, turbidity dropped to around 10 million tons per year.  Part of the change was due to the storage reservoirs in Oklahoma that acted as silt traps, but the majority of the change was due to the Little Rock District engineers.  They had eased bendways, reveted banks, and built dike fields that caused the river to be less subject to bank cavings and less likely to pick up sediment from its bed.  Because of the changes by the McClellan-Kerr System, the Arkansas River ran much clearer as the waterway did in its natural state.

            More environmental effects of the McClellan-Kerr System took place, and they will be discussed further in later sections.

LOCKS, DAMS, AND NAVIGATION POOLS—THE STAIRCASE OF WATER

The dams on the Arkansas River create pools where navigation channels are at least nine feet deep. Together, the series of pools are in a staircase of water that replaces the gradual fall of the natural river.  By raising and lowering the gates of the locks and dam, lockmasters regulate the level of water in the pool above it and maintain conditions for navigation.  The pool-to-pool heights vary greatly;  the Emmett Sanders Lock and Dam has a lift of only 13 feet, while the lift at Dardanelle Lock and Dam is 54 feet (Bolten,1995).

Locks separate the pools in the Arkansas River.  The Mcclellan-Kerr Arkansas River Navigation System consists of 17 locks—5 in Oklahoma, 12 in Arkansas (see Fig. 5).  The locks can be thought of as “an elevator to carry a vessel up or down from one pool to the next,” (http://www.swl.usace.army.mil/navigate/lock_dam.html).  Table 2 gives a list of the locks by their navigation mile.

            The physical structures associated with the navigation system—such as locks and dams—altered the appearance of the river (Bolton, 1995).  The navigational pools that resulted from the dams widened the Arkansas River upstream from each dam on a permanent basis, and the widening and the length of the pools were directly related to the height of the dam with respect to the height of the natural banks.  Most of pools for the low-head locks and dams were within the confines of the natural banks, yet Bolton (1995) stated that high-head projects, such as Dardanelle, created lakes that permanently covered the natural banks ad adjoining lands.  The process of raising the natural banks inundated sandbars that were in the natural river.  The rise in water level also created new looks for the surrounding areas.  Two of the new were the water level up to the level of the trees and forest replaced wetlands as the visual edge of the water.

FLOOD CONTROL

A main project of the Corps. of Engineers’ Multiple-Purpose Plan for the McClelland-Kerr Arkansas River Navigation System was flood control.  The U.S. government did not want another flood like the one in 1927—the flood caused half the state of Arkansas to be underwater, and over 43 million dollars of damage was done (U.S, Army Engineer District, 1975).

When the Flood Control Act of 1938 passed, the U.S. government utilized the Army Corps. of Engineers to build levees on the lower Mississippi.  One of the plans by the engineers was to strategically locate flood control reservoirs to limit the access water that would enter the Arkansas River which often caused flooding.  Another plan by the engineers was bank stabilization and channel straightening.  These changes would improve water quality and help make navigation more productive.

FISH AND WILDLIFE

            The pools formed by the Locks and Dams are stocked with bass, bream, crappie, catfish, and other fish native to Arkansas by the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission.  Fishing is good year round, and hunting is also available according to season.  Deer, quail, ducks, geese, squirrels, rabbits, wild turkey, and other animals are found in the areas surrounding different portions of the river.

            Improvement of the water quality helped to change the nature of the fish population (Bolton, 1995).  The development of fish communities was aided by the fact that the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission stocked each reservoir and pool with sport fish.   Furthermore, the impounded water crated a positive habitat for the fish, and the decrease in turbidity encouraged the development of a larger and more diverse fish population.  In fact, Bolton (1995) stated that sprot fish became one of the great success stories of the lower Arkansas River.

RECREATION

            Recreation activities along the river became more common along the river with the building and operating of the McClellan-Kerr System.  Bolton (1995) stated that the reason was because before the system was built, the Arkansas River was salty, muddy, and filled with untreated sewage.  The system improved the water quality, and it also improved the landscape around the river which enabled the building of recreational parks.

Recreation on the McClellan-Kerr Arkansas Navigation System includes a variety of activities both on and off the water—boating, fishing, hiking, picnicking, and camping are just several of the many choices.   There are also numerous trails like the Bona Dea Trails and Sanctuary near Lake Dardnelle and the Tollantusky Trail Cadron Settlement Park (five miles west of Conway) that anyone can enjoy.  In fact, there are more than 100 recreation areas ranging from 10 to 900 square miles in size that are available to the public (http://www.swl.usace.army.mil/navigate/rec/html).

SHIPPING BY WATERWAY

            Barges ship tons of commodities over the Arkansas River each year (Bolton, 1995).  Grain, oil, sand, gravel, fertilizer, and oil are among numerous goods the barges transport (US Army Corps of Engineers, Little Rock District, 1972). 

            The McClellan-Kerr Arkansas River Navigation System does a great job transporting commodities by barges, and records prove it.  During 1989, barges handled over 8,400,000 tons of cargo (Directory of Public and Private River Ports and Terminals…, 1992).  In 1997, this record was surpassed.  The US Army Corps of Engineers estimated 12,043,140 tons of commodities passed through the Arkansas River (http://www.swl.usace.army.mil/pao/tonage.html).  Table 3 shows a more detailed list of the types of commodities shipped in 1997.

            The Directory of Public and Private Ports and Terminals on the McClellan-Kerr Arkansas River Navigation System in Oklahoma Arkansas (1992) described barge transportation as the most energy-efficient form of transportation.  They believe that it is energy-efficient because of the barge’s lower costs and larger volume they are able to carry when compared to other modes of transportation (see Figs. 7 and 8).

CONCLUSION

            Waterborne transportation, flood protection, recreational facilities, fish and wildlife habitat, and usable water are many benefits the McClelland-Kerr Arkansas River navigation System has delivered to the Arkansas River Valley.  It has provided 25 years of service and will continue to do so.  Senator McClellan seems to have been right when he claimed that nothing the federal government could do would “be as big a boom to our state as this,” (Bolton, 1995).
