What is the availability of Arkansas maps?

 

           

During this research, there was a wide variety of Arkansas maps found. Books of

 

maps, single print maps, Geographic Information System maps (GIS), and digital maps were among some of the maps found. Each map is different. This is because each author and purpose of  the map is generally different.  Most of the research was done in the Torreyson Library, on the campus of University of Central Arkansas. The selection of Arkansas maps was excellent. The majority of these maps were found in the archive section of the library. A map of the Madrid Fault along with a regional stratigraphy (see attachment) was among some of the maps found. These maps mapped out the Madrid Fault located on the east part of Arkansas. The regional stratigraphy that was found, was  “a sectional view across Crowley’s Ridge extending from Little Rock eastward to the point where the Alabama, Mississippi, and Tennessee state lines converge” (Lowman).

 

The Historical atlas of Arkansas was very interesting in itself. This map is dated

 

So it is not too far off. This atlas of maps, or book of maps, had seventy-one maps total.

 

These maps ranged from: De Soto’s Journey across Arkansas, French Exploration, Civil

 

War battle sites, agricultural regions, vegetation patterns, natural regions, growing season

 

and frost dates. Some of the maps in this atlas can only be used for historical purposes,

 

but most of the maps could be used today in agriculture, or simply studying the geology


 

of the state of Arkansas.

 

Arkansas Geography was another book of maps that was found. This map is dated

1981. There were forty-eight total maps in this book. The chapters in the book “set the stage for a consideration of Arkansas’ natural regions, early settlement, population change, and other important human activities” (Stroud). The maps in this atlas include: governmental land holdings, precipitation, Indian sites, Indian tribes in Arkansas at 1790, and regional farming. Overall, this  book had a vast array of maps on the state of

Arkansas.

 

Agriculture Department’s Township, Railroad, Mineral and Crop Map of the

 

State of Arkansas was a single print map. The date of the map is 1919. The basic uses this map would be for historical research and to see if the geology of Arkansas has changed over the past eighty years. There were twelve maps on this print. These were: a basic geological map of Arkansas, Congressional Districts, distribution of livestock, deposits of minerals, timber, elevation, and soils. As an added bonus, on the back of this print, it had an overview of each county of Arkansas for “the homeseeker, the investor, the prospector, or the tourist” (Blaisdale).

Arkansas was a book of maps which was composed in 1927 in order to show

“why it is a good place in which to invest, to work, and to live” (Self). The total amount

of maps in this book was twenty-seven. There were maps ranging from population to temperature, and precipitation to agriculture. This book of maps basically covers every

area of Arkansas. As a side note, at the beginning of this book, we are given the “correct”

pronunciation of Arkansas (Ar – kan – saw).

Arkansas County Maps is an atlas of the counties of Arkansas.  The total number

of maps was seventy-five. Each map in this atlas was a basic overview of each county.

The legend contained symbols for: the county seat, a wayside, state/national forest,

state/national park, national forest recreation area, and the interstate highway. A

description of each county is given along with these maps. Information such as native

animals to that county is also given. The date of this atlas is 1985. The basic uses of this

book would be to study each county in Arkansas.

 

The next portion of the research was done on the internet. There was a vast

 

amount of Arkansas maps found during this research.  The majority of the maps were

ones that were to just be viewed through the internet browser. There were also some sites

that sold the maps.

MrSID (Multi-Resolution Seamless Image Database)

 

This site was very interesting. MrSID “enables the viewing of massive images

rapidly over the Internet without compromising the original image integrity during the compaction process” (cast.uark.edu). The maps on this page can be viewed in the browser in two different formats. The .GIF format is local to the web browser, but the .SID format requires a plug-in, which is easy to download from the website. There were several mapson this page to choose from. These include: Fayetville/Washington County DOQ's, GAP

Tasselcap Imagery, Simulated Color-Infrared Image, GAP Arkansas Vegetation,

Arkansas Historical Maps. The Fayetville/Washington County DOQ is a map of a northwest portion of the state of Arkansas. The definition of a DOQ (Digital Orthophoto Quadrangles) is “a computer generated image of an aerial photograph in which displacements caused by camera orientation and terrain have been corrected. These products combine the image characteristics of a photograph with the geometric qualities of a map” (cast.uark.edu).

There were three of these maps.  The GAP Tasselcap Imagery “transformation is a means to enhance the spectral information content from Landsat TM data. This transformation method uses different spectral bands to find the interrelationships between the entities, and each entity's spectral signature” (cast.uark.edu). The data that is received from the tasseled cap transformation process is used to interpret the natural landcover for the scene provided. There was one map of the entire state of Arkansas.  The Simulated Color-Infrared Image uses tem Landsat scenes which were seasonally-corrected, edge-matched, color matched, and mosaic together. The image uses spectral bands 2, 3, and 4 which are very good indicators of vegetation type and vigor, as well as, water sediment content” (cast.uark.edu). There was one map of the state of Arkansas.

The GAP Arkansas Vegetation is a map of the vegetation for the state of

 

Arkansas. “This classified image was achieved through an unsupervised classification

 

scheme of Landsat images.” (cast.uark.edu). This map would be useful and interesting to

 

anyone involved in agriculture in the state of Arkansas.

 

The Arkansas Historical Maps were maps that any history freak would enjoy.  “In

 

The two total maps were an 1854 Arkansas Map and an 1895 Arkansas Railroad Map.

 

            Spatial Analysis Lab Map Atlas

           

 

The Spatial Analysis Lab Map Atlas was a page with seven Arkansas maps. These maps were: Average Deer Harvest, Vegetation Classification, Elevations, Deer Population Distribution, Deer Killed by Vehicles, Deer Depredation Complaints, & Arkansas Roads. The maps were available in two formats, .TIF and .GIF. A deer hunter would make use of the Deer Population Distribution map and the Deer Depredation map. The other maps could be used for research for the state of

 

Arkansas.

 

            The Arkansas Interactive Mapper

           

 

            The Arkansas Interactive Mapper was very interesting. It was almost like a game.

           

The Mapper was a “web-based program which allows any user to generate maps of any

 

area in Arkansas” (cast.uark.edu). There were several options. It allows you to specify the

 

scale, the format, etc. It also gives you a choice between grid-based maps, line-based

 

maps, or point-based maps. Among these maps you can choose anything from soil types

 

to Forestry Commission districts, jeep trails to military bases, and caves to churches.

 

            The Perry-Castañeda Library Map Collection

           

 

            The website of the Arkansas Maps collection of the Perry-Castañeda Library was

 

very interesting and full of useful information. The maps on this page ranged from: city

 

maps: Van Buren, West Memphis, and Batesville, to historic city maps: Little Rock in the

 

1920’s. This site also had maps of national parks, monuments, and historic sites.

 

            Federal Emergency Management Agency

           

 

            This site gives information on federally declared disasters, specifically for the

 

year 1999. It also has an archive of the previous years. The two maps of Arkansas on this

 

page were disasters due to tornadoes. The format was .GIF. The maps were interesting.

 

The counties of Arkansas that had been declared disaster areas were highlighted with

 

specific colors.

 

            Arkansas State Fishing Zone Maps

           

 

            The Arkansas State Fishing Zone Maps is a website that contains maps of the

 

fishing zones in Arkansas. There were four total maps, and these were: Lakes, Streams,

 

small-mouth bass, and crappie. The maps were not really designed for research, but they

 

were designed for the common fisherman.

 

There were other sites that were visited during this research. Among these

 

include: The GIS Data Depot, USGS, and Maps.com. The GIS Data Depot

 

() is really what the site is: a depot for GIS data. There are a lot of Arkansas GIS resources on this page. On the USGS website (http://www.usgs.gov), there was a vast amount of Arkansas maps. There was a map of literally every town/city in the state of Arkansas. Maps.com () was more of an on-line map store. The maps on this page included: wall maps, folded maps, digital maps, atlases, recreation maps, and antique maps.

            After the research was completed, it was easy to look back and see that there is an

 

endless supply of Arkansas maps. Whatever your interests may be, there is probably a

 

map of Arkansas for that interest. If you are looking for an historic Arkansas map or a

 

map of fishing zones in Arkansas, it is out there. Just a few clicks away through the

 

internet and you would find a map of every city in the state of Arkansas.
