Is Lake Conway really that polluted?

 

            Pollution is defined as the introduction into the biosphere of materials that, because of their quantity, chemical nature, or temperature, have a negative impact on the ecosystem or that cannot be readily disposed of by natural recycling processes.  Pollution has become a serious issue all over the world, locally the polluting of Lake Conway has been the talk around the area.  Like devout fisherman Brian Choate said, “I like to fish at Lake Conway, but never would I eat one.  The thought of it makes me cringe.” 

            Surface water pollutants are the pollutants people are most likely to see.  Of course people usually just see the trash and the excess amount of sludge and slime but that is actually what only a small percentage of extremely polluted areas look like.  Most surface water pollution, like Lake Conway, is measured by the concentration level of the pollutants in the water (EPA,1999).

            In July 1998, concerned citizens residing in the area of Lake Conway requested that the Arkansas Department of Pollution Control and Ecology conduct a study to determine the extent of possible contamination to Lake Conway.  The citizens were concerned that the effluent discharge from waste water treatment facilities and poorly designed septic systems may be causing unacceptable levels of nutrients, metals and fecal coliform bacteria in the lake (       ).  So, as a result of this request, the Department personnel initiated the Lake Conway Study on the morning of October 7, 1998.  The researcher selected five sampling locations and confirmed the access points all around the shore of  the lake.  All five sites were tested for dissolved metals and fecal coliform bacteria.   Three of the five sites were tested for sediment. The water quality samples were collected, preserved, and analyzed according to the 18th edition of Standard Methods for Examination of Water and Wastewater.

            The method that is used to track the progress of Lake Conway is called the Indicator #12 from the U.S. Geological Survey (U.S.G.S.).  This indicator is intended to track, over time, the group of parameters that we have identified as significant pollutants.  This is a measure of ambient surface water quality, ambient meaning the quality of water in general, as opposed to water at a specific point impacted by an identified pollutant.

            The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) and the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) are working closely together to find a way to improve the concentration levels in Lake Conway and around the world.  Some ways they have come up with are monitoring the movement of waste more carefully and enforcing such pollution acts as the Clean Water Act more strongly.

            The EPA and it’s partners might need to upgrade treatment at sewage treatment plants and industrial facilities.  Recommended best management practices or policies to control non point sources and address ambient water problems may be in order to improve conditions.

            Surface water isn’t the only thing being polluted, our ground water has been affected as well.  Groundwater is the subsurface water that accumulates below the water table in the pores and cracks of rock and soil.  Most of our groundwater is located in aquifers, which can hold supplies of groundwater in such economically significant quantities as to supply water to wells and springs.  Groundwater is affected by the same four contributors but they are affected in different ways.

            Agriculturally it’s the fertilizers and the biocides that pollute the ground water.  The nitrates and phosphates used in chemical fertilizers and found naturally in animal manure, seep through the soil to the groundwater.  Here the excess nutrients hasten the process of eutrophication.  Eutrophication is when the nutrients stimulate the growth of algae, whose decomposition decreases the dissolved oxygen con tent of the water.  Groundwater contamination due to biocides exists in at least 34 states.  A survey done by the EPA states that biocides are in almost 10% of all community water systems.  One percent of the contaminated water contained levels that are potentially unsafe.  The concentration is often even more dangerous in rural farm areas where residents depend on wells for their drinking water.

            With industry and mining, it’s the waste that causes the pollution and not a part of the actual process.  The nuclear power industry has caused some groundwater pollution when the radioactive waste has seeped through the tanks in which they have been stored, generally underground.  At mining sites, the rainwater reacts with the wastes and cause dissolved minerals to seep through the ground into water supplies.

            Within municipalities and residences contamination has occurred when chemicals from landfills, ruptured gasoline and fuel‑oil storage tanks, septic tanks, and pesticides and herbicides from sprays filtrate into the aquifer.  The biggest problem for Lake Conway comes from problems with faulty septic tanks.  The pollution of aquifers is a real worry due to the fact that aquifers don’t have a natural cleansing process and that contaminants can remain for centuries.  Different types of pollution aren’t the only things that affect the pollution of Lake Conway, there are stresses involved as well.

            Most stresses that are involved in the outcome of pollution are directly related to human activity.  However, human activity is not the only cause of water pollution.  Leaves that fall from trees and decay, animal wastes, and other natural phenomena may also affect the quality of the water in Lake Conway, but these natural processes usually take care of themselves‑ naturally.  Problems start to occur when the quantities of wastes discharged by humans exceed the ability of our Lake Conway to purify itself.  As long as there are people in and around Lake Conway, there will be pollution.  Therefore, the pollution problem is one not of eliminating pollution, but of controlling it.

            One of the major problems of Lake Conway is faulty septic tanks.  This problem was evident by a foul odor after the Arkansas Game and Fish Commissions decision to drain the lake.  The falling water exposed a lot of raw sewage that had been draining into the lake.  Some of the septic tanks in question might have been OK at one time, but over the years they have fallen into disrepair.  Other systems were never installed properly in the first place.  And still others were designed carelessly, allowing raw human wasted to drain directly into the lake.  John Jones, of the health department, explained, “The inside of a septic tank is a bacterial stew.  It’s very warm and if your system is bad that stuff can get out.”  On the other hand, according to the Arkansas Department of Health, we should not worry about the sewage swimming in the water or the foul odor.  Stan Evans told the Log Cabin Democrat that there is no existing evidence of fish, or people that eat them, have been affected by the problem.

            Fixing the septic systems will create a financial burden for some with the septic misfortunes.  This is unfortunate, but it must be done.  There is no other possible way tog et the problem out of the water otherwise.

            Controlling the water pollution of Lake Conway may seem easy to do but in all actuality, Conway’s wastewater collection system consists of approximately 780,000 linear feet of pipe, much of it dating to the 1920's.  During rain events, sewer lines filled with infiltration and inflow, delivering as much as 20 mgd to the waste water treatment plant which normally average 4 mgd.  Conway was unable to comply with it’s discharge permit during wet weather, so the Arkansas Department of Pollution Control and Ecology and EPA Region VI issued an administrative order.

            The issue of it’s water pollution was in the hands of Conway Corporation, which is also the operators of the city‑owned electric and gas.  Furthermore, the regulation of water pollution consisted of several aspects of solutions.  These solutions included such things as the Pollution Prevention Board, Safe Materials Institute, Program Implementation, Water Pollution Control Board, the Clean Water Act, and also the Natural Handbook of Conservation Practices.

            The Pollution Prevention Board has such jobs as to direct the institute to study and formulate recommendations on particular issues and problems that arise.  The board may also conduct research studies and programs, collect and analyze data, and prepare reports, charts, and tables.  Likewise, the Pollution Prevention and Safe Materials Institute adopts guidelines for the problems of Lake Conway.  It also sets forth the criteria that needs to be applied.  The institute shall assist governmental agencies, businesses, and business organizations in developing methods and measurement techniques for assessing progress in pollution prevention per unit of output and shall explore the development of personal computer software for the purposes.

            Program Implementation consists of voluntary participation by businesses.  This program also encourages pollution prevention and does not discourage the use of recycling on treatment techniques determined to be acceptable for pollution that has not been prevented.   Moreover, the Water Pollution Control Board shall adopt rules for the control and prevention of pollution in waters of this state with any substance which is harmful to the public health to the prosecution of any industry or lawful occupation, or whereby any fish life or any beneficial animal or vegetable life may be destroyed or the growth or propagation thereof prevented or injuriously affected.  This department has the right, through any authorized agent, to enter at all reasonable times in or upon any private or public property for the purpose of inspecting and investigating conditions relating to the pollution of any water of this state.  It also may determine what qualities and properties of water that indicates a polluted condition.

            In the meantime, the Clean Water Act is serving an efficient purpose to Lake Conway.  The Clean Water Act has the goal of getting rid of the pollutants in the streams and waters.  This act was rewritten by Congress in 1972.  It was passed because of the demands by uninformed citizens to clean up surface waters.  The Clean Water Act has a specific mandate.  This included such areas as to restore and maintain the chemical, physical, and biological integrity of the Nation’s waters.  To fulfill this mandate, the Act set the following goals: to reduce pollution so that all waterways are fishable and swimmable by 1983, and to eliminate the discharge of pollutants into the Nation’s waterways by 1985.  However, there are some weaknesses to the current Clean Water Act.  For example, it has failed to make significant improvements in water quality because it does not enforceably regulate “non point” or diffuse sources of pollution such as agriculture, mining, construction, timber operations, storm drains, and urban runoff, which may be responsible for more than half of the nation’s water pollution.  In addition to regulating water pollution, the National Handbook of Conservation Practices is a useful tool in helping pursue such prevention's.  This handbook provides such information to help the environment overcome the issue of pollution.
