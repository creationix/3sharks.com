Do Tombstones Indicate Changing Sex Roles?

 

 

As we look back through time, it is apparent that the roles of men, women, and children have changed.  We think that these changes have resulted mainly from a changing society.  Today, unlike years ago, the man is not necessarily the "head of the household.”  Women are becoming more independent and they are sharing family responsibilities with the males.  Since couples are not forced to have many children in order to make ends meet, the wives have more time and energy to put to use outside her family.  We decided that these changes might possibly be seen by examining tombstones in cemeteries and graveyards around Conway and surrounding areas.  This would be a way to see how people ultimately felt about sex roles in their personal lives.  As the roles of men, women, and children have changed over time, so have their headstones.  Although economic status effects the choice of headstones, by looking at their sizes, we can determine if there has been any role changes. To better determine the roles tombstones play in gender roles, the historical changes of women in society must be addressed.  To better determine the roles tombstones play in gender roles, the historical changes of women in society must be addressed.

Over the years, women’s rights have progressed, but we can still see a slight difference between men and women in everyday life.  On August 19, 1920, Congress created an amendment stating that every American citizen has the right to vote (http://usinfo.state.gov/usa/womrts/homepage.htm). No one was to be discriminated against because of gender, no matter if that person was male or female.  This was a big deal to the women of the United States.  Not only did it allow women to vote, but it also made it where they would be considered more equal in our society.

            In today’s world, women are supposed to have the same rights as men, but we still see a little difference in the behavior of men versus women in the work place, as well as on the street.  For example, if there was a job opening where a man and a woman were both applying, most likely the man would be placed in the position simply because of how our society looks at the roles of men versus women.  It is not that the women could not do the job; it is that the employer would rather have a man working for him.

Another example of how women are viewed differently in society is in the home.  The woman is looked at as being under the man.  The man is supposed to go out and make the money, and the woman is supposed to stay home to cook and clean.  In addition, women are supposed to stay home and take care of the kids.  Now there are day cares and after school programs designed so that mothers can have jobs and not have to be at home all day with the kids.  Although there is nothing wrong with staying at home, not all women want to do this for the rest of their lives.  They want to get out and have a career just like their mate.  Not all homes are like this, but you still encounter some that believe this way.  Women have just as much right to be out in the work force as men do, if that is what they want to do with their lives.

Another example is the rights and roles of women in politics.  Today, you do see more women involved in politics than there used to be.  Women are now getting involved.  For example, Janet Reno is ranked among the most powerful people in the United States government.  Thirty years ago, you would not see a woman in public office or politics of any kind.  We have definitely seen a change in women’s roles over the past decade, but it is evident that there are still changes to be made.  Society needs to realize that women are just as important and can do just as much as men.

            Overall, the roles of women in our society has greatly increased over time.  To prove this idea, we visited Conway Cemetery and found this fact very apparent.  We noticed that from one hundred years ago (the oldest grave in the cemetery) up to the present, women’s tombstones have become increasingly larger and more elaborate.  Not only have their tombstones become larger, they are also now equal to their male counterparts.  Many women’s tombstones that we saw were just as big and as equally beautiful as their husbands’.

            However, it was also apparent to us that in some cases, the roles of women today may still be similar to those of years past.  One set of tombstones that we saw, for instance, was a grouping of a man, his wife, and their son.  For this particular example, not only was the husband’s headstone more than double the size of his wife’s, so was their son’s.  This obviously led us to believe that this family still held the notion of a dominant man and his subservient wife.  Perhaps this family, due to religion or tradition, still operated under a system where the man was definitely the dominant head of the household.

            Another tombstone we saw combined the names of an entire family—mother, father, and six children.  The dates of death of each ranged from 1889 to 1993.  We speculated that this particular tombstone was an example not of roles but of their low economic status.  Perhaps the family was not well off and simply found it more affordable to have a single headstone.  More recent tombstones, which have both husband and wife included on one, we believe to be more representative of a feeling of unity and closeness than that of an economic factor.

Oliver Cemetery is located on White Water Road in Cedarville, Arkansas.  This particular cemetery had the oldest gravesites that we encountered, ranging from 1854 to the present.  In the notes that we took while there, we divided the tombstones into five different categories, which are as follows: women, men, couples, families, and children.

The oldest woman’s tombstone that we noted was that of Rachel Thompson.  She was born on September 12, 1809 and died on October 8, 1872.  However, instead of simply saying her name as above stated, it said "Rachel, wife of B. Thompson.”  This seems to suggest that the woman cannot just be herself, instead having to be known by who her husband was.  Her grave was relatively large, measuring 12 ½ in x 30 in x 2 in.  Her grave was also covered with stones, as was common among older graves.  The second woman’s grave that we noted was that of Harriet Coleman.  She was born on March 19, 1826 and died on August 21, 1877.  Her stone was significantly smaller, measuring 8 in x 16 ½ in x 2 in.  Another woman’s grave that we saw, was that of Mattie Bell Maxey.  This grave was more recent than the following two.  However, it was approximately the same size, just differently proportioned, as the previous one.  She was born on September 5, 1885 and died on February 4, 1927.  Her tombstone measured 18 in x 9 in x 4 in.

The oldest man’s tombstone that we noted was that of T.J. Oliver.  His date of birth was not recorded on the stone, but his date of death was December 19, 1889.  His tombstone measured 14 in x 35 in x 2 in.  The other man’s tombstone that we noted was that of Allison Temple Neal.  He was born on January 8, 1911, and he died on October 28, 1937.  Its size was 40 ½ in x 29 in x 14 ½ in.  Also stated on his tombstone was "son of Bert and Iva Neal.”  The outstanding size of this stone could possibly be explained by the fact that he was a much loved

son, hence the inscription on it.  The other men’s graves that we noted were combined with either women’s plots or an entire family.

The oldest tombstone of a couple that we saw was that of Nathan and Elizabeth Shipley.  The stone measured 21 in x 24 in x 2 in.  Mr. Shipley was born on May 27, 1827 and died on February 22, 1863.  Mrs. Shipley was born on July 21, 1831 and died on October 1, 1882.  Their graves were encased by a square stone fence, which measured 113 in x 118 in.  Another couple that we saw were the Maxey’s—W.H. Maxey, the father, and M.M. Maxey, the mother.  Their tombstone was significantly larger than the Shipley’s, measuring 30 in x 34 in x 7 ¾ in.  W. H. Maxey was born on May 16, 1849 and died on March 7, 1909.  M. M. Maxey was born on January 19, 1851 and died on July 5, 1908.  The last couple’s grave that we noted at this particular cemetery was that of Elizabeth and W. M. T. Coatney.  This one was not combined as were the above two—they were, however, situated next to each other.  What was odd about these tombstones was the fact that the wife’s was significantly larger than the husband’s was.  Hers

measuring 8 in x 52 in x 8 in, while his was only 12 in x 30 ½ in x 3 ¾ in.  Although hers was quite larger, it did suggest something that a previously mentioned one did—instead of simple saying her name, it said, "Elizabeth, wife of W. M. T. Coatney.”  Therefore, although it tended to elevate her from its size, it also debased her by referring to her as someone’s possession.  Further curious was his tombstone, which simply stated his name and "1st Ark. Cav," and nothing more.

There were also a couple of sites in this cemetery, which exuded the impression that a whole family was buried together.  These sites were surrounded by stone fences, which encased several markers.  In both of the enclosed sites we observed, only one person had a marker, and it looked as if they were fairly new—suggesting that perhaps a family member had recently discovered that one of their kin was laid to rest there.  One of the markers was that of William Lester, who lived from 1802 to 1859.  The stone surrounding his resting place and that of four unknown people measured 139 in x 185 in.  The other marker was that of David Walker Lewis.  This enclosed gravesite also contained two unknown people and measured 102 in x 84 ½ in.

The children’s tombstones that we saw were all quite elaborate and large, especially

compared to the infant graves we saw at the Conway cemetery, which had nothing more than markers indicating the site.  Two of them were brother and sister, both died at the age of one.  They were William Walter Neal and Bertha Greenwood Neal.  William was born on August 23, 1879 and died on September 26, 1880, and Bertha was born on May 29, 1883 and died on June 25, 1884.  Both of there graves were encased by stones, and they were both approximately the same size.  The other child’s grave that we saw was that of an eleven-year-old boy.  His name was Walter Herald Moon, and he was born on November 8, 1909, dying on November 13, 1920.

His tombstone was diagonal (unlike any other we had seen so far) and measured 16 ½ in x 19 in x 6 in.

Morrison Cemetery on Big Tree Road in Hobbtown, Arkansas, was another one that we

visited.  At this particular cemetery, we came across many more recent graves, while still encountering a few older ones.

The first woman’s tombstone that we noted was that of Holly Kim Gentry.  She was born

On August 12, 1953, and she died on January 5, 1981.  Her tombstone measured 29 in x 20 in x 6 in.  The other woman’s tombstone was Lillian M. Hyatt’s.  She was born on March 10, 1923, and she died on November 19, 1996.  On her tombstone was her picture and an inscription, which read "Loving Mother," and measured 24 in x 23 in x 6 in.  Even more striking about her gravesite was that of the decorations surrounding it—there were flowers, a bench, and two bird feeders.  This was the first, and only, gravesite, which we saw with these characteristics.

The man’s tombstone that we noted was also quite large and elaborate, measuring 42 ½

in x 32 in x 6 ½ in and having a picture embossed on the front.  The man’s name was Wallace Karl Wells.  He was born on May 26, 1960, and he died on July 10, 1986.

The couple’s grave that we saw was that of Robert Allen and Edna Syneva Bonewell.  Their tombstone was also quite massive, measuring 32 in x 42 in x 9 in.  Mr. Bonewell was born on February 8, 1927, and he died on October 27, 1992.  Mrs. Bonewell was born on November 2, 1928, and she is still currently living.

We also saw a family site, which the members were buried side by side to each other.  This was interesting because each grave was covered with one large stone.  The father’s name was Harry Lewis Sr.  He was born in 1790 and died in 1860.  The mother’s name was Jane Ann Lewis, and she lived from 1810 to 1895.  The two children were Margaret Lewis (1842 to 1854) and an infant whose tombstone said nothing more than that.  From our observations at this cemetery, we can see that tombstones gradually became increasingly elaborate as the years went by.

Another modern cemetery that we went to is Olive Hill Cemetery, which is located in

Conway, Arkansas, in a residential area, which is predominantly occupied with affluent, retired people.  We use the adjective "modern" to describe it because of the dates on the tombstones and the style of the grave markers.  Most of the grave markers were not the traditional square or rectangular tombstones, but instead, were figures of angels, tall monuments, or plaques on the ground.  The tombstones that were there, mostly those of men, also had the old-fashioned foot markers.  The dates on these tombstones ranged from 1950 to 1960.

There was also a cremation site here, which was the only one that we encountered out of all the cemeteries we visited.  This consisted of a wall of names containing birth and death dates.  Only a handful of people were listed, three of them being babies, and all of their dates of death were in the last seven years.  This cemetery, like the Oliver Cemetery in Cedarville, had good-sized tombstones for babies.

We noticed that, for the most part, women and children had grave markers, whereas men had nice-sized monuments.  We also noticed that while the women and children’s tombstones had quotes on them, the men’s had their lifetime achievements listed.  Most of which, ended with "devout son, father, or husband.”  All of these suggested that men are still held far above women and children in our society today.  We further noticed that this particular cemetery had no "couple" tombstones.  Instead, the husband and wife were just buried next to each other with separate tombstones.

In the past, monuments were less elaborate and personal as they are today.  They were small, narrow monuments that were hand carved out of stone.  Many resembled mere rocks on the ground with nearly illegible carvings on them.  We first thought that double monuments were fairly new to the monument market.  However, Eddie Parker of Camden Monument Company (www.camdenmemorials.com/location.html), said that double monuments have been around for a while.  In some of the older cemeteries we looked at, there are some tall, narrow monuments with two names on them.  Those covered two grave plots and were the old-time double monuments.

Today, many corners have been cut while making headstones.  Instead of being hand made, they are now produced by machines.  This factory-style production allows for quicker and cheaper production.  Monuments have gotten larger and more personalized over the years.  The personalization involves elaborate etchings and magnificent portraits on the headstones that add up to six hundred dollars to the cost of the headstone.

The actual prices do vary according to size, material, color, and degree of personalization.  On average, most stones are three inches thick and about three feet tall.  The types of materials available are granite and marble.  The granite headstones are the most popular around here compared to the marble.  The colors that are used are light gray, dark gray, blue, rose, brown, red, and black.  The light colored stones are the cheapest and as the stones get darker, they become more expensive.  The single upright monument, at the base price, is roughly four hundred to five hundred dollars.  With changes in stone or color, the price can be up to one thousand dollars.  The double headstones for couples start at seven hundred to eight hundred dollars.  If all things are equal on the double headstones, then the all-around price is cheaper than buying two single headstones.  So, clearly many choose this option because of its economic worth.  Family headstones are as large or larger than the double monuments.  They usually cover four gravesites but can also cover up to ten sites.  Each person buried has a small, individualized marker at the foot of his or her plot, along with the family monument.  These prices start at one thousand dollars— all depending on the number of plots that it will cover.

In conclusion, by visiting several cemeteries and observing the characteristics of many different tombstones from various periods, we were able to establish that the roles of men, women, and children have changed over time.  We concluded that, for the most part, the roles of women have become more prominent, while men’s roles have decreased, or perhaps stayed the same.  This was seen by the fact that women’s tombstones have become larger and more elaborate over time, while men’s tombstones have not changed that much.  The roles of children have generally stayed the same over time; hence, their tombstones are practically the same size.  Tombstones for couples have become increasingly popular over time, becoming more elaborate and massive.  From our research and previously mentioned observations, we concluded that there is still a very obvious division between males and females.  However, we believe that this difference is not necessarily based on the idea of a dominant and inferior gender, but on the realization that men and women are different from each other.  This is most noted by the fact that women’s stones were more sentimental, while the men’s were less so.  Overall, by looking at headstones, we can see a notable, sex-based difference in the sizes and styles that suggest a move toward equality.
