What are plate tectonics?

            Plate tectonics is a theory which states that most of all earthquakes are caused by the movement of rigid blocks called plates that make up the earth's surface layer, or the lithosphere.  The lithosphere rests on the partly molten layer called the asthenosphere.  As the lithosphere rests on the asthenosphere it begins to slide over the denser asthenosphere layer.  These shifts are continually bringing the plates together or apart, which in turn is causing breaks or fractures in the earth's crust ( HYPERLINK http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com).   These breaks are felt through earthquakes.  The study also had this to say about tectonic plates, "the ultimate cause of tectonic quakes is stresses set up by movements of the dozen or so major and minor plates that make up the earth's crust"  ( HYPERLINK http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com) http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com).

            Due to subduction, which is the process of one plate giving in to another plate and either sliding above or below the plate which is moving past,  one study stated that "more frequent, and stronger earthquakes" will occur ( HYPERLINK http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com) http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com).   Since there are  many plates, such as the North American Plate, South American Plate, and the Pacific Plate, the plates can be forced into different directions.  This process is mainly caused by one plate pushing from one side and causing that side of the middle plate into subduction, while from another angle the middle plate can be pushing yet another plate into subduction.  This example shows how the middle plate can be moving in many different directions at the same time ( HYPERLINK http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com) http://comptonsv5.web.aol.com).  The attached figure shows a good example of how the subduction of plates occurs.  The figure gives an example of a lighter plate moving above the heavier plate.  (see attachment titled "Subduction")   

What are earthquakes and faults?

            Earthquakes are stresses in the earth's outer layer which are pushing faults ("thin zones of crushed rock between two blocks of rock, which can be any length") together.  As the stress grows the rocks will slip, and energy is then discharged in the form of waves, which is what is felt by people  ( HYPERLINK http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm) http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm).  There are four types of faults:  normal dip-slip fault, reverse dip-slip fault, strike-slip fault, and thrust fault.  ( HYPERLINK http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm) http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm).

1.      Normal dip-slip-  a general fault moves away from the crustal block, which causes faulting to occur due to expansion.

2.      Reverse dip-slip-  the reverse fault moves underneath the crustal block, which then causes faulting in response to "compression".

3.      Strike-slip fault-  this form of faulting occurs when the crustal blocks move past each other.

4.        Thrust fault-  "a reverse fault with a gently incline, or low angle of dip.  If the sip on a fault is partly strike-sip and partly dip-slip, the fault is called an oblique-slip fault."

When an earthquake occurs on a fault the rock on one side of the fault slips with respect the rock on the other side, which can be in any direction. ( HYPERLINK http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm ).  Evidence shows that faults exist through surface ruptures from earthquakes, earthquakes that have broken the faults, or from small earthquake recordings.  Sudden slips in these faults cause earthquakes.  The shaking and vibration felt during an earthquake is due to  the pressures and forces released when the fault splits.  Energy is pushed out in waves which causes the trembling of the ground.

What is an hypocenter and an epicenter?

            A hypocenter is the location where the earthquake begins, usually deep in the fault ( HYPERLINK http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm http://quake.ualr.edu/public/faq.htm ).

            An epicenter is "the point on the surface directly above the hypocenter"  ( HYPERLINK http://quake.ualr.edu) http://quake.ualr.edu).  This means that the epicenter is usually what is felt during the earthquake.  The attached map shows the zones in which Arkansas has had some form of activity.  The main area of action is located in the far north eastern regions, while some shakes have occurred in the central Arkansas, Conway regions.   (See Attachment of "Map of Arkansas Earthquake Epicenters)

The New Madrid Fault

            The New Madrid fault is a series of faults beneath the crust.  They are located in a "weak spot" known as the Reelfoot Rift.  This fault extends over five states and cuts the Mississippi River in three or more places.  The Reelfoot Rift was caused by forces pulling the Earth's crust apart, but instead of forming an ocean basin (which is what the separation usually causes),  the separation caused a large break and many major and minor faults.  These faults are the main cause of earthquakes in the New Madrid Seismic Zone according to the USGS.  ( HYPERLINK http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets) http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets).  The Reelfoot Rift can also explain how the New Madrid fault can be located in the middle of the North American Plate.  The attachment of the New Madrid Seismic Zone shows that Arkansas is located in the region in which most quakes occur, which is the darker of the shades on the map.  (see map of St. Louis, New Madrid, and Memphis)  The next figure shows the Reelfoot Rift, which is located under Arkansas and Tennessee.  This figure reveals how the sedimentary layers form over the break.  The rift is believed to have formed over 500 million years ago ( HYPERLINK http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets) http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets). 

            In Arkansas and the Central region of the United States, the soil is lose and sandy as compared to California and the West Coast region.  This allows for the shock waves to travel further and faster.  Also the shaking causes water to rise which in soft unstable ground can cause more damage due to the collapse of buildings and flooding from dams and rivers. ( HYPERLINK http://www.moon.com/road-trip/cullout/madrid-quake.html http://www.moon.com/road-trip/cullout/madrid-quake.html )  States that this includes are Arkansas and Tennessee, which both lay on the New Madrid fault line.  A massive earthquake in the Central region would affect the whole country because it would be like having a hole in the middle of the country. 

The five stages of an earthquake

The first occurs when the fault moves and the rocks are pushed together.

            Next, the rocks must change shape by dilatancy, or expansion, which increases them in volume.  Microcracks are formed when the volume is increased, and then you get the releasing of water.  This process can be compared to "stepping on wet sand" or the squeezing of a sponge. ( HYPERLINK http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/chrisg/article.html http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/chrisg/article.html )

            During stage III, water is forced back into the rocks, like when the sponge takes on its original shape.  This puts strain on the rocks and the rocks begin to loose strength. 

            In the fourth stage, the earthquake occurs.  The rocks can not withstand the pressure of the water any longer, and the rupture occurs.  In this stage the seismic waves are released.

            The final stage contains the decline in pressure and the aftershocks of the earthquake.  After the aftershocks, stability will return to the area.

            The stages occur over many years, such as in Honshu, Japan the second stage of ground uplift and tilt was measured for about 60 years.  The rate of prediction would greatly depend on which stage the earthquake was in since the stages can take such a long time to occur before they fully develop into the next stage and finally an earthquake.  ( HYPERLINK http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/chrisg/article.html http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/chrisg/article.html)

Are We At Risk? 

            For a large earthquake to occur in the central region of Arkansas we would have to wait about 550-1200 more years, but there is a chance of an 7.0-8.0 magnitude to occur within the next 15 years.  The damage of an earthquake of this size would cost close to several billion dollars.  2.0-3.0 magnitudes happen about 40 to 50 times a year, so the central region can expect those to happen pretty regularly.  Although an 8.0 magnitude or higher would be uncommon and unforeseen the attachment of "Arkansas Earthquake Hazard Zones"  shows the areas most likely to see one of 8.0 or higher scale.

What is the Government Doing?

            Hazard maps are one way in which scientists are determining some danger zones in the United States.  These maps update seismic dangers and allow for safer buildings and bridges to be constructed.  Building codes (such as the Uniform Building Code) have been made for different areas in relation to the diverse risk levels of the area.    ( HYPERLINK http://quake.wr.usgw.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets/RiskMaps/ http://quake.wr.usgw.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets/RiskMaps/ ).  Since 1990, two Bills have been passed in Arkansas.  One, passed in 1990, requires resistant designs of buildings.  Under this Bill all public structures to meet construction requirements in order to with stand an earthquake.  Faulkner county falls under the category designated as "low seismic damage".  ( HYPERLINK http://quake.ualr.edu/government/act1100) http://quake.ualr.edu/government/act1100).  Another Bill, passed in 1991, states that all new Federal buildings must meet certain construction requirements. ( HYPERLINK http://www.eeri.org/endow/leg/EO12699.html.) http://www.eeri.org/endow/leg/EO12699.html.).   Both of these Bills are part of the Government's way to protect the people.   The United States map shows the hazard zones, which increase in the darker shaded areas.  Arkansas falls into the second most hazardous area.  (See Attachment of U.S.)

            Another way to determine earthquakes is by a pattern either cyclical or recurrent.

This method is based on how often quakes happen in a given area and what date should they occur on again.  This method is based on recording the aftershocks, which is what most earthquakes are.  Forecasting the foreshocks, mainshocks, and aftershocks allows scientists to try and calculate when the next quake will occur. By having a map of the large earthquakes, scientists can determine when another big one may take place.  The attached map shows some of the largest quakes that have hit the United States.  (See Attachment of U.S., Alaska, and Hawaii, which shows the largest recorded earthquakes in the U.S.)  ( HYPERLINK http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets/QuuakeForecasts/ http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets/QuuakeForecasts/ )

            An attachment of the Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale is also included.  This shows how the earthquakes are measured and what damage each different size would cause.  Another scale shown is one of Faulkner County.  This scale gives an estimated damage report for a 7.0 to a 8.9 magnitude earthquake.  (see attachment of "Faulkner County, Arkansas").

            By the monitoring of geophysical precursors (ground up-lift and tilt, radon emissions, and underground water level) short-term predictions can be made ( HYPERLINK http://www.engr.wisc,edu/epd/uer/chrisg/article.html http://www.engr.wisc,edu/epd/uer/chrisg/article.html).  This system has five main parts.  The first consists in measuring the p-wave, which will change during an earthquake.  The change is calculated in Vp/Vs.  The Vp/Vs will decrease in stage II and increase in stage III.  The second part states that the increase in volume will result in the ground uplifting and tilting.  This can be measured by placing stakes in lakes which, if there is uplifting and tilting, one side of the lake will be higher than the other.  The third precursory method is the measure of radon, "a water soluble, radioactive mineral naturally found in rocks and ground water."  ( HYPERLINK http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/ http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/)  When the radon is washed out of the rocks it begins to increase, and in stage III the radon level will revert back to normal and the microcracks stop forming.  The fourth stage involves the decrease in the electrical resistance of the rocks.  This is caused by the microcracks creating a new path for the currents and because of the increase of water. The final stage has to the deal with fluctuations in the water.  The underground water level will begin to cause the surface movement resulting in the expansion or contraction of the surface.  These stages each occur over many years and depending on the stage at which measurements take place, scientists can give warnings of 3 days as in measuring the water level, or some predictions can be made years in advance. ( HYPERLINK http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/chrisg/atricle.html) http://www.engr.wisc.edu/epd/uer/chrisg/atricle.html).  

Ways to Protect Yourself

            Many things can be done to protect yourself  such as having a safe place to go, having food supplies , and by constructing safer buildings.  The Library Fact Sheet gives a list of ways to protect and even what to keep a supply of in order to survive if a large earthquake was to occur.  The methods to keep pets safe and what to do if caught outdoors is also discussed.  ( see attachment "Library Fact Sheet")  The CERI in Memphis also put out a "Earthquake Survival"  list.  This list discusses some things to do in an earthquake.  ( HYPERLINK http://www.ceri.memphis.edu http://www.ceri.memphis.edu) 

            One quote which sums up the on look to future earthquakes is "we can get ready and reduce our loses, or we can do nothing and suffer the full consequences of a damaging earthquake."  ( HYPERLINK http://www.ceri.memphis.edu http://www.ceri.memphis.edu)
