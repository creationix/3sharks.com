                What are the conditions of Arkansas soils, underlying structures and their history?

 

                There are many interesting aspects of Arkansas soils including the underlying structures of the soil and the way that soils are classified.  We have often wondered what types of soils are closer to home.  The latter part of this essay will outline what types of soil are common to Faulkner county, Arkansas.            Let us first take a look at the underlying structures of the soils of Arkansas.  Geologists agree that over the course of our planets history Arkansas was covered with water.  The Paleozoic Highlands of Arkansas contain some of the older rocks in the state (excluding “Crowley’s Ridge” in the eastern part of the State) and were covered by salt water in the southern and eastern parts of the state, however they show sedimentary deposits (rocks that were formed due to weathering and errosion ) that are predominantly fluvial (fresh‑water processes that wash move and recreate ).  Igneous rocks (indigious to the mantle of the earth ) are found in less than 0.1 percent of the entire area of the state.  Most of those being in Pulaski, Saline, Hot Springs, Garland, and Pike Counties (AGC Bulletin 24,1997 pages 1-3 ).  “A few small igneous dikes and sills are present outside the Ouachita region, mostly in the Arkansas Valley, and in at least one case, in the Boston Mountains.  Metamorphic rocks are found adjacent to Igneous rocks but only of low quality.  The rocks in the highland area are dominated by well‑lithified ( saturated by lime ) sandstones, shales, and dolostones of Paleozoic age .  A thin drape of younger unconsolidated clays, sands, and gravel, termed alluvium, ( meaning the sands, clays and gravels fan out over a wide area ) is often found in valley floors and associated with the streams and rivers.”(AGC Bulletin 24, 1997.                 The large round rocks located in prim Arkansas have been a mystery to me for some time. Large, 3 .5 ‘ across brown sandstone.  Sphere shaped generally, some are oval like an egg most of them though are more or less symmetrical. These stones are called concretions.  W.D. Keller (1973:Page 66 ) Describes a concretion as an aggregate of inorganic matter in the shape, roughly of a ball, disc, rod, or irregular nodular body. Usually the accumulation starts around a small center particle and continues to grow around the center like layers of an onion. Concretions can vary in size from less than a centimeter to twenty feet in diameter. The concretions in Prim Ar. Are composed of sandstone. These odd rocks are formed by mineral segregation. As ground water percolates through the existing rock the sediments or minerals are deposited on the host particle. Over time the stones grew to their present size.

                Soils are classified using a specific process.  In the past soils have been classified using a variety of methods.  They were classified according to color, richness, moisture, fertility, and many other ways.  A new system of soil classification was adopted for use in the United States on

January 1, 1965.  It is hoped that other countries will begin to use it.  This relatively new system groups soils together according to their similar physical, chemical, and mineralogical properties.  Soils are now put into six categories; Orders, Suborders, Great Groups, Subgroups, Families, and Series.

                The first and broadest soil category is that of Orders.  This category is based on differences in measurable and visible  characteristics of certain kinds of soil.  There are ten soil orders.  Many of these soils fall into definite geographic ranges.  The soil orders are: Entisols, Mollisols, Vertisols, Inceptisols, Aridisols, Spodosols, Alfisols, Ultisols, Oxisols, and Histosols.

                Next, soils are categorized into Suborders.  A Suborder is a group of soils from an Order which have somewhat similar developmental characteristics.  These characteristics include accumulation of soluble materials within the profile, degree of mottling as reflected by moisture conditions, and broad differences in mineralogy and chemistry.  However, the same criteria are not always used to classify soils into suborders within each and every order.

                The suborders are divided into Great Groups.  These Great Groups are determined mainly by the presence or absence of certain horizons.  A soil may have A, B, and C horizons.  The thickness of the soil layers is also important.  Also used to determine Great Groups are properties such as color, degree of base saturation, tonguing of one soil horizon into another, irreversible hardening, and the presence or absence of self‑mulching clays.

                The next category are Subgroups.  This category is determined mainly by the dominate properties of that particular soil.  A Subgroup name contains the same name as the Great Group with the prefix “typic”.

                After Subgroups, soils are categorized into Families.  This category is grouped together according to their textural classes, mineralogy, reaction (acid or non-acid), and soil temperature.

Generally, plants will react in a common manner to soils of the same family.

                The last category is the Series.  This group is determined basically by the texture of the surface soil.
