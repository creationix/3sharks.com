Arkansas tornadoes: What is a tornado, how are they classified, when do tornadoes strike and what have been some past storms?

When the great outdoors are mentioned, one tends to think of a wonderful serene day. Most of the time this picture is accurate, but every now and then Mother Nature unleashes her fury on Earth. One of the most devastating forms of her wrath is the tornado.  Tornadoes are violent, destructive, expensive, and a major risk in the state of Arkansas.

A tornado is a violently rotating column of air that always makes contact with the ground, and is brought forth by a cumulonimbus thunderstorm cloud (Lyons, 1997:175).  There are three main conditions during a thunderstorm that are required for the formation of a tornado to occur:

1)      The air must be moist in the lower to mid levels of the atmosphere.

2)      The air must be unstable allowing it to continue rising once it begins rising from the ground.

3)      The rising air is dependent on a lifting force.  The most common lifting force is the heated air near the ground.  The warm air becomes lighter causing it to rise, advancing cold air masses, which force the warm air upward (“A basic guide to what causes tornadoes”, 1-2).

 

Having all of these conditions present, the humid air will rise high into the sky, pulling into the strong updraft of an intense thunderstorm. This is illustrated by figure one. The cool air with the moisture begins to condense to form a cloud, and the rate of spin continues to increase.  With enough moisture in the air and a strong enough lifting force, the cloud can rise into the air carrying air upwards in an updraft (Lyons, 1997: 176). This is shown by figure two. Hiding inside the dark clouds of severe thunderstorms, lightning, crashing thunder, heavy rain and large hail precede its wrath (Mosier, 1998: 17).

            As air rises from the ground, the vortex is created.  This is where the tornado is formed. Figure three shows the completed process of tornado formation. The air then rushes into the vortex creating low-pressure cool air.  The cool air then compresses the water vapor creating a funnel-shaped cloud.  Even though the air rises in a tornado, the funnel-cloud grows from the cloud toward the ground.  The funnel cloud will not turn into a tornado until it reaches the ground.  Until there is evidence of rotation in the cloud and of dust, dirt, and debris scattering along the ground, there is nothing but a funnel cloud (“An introduction to tornadoes”, 1-2). Figure four shows a fully developed tornado. An animated picture of tornado development can be found at http://www.usatoday.com/weather/tg/wtorwhat/wtorwhat.htm

            The strongest thunderstorms originate from warm, humid air that is east or south of advancing cold air.  The most violent tornado-forming thunderstorms can be caused by a low level jet of warm, humid air blowing southward only 3,000 to 5,000 feet above the ground.  Dry winds that come from the southwest add energy to the thunderstorms.  The most dangerous types of thunderstorms are called supercells.  They are created under the jet stream where the winds are picking up.  Even though the jet stream is placed 25,000 feet or higher above ground, it still pulls the air upward creating a much more violent thunderstorm (“A basic guide to what causes tornadoes”, 1-2).

            The most violent tornadoes move across land with winds that are as fast as 250 mph.  Some may travel at speeds of forty to sixty miles per hour and cover distances as great as hundreds of miles before dying out. The longest officially reported tornado path stretched 293 miles across Illinois and Indiana on May 26, 1917.  The rotary speed of winds within a tornado has never accurately been measured (Weather, 1999:54-55).  Surface winds usually coming from the southwest are the main developers of tornadoes.  Fifty six percent of all tornadoes move from southwest to northeast, but tornadoes often do have the tendency to come from any direction.  They have even stopped their forward motion and looped their path (Tornadoes In Arkansas, Skiles, et/al).  Research has shown that approximately 1,000 tornadoes will be officially recorded, and the majority of them occur in the cloak of darkness, hiding within the shadows of rain or hail being unable to see.  They also often drop over remote, inaccessible landscapes and can flee at speeds of 40 to 60 mph (Faidley, 1996:68)

Tornadoes occur in many parts of the world, but they are most often found in the United States east of the Rocky Mountains during the spring and summer months (Lyons 1997: 178).  Figure two shows the peak tornado seasons across the nation. In an average year, 800 tornadoes are reported nationwide, resulting in 80 deaths and over 1,500 injuries. The most violent tornadoes can reach winds of up to 250 mph causing much destruction (Lyons 1997:179).  Damage paths can stretch as long as 50 miles. Once a tornado in Broken Bow, Oklahoma, carried a motel sign 30 miles and dropped it in Arkansas (Lyons 1997: 200)!

In the United States, tornadoes occur in all 50 states, but the region with the most tornadoes is known as Tornado Alley (“About Tornado Alley”, 1-2).  Alabama, Arkansas, Florida, Iowa, Kansas, Mississippi, Missouri, Nebraska, Oklahoma, and Texas are the states that are set within tornado alley (Bluestein 1999: 7).  About seventy-five percent of all the tornadoes occur in the states mentioned above (“About Tornado Alley”, 1-2). 

The requirements for a tornado are most often met in the central US. The Gulf of Mexico provides an abundance of tropical moisture blowing into the south and southeast Plain winds (“Tornado Alley”, 1-2).  Meanwhile, coming from the southwest, the higher and drier elevations of the Rockies allow a hot, dry layer of air to blow over the region at about 3,000 feet above the ground. Above 10,000 feet, cooler air races east over the region (“Tornado Alley”, 1-2).  These colliding winds can be prime developers of severe thunderstorms. Also, cool air over warm air is considered an unstable condition. The hot middle layer being on top of the low-level warm, moist air then acts a “cap”. If rising air is going to grow into a storm, it needs to break through this cap (“Tornado Alley”, 1-2).

Only the strongest areas of heating near the ground can penetrate the cap. But when they do, the low-level moist air flows in from miles around, as if it was a bathtub drain pointing toward the sky. The shifting winds then twist these updrafts forming supercell thunderstorms. A breaking cap, with the help of upper-level winds, can cause convection to grow explosively. This causes storms to rapidly become severe and tornadic. It's the unique combination of atmospheric conditions that create Tornado Alley (“Tornado Alley”, 1-2).

Tornadoes may be classified in three ways: weak, strong, and violent. Here are the characteristics of each type:

 

Weak Tornadoes

· 69% of all tornadoes  · Less than 5% of tornado deaths

· Lifetime 10+ minutes

· Winds less than 110 mph

Strong Tornadoes

· 29% of all tornadoes  · Nearly 30% of all tornado deaths

· May last 20 minutes or longer

· Winds 110-205 mph

 Violent Tornadoes

 · Only 2% of all tornadoes

 · 70% of all tornado deaths

 · Lifetime can exceed 1 hour 

· Winds greater than 205 mph

 

Figures six, seven, and eight are examples of weak, strong, and violent tornadoes (“Tornadoes….Nature’s Most Violent Storms”, 1-5).

Tornadoes are always a concern for citizens of Arkansas. There is no way to stop a tornado, and as a result of this, many times when a tornado touches down it causes massive amounts of damage.  The most devastating effects of tornadoes are the loss of human lives.  Arkansas is ranked forth in the United States, with a total of 281 fatalities, from 1950 to 1995 in deaths compared to population.  Some of the hardest hit years for tornado deaths were 1952 with one hundred and twelve deaths, 1968 with sixty-five deaths and 1982 with nineteen deaths. Figures nine, ten, and eleven show just how deadly tornadoes can be.  Figure eleven shows the number of deaths by tornadoes in just one day (“Arkansas Tornadoes”, 1-3).

Even though the number of deaths is the most talked about effect of tornadoes, many times the statistic does not accurately measure the destruction of tornadoes.  Most of the time the number of people injured is very high even when the fatality count is low.  For example, in 1980 only one person was killed by tornadoes but 113 others were injured.  Arkansas in the same forty-five year period from 1950 to 1995 was ranked fifth in injuries.  Over this period Arkansas had 3700 tornado-related injuries from just 878 tornadoes (“Arkansas Tornadoes”, 1-3). 

Tornadoes are also one of the most expensive problems in Arkansas.  Arkansas is ranked seventeenth in the nation for damage costs due to tornadoes.  From 1950 to 1995, Arkansas spent about $ 518,000,000 for tornado aid.  January 21, 1999 was one of the most prolific days of tornadoes in Arkansas history.  Seventeen counties were hit by approximately thirty-eight tornadoes killing seven and injuring numerous others.  According to a report put out by Arkansas senator Tim Hutchinson, more than thirteen hundred homes and one hundred twenty five businesses were destroyed or damaged (“Tornadoes in Arkansas, 1-25-99, 1-2).
