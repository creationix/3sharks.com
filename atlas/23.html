How do different cities evolve after their initial start? What factors contribute to their future and in what way do they affect the cities?

B7: Human Geography: Urban Geography

"Different Evolutions of Arkansas Cities"

By Kerry Rodtnick, and Steven Ziegler

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2519

Prepared: May 4, 1999

 

How do different cities evolve after their initial start?  What factors contribute to their future and in what way do they affect the cities?

 

            The placement of a city within a state can often times determine how the future of the city will turn out.  A city can prosper by being located next to another larger city; a city can take a place adjacent to similar cities and become a part of a chain of cities; or a city can die out for the simple fact that the residents moved on to other cities that were near the dying city.  When Arkansas was starting to become inhabited, towns and villages popped up all over the landscape.

            The future of the towns depended upon various factors.  Among these influences was the commitment of the people, the economic structure, and the placement of the town among others.  We will take a look on how three types of cities faired because of their placement in the state of Arkansas.  These cities are Argenta, Cord, and Tomato.  Argenta blossomed into North Little Rock, Cord remained relatively the same size, and Tomato vanished in the shadow of a rival town.

 

Argenta to North Little Rock

            A city located next to another large city can be successful and thrive.  It uses the Aoverflow@ of the larger city and establishes a community that then takes on its own prosperity.  A good example is North Little Rock.  It emerged from the city of Argenta by being strategically located next to Little Rock and playing a strong role in the railroad industry.  Between 1890 and 1940, North Little Rock established itself as a major city within the state of Arkansas.  The [graphic: opposite.tif] process by which North Little Rock grew from the small settlement of Argenta to a city of the first class is an important part of the development of central Arkansas' urban center.  The small "town" was never a legal city but it now has it's own Historic District within downtown North Little Rock. 

 

            As early as 1810, settlers occupied the area that map makers referred to as "Opposite Little Rock" (Barry, 13.)  In fact, the area had many informal names. 

- DeCantillon 

In 1845, an army officer named Richard DeCantillon Collins, who surveyed roads and rivers in Arkansas for the government, registered with the Pulaski County clerk's office eight square blocks for sale on the north bank of the Arkansas River.  It was first named DeCantillon.  (Barry, 22)

- Huntersville

Huntersville also appeared, named after Capt. R.L. Hunter who surveyed a route for the Little Rock ‑ Fort Smith railroad.  A route linking Memphis and Little Rock was the first rail route.  Completed in 1862, the railroad ended at a depot on the north river bank "opposite Little Rock" from where passengers were ferried across the river to Little Rock.  An 1871 editorial in the Arkansas Gazette attempted to clear confusion about the name of the town "opposite Little Rock".  It explained that during the Civil War the military superintendent of the north side depot was William Hunter and the location was known as the military post of Huntersville.  The next year, the remains of the Huntersville settlement were destroyed by fire.  (Unique City, 5)

- Quapaw

Yet another development on the north side, though not as well known as DeCantillon and Huntersville, was the town of Quapaw.  In 1865 William E. Woodruff advertised riverfront lots adjacent to "Huntersville" but there is no evidence of any deeds recorded. (Barry, 24)

-Argenta

In 1866 the town of Argenta was surveyed and mapped.  (Argentum is Latin for Silver)  Folklore says that silver had been discovered about ten miles north of the town site in Kellogg's Diggins.  Kellogg's Diggins was actually a lead mine in the 1920's and some silver was found.  There's also a story that a Frenchman named A.J. Burnett who built two story hotel called the "Argenta" before the Civil War.  It is said that the area around the hotel and the town of DeCantillon were combined and named Argenta. (Secession, 8)

 

            Even though there was a post office established in Argenta in 1871, the town had not yet incorporated into Arkansas.  It began to harness some of the success of Little Rock by building a bridge connecting the two areas.  The Baring Cross bridge spanned the Arkansas River in 1873 just to the west of Argenta.  It connected the city with Little Rock and the southwest bringing an era of growth to Argenta.

             In the late 1870's, Argenta was a major railroad line crossing (Secession, 16.)  The depot of the Memphis-Little Rock railroad brought shops, warehouses, and workforce and increased the wealth of Argenta.  Railway labor migrated and most of them passed through Argenta. Those who didn=t go to Little Rock stayed in the area around Argenta.  The railroad people who settled in one place were called "homeguards" (Arkansas Times, 32.) 

            The town was almost taken in by the bigger city across the river.  An incorporation petition was filed in 1890 (Secession, 22.)  The Little Rock city attorney advised the City Council that the boundaries of the cities of Little Rock and Argenta extended to the middle of the Arkansas River between them and therefore considered Argenta contiguous to Little Rock.  In an election in April, 1890, Little Rock voters decided to annex lands contiguous to their city (Secession, 22.)  Argenta was made the eighth ward of the city of Little Rock.

            Argenta was considered a "rough town" when it was annexed by Little Rock.  It was "off limits" to the soldiers of Fort Roots Military Base.  There were saloons and gambling houses along what is today Main Street.  Urbanization was limited.  There were no paved streets, no water system, plank sidewalks, a small police force, and only bucket‑brigade fire protection.  Low lying streets with no drainage system often flooded, leaving Argenta a muddy, mosquito infested area (Secession, 30.)  Only slight improvements were made by Little Rock including a "free" wooden bridge across the Arkansas River in the late 1890's.

            After being elected as an Eighth Ward (Argenta) alderman, William C. Faucette pushed for improvements in the swampy area.  In 1901, a group of men from the Eighth Ward led by William Faucette and his brother secretly organized for the purpose of separating Argenta from Little Rock.  An area immediately north of the Argenta site was incorporated as the "Town of North Little Rock" in 1901 (Secession, 32.)  Frank O. Cook was elected mayor while the Faucette brothers paid the filing fee.

            State senator James P. Clarke prepared a bill to permit the annexation or consolidation of one municipal corporation located within one mile of any other municipal corporation.  The group then manipulated the bill through the Arkansas Thirty‑fourth General Assembly in 1903.  They called in the "Hoxie‑Walnut Ridge Bill" and the men paid a lobbyist from Walnut Ridge to make sure the bill passed.  The General Assembly paid little attention to it, thinking it was a special interest bill written to permit the cities of Hoxie and Walnut Ridge to consolidate.  The Hoxie‑Walnut Ridge Bill was signed into law in March, 1903 (Guida, 104.)  On May 1, 1903, the day after the legislature adjourned, William C. Faucette announced that he had a petition signed by Argenta property owner asking to be annexed to the incorporated town of North Little Rock under the provisions of the Hoxie‑Walnut Ridge Bill.  Citizens of Argenta voted to secede from Little Rock and become part of the incorporated town of North Little Rock.

            The annexation of Argenta by North Little Rock was in dispute in the courts for over a year, but in February, 1904, the Arkansas Supreme Court decided in favor of the North Little Rock‑Argenta annexation.  With a population of 6,500 and an established city government, North Little Rock became a city of the first class.

            The city began its progress toward a major working city.  City improvement districts were established which included paving streets and installing a drainage system.  Manufacturing and railroad business expanded and the city began to flourish.

            President Theodore Roosevelt visited the city on October 26, 1905 and gave a short speech at the City Park.  It is said that Roosevelt remarked, "Mr. Faucette, I am delighted to meet the mayor of Argenta."  In January, 1906, a city ordinance was passed changing the name of the city to Argenta (Barry, 30.)  [graphic: districtmap.tif]

            Argenta experienced an economic boom in the first decade of the 20th century.  Four railroads were located in the city.  Cotton oil mills and the lumber industry provided a strong economic base for growth.  In 1910, Justin Matthews, a businessman and real estate developer, built a two‑story building with full basement covering the entire ease side of the 200 block of Main Street.  Other local businessmen built brick buildings along Main Street.

            Argenta changed back to North Little Rock in 1917 and has remained since.  The name change was fostered by James P. Faucette after a trip to California where he saw the economic benefits to a small town close to Hollywood after changing its name to "West Hollywood."  J.P. Faucette was convinced that changing the name of Argenta back to North Little Rock would be a financial boost to the city because it was so close to Little Rock (Unique City, 33.)  Even today, West Little Rock is considered more of a residential area and has increased in residence.

            Argenta started out as a small town with a bad reputation.  The influence from Little Rock and the railroad helped the city emerge into North Little Rock and eventually become the twin city to Little Rock.  Argenda was a success.  There is a historic district named in its honor in downtown North Little Rock that is being redeveloped.  The history of Argenta will never die.

 

Cord

            Some towns continued to function but never grew nor wasted away.  Cord is a small community in Independence County, AR, about twenty miles east of Batesville.  Local historian Nita Walden, who has lived in the Cord area all of her life says, “the Cord community was first known as McCord, which was named after a man who owned a saw mill there, around 1860.”  Most people that first lived in this area either farmed or worked in the sawmill.  Then the people of the community got together and built a school in McCord.  Some years later, a man named I. C. Nuckolls built a store one-mile south of McCord.  This store began to bring many goods into [graphic: cord.gif] the McCord areas that were greatly needed.  The area where I. C. Nuckolls built his store began to be called lower McCord.  Over time, the people began to do most of their business in lower McCord and the people of the community began to shorten the town name to Cord.  Mr. Nuckolls store had the first post office in Cord according to Nita Walden. This post office was established in 1880 (Britton and Tebbetts 1986).

            The Black River runs about five miles east of Cord.  This made farming good, because land around the river is very fertile.  There was a ferry that crossed the Black River, making it easier for people to travel to Newport, located 20 miles from Cord.  Newport had many goods and services to provide, because of its larger size and location on the White River.  The White River was heavily used as a transportation and supplies route.

            Cord always had something going on even though it was not a very heavily populated town.  Local resident Fayna Spragg recalls that her mother would take the kids on Saturdays to the theater in Cord where they would see silent movies.  Even in the middle of the depression, Cord was having community fairs.  The times were not easy and everyone had to work very hard but there was plenty of food on the tables due to the great farmland in the area.

            “During WWII, many of the women of Cord started working in the factories in nearby Batesville to help with the war effort,” says Fayna Spragg.  After WWII tractors began to be a very much-wanted piece of machinery on farms of all sizes.  The farm began to get larger and more specialized.  Many farmers could not afford a tractor but without one they could not produce enough from their crops to be financially secure.  Not only farmers but also the farm hands and their families began to move to Batesville for work in the factories.  This also began to cause family businesses to go under in the small communities in Independence County (McGuinnis 1976).

            These effects slowly caused the family farm to go from around one or two hundred to a few thousand acres.  The big seed companies began to play a major role in the going-out-of-business of the small farmer.  The seed companies started selling hybrid seed and the small farmers could not afford to buy seed every year.

            Another reason for people leaving small communities was “with farms continuing to grow in size, and cotton, once the counties major crop, cotton completely disappeared from Independence County farms by 1974”(McGuinnis, 1986).

            Over time in the community of Cord people have moved off because they either could not make a living by farming or they could make money working in the factories.  Children began to leave home instead of following in their parent’s footsteps.  Where once cotton was the major crop of the area now soy beans, milo, and rice constitute most of the crops farmers now grow in their fields.  Several stores have come and gone since I. C. Nuckolls built the first store there.  There is still activity in Cord, but transportation and communication over the years has made trading in Cord less and less.  Most people can now go to Batesville and get goods and services cheaper than they ever could in Cord.

 

Tomato to a memory

            The town of Tomato, Arkansas was located less than a half of a mile away from the Mississippi River around the late twenties and early thirties, surrounded by cotton and soybean fields, it was considered nothing but a farming town. Tomato, located between Luxora and my hometown of Blytheville, was also known as Canadian Reach then later called Cedar Landing. Other than the farming, it also had significance in logging that came down the river. Most of the logging in the area was just to clear the land for more farmland.

            Tomato received its odd name years ago, in the early thirties, when a small group of community folk at the only store was arguing over the name of the post office. When the owners' little daughter came in for a can of tomatoes and said, "Why don't you just call it Tomato," which they did. (History of Mississippi County Arkansas, 1962)

            Today the name Tomato is considered just a spot out by the river where people farm. If one were to go out there the only thing they would see would be the remnants of a few houses and the small grocery store. At one time there was also a post office and a one‑room school. From what I have heard, there were close to one hundred and fifty people in the area. (Joe Tillman, old farmer in the area)

            Some of the reasons why Tomato never really prospered was because of the location that was so close to the river, and the threat of flooding at least once a year. Also the river's channel would change its course and move to the other side of the river in a matter of years. Another reason was that the town of Blytheville was created. Blytheville exploded in a matter of a decade, so a majority of the people living in the country started to migrate toward the prospering city looking for work, and also people had more of a selection of products instead of one small store.

            There were also a few railroads that ran through the county that transported some of the natural resources around the area, such as all the logging that was going on to clear the land for the over abundance of farmland. Because of the vast number of acres being logged, the majority of the northeast portion of Arkansas became nothing but a huge dust bowl.

            To date, Blytheville would probably not even be half the size it is now without the industry it has. The only thing keeping the town going is the steel industries all along the river. The steel mills have a great location on the river, more than half of the transportation of the steel beams is done on barges being shipped up and down the Mississippi River. 

            Today the only thing that will be found in the area that Tomato existed is farm‑houses and enormous shops that contain farm equipment worth hundreds of thousands of dollars.

            There are a few other small towns that should not have survived the growth of Blytheville, but the natives to these villages stuck to their heritage, remained in the area, and kept the small communities going with their farming. Even a few of the communities have schools, ranging from kindergarten through the twelfth grade, as the heart of the small towns.

           

            Every town, big or small can disappear in a matter of years. These communities have very distinct pasts, varying from now non‑existent towns to some of the thriving metropolises that we may live in today. Whether these towns can compete with other larger cities and towns in the area is not the only reason why communities can disappear; there are many theories why, such as geographical, political, natural, or economic reasons. The life‑cycle of the three towns that are being discussed have their own distinct personalities, one has been engulfed into a huge metropolis, the next has all but become extinct due to the larger towns located near by. The last community has been literally blown off the face of the earth, there are a few remains that are spread out over the area, but you would never know that it existed
