How does spatial behavior relate to a college campus?

Introduction:            To understand how spatial behavior is connected with a college, we must first define spatial behavior.  According to Introduction to Geography,  spatial behavior is the movement, spread, and arrangement of things on the earth.  Spatial behavior encompasses many things including diffusion, distribution, and interaction.  Things or people interact and spread in many aspects of our world and specifically on a college campus.  This paper will cover three specific areas that are connected to spatial behavior here at the University of Central Arkansas.  First, we will look at the accessibility of public places to the disabled here on campus.  Second, we will explore the use of signs to identify buildings and display information.  And third, we will cover the migration of football players and coaches from their homes to the University here in Conway.

Spatial Interaction:  Accessibility for the Disabled

            Spatial behavior exists in many areas often overlooked.  One area that falls under spatial behavior has to do with the accessibility of public places to the disabled.  Two questions that will be covered are as follows:  “Is the UCA campus in full compliance with the Disabilities Act?” and “Is the entire campus accessible to the disabled?”.

            State and federal laws, including the Rehabilitation Act of 1973 and the Americans with Disabilities Act, ensure that no qualified person with a disability will be excluded from participation in, be denied benefits of, or subjected to discrimination under any curricular or co-curricular  programs or activities (Section 504).  The Office of Disability Support Services was established at UCA to ensure compliance with these two laws, state and federal.  The office works alongside the State Rehabilitation Services to meet the needs of particular students.  Programs and information are given to widen the community’s awareness and sensitivity to the disabled.

            The Disability Support Services has an office located on the bottom floor of the Student Center in room 01A.  The main goal of the people in the office is to “provide access to all programs and reasonable accommodations to qualified students” (Holbrook 1).  A qualified person is someone with or without accommodation that has met the requirements of eligibility for admission to UCA and can perform the basic requirements of a particular field of study.  The coordinator, Wendy Holbrook, pointed out that it is the responsibility of the student to notify the University of any Disability or disabilities.  These include the following disabilities: mobility impairment, visual impairment, hearing impairment, or learning disability.  Students are required to provide documentation of disabilities.

            Once a student has given certification of his or her disability, the staff and director work with the student (as well as faculty and staff members) to provide accommodations that are necessary for the classroom.

            Other than classroom accommodations, how accessible is the entire campus at UCA for the disabled?  There was only one way to find out, firsthand.  Because evaluating the entire campus was nearly impossible, we included our observations of parking and four major buildings on campus: Torreyson Library, Old Main, the student center, and Christian Cafeteria.

            Every parking lot observed contained spaces reserved for the disabled.  These spaces are outlined in blue.  And a sign with the handicapped symbol is posted in front of each space.  Do not try to park in any space marked like these unless your vehicle is designated for it.  Doing so could and most likely will result in a $50.00 ticket, courtesy of the UCA Police Department.  Access to sidewalks is made possible because of curb cuts and ramps for wheelchairs.

            Torreyson Library is one of the best examples at UCA of accessibility for the disabled.  A ramp with a relatively even grade is located out front.  The doors are electrically opened at specific entrances and are operational at the push of a clearly marked button.  Elevators are located inside and are large enough for wheelchairs to maneuver in and out of.  The computer lab even has specialized desks that are made for a person in a wheelchair.  If a disabled person cannot use the copiers, a photocopy center is available for use at no extra charge.

            Old Main, however, is not as user friendly for the disabled.  The main entrance and two side entrances are not wheelchair accessible because of steep stairs.  Two doors around back of the building are the only entrances accessible for wheelchairs.  It must be pointed out that due to the old age of the building, certain compliance to the Disabilities Act does not have to be met.  This falls under what Wendy Holbrook, director of Disability Support Services on campus, calls the “grandfather clause.”

            The Student Center is well equipped to provide accessibility to the disabled.  Electronic doors that open by pressing a marked button make it easy to get in and out.  Elevators are available for use inside the building.  Levers, that are much easier to operate than knobs, open doors into the bathrooms.

            Christian Cafeteria is the last building observed.  It too has electronic doors.  A wheel-chair ramp leads to the entrance.  And the tables inside are approachable in a wheelchair.  However, construction outside has temporarily made the sidewalks rough and in some places, non-existent.  Plywood is used for a make-shift ramp.  Maybe soon this inconvenience will be taken care of.

            It is difficult to say that the entire campus here at UCA is accessible to the disabled.  But it is safe to say that most of the campus is.  The friendly staff at the Disabled Support Services along with improving physical features on campus makes UCA an accommodating institution to the disabled.  A map of the campus is represented in figure one, with the buildings discussed in the text highlighted on the map.

Spatial Distribution: Signs and Buildings

            The next section connected to spatial behavior has to do with signs.  There are many signs around the campus of the University of Central Arkansas.  The signs are displayed to inform and protect people on campus.  Different groups such as the campus police department, physical plant and the administration put up the signs for our benefit.  The signs put up by the police department resemble regular traffic signs. These include stop signs, speed limit signs, no parking signs, and more.  The signs are there to delegate order and protect motorists and pedestrians(Parking and Traffic).

            The administration also puts up their fair share of signs on the UCA campus.  They spent thousands of dollars on those banners that are attached to the light posts surrounding the campus.  These signs are strategically located to inform anyone who is not familiar with the city of Conway or the campus.  The administration also puts up signs to direct new students and guests to the office of admissions and Bearfacts day parking. 

The University of Central Arkansas Physical Plant also has their own signs to protect and inform.  These signs help to keep us free from serious injury.  Signs that display “danger” are located near high voltage areas.  Signs are also put up when some type of construction is underway on campus.

            Buildings on the campus vary in their appearance.  Most of the older buildings have a gabled roof, while the majority of the dorms have flat roofs.  All the buildings are built of red brick with a hint of gray.  The new buildings being constructed are supposed to feature similar styles.  Names on the buildings are difficult to see because of the positioning of them.  If the signs were posted at eye level, making your way around campus would be easier.  Also, if the signs were a contrasting color to the color of the building, they would stand out much more.  This would improve maneuvering around campus.  The buildings are dimly lit at night, but maybe to dimly lit according to whom you ask.

The campus at the University of Central Arkansas is not the easiest place to get around due to inconspicuously marked buildings.  Perhaps larger signs that are well lit at night could make navigating the campus easier.  Also, better maps with more detail need to be given to visitors and new students.  These things would make UCA a more accessible school.

Spatial Diffusion: Migration of Football Players and Coaches

The last topic to be discussed dealing with spatial behavior at UCA concerns the migration of players and coaches on the UCA football team.  Patterns of migration seem to be similar year to year.  From 1994 to 1998, UCA recruited high school athletes, junior college and division one and two transfers that were mostly from the state of Arkansas.  Nearly 70% of recruits come from this state.  The remaining 30% percent of athletes come from states including:  Tennessee, California, Texas, Oklahoma, Mississippi, Florida, Arizona, Illinois, and New Mexico (East). 

Distinct migration streams in Arkansas are mainly from the central region of the state.  Thirty-one recruits hail from central Arkansas, nineteen come from northern Arkansas, and sixteen come from southern Arkansas.  The UCA coaches have personal contacts with high schools such as Conway, Morrilton, Siloam Springs, North Little Rock, Benton, and Lake Hamilton.

The number of junior college transfers that have signed with UCA in the last four years tallies to sixteen.  The number of division one and two transfers that UCA has signed in the last four years totals twelve.  These schools include Rutgers, Arkansas State, Mississippi State, and the University of Nevada-Las Vegas (East ).

In the past four years, coaches have come and gone, but we have had only one head coach.  Coach Mike Isom is a graduate of Conway High School(East 8).  This common tie obviously aids in recruitment from Conway High School.  This bond also is likely partly responsible for the longevity of his stay here at UCA.  The University has seen two defensive coordinators, two offensive coordinators, and six different assistant coaches.  Mark Hudspeth, defensive back coach from Mississippi, is instrumental in drawing a few athletes to UCA.  This is according to reports from various players who had previously known of Hudspeth’s coaching tactics and personality before coming to UCA.  A map of the United States is attached and has the states that recruits, transfers, and coaches are from highlighted (figure 2).

Conclusion:  In conclusion, we have defined spatial behavior and have shown some various areas that are encompassed by it.  Accessibility to the disabled, signs and buildings, and migration of football players and coaches are included in spatial behavior.  However, spatial behavior has a broad scope and is not limited just to these three areas.  Spatial behavior encompasses a wide range of topics and we encourage you to explore as many as possible.
