How can the urbanization effected Conway’s ecosystem?


Introduction:


During the centuries following the creation of the world, people have lived in coordination with the nature , respecting her as she fed them and fearing her as she showed her strength. With the coming of the industrial revolution, man was more interested in developing new techniques of production and in increasing his productivity than in preserving the nature. Thus by the middle of the twentieth century, man had been responsible for serious environmental disasters, many of which are considered irreversible. At the end of the century, however, man has become conscious of the environmental problems as the hole of the ozone layer, the global warming of the planet, pollution, etc. But the problems are also important at a minor scale since today, each city, no matter How small it is, has to face ecological problems due to the urbanization. Indeed, some animals and plant species are disappearing and pollution begins to occur also in the small cities.

First, we are going to explain what’s an ecosystem as it is the key-word that resumes


all the environmental matters. This first part will then help us focus on the Conway’s ecosystem and we will try to find out whether this small city suffers from ecological problems or not.

In our textbook, an ecosystem is defined as “ an interrelated collection of plants and animals and the physical environment with which they interact ” ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 137). The ecosystem is composed of many necessary elements ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 137 ). It contains many organisms that have a complex pattern of interdependence and interaction with one another and with their environment ( International Congress of Ecology 1974: 120 ). The green plants which are the producers of food use photosynthesis to make their own food and provide food to the consumers that eat the plants. The animals that eat the producers and other animals are the consumers of food. When a plant or animal dies, the decomposers, such as bacteria, fungus, insects, and worms, come in, digest and then recycle the remains ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 137 ). There are also the materials and energy that are necessary for all of this to occur. These include oxygen, carbon dioxide, water, mineral nutrients, and energy such as heat and light ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 138 ). Ecosystems are energy processing units, but are constrained by the availability of water and other essential nutrients. Energy fuels all of the processes in an ecosystem but the speed at which they occur depends on nutrient availability ( International Congress of Ecology 1974: 11 ). Therefore, all of these elements must be present for the ecosystem to function as a whole. It is necessary for all of these to be present to have a complete ecosystem that carries out all of its processes.

The sun begins the processes of the ecosystem by providing the green plants with the ability to produce carbohydrates which are used as food. This begins the process known as the food chain. The green plants supply the plant-eating animals known as herbivores with food that is stored in the plants. Meat-eating animals known as carnivores then eat the herbivores. The herbivores and carnivores may then be eaten by omnivores which eat both plants and animals. We, humans, are an example of an omnivore. Although much of the food consumed by animals used to keep their bodies functioning correctly, the animals do excrete some of the food as waste and their bodies are also full of stored nutrients when the animal dies. Small organisms, we call decomposers, feed on these excretions and the animals’ bodies when they die. The decomposers return the nutrients to the soil and to the atmosphere which competes the cycle ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 138 ).

Another process in the ecosystem is the adaptation of plants to their surroundings. Plants are in constant competition with one another for things such as space, light, water and nutrients. Plants do need all of these things to grow. However, at least one of these is usually restricted which in turn forces competition within the ecosystem and adaptation to the surrounding area. In different environments the plants will compete for different things. For example, an area with an adequate supply of water and sunlight may have poor soil. Therefore, the plants should have to compete for the small amount of nutrients in the soil. The plants that have adapted the characteristics and mechanisms to succeed in their environment through evolution have survived. The plants that compete best will be the dominant force in their environment ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 140 ).

Man’s role in an ecosystem can be both good and bad. Air pollution definitely has a negative effect on ecosystems. The rising amounts of air pollution are causing changes in the structure of natural ecosystems and the functions that the ecosystems carry out

( Matthews, Smith, Goldberg 1971:47 ). The effect of air pollution on crop plants can cause great agricultural loss and the effect on trees can range from leaf damage to changes in regional climates ( Matthews, Smith, and Goldberg 1971:99 ). Air pollution’s effect on crop plants causes an estimated loss of $ 500 million dollars annually. Most of this loss is due to the suppression of growth or chronic injury to plants due to the pollution problem ( Matthews, Smith, Goldberg 1971: 101 ). The main damage to forest and shade trees is a result of stack gas from coal-burning power plants and from toxicant in urban smog. Where thousands of acres of trees are killed out around a smelter, the area’s climate is altered adversely. Trees help to screen out polluting industries and if they are used wisely can help with the struggle against air pollution ( Matthews, Smith, Goldberg 1971: 116 ). However, just one fifth of the world’s original forests remain and the cutting and burning of these forests is increasing. By devastating these ecosystems, we are also losing many species of animals and many important resources. Road building and other infrastructure developments are quickly proceeding globally. Population pressure and loss of land in some parts of the world will cause even more of the only remaining forests we have to be altered by migration of humans to these locations ( Bryant, Nielsen, Tangley 1997: 5 ). The major threats to the ecosystems today include the logging industry, new infrastructures used for mining and energy development, land clearing for agriculture, over hunting, etc. In many cases, the forests are endangered by more than one of these ( Bryant, Nielsen, Tangley 1997: 15,16 ). There are efforts by humans now to stop the destruction of forests and to stop pollution, but what they are fighting are examples of man’s disregard for natural habitat and the ecosystems of the world. Much of the world is being urbanized by humans which constantly disturbs the few natural ecosystems we have left. For thousands of years now the ecosystems have been constantly disrupted ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 151 ). Humans have unbalanced the ecosystems and have made them less natural. Distribution of biomes have been altered and many ecosystems are close to elimination if not already eliminated ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 151 ). For ecosystems that have been eliminated there is still hope for a return to a flourishing environment, but not much. Mainstream science is now accepting ecological restoration as a new developing field. Meetings are held to promote and discuss this restoration process. However, the success is often inadequate because of natural reoccurring events such as droughts, floods and hurricanes. For example, in the process of restoring a wetland, a drought can occur that will eliminate all recovery process that is made. This discourages society from investing further funds into the effort

to restore the wetlands ( Cairns 1995: 9 ). A few thousand years ago, Earth regulated itself and humans were no different from any other common species. There was no human destruction of habitat and therefore, no need for restoration. Today, humans dominate the Earth’s environmental processes and have a major effect on the ecosystems of the world. The future of the Earth is uncertain. It will depend on humans and the ways that we manipulate the surface of the Earth. However, human impact doesn’t look good for ecosystems right now as the natural ecosystems in which humans have a small impact are becoming increasingly small and the future of these natural ecosystems is looking dark ( Bergman, Renwick 1999: 151,152 ). But what about Conway’s ecosystem?

Conway is located in Faulkner County approximately thirty miles northwest of Little Rock at the intersection of interstate 40 and U.S. highways 64 and 65. It is located alongside the Arkansas River.

Conway, the county seat, was settled in 1871, and incorporated to the Faulkner County as a town October 9th 1878. At that time , it contained a population of 1500 so it was a village rather than a city. But today, Conway’s landscape is no longer formed by land and farms. Indeed, according to the 1998 U.S. census data, Conway is now the eighth largest city in Arkansas. In eight years, Conway’s population grew from 12,683 in 1990 to 40,000 in 1998. Conway is growing so fast that according to Bill Hegeman – the general manager of the city-owned utility provider – “ by 2020 Conway should be competing well with North Little Rock for the rank of the second largest Arkansas city ”

( http://www.thecabin.net/stories/072497/hegeman.html ).The first document shows the evolution of Conway’s population and the evolution of the Faulkner County population. This chart shows the growing importance of Conway in the Faulkner County. Indeed, Conway’s population estimate for the year 2010 will almost represent the half of the county’s population. And as you can see on the fourth document, a street plan, in order to make a proper zoning, is conceived with every major growth of the population.

The growth of the population is followed by the expansion of the enterprises, a fact that convinces people either to move in to Conway or to commute since more jobs will be provided and the activity will boom. During the past twenty years, the employment has nearly tripled. The number of manufacturing jobs grew from 1,291 in 1959 to 7,302 in 1996. Besides, according to Bill Hegeman, Conway has been the first small city in Arkansas to develop the idea for an industrial park. The Conway development corporation originally acquired 174 acres of land in 1962 south of the city near the airport. In 1965, the corporation purchased an additional 140 acres and in 1976, they acquired 160 acres of land east of Interstate 40. According to Conway Area Chamber of Commerce President and CEO Lloyd Westbrook: “ we’ve got a lot of industry for a town this size ” ( http://www.thecabin.net/stories/070199/loc_0607990003.html ).

According to Alderman Andy Hawkins, the city council’s liaison to the planning commission: “ if you just see the number of cars on the road and the number of houses being built, you know there’s been growth ”. Indeed, as shows the second document, the number of building permits issued and family units constructed in Conway grew from 322 in 1990 to 817 in 1998. And as far as the number of vehicles sold is concerned, it increased by 1.9 % between 1994 and 1998 ( document 5 ). Well, this growth has many consequences on Conway’s ecosystem ( fauna, flora ).

The first consequence appears on the third document, as the city grows it annexes more and more areas. In other words, as the city grows we have to burn more land and cut more forests. But the story doesn’t end here, indeed, a city needs roads, adequate infrastructures, water and wastewater capabilities, access to major highways and rail lines, etc. Thus, not only we will increase the number of flooding homes and businesses due to an abundance of more concrete and asphalt but we are also going to increase the air pollution. Indeed, each one of us breathes about 3,400 gallons of air every day. Yet, daily our air is contaminated by human activities such as driving cars, burning fossil fuels and manufacturing chemicals ( see document 6 which deals with the air pollutants emissions for Faulkner County, or http://www.tree2.epa.gov/CEIS/CEIS.nsf/$$All/0505045AIR ) . Because of its rapid growth, today Conway is concerned with water and energy misuse and land abuse; for example in July 1999, the council approved a water conservation plan. That means that residents will be ask to “ voluntarily curtail their water usage by irrigation only every other day ” and business and industry will be asked “ to reduce water consumption ” ( http://www.thecabin.net/stories/072899/con_0728990019.html ).

The fauna is also altered by the growth of Conway as it threatens the species living around its area as well as in the city. Indeed, how many times have you seen dead animals on the shoulders of the roads ( like squirrels ) ? I’m sure that you can’t even remember as there are so many of them killed every day! As far as deer are concerned, their population in Arkansas has increased dramatically in the past 30 years. The problem is that the city is taking their territory and according to Dr. Philip Tappe – a professor of wildlife ecology and management – “ what we’re seeing in urbanization moving into areas where the deer are increasing and the number of hunters is either stable or decreasing. Deer populations and human populations are starting to collide ” ( http://www.thecabin.net/stories/112797/new_citydeer.html ). As deer are eating their bushes or trampling their properties, the residents are upset, but what they don’t think about is that man has taken the deer’s habitat and that they have no right to be upset or to ask the city to allow more hunting!

As the nickname, the natural state, implies more than half of Arkansas is covered by forests and water, Arkansas is host to a thriving “ eco-tourism ” market. But by expanding our cities, aren’t we destroying this source of revenue?

Conclusion:

As mentioned earlier in the introduction, all of the elements in the ecosystem must be present and held in check in order for the ecosystem to function properly. Since most of Conway’s growth has occurred within the present decade it is hard to examine the full extent of damage done to the environment and these elements. Yet, as indicated by our charts, Conway has no indication of doing nothing but expanding even more rapidly in the coming years. Consequently, negative effects due to industrialization and pollution created by expansion are sure to come. More industrialization, commuting to industrialization, and an increasing population have all contributed to the rapid urbanization of Conway negatively effecting its ecosystem. Yet, there has been an overwhelming lack of concern for the environment which has created a lack of research on the effects simply because few people have cared to research it.

The rapid sprawl of urbanization from Little Rock has drawn little attention from the Conway community and other communities as well, as far as the welfare of its natural environment is concerned. Action for Conway’s Tomorrow ( ACT ) is currently trying to guide the dramatic growth of Conway. Many participants in this organization are concerned with the development of a “ family friendly city ” consisting of more parks, sidewalks, bicycle paths, youth and education centers ( http://www.htecabin.net/stories/071399/act_073990004.html ) which could definitely present some potential positives. Yet, what about the concern of an “ environmentally friendly city ”?

Conway’s urbanization fragments landscapes disrupts wildlife habitats, alters rivers and streams and increases pollution ( Newsweek, July 19, 1999: 25 ). Considering that Conway is the fastest growing city in Arkansas, there needs to be more environmentally educated representatives of a growth management team for this city. We can only conclude that the cities planning commission is more concerned with the city’s image and its revenue capacity. Yet it is hard to expect a more environmentally aware representation if Little Rock’s planning team can’t even get off on the right foot. The Little Rock Planning Commission proposed a resolution which would have donated $ 250,000 to $ 300,000 to extend the smart growth process for up to eighteenth months. This would help the commission to provide more ways to protect the environment by giving them more time to create a course of action ( Arkansas Democrat Gazette 1999 : 3b ). Agreement was inconclusive and still remains in limbo. Yes indeed, it is even harder for Conway to focus on an environmentally safe city if big brother cannot even get its act together.
