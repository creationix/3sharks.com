How has the Bald Eagle population returned from the brink of extinction?

 

            The Bald Eagle, or haliaeetus (eucocephalus, has come into contact with a huge problem over the past 30 years.  The United States Game and Fish Commission has studied the cause of the decline of the bald eagle.  A series of studies determined that a certain type of pesticide found in steams and food the bald eagles were exposed to caused the decline in the bald eagles population (http://www.aristotle.com).

            The decision was made to do whatever possible to prevent these deaths.  Over the past few years, the bald eagle has gone from the endangered to the threatened species list.  This progression measures the bald eagle’s return from the brink of extinction (http://www agfc).

            Bald Eagles are frequently found at the Holla Bend National Wildlife Refuge in Dardanelle, Arkansas.  They perch in trees and are observed by people every day.  For 30 years, the bald eagle has been on the verge of extinction due to the pesticide Dichloro-Diphenyl-Trichloroethane, more commonly called as DDT.  This pesticide was found in the food and water that they were exposed on a daily basis.  Today the bald eagle suffers from habitat destruction, illegal shooting and combinations of its food source (http://198.)  The lakes in Arkansas where the bald eagles are concentrated Are Lake DeGray and Lake Ouchita in Southwestern Arkansas.  The Game and Fish Commission fines individuals up to $5,000 dollars and one year in prison for shooting a bald eagle.  With the eagle on the threatened list, it is a priority of the Game and Fish Commission to protect the few bald eagles (Game and Fish).

The Disease and Decline of the Bald Eagle

            A mysterious diseased caused the Bald Eagle to come close to the extinction level.  This disease spread in several southern states.  It affects the cerebella portion of the brain, which controls the automatic body functions such as balance.  Like multiple sclerosis in humans.  The myclin or “insulation” surrounding the nerves is stripped off because of the holes that are formed.  The greatest threat to the bald eagles existence arose after the wide spread use of DDT after World War II.

            DDT is a chlorinated hydrocarbon with a low solubility in water and has a tendency to chemically attach to soil particles.  It was originally thought to be safe to humans and the environment but later it was discovered to accumulate in the environment but later it was discovered to accumulate in the environment and build up to toxic concentrations in food chains (http://pmep.).  DDT was sprayed on croplands throughout the country and it residues washed into lakes and streams.  There, aquatic plants and small animals that were eaten by fish absorbed them.  The contaminated fish, in turn, were eaten by the bald eagles.  DDT interfered with the bald eagle’s ability to develop strong shells for its eggs.  As a result, bald eagles and many other bird species began laying eggs with shells so thin they often broke during incubation or failed to hatch.  This disrupted their reproduction and the population plummeted.  The start of the banning of DDT began in 1972 (http://www.fws).  DDT is considered as the pesticide as the pesticide of the greatest historical significance, due to its effect on the environment, agriculture, and human health.  The ban of DDT was final on January 1, 1973.  The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) officially canceled all uses of DDT (http://micro).

            The discovery of this disease was not easy to find.  An associate professor of wildlife ecology at Arkansas State University tagged 14 eagles this year.  His group studied the Eagle’s eating habits from observations in the field and from the eagles droppings.  Their findings were that a short stay at any lake may reduce the toxin intake because the shorter the stay, the exposure to the toxin was lessened.  Lake Degray is the likeliest source of the toxin in Arkansas and Lake Ouchita is the eagles number one destination after Lake Degray (http://www.tws).  Scientists ruled out bacteria, viruses and parasites.  The three main microscopic organisms that are known to affect birds.  In the winter of 1994-95, 29 bald eagles died at DeGray Lake in Southwest Arkansas.  This set off an environmental alarm (http://proquest).  On the next page is a graph of total bald eagle death statistics in Arkansas from 1994-98.  This graph shows the decline and progression of bald eagles over the past four years (http://www.aristotle). 

            The bald Eagle’s increasing population despite the negative out look for the bald eagle existence they have managed to come back from the brink of extinction.  The good news about the bald eagles is that their populations are up and are continued to rise (http://www.defenders).  In 1994 efforts to save the endangered species reached a milestone with the announcement by the U.S. and Wildlife Services that the bald eagle had recovered sufficiently to change its status from endangered to threatened in most parts of the nation (http://www.agfc).  A recovery method, as well as habitat improvement and the banning of DDT, has made this change possible.  Bald eagle numbers in the lower 48 states climbed from 417nesting pairs in 1963 to more than 4,000 pairs in 1994.  In addition, 5,000 to 6,000 juvenile bald eagles live in the lower 48 states.  Federal protection and tremendous support led to this recovery through stricter law enforcement, protection of important habitat, reintroduction a strong public education program and the primary reason for the continued survival was the banning of the pesticide DDT (Anonymous).  While habitat loss still remains a threat to the bald eagle’s full recovery, most experts agree that its recovery to date is encouraging.  Soon the bald eagle in Arkansas will be a common sight for Arkansasans to see soaring the skies (http://www.fws).

Management and Protection

            Prohibited acts state that whoever, with in the United States or any place subject to the jurisdiction therefore, without being permitted to do so as provided in this subchapter, shall knowingly, or with want on disregard for the consequences of his act take, possess, sell, purchase, barter, transport, at anytime or in any manner any bald eagle, dead or alive, or whoever violates any permit or regulation issued a pursuant to this subchapter, shall be fined no more than $5,000, or imprisonment for no more than one year, or both (Game and Fish).  Nest sites are protected under management programs on such federal lands as National Wildlife Refuges and National Forest (McClelland).  Although the progression or the bald eagle’s population is on the rise DeGray Lake Task Force, with staff members of the Arkansas Game and Fish Commission, U.S. Army corps of Engineers, and DeGray lake Resort State Park, continued to deep a close eye on the eagle population at the lake, but so far has found no definite cause for the problems (Game and Fish Commission).

            The bald eagle’s population continues to rise.  The “Endangered” list means that a species is considered in danger of extinction throughout all or significant portion of its range, while “ threatened” is a less dire category, meaning a species is considered likely to become endangered, but not in danger of extinction. The bald eagles progress is an endangered species will continue through annual surveys and bird banding.  Continuing education on this subject will also help to increase the population.  The bald eagle is not yet fully recovered, but it will soon be more common by soon soaring the skies of Arkansas (McClelland).
