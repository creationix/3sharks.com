What does folk music mean to a society? Why and how has society used folk music to express themselves? What are some of the means of its development and diffusion?

Folk music is “Music originating among the common people of a nation or region and spread about or passed down orally, often with considerable variation” (Mifflin,

Houghton 1991).Folk music, while constantly developing, usually also remain in

its homeland. Occasionally, however, it moves from one country to another, its

style changing in the process. This is the case with American folk music. Folk music in

its purest form was started in Ireland as Celtic songs. (refer to figure 1 and 2 for map

and sound byte link.) It then migrated to America for many different reasons.

There were two main reasons for the Irish to come to America. One of the main

reasons for the migration of the Irish was the famine of 1846 to 1850. This famine was

known as the Great Potato Famine. “It began with a blight of the potato crop that left

acre upon acre of Irish farmland covered with black rot” (http://www.people.Virginia.EDU/~eas5e /Irish/famine.html). With nothing good to eat the Irish peasants had to eat the rotten potatoes. “The peasants who ate the rotten produce sickened and entire villages were consumed with cholera and typhus”. Due to the great spread of sickness, land lords were forced to pay for their tenants to emigrate elsewhere sending hundreds of thousands of Irish to America along with the songs of past loves, sorrows, and new hopes. The civil war is the other main reason that the Irish immigrated to America. ”Federal agents of the United States lured Irish men to the United States with the promises of jobs on the rail roads when the real purpose was to get them for the army”( Carl, Wittke 1956: 136 ).

These two forces caused relocation diffusion (refer to figure 3). Relocation diffusion is “ The transfer of ideas, behaviors, or articles from one place to another through the migration of those possessing the feature transport…”(Getis, Fellmann 1998: 525). Once arriving on the East Coast of the United States the Irish mixed with the other immigrants from other countries. Folk music was then spread through contagious diffusion as it mixed with other cultures and adapted. Contagious diffusion is “the spread of a concept, practice, or article from one area to others through contact and/or the exchange of information”(Getis, Fellmann 517). The immigrants then made new songs due to their own obstacles and daily life activities. Then the immigrants went west in search of a new life while changing the style of folk music in the process.(refer to figure Due to this movement folk music went through another time period of relocation diffusion. Due to this diffusion many styles of folk music formed. There was the Northern Song, Black Song, Western Song, Southern Song, and the main style to hit Arkansas the Southern Appalachians Song.(refer to fig 4) Through this , geography, diffusion the spread and development of folk music was accomplished in the United States. Human geography is defined as: The interaction of humans with their environment. Music is a good medium through which to describe this interaction. Folk music particularly gets to the heart of the human soul and its relationship to the environment. People sing folk songs for many reasons, and they are a major centripetal or uniting force of society. Learning the old folk songs in my family has almost been a rite of passage. They have been passed down from generation to generation. Through folk songs we have kept the memories of our ancestors alive. We have also understood their life, their beliefs, their aspirations, their ideas, how they lived and how they died, through song. In other words folk songs are a reflection of the culture itself. Folk songs are an excellent cultural artifacts of our society. From these artifacts geographers could obtain a clear picture of the environment and the human geography of the places they were sung.

Folk songs are sung all over the world. The folk music of the United States is a rich mixture of lyrics and tunes that are not only indigenous, but that has been gathered from many different places. The tenacious folk song has diffused from all over the globe. This proves the necessity and the importance of them. The human spirit desires expression. The folk song is the manifestation of that desire. It exemplifies the feelings of the human soul like nothing else can; thus the reason for its tenacious diffusion. Not only has the diffusion been contagious, which means it has spread from person to person in close contact, it has also spread through relocation diffusion. Relocation diffusion is the spreading of an idea, disease or song through people who have actually moved or relocated bringing the song, idea or disease with them to the new place. The songs are then assimilated into the new culture. For instance the Europeans that settled in America brought their folk songs with them, along with the Africans, and they are responsible for many of the folk songs that have been assimilated into our culture. Not only has folk music been brought and assimilated, it has been developed over the years.

Folk music is still developing, because as it is being developed it is changed and new songs are created. Every one of us can participate in the progress of folk music. For instance, I like to sing old folk songs to my two-year-old child. There is a beautiful old lullaby from the Southern Appalachians called “Buckeye Jim” that I love. But there is a line in the song that I do not like about an old woman dying of the whooping cough, so I changed it. Now the old woman just had the whooping cough she did not die of it, and that is the way my children will sing it to their children and on and on, it is changed forever in my family and the generations to follow. Most folk composers are adapters of old material rather than creators of original set pieces. The songs are changed to fit new situations and people participate in its growth by reweaving the songs. It’s like making a new quilt out of an old one by adding new scraps and pieces (Lomax 1947:3). That is the beauty of folk music and also what sets it apart from other music - change.

Continual change is part of folk music. It is created by nonprofessionals and it lives by being passed on from person to person, town to town, and country to country changing and evolving in the process. Alan Lomax, in his book “Folk Song USA,” says, “The art of folk music lives on the lip, is transmitted by the grapevine and it reflects the deepest emotional convictions of the common man” (Lomax 1947:1). Since folk music is of the oral tradition, all the rules of professional music do not apply. Folk music is not published or recorded. There are no publishing rights, nobody owns it, nobody receives any royalties from it and there is no name attached to the bottom of the songs saying: Written by ________________. All of us own the folk song, we are the folks. Folk music is sung by all, it is part of our culture, our legacy and it is our national treasure. America’s folk songs are homemade hand-me-downs of cultivated poetry. Alan Lomax sums up the connection between folk music and American culture beautifully in his preface to “Folk Song USA”:

Join your sin-ridden forefather as he bellows and thrashes all over the

camp-meeting ground. Stomp and yell the lines of the hell-for-leather

breakdown tunes. Raise up your head and howl with the cowboy over the lonesomeness and wonder of the Big West. Rock your own child to sleep

with a tune that has lulled babies in log cabins and shanties. Only then will

you feel how close these songs lie to your own and your country’s

marrow. Only then will you feel the surging life and the violent passions

that lie hidden at times beneath the surface of these poker-faced songs.

Only then will you feel the invigorating strength of this powerful folk

art, the quality that sets it apart from popular song with its surface

emotion and its cloying sweetness (Lomax 1947:vii)


Folk music has been labeled, “The ancient bonds that link the races and the nations into the big family of humanity” (Lomax 1947:10). When you talk about humanity you encompass all of peoples lives. Everything that people has gone through, they have sung about and wrote songs about. To understand their songs is to understand the people and their lives. Folk songs have flowered up out of the lives of people as liberally as wild flowers. Alan Lomax (1947:3) said that folk music painted a portrait of the people that settled this land, an honest and valid portrait.

The songs were created by people who had a story to tell: Slaves dreaming of freedom, cowboys driving their cattle, prisoners working on the chain gang, farmers planting fields, or workers on the picket line. The people told their stories, laughed at their misery, and expressed their protest, joy and despair in song. The best and most moving folk song grew from misery and frustration: The moans and grunts of slaves, the men and women working hard labor, and the prayers and protests of the people (Berger 1976:33). Woody Guthrie, one of the best known and well-loved folk singers and writers, has said, “Hard luck is one thing you sing about louder than anything else” (Berger 1976:20) (refer to figure 2). The folk songs reflect the deep shadows on the landscape, the poverty, the graceless toil, the bitter hard lives, the violence and the cruelty of the American people (Lomax 1947:3). Americans have made a 3000-mile march between the eastern and western oceans, their songs have traveled with them and were born along the way. “Whatever is sincere and honest has in itself the power to hold the human attention” (Spaeth 1927:8), which explains the permanence of the folk song.

Folk music has always been a part of our American culture. The Revolutionary War brought us “Yankee Doodle.”(refer to figure 5) Settlers heading west to California in 1849 sang “Sweet Betsy from Pike.” In 1900 Texas farmers laughed at their misery in “The Boll Weevil.” The victims of the Oklahoma Dust Bowl tragedy sang “So Long It’s Been Good to Know Ya.” These songs were carved out of the rock of their lives and they are universal realities. But very few folk songs were sung just to entertain, most have a purpose or a function (Berger 1976:11). For instance, many were sung to work, dance, or play by. Some were sung to pass the time or tell a story. Many were written to protest an injustice, express courage and stir enthusiasm. Some were sung to carry the legends and history of a people. Songs accompanied heavy work, some to help sailors heave up anchor or railroad workers lift heavy track, some to help the men who were wielding the ax, picks, sledge hammers, hoes, logs or bales of cotton. The songs gave rhythm to the work, helped pass the time and in some situations helped release the anger. Coal miners in the coal pits of Kentucky and West Virginia sang of their misery, accidents and diseases. The mill workers in South Carolina had their own hell working at the giant cloth looms sixteen hours a day in a life without hope, which they sang and wrote songs about. These songs helped promote self-preservation and social solidarity, they also helped bring about social change and civil liberties. Melvin Berger writes, “Songs were the strongest weapons of union members, don’t ever underestimate the power of a song. It sometimes brings about more change than a law or a bullet” (Berger 1976:15). It has always been hard to silence a song of protest. The best known American songs of protest were the songs of the struggles of black slaves for freedom and equality.

Blacks from West Africa had a big impact on American folk music, as well as immigrants from Italy, France, Germany and many other lands. The immigrants brought the songs of their native lands and added them to the mixture of American folk music. “The modern American folk song is made up of the best ingredients, collected from countries all over the world” (Berger 1976:23) The British played a most important part in settling this land and the music they brought with them dates back to the 15th and 16th centuries. The traveling minstrel, who sang the news of the day, has been credited with many of the English tunes that were brought over. The British themes of love, war, battles, heroes, sea voyages and crime were changed to fit the tunes. The English, African and American Indian music is mingled in the American folk song. The story- telling of England joined the exciting rhythms of Africa (Berger 1976:30).

Americans have not only created songs, but they have preserved them as well. The first settlers along the Atlantic coast lived in close proximity with much social contact and their songs changed as members of other countries arrived. But as more and more new settlers arrived they were forced to move to, and farm the hill country of the Southern Appalachian Mountains. There were no roads, few people passed through, they were isolated and their songs were preserved with little or no change. There you could find the English folk song in its original form (Berger 1976:32).

As you can see, folk music is the very essence of a society; society, culture and

music go hand in hand. Folk songs are a form of expression that people have used to express their joy, heartache, sorrow, terror, unfairness, loss, etc., etc. The list goes on and on. All the feelings that man has felt, he has written about and expressed them through song. The folk song has given us an outlet, it has given us a voice and a demand for change, it has protested all the wrongs of the world, it has praised and it has cursed. As long as people live and hope they will express themselves through their voice. Therefore, the folk song will never die.
