How are Arkansas' Aquifers Threatened by Non-Point Source Pollution?

Atlas Project No

Lori Stock

Mr. Cossman

Research Question:  How are Arkansas’ aquifers threatened by non-point source pollution?

 

            Pollution threatens waters all over the  world.  However, in our small little world here in Arkansas our waters come mainly from one source and that is ground water, specifically, aquifers.   This paper will focus on where comes from and why that makes it prone to the threat of non-point source pollution. 

 

            First, an aquifer is  defined as being, by the USGS, when a water-bearing rock under the surface of the earth readily supplies water to well and springs.  A more text book definition is, “a geologic formation that is water bearing.  A geological formation or structure that stores and/or transmits water, such as to wells and springs.  Usually implying that they are capable of yielding water in sufficient quantity to constitute a usable supply for people’s uses. (http://proquest.umi).”Ground water makes up much of the percentage of water the earth holds (Figure A). 

 

The type of aquifer that  Arkansas water comes from  are called artesian aquifers.  The way that an aquifer of this type works is that water permeates the ground and seeps into the layers of the earth (Figure B).  The water is soaked up by dirt and plants but not all of it stays there.   Much of it seeps down further and gets stuck there(Bergman, 349).  Some of this water gets trapped in confined areas and when a well pump is plunged into it the pressure built up in side of it pushes the water out (Poland).

Arkansas is unique, in that, it uses quite a lot of water for being such a small state.  Of all the states we are ranked fourth in the amount of water used.  This is most likely because of our readily available amount of water which in turn has lead to great amounts of agricultural use.  In fact, most of  Arkansas’ waters do go towards agriculture (http://proquest.umi ). 

 

There are three aquifers located  within Arkansas borders.  The principle aquifer is the Quaternary.  It is considered a very large and mass producing aquifer because it covers 30% of the eastern part of the state and yields a high amount of water.  It is also makes for easy access to the water because a well only has to penetrate 50 ft of earth for the water to be pumped.  A small amount compared to the other two smaller aquifers, the Wilcox and Sparta Sand, considering one must dig 400 ft into the earth’s surface.

 

Because our groundwater is located so close to the surface of the earth it is considered threatened pollution problems caused by run-off.  This run-off pollution has a name now, though.  It is called non-point source pollution.  It is called this because a specific source, like point source pollution, can not be pin pointed.  It can be caused be run-off from a oil covered parking lot, a pesticide covered field or, in Arkansas especially, by chicken farming(http:proquest.umi ).

 

Today, 90% of  poultry farms are owned by ten companies.  One of which is located in Arkansas.  The problem with this is the amount of fecal matter it is producing.  This matter, when at high amounts, causes large amounts of nitrogen to leak into our water sources.  This causes high growth of toxic microbes.  The aquatic plants and fish need oxygen to live in the water. When toxins, like the fecal matter produced by chickens runs off into the water it absorbs the oxygen needed to maintain a balance of bacteria. It has been proven that the amounts of agricultural use effects the levels of bacteria in our springs and wells(Figure C and D ).

 

There are many different groups of people that are trying to help put an end to the pollution of our waters.  Some are governmental agencies, like the Environmental Protection Agency.  There is also non- profit organizations like the Sierra Club.  However,  many individuals have begun to take up the fight also( Zakin ). 

 

In conclusion,  while we are blessed with an abundance of groundwater in this state, it has only become its own worst enemy by encouraging poultry farming.  This new industrial type chicken farming is creating a mass amount of fecal matter, which in turn could be a serious threat to our shallow source of groundwater’s.
