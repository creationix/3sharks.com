"Earthquakes and Arkansas"

A2:  Physical Geography:  Earthquakes

"Earthquakes and Arkansas"

By Laura Lyons, Brandee Moss, Matt Reese, and Carmen Winslow  (Not At Fault)

Principles of Geography:  Geog 1305, Section A

Prepared:  September 26, 2000

 

 

"Why is Conway, Arkansas susceptible to earthquakes, and how are people in this area preparing themselves for experiencing one?"

 

            Two pieces of Earth’s crust sliding violently past each other along a gigantic fault is one way to describe an earthquake.  This is what could occur, and did in 1811 and1812, along the New Madrid fault which stretches from Cairo, MO to Marked Tree, AR on the North American plate (Arkansas Democrat Gazette 1999).  Earthquakes are not a matter of if they will happen, but when they will happen.  To explain earthquakes, one must first understand how they occur, and then learn how to be prepared for these unpleasant occurrences.

            Rocks beneath the crust, called mantle, are fluid enough to move slowly in the convection current driven by heat stored in the Earth’s core.  This motion of the mantle causes Earth’s crust to move in pieces called tectonic plates (Bergman & Renwich 1999:91).  In 1968, W. Jason Morgan introduced the concept of plate tectonics in which the Earth’s crust is considered to be divided into a series of rigid plates bounded by mid-oceanic ridges, oceanic trenches, great faults, and active fold belts (Seyfert & Sirkin 1973:12).  The theory of plate tectonics describes the horizontal surface movement of large, continent-sized slabs which explains how the outer shell of the Earth is divided into a small number of vast pieces, called plates (Lamb & Sington 1998:8).  The plates are constantly moving, relative to each other, over the surface of the earth; sometimes at divergent boundaries, moving apart at the mid-oceanic ridges; sometimes moving past each other along transform faults; and sometimes moving together at converging boundaries (Lamb & Sington 1998:79).  Movements of these plates at transform boundaries, where the crust is grinding past each other, can cause earthquakes. 

            Since Conway is not located exactly on the New Madrid fault the concerns of this particular area are based around seismic vibrations.  To see areas in Arkansas and surrounding states that were affected by the New Madrid Earthquakes from 1975-1995 go to http://www.eas.slu.edu/Earthquake_Cen.../newmadrid1975-1995htm or see Figure A.  A large earthquake releases in the form of seismic vibrations more energy than a 100-megaton bomb.  These vibrations form waves which spread out from the source of the earthquake like ripples in a pond (Lamb & Sington 1998:94).  These waves cause characteristic ground shaking.

            There are very few clues on the Earth’s surface as to what really causes earthquakes along the New Madrid Seismic Zone due to years of sediment layers.  Far beneath these sediment layers geophysicists have found an extraordinary feature known as the Reelfoot Rift.  The majority of earthquakes along this fault occur within this structure.  It has been estimated that this rift was formed more than five hundred million years ago (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov/QUAKES/FactSheets/HiddenHazs/, 9/20/00).  A rift structure is created when powerful geologic forces begin to pull the Earth’s  crust apart.  If this process continues long enough, the crust separates to form an ocean basin, as happened to form the Atlantic Ocean basin.  The Reelfoot Rift is called a failed rift because the Earth’s crust did not separate enough to create a new ocean basin.  However, the crust was disturbed enough to form major faults that mark the axis and margins of the rift and now contribute to the occurrence of earthquakes in the [New Madrid Seismic Zone] (http://quake.wr.usgs.gov./QUAKES/FactSheets/HiddenHazs/, 9/20/00).  Although this rift is often referred to as "inactive", it greatly influences central regions of the US (http://quake.ualr.edu/public/reelfoot.htm, 9/20/00). 

            An Arkansas resident may find it hard to believe, but earthquakes do actually occur in "The Natural State."  To view a map of Arkansas earthquake epicenters go to http://quake.ualr.edu/public/epicenters.htm or see Figure B.  The northeastern part of Arkansas is noted for relatively light-intensity shocks felt over extremely large areas.  The 1811-1812 great shocks that occurred in the New Madrid seismic zone seriously affected the northeast section of Arkansas.  These shocks were some of the largest in the United States since its settlement by Europeans.  Arkansas’s 40-mile-long, half-mile-wide Lake Saint Francis was formed by these earthquakes (Zirbes 2000: http://www.usgs.gov/).

            The first shock listed in Arkansas occurred in October 1882 with an estimated  epicenter placed near El Reno, Oklahoma.  It not only shook houses strongly at Fort Smith but also was felt about 135,000 miles away.  A little over a year later another occurred 95 miles northeast of Little Rock near Melbourne.  In March 1911 about 40 miles south of Little Rock a severe shock that broke sections of the city of Pine Bluff was felt throughout southeastern Arkansas and in adjacent states.  From 1921 to 1933, two earthquakes reaching intensity five on the Richter scale alarmed much of the populations of Pocahontas and Little Rock.  Although they did not cause property damage, both were felt over 30,000 square mile areas.  Few earthquakes are centered in southwestern Arkansas.  After a damaging quake did occur in June 1939, northeastern residents were awakened on January 25, 1955 by the sounds of walls and ceilings cracking due to an earthquake covering 30,000 square miles.  After being seismically tranquil for 14 years a tremor centered about 19 miles northwest of Little Rock took place on January 1, 1969.  The effects of cracked plaster, moved furniture and swaying trees were recorded in southern Missouri and western Tennessee (Zirbes 2000: http://www.usgs.gov/).

            Some of the most prominent earthquakes in Arkansas took place within the last couple of decades.  A 3.9 Richter magnitude earthquake on December 5, 1985 caused much commotion by residents of Blytheville. Earthquake intensity is measured on a 0-to-9 logarithmic device called the Richter scale that records the earthquake’s seismic waves.  Magnitude 3 to 4 earthquakes on the Richter scale are minor, magnitude 5 to 6 earthquakes can do some building damage, and magnitude 7 to 8 earthquakes can be devastating to highly populated areas (Bergman & Renwich 1999:91).  Although there was a lot of rattling going on, there were no injuries or damages.  The 1996 earthquake in Blytheville affected five states with no serious damage reported.  The 1997 quake near Jonesboro had only minor damage (UALR 1998: http://quake.ualr.edu/).  There was an earthquake just this past June that measured 3.9 on the Richter scale that occurred near Russellville, Arkansas (Yahoo 2000: http://folkworm.eri.memphis.edu/).  Most earthquakes occur in the northeastern section of the state and have relatively low Richter intensities.  As one can tell from the low magnitudes and sporadic occurrence over the years that Conway, Arkansas is not at great risk of a major, damaging earthquake. 

The New Madrid Seismic Zone has been the site of some of the largest earthquakes in North America.  The zone includes areas within the Mississippi Valley which contains northeast Arkansas, southeast Missouri, western Tennessee, western Kentucky, and the southern part of Illinois (http://www.eas.slu.edu/Earthquake_Center/SEISMICITY/NewMadridGeneral.html, 9/20/00).  About one hundred and fifty earthquakes are felt per year in the New Madrid zone, but most are too small to be felt by people (http://www.ceri.memphis.edu/public/facts_short.shtml, 9/20/00).  Since the installment of seismic instruments in 1974 more than four thousand earthquakes have been located in this zone (http://www.eas.slu.edu/Earthquakea_Center/SEISMICITY/NewMadridGeneral.html, 9/20/00).

 

       Facing the fact that earthquakes do not happen only in the western part of the United States, should the city of Conway fear them?  The residents of Conway might want to consider this considering the following quote:  "Scientists estimate that the probability of a magnitude 6 to 7 earthquake occurring in this seismic zone within the next 50 years is higher than 90%"(USGS). With all the damage that earthquakes cause, residents of all areas should have the need to prepare themselves for earthquakes.  That quote, taken from the United States Geological Survey, is talking about the Mississippi Valley Region which is very close to Conway.  If this holds true, people and residents of Conway might want to prepare.  A way of going about this preparation would be earthquake insurance or what is called earthquake coverage.  The Arkansas Act 1343 of 1999 was aimed at making earthquake insurance more available, and discussed revisions to the state earthquake building code(USGS).  This act obviously affects how insurance companies go about their earthquake insurance policies.  So "Not At Fault" decided to make a few phone calls to some local insurance companies and find out exactly for themselves. 

       Four simple questions were posed to the two agencies that the group got in contact with on September 20, 2000.   The first question was: Do you normally offer earthquake coverage if it is not specifically asked for?  The second was: How much approximately does it cost to add earthquake coverage?  The third was: Do most people around this area have this coverage?  The fourth and final question was: Do you believe this coverage is a necessity for this area or just an overly precautionary measure?

              The first company I got a hold of was Farris Agency Insurance in Conway, AR.  I spoke briefly to a representative who worked there and she had some interesting things to share.  To the first question, she answered that under a new homeowners law, earthquake insurance or coverage must be offered by agents when dealing with their clients.  Her answer to the second question was somewhat short in the sense that she said it simply depends on the value of the house as to how much it costs to add earthquake coverage.  To the third question she said in her own opinion a small percent of about 10% might carry  earthquake insurance in the Conway area.  The last question she bluntly stated that earthquake insurance was strictly a precaution, and not a necessity. 

       The second person I contacted was an actual insurance agent.  His name was Mike Wallace with Farm Bureau Insurance of Conway, AR.  In regard to the first question, Mr. Wallace said earthquake insurance is offered and the majority of people turn it down.  He stated that if one turns it down, they must sign an exclusion stating that they turned the insurance coverage down.  When faced with the next question of how much it costs to add earthquake coverage, he thought it through and gave me a close figure.  He said that per $1,000 coverage of a brick house costing $100,000, it would cost $104 a year.  This is a rough estimate he said, but he also said a person’s deductible plays a role too.  The third question dealt with people carrying the insurance, and Mr. Wallace said he only has about one in twenty customers carry it.  The last question about earthquake coverage being a necessity or a precaution made him chuckle a little.  Mr. Wallace said very few take it and he stated something very interesting.  He said that no one in their particular office, or that worked for the company carries earthquake insurance.  I found this very odd considering the fact that they all are in the insurance business.  That made me pose a question.  Do local insurance agents know something that we do not, or do scientists know something that insurance agents should?

       After doing research on the topic of earthquakes, earthquake insurance, and coverage, we concluded that the people of this area have their own thoughts and opinions.  Conway, AR is susceptible to earthquakes of a 6 to 7 magnitude on the Richter scale, and the majority of the population of Conway see no need for the extra coverage.  All in all it seems that a few people are becoming a little more concerned about earthquakes and protecting themselves from these natural disasters.  The government is also showing more concern in recent years by passing such things as the Arkansas Act 1343 of 1999.  This led the group to a conclusion:  If an earthquake does affect the Conway area, very few people will be prepared and many will be lining up at insurance companies in the weeks after the aftermath.
