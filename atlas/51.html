How high is the probability that Arkansas will be affected by a major earthquake; should we in Conway be concerned?

 

            How high is the probability that Arkansas will be affected by a major earthquake; should we in Conway be concerned? These are questions few Arkansans bother with even though the New Madrid fault line is just a stones throw away from our northeastern border.  Before we begin to answer these questions, we must first know what causes earthquakes and what living near a fault line means.

            The earth consists of a solid superheated iron and nickel core surrounded by a mantle consisting of molten rock, which in turn is covered by a solid crust.  The mantle is a constantly churning molten mass driven by convection from the heat of the inner core.  The core gives off tremendous amounts of heat into the mantle, causing the molten rock to rise to the surface to be cooled.  When it reaches the crust it loses heat, depositing part of itself on the underside of the crust and cooling enough to be heavier than the hotter mass below.  It then sinks only to be heated once more by the core, much like a pot of pudding boiling on a stovetop.  The convection of heat from the core produces a churning mass of slow motion rivers with enough force to move continents (Exploratorium, 1999). 

            Over eons the earth’s crust has cracked as a result of the swirling mass below it, creating seven large and a dozen smaller pieces called tectonic plates.  These plates, an average 28 miles thick, “float” around on the liquid mantle, in most places moving only centimeters a year.  The places where the plates touch each other are called fault lines.  Pushed and pulled by the currents of the liquid rock beneath them, the tectonic plates move in different directions causing friction at these peaceful seeming junctures. When enough pressure builds up over time, one or both break free, causing sudden movement of the earth.  This rapid and destructive movement is called an earthquake. There are three ways that that a fault line can be affected by the release of this pressure, depending on the direction of plate movement. 

            When two plates are moving in opposite directions along a fault line it is called a divergent plate boundary.  This movement usually occurs along the sea floor, which causes a crack in the earth, sometimes allowing the liquid rock to break free to the surface.   The cooling lava is rapidly cooled producing new seafloor crust.

Two plates moving towards each other is called a convergent plate boundary.  These plates push against each other until one slides over the other forming mountains or, if along the sea floor, erupting volcanoes. 

            If tectonic plates are moving horizontally in opposite directions it is called a transform plate boundary.  This grinding of the uneven plates causes them to bind against each other until sufficient force causes them to break free in a shearing motion.  This is the cause of most earthquakes in the United States (Volcano World, 1999).

     The strength of an earthquake can be measured with the use of an instrument called a seism scope.   The earliest known seism scope was invented in 132 A.D. by a Chinese philosopher Chang Heng.  It consisted of a pottery jar with eight open mouthed dragonheads on the outside, facing in the eight principle directions of the compass.  Each mouth held a ball that would, in the event of an earthquake, drop into the open mouth of a ceramic toad situated below.  This device would reportedly detect and denote the general direction of an earthquake.  The inner mechanisms of the jar are unknown (Dewy, Byerly, 1999).

          In 1751, Andrea Bina proposed the use of a simple pendulum with a point at the lower end, suspended over fine sand to detect earthquake motion.  This primitive pendulum device has been improved upon over the centuries countless times.  Pendulum devices are still being used today although they have been improved with the advent of electricity and now not only detect, but also record the shaking of the earth.  A device that records earthquakes is called a seismograph and the recording, a seismogram (Dewy, Byerly, 1999).

     By placing three or more seismographs different places across the globe, scientists are able to pinpoint the origin of an earthquake by calculating the distance and strength of readings obtained.  This origin is called the focus.  The strength of an earthquake is measured using two scales (Biddle, 1998).

     The Richter scale was devised by American seismologist Charles Richter and measures the amount of energy released at the focus of an earthquake.  It has values from one to nine increasing in intensity ten times for each number.  Thus, an earthquake with the intensity of 5 has a magnitude ten times greater than an earthquake with the intensity of 4 (Biddle, 1998). 

     Italian seismologist Giuseppe Mercalli introduced the Mercalli scale at the turn of the twentieth century.  This scale measures the intensity of the shaking of the ground.  Measured on a scale of 1 to 12, based on observed effects.  A number 1 would be imperceptible shaking and 12 would be mass destruction (Biddle, 1998).

            When an earthquake occurs, two types of seismic waves travel outward from the epicenter.  The first wave is called a P-wave for primary or pressure waves.  This type of wave moves longitudinally.  The second type of wave is called an S-wave for secondary or shear wave.  This much slower more destructive wave moves in a shearing motion and moves only through solids (Exploratorium, 1999). 

            These waves when combined form one of the most destructive forces on earth.  Although scientists have been studying earthquakes for over a thousand years, there is still no accurate way to predict them.  History shows that the effects of these earthquakes can be catastrophic. 

Three of the most powerful earthquakes in United States history occurred in the New Madrid Seismic Zone, which lies within the central Mississippi Valley, extending from northeast Arkansas, through southeast Missouri, western Tennessee, western Kentucky to southern Illinois.  Located on a weak spot in the middle of the North American Plate, the fault is in an unusual place considering that most earthquakes occur on the edges of tectonic plates, rather than in the middle.  The New Madrid fault derived its name from the town of New Madrid, Tennessee, which was the closest settlement to the epicenter of the historic earthquakes in 1811 through 1812.  Today, it is the most earthquake prone region in the United States east of the Rocky Mountains, averaging two to three earthquakes a week; yet, most of these tremors are never felt.  Individuals living in this area experience one to two earthquakes each year, which are large enough to be felt, although little or no damage occurs.  The last earthquake that shook the New Madrid fault in which damages occurred was on New Year’s Day in 1969.  The tremor, measuring 4.4 on the Richter scale, centered about 19 miles northwest of Little Rock and caused a significant amount of commotion in the area.   Damages were reported in Little Rock, where plaster cracked and furniture was moved in some homes; trees and utility wires swayed and shook throughout a wide area.  Taking into consideration the time lapse since these earthquakes occurred, scientists estimate that the probability for an earthquake of magnitude 6.0 or greater is significant in the near future, with a 50% chance by the year 2000 and a 90% chance by the year 2040.

Three catastrophic earthquakes, with surface-wave magnitudes estimated at greater than 8.0 on the Richter scale occurred during the winter of 1811 through 1812. Thousands of aftershocks followed these gigantic earthquakes throughout the spring and remainder of 1812.   The first earthquake to occur during the winter of 1811 was on December 16, having a magnitude of 8.6 on the Richter scale and having an epicenter intensity of 11MM.  It occurred on the southwest branch of the New Madrid fault (see fig 2). The aftershocks continued intermittently until the second earthquake took place on January 23, 1812.  This earthquake, located on the north trending branch of the fault, had a magnitude of 8.4 on the Richter scale and the epicenter intensity was 12MM.  In the two weeks that followed there were no tremors until February 7, 1812.  This earthquake, on the northeast branch of the fault, had a magnitude of 8.7 on the Richter scale and the epicenter intensity was 12MM. 

This was an extremely difficult time for the European settlers on the Mississippi Valley. On the basis of the large area of damage, 600,000 square kilometers, the widespread area of perceptibility, 5,000,000 square kilometers, and the complex topography effects, the New Madrid earthquakes of 1811 through 1812 rank as some of the largest in the United States since its settlement by Europeans.  These magnitude 8 earthquakes devastated the surrounding region and rang church bells 1,000 miles away in Boston. Large areas of land sank into the earth, Lake St. Francis was formed, the course of the Mississippi River was changed and forests were destroyed.   The earthquakes caused only slight damage to man-made structures.    The land was still sparsely populated at that time with a population of no more than 5,000 in the area of intensity of 10.  Only one life was lost in the falling buildings.  The extent of the area that experienced damaging earth motion is estimated to be 600,000 square kilometers.  However, shaking strong enough to alarm the general population occurred over an area of 205 million square kilometers.  At the onset of the earthquake the ground raised and fell, bending the trees until their branches intertwined and opening deep cracks in the ground.  Landslides swept down the steeper bluffs and hillsides and large areas of land were uplifted.  Huge waves on the Mississippi River overwhelmed many boats and washed others on to shore.  High banks caved and collapsed into the river, sand bars and whole islands disappeared.  Raised or sunken lands, fissures, sand blows and large landslides that covered an area of 78,000 - 129,000 square kilometers, characterized the region most seriously affected. White sand blew in the air with crater like depressions in the middle.  They blew at a height of 3 to 6 inches high; 100 feet in diameter and occurred frequently over an area of 1,400 square miles. 

            Although the New Madrid earthquakes have caused relatively little casualties or destruction to human structures, the same would not be true today.        For some reason, most fault lines today have been highly urbanized and heavily populated.  This poses many problems.  The earthquakes along the New Madrid Fault line in 1811 and 1812 were not very destructive, despite the fact that they were very severe, because the area was less populated and the structures could withstand damages.  There were no issues of leaking gas lines and toppling high-rise structures.  Communication did not depend on phone lines and transportation did not depend on automobiles.  Life was simpler and so were the damages caused by the earthquakes.  However, in today’s modernized world, earthquake damages have not been a factor.  Since earthquakes along the New Madrid have not happened in almost two hundred years, those who built the buildings and laid the roadways gave little or no concern to making them earthquake proof.  This is why the area around the New Madrid Fault is in extreme danger of massive earthquake damage if another tectonic movement occurs.

            Damages from earthquakes are similar for most parts of the world, so an analysis of any major fault line will give pertinent data concerning damages in general.  In 1981, Otto Nuttli, from the Department of Earth and Atmospheric Sciences at Saint Louis University, published an evaluation of most of the studies on the New Madrid Fault line and compiled a manual about their effects.  His information is very clear and thorough concerning damages from earthquakes in the past as well as contemporary issues on what to do in today’s time to minimize these damages.

            Nuttli first tackles the epicenter of the earthquake, or the Macro seismic area.  He denotes this region around Memphis, Tennessee, by using the maximum Mercalli  (MM) intensity of X.  This is the most severe region, and is indicated (figure1) on the New Madrid Impact Area map (Nuttli, 1981:12).

            He explains that the first priority and problem to be dealt with immediately after the earthquake had stopped is the evacuation of people.  This not only entails evacuating people out of the damaged zone and into shelters but also getting the wounded to a medical treatment center.  The problem is that overpasses will be down and roads will be blocked with debris or torn up by folding or through soil failure underneath.  There are seven major highways in Arkansas that would be affected: I-40, I-30, US 64, US 79, I-55, US 49, and US 67(ANG SOP).  The disruption of these roads would inhibit the transport of injured from the area of damage and prevent rescue workers from getting in.  Bridges will have collapsed and the major railroad tracks that run along the Arkansas and White River valleys will be bended into twisted metal (ANG SOP).  The only vehicles available to transport the victims would be overland vehicles, helicopters, and light aircraft (Nuttli, 1981:14).

            The next problem would be finding sufficient housing for all of the evacuated people and establishing nearby medical treatment centers (Nuttli, 1981:14).  These would have to be set up miles away from the epicenter of the earthquake, in case of aftershocks.  Also the problem of feeding them would have to be dealt with.  The facilities themselves would have to be checked for structural integrity (Nuttli, 1981:14).  Inspection of the important standing buildings would be necessary, such as police buildings, and places such as hospitals and jails.  The integrity of dams would also have to be checked (Nuttli, 1981:14).

            There are six major natural gas pipelines that run underground in the New Madrid area and three major petroleum pipelines (ANG SOP).  These pipes are essential to the entire United States.  If an earthquake happened along the fault, these lines would be torn to pieces.  Not only would the gas be a danger to the damage area because of the fumes and of fire, the remainder of the nation would suffer from lack of natural gas.  This would be bad if it happened in the wintertime, when most people rely on gas to heat homes (Nuttli, 1981:15).

            Especially in the Mississippi River region, flooding is a great danger.  There are five major rivers in the New Madrid Fault Area: the Arkansas River, the Mississippi River, the White River, the St. Francis River, and the Cache River; and three reservoirs:  Greers Ferry, Bull Shoals, and Norfolk (ANG SOP).  A large earthquake could easily cause soil to loosen, causing failure of levees and flooding of many populated areas, especially around the Mississippi River and the St. Francis, which are contained mainly by levees.  This would be very dangerous if the rivers happened to be high at the time (Nuttli, 1981:15).

            Power failure would be a is definite result of an earthquake, with three major oil/gas fired electrical plants in North-East Arkansas that would be affected by an earthquake in the New Madrid area (ANG SOP).  This would disrupt power to all areas nearby, causing great problems for hospitals.  Downed power lines would also contribute to power failure.

            Nuttli next tackles the area on his Impact Area map (Nuttli, 1981:12) that has a MM intensity of VIII and IX.  In this area, the impact of the quake is slightly less severe, but still very damaging.  This area could be as big as 100,000 square miles.  The main damages here would be the collapse of residential buildings, which could be responsible for many deaths.  These buildings also include the police, hospitals, and fire departments, which are already going to be taxed heavily with responsibility to their communities (Nuttli, 1981:16).

            Last Nuttli explains about the damages that would occur in the area having a MM intensity of VII.  This will be the lightest area of damages from the earthquake.  Most buildings will remain intact, but will suffer chimney damage.  Fire is a danger in the structures still, as well as flooding from burst water pipes.  Another problem is the shaking of high-rise structures.  Although the earthquake will not be close enough to this area to bring them down, the high-rise buildings will shake, causing windows to shatter, elevators to jump cables, power failures, and blocked stairways.  These conditions will cause panic, which could lead to injury and death (Nuttli, 1981:17).

            Since earthquakes cannot be prevented, preparation for them is imperative.  It is too late to earthquake proof all of the buildings that are along the New Madrid Fault line.  Basically, the only option is to develop systems to minimize earthquake damages and prepare for them to happen.  The Arkansas National Guard has recently developed a Standard Operating Procedure in the event that an earthquake happens in the New Madrid area.  This SOP follows the same format as Nuttli’s report, which states that the first priority is to procure a greater number of evacuation vehicles.  In the event that another major quake strikes the New Madrid, more helicopters and off-road vehicles will be needed (Nuttli, 1981:15).  Captain Shillcutt, Army National Guard Public Affairs, explained how the National Guard, which would perform most of the initial rescue operations, always received equipment late.  He stated that most of the rescue helicopters were Hueys, an aircraft dating back to the Vietnam days, and while the Guard does have several Black hawks (an upgraded helicopter, better than the Huey), they only received them a year ago, and the new helicopters have been around for fifteen years (Shillcutt, 29Feb00).  It is clear that more money will have to be spent on equipment if damage is to be minimized.

            Facilities must be reserved for emergencies.  There should be enough to hold all of the inhabitants of the major zone of destruction, and they should be capable of housing and feeding the people.  Food sources will be a problem because most of the grocery stores will have been destroyed (Nuttli, 1981:15).  Military rations (MREs), which are extremely nourishing, should be stockpiled for such an event.

            Next Nuttli states that all of the available manpower should be inventoried.  This includes police, firemen, the Army, hospital staff, and anyone else capable of helping during the time of need.  Their facilities should also be placed in key locations of easy access or at least possible to get to after an earthquake (Nuttli, 1981:15).

            Gas lines in the area should be checked and people living nearby should be notified of their existence.  Plastic or metal pipelines could be replaced with elastic or rubber lines, to resist breaking.  This may prevent fire or explosion as well as harmful fumes reaching victims.  Levees should be checked for integrity and reinforced if needed  (Nuttli, 1981:15).  Roads will have to be cleared of debris, and bridges will have to be repaired, no matter what kind of engineering is used in their creation.  According to Captain Shillcutt, the Engineers of the National Guard have the ability to repair or make temporary bridges (Shillcutt, 29Feb00).  This ability could be supported with better equipment to remove the problem of downed bridges from the equation.

            These solutions are not perfect, but they would increase our ability to deal with any earthquakes that happen along the New Madrid fault line, as well as other unprepared fault areas.  Even though an earthquake has not happened here in a very long time, the possibility is high, and should not be taken lightly.

            What does this entire mean to the citizens of Conway?  Conway is a city with a population of xxx with an elevation of 300 feet above sea level.  It is situated near the Arkansas River at a point only x miles downstream from the Dardanelle lock and dam.  If an earthquake the size of the New Madrid earthquakes of 1811-1812 were again to hit this region the results would be catastrophic.  Picture the river at flood stage and the dam giving way all at once.  Now picture the devastation occurring in the city of Conway.  A wall of water is making its way southward overflowing its banks and clearing the landscape of automobiles, houses, trees and people.  The levees are no longer in place because they have sustained considerable structural damage from the earthquake, and Conway is flooded. Conway regional Medical Center would be the only treatment facility that would be an option considering that the bridge that crosses the Arkansas River between Conway and Little Rock has been shaken apart and washed away.  While the Army National Guard helicopters would have been a great resource to draw on for evacuation purposes, most of them were on the ground during the earthquake and are now inoperable.  Survivors of the earthquake and flood now try to evacuate the area, as they are hit by aftershocks that cause more destruction and loss of lives.  While it is hard to imagine being a victim of an earthquake, the possibility of this scenario is very real.  With scientists predicting that an earthquake with a magnitude equal to or greater than 8.0 of the Richter scale will occur in the New Madrid region in the next 40 years, you have to wonder if Conway is the best place to live.
