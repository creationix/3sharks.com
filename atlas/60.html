What is the New Madrid Fault Line and what would be the impact of an earthquake in Arkansas if one were to occur today?

 

            The New Madrid Fault Line is not a true fault line.  Instead, it is revealed in the presence of a rift valley, which is dropped through in the plate rock.  Volcanic activity weakens the plates and forms rifts in the plates.  The New Madrid Rift, also known as the Reelfoot Rift, was formed 500 million years ago.  Existing twenty-five miles below the surface, it is considered a “failed” rift because a successful one would have eventually split the plate.  Therefore, it is actually a zone of seismic activity (Stewart and Knox 1995).  This zone begins in Cairo, Illinois and extends 150 miles to Marked Tree, Arkansas.  Besides crossing five states, it crosses the Mississippi River three times.

Seismologists classify earthquakes in eight categories.  They are tiny, very minor, minor, light, moderate, strong, major, and great.  Tiny earthquakes range from 1.0 to 1.9 on the Richter scale and occur once every two days.  Next, very minor earthquakes measure 2.0 to 2.9 on the Richter scale and occur once every two weeks.  These two earthquakes are unfelt and detected only by instruments.  Then comes the earthquakes that are felt, but cause little or no damage.  They are the minor earthquakes, which measure 3.0 to 3.9 and occur once every  four months, and the light earthquakes, which occur once every four years measure 4.0 to 4.9.  The next set of earthquakes is generally what we hear about from California, which are destructive, but not devastating.  Moderate earthquakes measure 5.0 to 5.9 on the Richter scale and occur once every four decades.  Occurring once every eight decades, strong earthquakes measuring 6.0 to 6.9.  Lastly, the most devastating earthquakes are the majors and greats.  In fact, the only known occurrence of an earthquake of this magnitude is the New Madrid earthquake of 1811-1812.  The major earthquakes measure 7.0 to 7.9 on the Richter scale and occur once every two centuries.  Finally, the most disastrous are the great earthquakes, which occur once every five centuries and range from 8.0 to 8.9 on the Richter scale (Stewart and Knox 1995, 282).

            There are hundreds of very small earthquakes on the New Madrid Fault each year, but there have only been five great earthquakes.  All of these earthquakes took place in 1811 and 1812.  The first of these earthquakes was at 2:30 a.m. on December 16, 1811.  Its epicenter is thought to have been just southwest of now day Blytheville, Arkansas.  It supposedly measured 8.6 on the Richter scale.  This was the first of 2,000 earthquakes in five months.  At 8:15 a.m. the same day another earthquake measuring 8.0 on the Richter scale hit around Cuote, Missouri.  A lot of tremors continued to occur throughout the morning, and then later in the morning another 8.0 magnitude earthquake hit at present day Caruthesville, Missouri (Stewart and Knox 1995; 132-134).

            The first earthquake was so powerful that it woke up President Madison in Washington D.C. 800 miles away.  Where the epicenter was located in the first earthquake lake known as Big Lake, which is ten miles long and four miles wide was formed.  It is located between Blytheville and Leachville, Arkansas.  When all of the earthquakes were over ten lakes had been formed (Steward and Knox, 137-145).

            There was then another great earthquake at 9:00 a.m. on January 23, 1812.  This earthquake measured 8.4 on the Richter scale and was located near Point Pleasant, Missouri.  The earthquake destroyed the community and threw it into the river.  There were no casualties because the people had evacuated the city.  After this earthquake, many aftershocks occurred for some time.  “One man counted 500 by February”(Stewart and Knox, 174).  William L. Pierce wrote:

“A few yards from the spot where we lay, the body of a large oak was snapped into…During the first four shocks, tremendous and uninterrupted explosion, resembling a discharge of artillery, were heard from the opposite shore; at the time imputed them to the falling of the river banks.  This fifth shock explained the real cause.  Wherever the veins of the earthquake ran, there was a volcanic discharge of constable matter to a great height, as incessant rumbling was heard below, and the bed of the river was excessively agitated, whilst the water assumed a turbid and boiling appearance- near our boat a spout of confined air, breaking its way through the waters burst forth and with a loud report discharged mud, sticks &c, from the rivers bed, at least thirty feet above the surface (Fuller 1996).”

            The people of the New Madrid reported that on February 4 the earth began to give in like a side of freshly killed beef.  (Stewart and Knox, 189).  These little jerks and spasms continued for several days. Then on February 17, at 3:15 a.m. the “Big One” hit.  It measured 8.8 on the Richter scale.  This is equivalent to the energy released in 8,000 atomic bombs.  The earthquake caused a giant wave from the Mississippi River destroying New Madrid and killing lots of people.  The quake was so long that it woke people in Quebec at 3:19 a.m.  If the people in Australia had had seismographs, they would have recorded it 33 minutes after the initial eruption.  Sand boiled along the Mississippi River and some people recorded seven explosions (Stewart and Knox 1995, 189-200; Penick Jr. 1936, 29-67).

             The earthquake was so strong that it made the Mississippi River run backwards for seven hours.  Violent waves were thrown over the banks and destroyed forests and anything that got in its way.  The earthquake also formed waterfalls, which killed many men.  These waterfalls only lasted two to three days (Stewart and Knox 1995, 200-203).

            The number of people killed on land due to these earthquakes is unknown. There were at least one hundred deaths but probably two to three times that.  A couple of hundreds of Indians were probably killed and never recorded because Indians did not keep records of their deaths.  The Indians probably died in their canoes because empty canoes were seen floating down the river.  Many white men also died on the river by falling off waterfalls or banks caved in on them.  Those cave-ins usually would cover boats with the crew onboard.  No less than 500 people died due to these earthquakes (Stewart and Knox 1995, 204-214; Coffmer 1973, 37-46).

            The town of New Madrid was completely destroyed by the earthquakes.  There was nothing left standing in the town.  The earthquakes destroyed the banks of the river along the New Madrid, and the floods of 1812 completely washed away whatever remains were left after the earthquakes (Stewart and Knox 1995, 209-212).

            Because of the New Madrid six settlements and two islands were wiped from the face of the earth.  Every map of the area had to be redone.  The town and islands that were lost were Little Rock Prairie, Missouri on December 16,1811; Island #94, Mississippi on December 16,1811; Island #32 on December 21, 1811; Point Pleasant, Missouri on January 23, 1812; Fort Jefferson, Kentucky in February of 1812; Spanish Mill, Missouri in February of 1812; and New Madrid in April and May of 1812.  There was also a lot in Indian settlements that were lost that nobody paid attention to (Stewart and Knox 1995, 210-213, 241).

            Many of the people who experienced the earthquakes were afraid of being swallowed alive.  There were accounts recorded of the ground opening up and causing fissures that could reach 300 feet deep.  There were several people who were swallowed up by these fissures, and only a few of these people survived to tell about it.  Many people also recorded seeing light coming from the ground after the earthquakes. They said that the light was coming from Gods in the ground.  Scientists have no explanation of this and many do not believe such an event could have happened.  Many people also reported that the water was very warm.  Scientists also do not understand how this could have happened because there is no igneous, volcanic, or geothermal activity in the New Madrid fault Zone (Stewart and Knox 1995, 230-239; “The New Madrid Fault System”).

            Eastern earthquakes are more powerful than Western earthquakes. This is true because the crust in the West is younger than the crust of the East so the seismic waves are not as strong and they do not travel as far in the younger crust.  The crust in the West is very old and the seismic waves can travel very far and stay fairly strong.  So when an earthquake occurs in the East, it is stronger than the same size earthquake in the West (Stewart and Knox 1995, 240; “Natural Disasters”).

            The “aftershocks” of the Great New Madrid earthquakes still continue today.  There is a greater than 90% chance of an earthquake with a magnitude 6.0 or greater by the year 2,040 A.D.  If you live within 200 miles of the fault, you can expect minor damage.  If you live within 600 miles, you will at least feel such a quake.  The New Madrid Fault is more than a local phenomenon.  It is ongoing and ever active.  During our lifetime and probably not even during our grandchildren’s lifetime, there will not going to have a great earthquake on the New Madrid that ranges from 8.0 to 9.0.  We will, however, probably have a strong quake of 6.0 to 7.9.  That earthquake has the potential of being the disaster of the century.  There will definitely be a numerous amounts of 4.0 to 5.9 earthquakes.  As Dr. Otto Nuttli says, “minor chest pains do not prevent a major heart attack.  They are warnings.  Likewise, minor earthquakes do not prevent major ones.  We must take them as warnings (Stewart and Knox 1995, 284).”  Research indicates that the average reoccurrence interval for great earthquakes in the New Madrid Seismic Zone in 425 to 675 years.  The probability of one occurring that is the size of the 1811-1812 earthquake in the year 2008 is only 0.3% to 1.8% (Stewart and Knox 1995, 244-290; “The New Madrid Fault System”).  

           

            Around twelve million people now live in the New Madrid area.  Most of the buildings, roads, and bridges are not built to sustain such damage as an earthquake.  The people living in the urban area buildings will not likely be killed or injured due to collapse of their homes.  The transportation system will be greatly disrupted and traffic will halt.  Flooding of the Mississippi River will cease river traffic and cause local damage.  The welfare and health of the population will be adversely effected by the widespread outages of utility and communication facilities.  Relief efforts will go far beyond the potential of local and state governments.  In an unpublished paper to the Missouri Earthquakes Hazard Mitigation Panel, Otto Nuttli stated: 

“The earthquake hazard in Missouri is unique.  The other natural hazards, tornadoes and floods, affect only a relatively small area and a small part of the population of the state.  However, when the next major earthquake occurs in southeast Missouri or one of the neighboring states, it will produce a disaster magnitude and scope or such that they will tax all the resources of the state, both in the immediate response and in the long term follow up.  They will tax the state manpower and its treasury to a high degree.  Even with massive federal relief, the effects on the state, its people and its commerce, will be severe and long lasting.(Drabek, Mushkatel, Kilijanek, 1983, 68)”

 

            In 1980, the Federal Emergency management Agency or FEMA extended its earthquake research to the New Madrid area.  The goal was to develop response plan, develop a mitigation policy that reduces earthquake hazards, and develop a public awareness program.  Since state and local government cooperation depends on public awareness. FEMA is working to educate the people of the area.  This is a trying task because the last major earthquake occurred many generations ago.  Positive effects are finally detectable from FEMA’s work.  In a study, about one-half of the respondents were aware of the program.  Because of this an unprecedented process between the seven New Madrid states has been created.  It is a mitigation and response plan that will effect all seven stated, especially in the Bootheel area.  The plan should also increase public awareness (Drabek, Kilijanek, and Mushkatel 1983, 75-78).
