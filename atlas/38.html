Gated Communities

A 2: Physical Geography: Gated Communities

“Gated Communities”

By Jason Hedrick, Ricky Montgomery, and Todd Hull (The Gate Keepers)

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2612

Prepared May 2, 2000

                   In the 1980s the growth of gated communities sky rocketed. Gated communities is where they “control their social environments as well-ultimately trying to create whole neighborhoods of people of the same age, profession, sexual orientation, religion or even ethnic origin” (Nelson 222). Gated communities are not a new idea. The theory has been used since the armies and the city-states of the past, like the dark ages, realized a wall around the area they lived would help to repel invaders (http://proquest.umi.com/pqdweb). People built fortresses such as Megiddo in the 500 B.C.E (Buckler 41).  The theory is sound, but is it applicable to today’s neighborhoods? This is the question that has been asked over the years. The answer is different for everyone. In general most people that will move or already live in these types of habitats do so because they feel safer (Tucker 24). Our research question is are gated communities the modern day fort?  In this report we will look at why people move to gated communities, the three types of gated communities, and the effects that gated communities have on non-gated community residents.

          The wealthy citizens of this country have historically wanted gates and walls around their estates (Waxler 438). This kept them socially isolated from the more common or lower class people. The same can be said about the higher classes of today. It is not common for a person of wealth to live in a home that is not protected by a gate. Another reason for gated communities is the idea that it keeps your property safe from those that might want to take it from you. It is almost like the city-states of the past looking for protection (Baron 93).

Is this protection from criminals a realistic idea? The answer to the question is no. These gates and fences are not that much of a deterrent for criminals. If a criminal wants something then they will find a way to get it. Another aspect for people to look at is the fact that “ police have reported that crime was as likely to come from people living within the gates as from outside.” (Tucker 22). The gated communities give many people a “false sense of security”. If you look at most of these areas of safety you will see that most of the walls do not go completely around the complex (see Figure 7).   Homeowners are also attracted to gated communities because the property values are higher. This is a way that the realtor feeds on the ignorance of the customer that wants to buy a home they believe is protected (Baron 95).

          There are three types of gated communities in the United States (Tucker 23).  The first type is the life style community (see figures 1 and 2). This type of community is typical of the retirement, golf, and leisure communities. These types of leisure activities are the common bonds on which the community is established.  The walls around these areas give the residents a sense of separation from the outside world so they can relax and enjoy life. These types of communities are becoming popular with the upper middle class (Tucker 24).

          This brings us to prestige communities (see figures 3 and 4). They are the homes of the rich and famous. Areas such as these tend to show off the status of the inhabitants. It is a barrier between the upper class and the lower classes. These borders help to feed the dislike of the affluent by those of the lower classes.  The walls also allow the realtor to charge a higher price for the home (Tucker 23).  We looked through a reality magazine of homes in Arkansas and we found that homes in gated communities were listed for $300-$400 thousand dollars where as homes of the same equivalent but not in gated communities were around $200 thousand dollars (The Original Homes Magazine).    

          The third type and perhaps the most popular is the security-zone community (see figures 5 and 6). This is where groups of people get together for the purpose of segregating themselves from their neighbors. They do this for protection and an added sense of security, hence, the name security-zone community. You will find these communities located in the inner-city and lower-income areas (Tucker 23).

          The affects of these communities can be felt from outside of the gates as well as inside. If we as a society want to bring everyone together geographically with no thought of income or race, then we have a long way to go. These gated

 communities are a major set back. Most of these communities handle all of their street maintenance, garbage collecting, and security. This makes the lower income citizens more dependent on the city for such help. Without the help of the rich and powerful to keep such luxuries in place, then the lower class might lose this as well (http://proquest.umi.com/pqdweb). This is a clear case of the people that have the power taking every advantage they have. These people want to distance themselves from the poor so they can feel better about themselves. The walls represent the thin line that is between them and poverty. The walls also keep them ignorant of how poor some people really are.

When it comes to forts, which is a military installation that holds a particular piece of ground, the militaries of the past and present are the true masters. This is their job. They have to keep the land that they have taken theirs. The best way to do this is to put up walls and guard these walls (FM 7-8).

Is this the way we want our neighborhoods set up? If so then we want to live the future that most science fiction holds for us. If this happens then we might have city’s set up where everyone is separated (Davis 253).

Gated communities are the modern day fortresses. In the end there will always be those whom are affluent and those whom are not.  These affluent will always be in power. This is what history has taught us. The people with the

 

Power, i.e. the people with the money, control the access to their land. If the affluent want there to be more equality, then they will have to get rid of gated communities that serve the purpose of segregation. Of course, there are always exceptions to the rule. An example would be the military. Most of these

communities will have to open their gates. They do not provide protection like one might think, and they affect the outside community in a negative manner. 

They just do not work unless you do not want people of a different social class around.
