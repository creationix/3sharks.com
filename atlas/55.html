How Devastating Would an Earthquake in Arkansas be as Compared to one of the Same Scale in California?

A2:  Earthquakes

"Earthquake Damage in Arkansas"

By Katherine Case, Miranda Freeman, Yolanda Lynch, Seth Shelby, and Holli Williamson (Estimated Damages)

Principles of Geography:  Geog 1305, Section 2519

Prepared:  March 4, 1999

How Devastating Would an Earthquake in Arkansas be as Compared to one of the Same Scale in California?

            On Tuesday, October 17, 1989, a Richter magnitude 7.1 earthquake struck the San Francisco Bay area of California.  The Loma Prieta earthquake, as it would later be called, killed 63 people and caused $6 billion in property damages (EQE 1997).  While earthquakes like that are most often associated with California, there is a definite possibility that such an earthquake could strike in the Central United States.  The question is, how devastating would an earthquake in Arkansas be as compared to one of the same scale in California?  Several factors, such as ground type, architecture, and emergency services, contribute to the amount of damage an earthquake can cause.  Therefore, each of these factors must be carefully considered before an answer can be found.

            First of all, an individual must have some knowledge about the way an earthquake works, so that he will know what he is dealing with.  The amount of damage caused by an earthquake cannot truly be understood without first understanding the forces behind that earthquake.

            Many earthquakes occur and for most of us probably do not know why they happen.  Earthquakes are caused by faulting, a sudden lateral or vertical movement of rock along a break in the earth's surface.  The surface of the earth is in continuous slow motion.  This motion is called plate tectonics-the motion of rigid plates at the surface of the Earth in response to flow of rock within the earth.  Plates cover the entire surface of the globe.  Because these plates are constantly moving they begin to rub against each other in some places like the San Andreas Fault in California.  They can also sink beneath each other like the Peru-Chile Trench along the western border of South America, or even spread apart like the mid-Atlantic Ridge.  In these places the motions are very rough.  The plates are stuck together at the edges but the rest of the plates are continuing to move around, so the rocks along the edges are distorted or strained.  As the motion continues, the strain builds up to the point where the rock cannot withstand any more bending.  With a small movement, the rock breaks and the two sides move.  An earthquake is the shaking that results from the breaking rock.

            People have known about earthquakes for thousands of years but still remain clueless as to what causes them.  People believed that the breaks in the Earth's surface (faults) which appear after earthquakes, were caused by the earthquake itself rather than the cause of them.  Despite the different ways of explaining earthquakes, we all know that they can result in mass destruction.  There was a report of at least 8,928 people killed from earthquakes world wide in 1998, according to the U.S. Geological Survey National Earthquake Information Center in Golden, CO.  Although this is a huge increase over the number of deaths that occurred in 1997 and 1996, it is still under the long-term average of about 10,000 earthquake-related deaths in the world each year.

            The first thing to consider when trying to determine the damage a possible earthquake could cause is ground type.  The type of ground a structure is built upon can greatly affect its chances of surviving an earthquake.  Scientists have recently proven that softer ground shakes more violently than harder ground during an earthquake.  Records obtained during the 1989 Loma Prieta, California earthquake and its aftershocks show that soft clay will shake more violently than the sandy soil, which will shake more violently than hard bedrock (Brown http://quake.wr.usgs.gov).  Therefore, any structure constructed on soft clay or soil will suffer more architectural damage in an earthquake than a similar structure built on hard rock.

            Ground type would be a major factor for damage caused by an earthquake in the state of Arkansas.  For the most part, the surface area of the state is made up of sedimentary rock and clay.  Sedimentary rocks are made up of several materials and are fairly weak in nature.  Both the sedimentary rock and clay would shake violently in the event of an earthquake.  Any structures built on these materials would suffer sever architectural damage during an earthquake.  Thus it is evident that ground type would be a major factor when considering possible earthquake damage in Arkansas.

            Another factor to consider when trying to determine the damage an earthquake would cause in an area is the architectural practices of the region.  The type of structures in an area and they way they were built would determine how well they would stand up during an earthquake.  Thus architecture must be considered when trying to determine how much damage an earthquake in Arkansas would cause.

            The first line of defense against earthquakes has always been the ability to construct buildings that can resist severe shaking (Celebi http://quake.wr.usgs.gov).  Because of seismic activities across the country building codes are different for each region.  Since large-scale seismic activity is more common in California, codes for California are stricter than codes for Arkansas.  Therefore, buildings in California would be better constructed for earthquakes than buildings in Arkansas.  The fact of the matter is that most buildings and other structures in Arkansas have not been constructed to withstand severe shaking.  Thus an earthquake in the state would certainly be a disaster.

            Finally one must consider the quality of emergency service programs in the region when trying to determine what damage an earthquake could cause.  In October of 1977, Congress passed the Earthquake Hazards Reduction act.  This act set up four agencies, led by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA).  These agencies are responsible for the improvement of construction of structures in earthquake risk areas, as well as risk reduction by investigating earthquakes and education programs (FEMA http://www.fema.gov).  Science has allowed the prediction of where earthquakes are likely to occur and the extent of force that they will produce.  With this knowledge, emergency service agencies can create building codes and regulations for areas of seismic risk.  Minimum standards prevent structures from collapsing.   This helps to ensure the safety and welfare of the public.

            FEMA funds disaster preparedness programs in both California and Arkansas.  City employees of Pacific Grove, California, for example,  were sent to training courses and a disaster plan was developed in case of an earthquake.  In California, the Governor's Office of Emergency Services (OES) coordinates activities of all emergency services for the development of the California Emergency Plan.  States in the new Madrid Fault area, including Arkansas, have also begun preparing for a major earthquake, however they are years behind California.

            Lifeline services power and water systems, are important areas of consideration for both California and Arkansas.  Their departments of emergency services must make preparations to protect these services in the advent of an earthquake.  Lifeline services are covered in California by the California Emergency Plan.  The Memphis Light, Gas, and Water Division has already initiated a seismic project in which steps are taken to reach the best possible outcome in case of an earthquake in the New Madrid region (FEMA http://www.fema.gov).  However, California is still far more prepared when it comes to earthquake readiness.

            It has been estimated that an earthquake in the New Madrid region would cause widespread loss of life and billions of dollars in property damage.  "Because of differences in the geology east and west of the Rocky Mountains, the effects of a magnitude 7 quake in the mid-continental United States could be far worse than those of the 1989 magnitude 7 Loma Prieta, California, earthquake" (Brown http://quake.wr.usgs.gov).  Experts also point to the weak building codes of the region as a source of potential danger.  This is because most buildings and other structures have not been constructed with the possibility of an earthquake in mind.  Thus structures in the region would suffer severe damage during an earthquake.  Secondly, Arkansas is years behind California when it comes to earthquake readiness.  The residents of the state, for the most part, have no idea of the region's earthquake history.  Finally, Arkansas does not have as many earthquake-related emergency service agencies as California.  Therefore, it is evident that an earthquake in Arkansas would be a huge disaster.

            When one considers all the facts, it is clear that Arkansas is not truly prepared for a major earthquake.  The combination of soft ground, a lack of building codes, and a weak emergency service program would certainly add to the loss of life and destruction an earthquake in Arkansas would cause.  Therefore, an earthquake in Arkansas would cause at least as much damage as one of the same scale in California, if not more.
