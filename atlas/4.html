How were some Faulkner county churches established in a geographers aspect?

In the beginning, Conway, Arkansas may have not seemed like a town that would have 82 churches to come in its future (conwayarkcc.org 1998, 26 November).  After all it did have six saloons, a racetrack and a dance hall (Gatewood, 1961).  The Conway of today is a very different from the Conway of the 1800’s. Two of those 82 churches are St. Joseph Catholic and Pickles Gap Baptist, two very different churches with two different histories, but both with one very similar mission: saving souls (Gatewood, 1961).

Many factors have contributed to the immigration of Catholics into Arkansas and subsequently to the settlement in Conway.  Important among these were the character and background of the immigrants along with the skills and resources, which they brought with them.  Personal traits were not the only factors of this immigration, many geographic factors greatly contributed to, if not caused, the influx of German Catholics (Lincoln, 1890).

            Prior to 1832, there were some 2000 scattered Catholics in the Arkansas Territory but not a single priest.  In that year, Bishop Rosati of St. Louis ordained several priests for his diocese and one was sent to the Arkansas Territory.  From 1817 to 1844, traveling priests going between Missouri and the Indian Territories did Catholic missions in Faulkner County (Brettels, 1966).  On November 28, 1843, the Diocese of Little Rock was created.  The Catholic population had increased enough to justify establishing this new Diocese.  Reverend Andrew Byrne, an Irish priest from New York, was consecrated Bishop of Little Rock on March 10, 1844.  These missionaries had a profound impact on Faulkner County as well as the other regions in which they occurred.   The missionaries served as a way to diffuse the Catholic religion as well as the culture ascribed to it, as one would assume.  This type of diffusion, called contiguous diffusion, is the process of an item or feature spreading though time, from one place directly to a neighboring area.  These missionaries made it easier and more attractive for German Catholics to migrate to Faulkner County (Brettel, 1966). 

By the end of the Civil War, Arkansas was in disarray after being occupied for two years by Union troops.  Some of the problems that faced early Arkansas after the Civil War included a decrease in the labor force, lawlessness, and personal debts.  The condition of Arkansas at this time made finding a successor for the recently deceased Bishop Byrne difficult.  On February 5, 1867, the problem was solved when Reverend Edward Fitzgerald, an immigrant from Limerick, Ireland was consecrated Bishop of Little Rock.  The native Catholics were impoverished and disheartened from the aftermath of the war, making the new Bishop’s role extremely demanding.  The period of Reconstruction was a hard time for the Diocese of Little Rock, which at the time comprised the State of Louisiana and the Indian Territory toward the West (Chester, 1927.)

In order to truly understand what caused German immigrants to come to America and Arkansas, one must understand the concepts of push and pull factors of migration (Brettel, 1966).  To understand a push factor it gives a clearness how people or objects are drawn away and a pull factor which is opposite of drawing away, but attracting to.  Several push and pull factors played a huge role in the surge of German Catholic immigration that occurred in the mid to late 1800’s. During this period in history Otto von Bismarck was the Chancellor of Prussia (What would later become modern day Germany?), and German Catholics were a minority in Prussia.  Bismarck was not overly fond of the population of German Catholics due to his fears that they were more loyal to the Roman Catholic Church, than to Prussia.  Bismarck assumed that the German Catholic Church was merely an instrument in the hands of two foreign powers, the Vatican and France.  These fears were strengthened when the population of German Catholics formed a new political party to ensure they would not suffer injustice, the Catholic Center Party.  The formation of this party placed the German Catholics directly against Bismarck and Prussia, and in line with Prussia’s recent enemy France, or so Bismarck thought.  These conditions and feelings of mistrust and treachery made life unbearable and often dangerous for German Catholics in Prussia, made all the more miserable by the poor economic conditions brought on by Bismarck’s economic plan.  These conditions were the push factors for many German Catholics that made America seem a much better place than Prussia at that time.  However, immigration did not occur simply because of the horrid conditions in Prussia, the atmosphere in America made it a likely new home for many immigrants, including the German Catholics.  During the 1800’s America was actively seeking immigrants to strengthen itself, and Arkansas was one of many states doing just that (Idom, 1998).

            Bishop Fitzgerald, wanting to strengthen German settlers in the state, secured from the Fort Smith-Little Rock Railroad Company grants of land.  One of these grants was located in Conway, and the lad was offered to the Catholic settlers coming into Arkansas, this is how St. Joseph’s Parish began.  The history of St. Joseph’s Parish dates back to the arrival of the first Catholic family, which took place shortly after Faulkner County was organized in 1873.  The county seat was originally called Conway Station, but two years later it was changed to Conway.  The first family to settle in what is now St. Joseph’s Parish was that of Jacob Schichtl, who settled one mile east of Conway.  The Schichtl settlement is now the site of the Fryaldenhoven Nursery (Gateway, 1961).

            In 1876, other Catholic families taking advantage of Bishop Fitzgerald’s land grants joined the Schichtl family.  Most of these new immigrants came to Faulkner County after reading an advertisement in a pamphlet called the “Lichtern Stern”.  In the early years, there were not enough priests to attend to all the new immigrants; therefore, the spiritual wants of the few Catholics in Conway were attended by a secular priest, Father Brehm, who traveled from Little Rock to Fort Smith, and occasionally stopped in Conway should the mood strike.  In 1877, the first Holy Mass was given in Conway.  It was offered on a makeshift altar in the humble cabin of Joseph Schichtl where the seven Catholic families were in attendance.  At this time, the small group of settlers was suffering many hardships.  Their first few years had witnessed a number of dry seasons.  As a result, many of the immigrants got sick and died, while others left the county.  Those who did stay paid high prices for living necessities, making life that much more difficult(Pope,1998). 

            In 1878, the Fathers of the Holy Ghost took charge of the struggling parish (Herndon, 1927).  The Holy Ghost Fathers came to Arkansas from Zamesville, Ohio, where they had resided since coming to America from Germany.  While in Ohio, Father Joseph Strub persuaded a group of their parishioners to migrate to Faulkner county and Arkansas.  By assisting the people and encouraging others from Germany to settle here Reverend Strub would soon transform the little mission into a promising religious community. This time the United States Government offered large grants of land on each side of the railroad.  The Missouri Pacific Railroad Company made a grant to the Holy Ghost Fathers establishing the property, which was to become St. Joseph Church.  Due to this, there was a steady flow of settlers, because around 1880 many Polish and German Catholics migrated to the United States, and subsequently to Arkansas.  This cheap land, which was often sold for $2.00 to $6.00 an acre depending on the location, and the interest, was as low as 4% (Thomas, 1947).  As if the price of the land was not enticing enough Catholics received a discount ranging from 10 to 15% off the price of the land.    Again advertisements in the “Lichtern Stern”, now called “St. Joseph’s Guiding Star”, many immigrants established themselves in Conway.  This cheap land, advertisements, and a genuine need for skilled labor all pulled dissatisfied German Catholics away from Prussia to the less confining United States of America (Idom, 1998). 

            The Holy Ghost Fathers sent Father Wilms, the first resident priest, to Conway.  Father Wilms soon began the task of consolidating the 85 local Catholic families into a parish family.  The residents soon erected a church in 1878, under the guidance of Father Wilms.  The first church was a modest frame construction, but boasted an eighty-foot steeple.  In the late 1870’s, an additional 300 immigrants came into Faulkner County to swell the parish role that had been depleted by a typhoid epidemic.  The church that had been completed in 1879 was destroyed by a severe windstorm in 1883.   The parish priest at the time, Father Steurer, left for Europe seeking aid in construction of a new church, after seven years without a church Father Steurer returned from Germany with aid soon to follow.  In 1890, a new church was erected with the assistance obtained from the Leopold Verein in Austria and the Bonafatius Verein in Germany.  Yet even with this assistance, the parish would still be in debt for many years.(Bettels,1966)
            In 1908 Father Zell came into power, and quickly set out to clear the church of its heavy debt, which he was successful at. 

            In March 1924, Father Joseph Pobleschek succeeded Father Zell as Pastor.  Father Pobleschek along with the growing parish family made plans for a $60,000 new church in May 1924.  The cornerstone laying took place on March 22, 1925 by Bishop Morris of Little Rock.  Visitors numbering around 3,000 were present for the occasion.  The church was dedicated a year later, December 13, 1925.  A few months after the dedication, disaster struck the church again.  A cotton compress near St. Joseph Hall caught on fire.  The burning embers were carried by a high wind, destroying the Hall.  Father Pobleschek immediately set upon the task of rebuilding it, having only recently completed the new church (Idom, 1998).

            Cultural imperialism is the substitution of one set of cultural traditions for another, either by force or by degrading those who fail to acculturate and rewarding those who do.  One distinct example of imperialism from St. Joe’s history is when the Germans decided to convert to Catholicism.  This was a major factor in why some Germans decided to relocate in Arkansas.

             Cultural imperialism, in this case, was put into effect by force by Bismarck.  Protestantism was the accepted religion in Germany.  Those who decided to go to Catholicism were “degraded” by Bismarck.  Protestantism was the only religion that was to be accepted in Germany and this was enforced by any means possible.

              Since France, one of Germany’s enemies, was the “traditional protector of Roman Catholicism…Catholics were often viewed with distrust by there Protestant countryman”(Idom, 1998).  To protect themselves, Catholic leaders founded the Catholic Center Party.  This party was founded to protect the rights of Catholics at all costs.  Bismarck tried everything in their power to destroy the Catholic Center Party.  This struggle is probably better known as The Kulturkampf (Idom, 1998).  It first started out with Pope Pius IX making it illegal for any member of Catholic or Protestant parties to discuss state matters in public.  Then the “May Laws” came into effect in 1873.  This was an attack on the priests and ministers of the church.  The “May Laws” said that any priest or minister was to pass a test with the state board and their appointment was dependent on whether or not they attended German high school and a German University (Idom, 1998.)              

The struggle continued over time, but most German Catholics decided to immigrate to the U.S. to seek religious freedom, like so many others.  The force of cultural imperialism drove most of the German Catholics right into America.  Arkansas wanted to attract these immigrants with different ways of financial security and religious freedom at the same time (Idom, 1998). 

The Church of Pickles Gap Baptist is a very unique house of worship (History of Pickle Gap, 1997).  The spreading of architecture from the original place to adapt to another culture is cultural diffusion.  Many European people in the 1800’s merged to the United States for opportunity (Sutherland, 2000).  Founded by John Wesley Mc Pike, the first pastor, and organized in 1878.  Pickles Gap Baptist is understood to be a light for the weary sinner (History Pickle Gap, 1997).

The first members of the congregation were R.D. Blackwell, William Butts, and J. M. Day and their families. They originally called the church Pickle Gap Creek Church (History Pickle Gap, 1997).  The first members of the church migrated from Europe and the people who had membership at Pickle Gap Baptist were from Irish descent (Sutherland, 2000).  Pickle Gap community was a rural area. The Pickle Gap Baptist was from a European decent; which currently Caucasian are present in the church.  The people who first attended the church were farmers and planters; at that time they were members of the church because of convenience (History Pickle Gap, 1997).   

            In 1903 there were a total of approximately fifty-five members.  Today the church has over one hundred and eighty members. This is due to today’s modern day amenity of the automobile. Church, today, is easier to attend because of vehicles (History Pickle Gap, 1997).

            Pickles Gap Baptist has had numerous pastors through out its history. The most a pastor stayed in office for preaching was about twenty-two years.  Revered Hayes presided at Pickles Gap Baptist from 1935-1957, pasturing the church for twenty-two years longer than anyone else in the church’s history. Reverend Hayes was responsible for transforming the church with many improvements. He designated each second Sunday and the Saturday night before the second Sunday for worship service. For all his efforts, Reverend Hayes would get a monthly salary of ten-dollars that ultimately increased to fifty-dollars. While Reverend Hayes was in office there was a slight decline in the church’s history (Pickle’s Gap History, 1997).  Some of the immigrants adapted to the United States culture and fought in World War II, which is called acculturation (Sutherland, 2000). 

The 1960’s and the 1970’s created an up-to-the-minute appearance for the church. Flowers were brought in.  Indoor and outdoor carpet was brought in.   A nursery was added. The church also got Sunday school classrooms, in addition to a new stairway.  The outside of the church acquired a makeover also. The church was fully re-bricked, and the old gravel parking lot was black topped.

            Pickles Gap Creek Baptist Church has grown from a one-room building with a nominal amount of members that met one a month, to the contemporary Pickles Gap Baptist Church of today. Although the members no longer bring their lanterns to church with them, they still believe Pickles Gap Baptist is a light for the weary sinner (Pickle’s Gap History, 1997).  The Pickle Gap Baptist was from a Irish decent, which now the church has a lot of Caucasian, because the advertisement of the church are not diffused as much (Sutherland, 2000) The church diffuses in a contagious way by the members contacting through phone calls, invitation, and word of mouth (Matthews, 2000).   

Although St. Joseph and Pickles Gap were started around the same time, St. Joseph has out grown Pickles Gap.  Catholics are more of a popular culture in Faulkner County than Baptists.  The diffusion of the membership of people, building structure, and historical importance has increased in St. Joseph because the church is in town of Conway.  They also seem to contribute more financially than Pickles Gap Baptist (St. Joseph Church History, 1995).  St. Joseph has a catholic school while Pickles Gap has a Sunday school (St. Joseph Church History, 1995; Pickle’s Gap History, 1997)

Both these churches have experienced dramatic changes through the years. They have both grown in memberships and in structure. They both have historical bonds in our communities, as Conway has grown they have grown. They both practice Christianity, while believing in Jesus Christ. They are both pillars of their communities. There denominations may divide them, but their love for their lord relates them. It is amazing to understand that Conway revolved around a railroad, and how because the railroad the town diffused outward (Sutherland, 2000).
