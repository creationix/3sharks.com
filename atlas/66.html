 To what extent and with what chemicals is the Arkansas River polluted, and how does this pollution affect the ecology of the surrounding areas?

 

            This report will explain what types of chemicals may be found in the Arkansas River, how they got there, and where they came from.  We will also explain what effect these pollutants may have on the ecology of the surrounding areas.

            Water pollution can be defined as anything, chemical or physical, that affects the natural condition, or the intended use of water.  In the past, people thought that it was alright to pollute streams and rivers due to their natural ability to dilute the pollutants and purify themselves.  But with the immense number of industrial factories and the growing complexity of the chemicals being dumped into the water, the amount of pollution has surpassed the water’s natural ability to cleanse itself (Wilber, 1969).   There are two types of pollution as it pertains to water: point source and non-point source.  Point source pollution is pollution that can be traced to a specific source, such as a pipe discharging wastewater from a factory.  Non-point source pollution comes from a larger, more diffused area that cannot be pinpointed, such as fertilizers from farms in the area which wash off into the river when it rains.  The most prevalent source of pollution in the Arkansas River is agricultural runoff (AR NPS Pollution Assessment Report, 1991).

            Arkansas has an abundance of surface water.  It contains over thirteen major rivers, most of which are tributaries to the Mississippi River.  It also has countless lakes and perennial streams, mostly located in the Springfield and Salem Plateaus, the Ouachita  Mountains, and the northern half of the West Gulf Coastal Plains (Hunrichs, 1983).  All this water makes for great recreation.  Per capita, boat ownership in Arkansas is second in the nation (Arkansas Department of Parks and Tourism, 1995).  Participation in boating, water skiing, swimming, fishing, and canoeing is above the national average (Arkansas Department of Parks and Tourism, 1995) here in the natural state.  One-half of the population of Arkansas receives their drinking water from rivers, streams, and lakes (U.S. Geological Survey, 1990 pg. 166).  Arkansans use around 2,100 million gallons of water a day.  Most of this is used for agriculture (U.S. Geological Survey, 1990 pg. 166).  The area surrounding the Arkansas River is primarily used for growing crops and raising livestock.  The rest is forests and woodland (Anderson, 1967).  Most of Arkansas’ population is stationed along the Arkansas River, the majority being located in the Northwestern region (U.S. Bureau of the Census, 1990 decennial census files).  Arkansas is home to about 2.5 million people, which increased about 3% in the last decade (U.S. Bureau of the Census, 1990 decennial census files). 

The Arkansas River originates in the Rocky Mountains and flows eastward for 1,450 miles before discharging into the Mississippi River on the eastern border of Arkansas.  The  drainage area in Arkansas alone is 11,180 square miles (AR NPS Pollution Assessment Update, 1991).  Along with the population, industry and agriculture are also centered along the Arkansas River, posing an environmental threat, despite the fact that they provide jobs and economic growth.  The environmental threat manifests itself as agricultural runoff and wastewater discharge from the farms and riverside industry, respectively.

In 1990, a fish consumption advisory was issued in April by the Arkansas Department of Health due to substantially high levels of dioxin in the fish caught in the northwestern region of the Arkansas River.  Dioxin is a carcinogenic hydrocarbon.  This means that it causes cancer and is composed of the atoms hydrogen and carbon. It is found in petroleum-derived herbicides, which are chemical substances used to destroy plants, and it is also found in pesticides, which are chemicals used to kill insects that feed on crops.  Farmers along the Arkansas River use these pesticides and herbicides to kill insects and weeds, respectively.  When it rains, the water washes the pesticides and herbicides off of the plants and they run down with the rest of the overland flow into the Arkansas River, where they mix with the water.  Fish living in the river absorb the water around them and whatever impurities it may contain.  When the fish absorb the dioxin, they carry it in their system until they are eaten by a predator.  When they are eaten, the fish passes on the dioxin to the predator when it is digested.  Although the dioxin may have been relatively harmless to the fish, the predator accumulates more and more dioxin with every fish that it eats.  The food becomes metabolized and excreted, but the dioxin remains behind.  Therefore, the higher up the food chain, the higher the concentrations of dioxin.  This process is called biomagnification.  Pesticides such as dioxin and DDT decrease the breeding capacity of animals such as fish and birds. When animals with pesticides in their systems lay eggs, proteins needed to harden the shells sufficiently are inhibited, rendering the eggs brittle and vulnerable to predators. It can also cause mutations at birth.  When ingested by humans, pesticides can cause liver problems, hyperpigmentation (a skin disorder) and even cancer.  

            Of all the pollutants found in water, the most prevalent are sulfates, chlorides, nitrogen, and phosphorus.  These chemicals pose the threat of eutrophication, which is when the chemicals in the water oxidize, or bond with the oxygen atoms, and use up so much oxygen that nothing can live in the water.

Sulfate....................................53mg/L

Chloride.................................110mg/L

Nitrogen.................................0.29mg/L

Phosphorus.............................0.09mg/L

(Source: Data from U.S. Geological Survey and Arkansas Department of Pollution Control and Ecology files.)

            Sulfate is found in rocks, runoff from mines, industrial wastewater, and may be deposited by the atmosphere from fossil fuel combustion.  Sulfates are atoms of sulfur which have bonded with other atoms or compounds, such as sodium, potassium, nitrogen, and ammonia.  Industries known to pollute waters with sulfates are tanneries, steel mills, sulfate pulp mills, and textile plants (DeZuane, 1997).   When the sulfate level reaches 250mg/L, a funny taste and odor are present in the water (USEPA, 1994).  At levels over 500mg/L, diarrhea and dehydration occur when the water is absorbed by the intestines.  Infants are especially susceptible due to their immature immune systems (NAS, 1997).  At 53mg/L, the sulfate concentration in the Arkansas River is above the natural level, but not high enough to be toxic (National Water Summary, 1990-91).  Sulfate concentrations have risen along the northwestern region of the Arkansas River(Arkansas Stream Water Quality Report, 1991). This is most likely due to the raw sewage being dumped from Fort Smith and Van Buren (ADPCE, pg. 130).

            Chlorides are compounds of chlorine.  They can be found in rocks, groundwater discharge, industrial wastewater, urban runoff, and in the atmosphere as air pollution.  The most common source is from road deicers—the salt applied to roads when they ice over.   A level of around 10mg/L is natural in water (DeZuane, 1997).  Chlorides are only a health risk to those who require a limited salt intake, such as people with heart disease or obesity, and pregnant women.  It becomes a threat to these people when the level reaches around 800mg/L, but its presence may be detected as a salty taste at around 500mg/L (DeZuane, 1997).  At 110mg/L, the chloride level of the Arkansas River is above the natural level, yet not enough to be toxic. This level has remained steady in recent years (Arkansas Stream Water Quality Report, 1991).

            Nitrogen combines with oxygen to form nitrite (NO2), and nitrate (NO3). Bacterial action on ammonia in raw sewage forms nitrites, which oxidize into nitrates upon contact with water.  Nitrates are also used in fertilizers.  When raw sewage is dumped into water, or fertilizers wash off of crops and into water when it rains, nitrate pollution occurs. The health effects of nitrates are still being determined (DeZuane, 1997).  The USEPA classifies nitrate/nitrite under “Group D”, or “inadequate data to classify” for a carcinogenic risk assessment.  It has been found to cause cancer in animals, and when ingested by humans it is known to oxidize with the hemoglobin in blood, which may cause heart problems, according to the NAS. The level of nitrates in the Arkansas River (0.29mg/L) is low enough to be considered not polluted, since the limit set forth by the USEPA is 10mg/L (DeZuane, 1997).

            Phosphorus is needed in the human diet and has a recommended daily allowance of 800mg (NRC, 1974).  Therefore, phosphorus is harmless to humans, but posses a threat to aquatic organisms.  When phosphorus gets into water, it nourishes algae, which consumes all the oxygen in the water, suffocating all other aquatic organisms. The most common sign of phosphorus pollution is the growth of a blue-green species of algae called Oscillatoria Rubescenes.  The most common sources of phosphorus pollution are fertilizers, which wash off the crops when it rains, urban runoff, and leaks from phosphorus mines.  Due to it not being a threat to humans, phosphorus has not been regulated by the USEPA (DeZuane, 1997).  They do however, make recommendations to control phosphorus, which is the most common cause of eutrophication.  Phosphorus levels should not exceed 0.05mg/L in a stream at a point where it enters a lake or river, and phosphorus levels should not exceed 0.1mg/L in streams that do not discharge directly into lakes or rivers, according to the USEPA (AR – OK Compact Commission Report, 1997).  The level of phosphorus in the Arkansas River, at 0.09mg/L, is higher than that recommended by the USEPA (0.05mg/L).  Since the majority of the land surrounding the Arkansas River is used for farming, this is most likely due to agricultural runoff of fertilizers (Arkansas Stream Water Quality Report, 1990-91).

            The Arkansas River can be considered polluted since the level of most of the above chemicals is above the acceptable levels set forth by the USEPA.  However, the levels are not yet toxic. Since the majority of the above chemicals have remained at steady levels (Arkansas Stream Water Quality Report, 1990-91), it is reasonable to conclude that the pollution of the Arkansas River is not getting worse.   However,  pollution must still be contaminating the river to maintain those levels, so it must not be getting any better.  Of all the surface water in Arkansas, 80% meets the water quality goals for fishing and swimming, while only 35% is safe for drinking (ADPCE, 1990).  According to the 1974 Arkansas Municipal Needs Survey, it would cost a total of $627,264,000 to construct and/or upgrade enough water treatment facilities to achieve compliance with the goals set forth by the Arkansas Department of Pollution Control and Ecology.  In order to control water pollution, attention must be brought to its effects and further research conducted into those effects and of possible methods of cleaning the pollution.
