Where did folk music come from and how did it diffuse to Arkansas?

“The first folk music was probably just a rhythm, a repetition of sounds by early man. One tribe may have had this genuinely grooooovie member who instead of saying “Oohg” when he pointed or grabbed at something started saying “Oohg-a-doog.” “Far out!” said the rest of the tribe, and pretty soon, like all good songs, it caught on, one tribesman passing it on to another, to the point where someone from the next tribe over the hill picked it up. When he came home that night and tried it on his wife, she probably bopped him on the head and said the equivalent of “Where did you learn that crazy riff?” Her husband probably replied, “Oh, it’s just a song the folks over the hill were singing.” Songs of the folks. Folk song.

-Brian Van Der Horst


Folk music has very distinct characteristics and plays a large role in revealing the heritages of various groups of people. Folk music is defined as “songs that truly express the hopes, fears, cares, concerns, and styles of living of a people” (Horst, 4). There are many types of folk music because of the various cultures all over the world. The different types of folk traditions in the United States have been brought here from different countries (Knott, File 2). The history of folk music began with the first settlers from Europe and eventually diffused to the rest of the United States. The type of folk music that has had the largest impact on Arkansas is American folk music. Jimmy Driftwood, who is known as the Father of Folk Music, was born and raised in Arkansas. “He came up with the idea for the Ozark Folk Center at Mountain View and helped establish the annual Ozark Folk Festival” (http://www.findarticles .com).

Folk music tells about the various experiences that different people have, which is why there are so many diverse types of folk music (Forcucci, 16). American folk music is comprised of different types of songs. For example, some songs are about the Civil War (Randolph, 199). Most people of the Ozarks have a lack of interest in talking about these songs due to the memories that are accompanied with them (Randolph, 200). This shows how closely related the songs are to the experiences and feelings of the people. Another type of song is the American Indian Song. “In any discussion of American folk music it would be unfair to omit the music of the American Indian, even though (in the final analysis) it did not play a significant role in determining the way of American folk music” (Forcucci, 31). The colonists were intrigued by the music of the Indians, but considered their own more advanced (Forcucci, 31). Indian song was passed down from father to son (Horst, 8). A third type of song is work songs. During Colonial times, people were constantly traveling and building new homes (Horst, 12). “They sang with the rhythm of their work to help them with their labors or to while away the time on their job” (Horst, 13). Folk music has proved to be influential in modern times. In the 1960’s, social change caused Civil Rights leaders to turn to folk music to express their feelings (Ferris, viii). “The folk music revival of the sixties also established a commercial market for protest folk singers such as Joan Baez and Bob Dylan” (Ferris, viii). There are many different types of American folk music. Some more examples are: Sea Chanties, Ballads, Spirituals, Blues, Pioneer Songs, and Western Music. Out of these various types of songs, the religious hymns had the largest impact on Folk Music (Horst, 12-17). Map I shows the diffusion of Protestant Religious Folk Songs throughout the United States. (See Appendix.)

The diffusion of folk music has been possible due to the oral tradition. Oral tradition means that music is passed on from generation to generation by word of mouth (Sandberg, ix). American folk music is a combination of British and Irish tunes (Nettl, 201). It has changed with American history and the development of American culture (Nettl, 201). “The frontier, the religious revival of the 1800s, the love of humor and exaggeration, the presence of various ethnic minorities, and the particular occupations in which Americans had to engage while building a new country”, influenced the development of American folk music (Nettl, 201). In order to understand the movement of folk music to Arkansas, some geographic terms need to be explained.

One term that should be defined is friction of distance. Friction of distance is the effort or cost required for ideas to travel from one place to another (Bergman and Renwick, 17). Illustration I shows the friction of distance through an isotopic plain. (See Appendix.) The friction of distance remains constant throughout. The friction of distance for folk music was the effort to spatially diffuse from Europe and other countries to and throughout the United States. Since there was a lack of transportation during the seventeenth century the friction of distance was high. “The seventeenth-century Antiquary John Aubrey once remarked that ‘before women were readers, the history of England was handed down from mother to daughter…’” (Hudson, 20). If it were not for the parents’ efforts to pass on traditions of their cultures, folk music would not have survived. The children use these songs to reflect on the history of past generations (Jackson, 269). With the advancement of transportation technology, such as airplanes, automobiles, and trains, the friction of distance was lowered. This made the diffusion of folk music easier. Communication technology, such as radios, the Internet, and recorded materials, also played a key role in the spread of folk music. These communication devices made folk music more accessible to a wider variety of people. Books were also an improvement in communications technology. They were used to record the stories of past cultures. In order for folk music to spread to the rest of America, the travelers had to put forth the effort and money to move and create homes for themselves and their families, which is an example of relocation diffusion. Relocation diffusion is when someone moves to a new location and takes their culture, customs, foods, etc…with them (Cossman).

Another term that should be explained is diffusion. Diffusion is the spread of different aspects of a culture from where they originate to other places (Bergman and Renwick, 203). There are three types of diffusion (Cossman). Relocation diffusion and contagious diffusion are shown in Illustration II. (See Appendix.) Contagious diffusion is passed when groups of people are close to one another and interact (Cossman). An example would be a drop of wine on a cloth (Gould, 93). The wine slowly spreads from where it was dropped throughout the rest of the cloth (Gould, 93). Folk music was passed from generation to generation by contagious diffusion. Hierarchical diffusion occurs when culture, customs, foods, etc…pass by jumping from one place to another (Gould, 93). An example would be the diffusion of fashion from New York to larger cities and then neighboring smaller cities (Gould, 93). Folk music was spread when the colonists moved to other regions of the United States. Diffusion does not always explain the exact origin of an idea or aspect because the idea could have originated in two different locations at the same exact time (Bergman and Renwick, 206). This explains the various types of folk music in the world today. The world contains many diverse cultures. Many of these cultures have folk music that probably originated around the same time period. These types of folk music are very different from each other due to the fact that the people are telling stories about the culture that they come from (Horst, 5). The songs that came to the United States from other cultures traveled with the people and were even created along the way (Lomax, vii). “Folk music came by ship with settlers from England, France, Spain, and all of the seafaring nations that colonized America in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries” (Horst, 6). Many of the settlers came to the United States in search of religious freedom (Horst, 9).

Folk music in the United States was first brought over by a group of Anglo-Saxons who were also known as Pilgrims (Forcucci, 25). “The Pilgrims were a religious group who had separated from the Church of England in order to pursue their own method of worship” (Forcucci, 25). The Pilgrims songs were derived from the Bible (Forcucci, 25). Along with their songs of worship, the Pilgrims also sang during their leisure time (Forcucci, 29). The Anglo-Saxons’ songs had an enormous influence on folk music (Forcucci, 30). As the settlers moved throughout the United States (relocation and contagious diffusion), they carried their folk music with them.

The movement of folk music throughout the United States began in the 1700’s, traveling “down” the Appalachian Mountains, then West and South (Bergman and Renwick 209). Folk music reached the state of Arkansas around the late 1800’s (Bergman and Renwick 209). The primary region of Arkansas known for folk music is the Ozarks. The Ozark region is shown in Map II. (See Appendix.) Folk music is a major aspect of the Ozark culture. The Ozarks are known for its “deep affection for its folk music and how the music has helped shape the character of the area” (Carney, 41). The Ozarks produced a famous folk musician by the name of Jimmy Driftwood. He was born on June 20, 1917, in Mountain View, Arkansas. “He gained his background in the music naturally from his upbringing in the Ozark Mountains (Stambler and Landon, 85). He received a B. S. in education from Arkansas State Teachers College in Conway in the early 1940s (Stambler and Landon, 85). He started his musical career playing at dances, picnics, and other events during his spare time. During his musical career he wrote around 6,000 folk songs and 300 were published or recorded (http://www.findarticles.com/). Driftwood went on to perform with the Grand Ole Opry in Nashville, Tenn., and on stages across the United States and Europe (http://www.findarticles.com/). Though he loved traveling and performing, he spent most of his life at the family farm, just west of Mountain View in North Arkansas’ Ozark Mountains.

Jimmy Driftwood established both the Ozark Folk Center at Mountain View and the Ozark Folk Festival (http://www.findarticles.com). Ozark folk festival sites are shown in Map III. Map IV shows the route from Conway to Mountain View. (See Appendix.) Starting in 1963, the Ozark Folk Festival is held in April every year. The main goal of the festival is to preserve the folk music heritage of the region. They use specific instruments such as “bass fiddles and drums” which pertain to the origin of folk music (Stambler and Landon, 11). Through the success of the Arkansas Folk Festival in 1966, Congress awarded approximately 1.4 million dollars for the development of the Folk Cultural Center of America located in Mountain View (Stambler and Landon, 11).

“Driftwood and his wife, Cleda, opened the Driftwood Barn near Mountain View to give him a permanent place to perform on weekends. He never charged admission but did pass the hat to help cover expenses” (http://www.findarticles.com). The barn was deeded to Arkansas State Teachers College, now the University of Central Arkansas, Conway, with the understanding that students would be able to study the history of folk music (http://www.findarticles.com). UCA no longer owns the barn. It is now deeded to the Racken Sack. The UCA Archives also contains the entire Jimmy Driftwood collection. Jimmy Driftwood died at the age of 91 in Fayetteville, Ar.

In conclusion, the many different types of people who have come to live in America have influenced American folk music. The diffusion of folk music from Europe to the United States and then throughout the United States was a long process due to the friction of distance. Folk music has, in many ways, shaped the way that people look at their heritage. Folk music tells about the past experiences of people and offers a look into their way of life.
