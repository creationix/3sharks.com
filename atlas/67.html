What are the water quality standards and chemical pollutants in Rivers in Arkansas?

     Our river systems are constantly being changed.  They are changed mainly by pollution, which in turn hurts humans and the river's inhabitants.  Pollution is a term applied to any environmental manifestation, which is defined as anything harmful or unpleasant to life (Calow, 3).  People everywhere like to have things clean, such as, the restaurants they eat in and the places they stay.  They expect the same from the water they drink.  The residents of Arkansas expect no less.  To ensure water safety for Arkansas, the local waters have to be tested.  When monitoring water quality in Arkansas the methods include testing chemical, physical, and biological data.  They are used to determine the present quality of the state’s water and to identify the sources of pollutants in the water.

 Water Quality Standards

                 Arkansas is in region six of the United States Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).  Region six is made up of the states of Arkansas, Louisiana, New Mexico, Oklahoma, and Texas.  Under the Clean Water Act states are required to submit a report every two years that includes the description of the water quality of all navigable waters within the state, an analysis of the protection and reproduction of the balance population

of wildlife in these waters, and an analysis of the extent of the elimination of the discharge of pollutants in the waters (http://www.epa.gov/).  This Act started in April of 1975.  These reports are to ensure water safety for Americans. 

                 The United States Geological Survey (USGS) provides technical and financial support to the Arkansas Water Resources Research Center; the center focuses on water quality issues related to animal waste in northern Arkansas and pesticides in eastern Arkansas (http://h2o.usgs.gov/).  The Arkansas Department of Pollution control and Ecology reported that sixty-two percent of there surveyed rivers and streams.  “Agriculture is the leading source of pollution in the State’s rivers and streams and has been identified as a source of pollution in four lakes (http://www.epa.gov/).”  Arkansas can identify land uses within each region and address the issues that threaten the water quality. 

            Chemical Pollutants

                 Even though drinking water in Arkansas is typically safe, there are a few threats to the safety of drinking water, such as chemical and microbial contamination.  Bacteria, viruses, and pathogens are examples of microbial contamination (http://www.epa.gov/).  Filtration and disinfecting can effectively remove microbiological contaminants from drinking water.  Chemical contaminants such as pesticides, herbicides, and petroleum can also have harmful effects on humans and wildlife.

                 Pesticides are substances used to destroy insects that are harmful to reaction.  Even though pesticides are supposed to be helpful, they can also interfere with the normal functioning of the reproductive system in humans and wildlife.  In Arkansas pesticides are uses to spray on crops such as soybeans and rice in order to kill insects that could hurt the growth of crops.  These pesticides can eventually get into the water system through drainage and run-off.  When the pesticides reach rivers and streams, they become a threat to humans and wildlife. 

                 Herbicides are chemicals used to destroy unwanted vegetation.  Like pesticides, herbicides are used to spray on crops, but herbicides are used to kill wild plants and weeds that can be harmful to the crops instead of insects that can injure crops.  Toxicity is very important when considering the effects of herbicides and organic chemicals on humans.  Both chronic and acute toxicities must be taken into account.  These materials may be taken into the body from air, food, or water by ingestion or absorption through the skin and residues in water, which may not be the source of the compound.  Many organic chemicals can make a bad taste and odor in water (Crompton, 495). The most obvious entry of herbicides into water is by direct application to the water in order to control aquatic vegetation (some may be sprayed directly on the water – other is runoff). 

                 Globally oil has become the most frequently encountered water pollutant, and oil seepage is becoming more frequent.  Oil pollution has harmful effects on aquatic life and aesthotic appearance of inland waters.  Wherever oil is produced, stored, and transported, there is a potential source of oil pollution, either directly by surface drains and runoffs, or indirectly by absorption of the ground (Crompton, 1).  Oil surverely damages the recreational useage of the body’s of water, and the inhabitants.  Sediment oil in a lake or river can act as carriers of toxic organic chemicals.  Heating fuels, due to it’s widespread industrial and domestic utilization, and diesel fuels are the most commonly ocurring oil pollutants (Crompton, 2).

                        Since our river systems are constantly being changed by pollution, and the quality of water in Arkansas is an important part of daily life for its residents, everything possible is done in order to ensure the safety of water for both humans and wildlife.   The processes for ensuring safe water include testing the local waters for harmful chemical, physical, and biological data.  If this process is done affectively, then Arkansans don’t have to worry about the quality of water in our state and can feel at ease when using the local waters.  The national and state requirements for the water testing make the purification of our water possible.
