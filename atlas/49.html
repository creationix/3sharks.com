How are humans, livestock, and vegetation of Arkansas effected by extreme climate conditions?

 

            When talking about weather it is difficult to learn all the intricate factors that are involved with weather.  Climate is weather and other factors combined into one.  Climate by definition is long term average weather conditions in a place or region.  Certain climate conditions, temperatures, and other determinants affect a particular climate in a given region, dependent upon the season.  Sometimes these conditions become extreme and can drastically change the climate, causing unusual occurrences to transpire.  These factors are going to be explored and explained how they affect humans, livestock, and crops that inhabit Arkansas. 

              The first extreme climate condition discussed will be temperature and it is effects.  The hottest temperatures on Earth are found between 15 and 35 degrees latitude, which is from the southern most part of Mexico to the most northern point of Oklahoma.  Arkansas is between 30 North and 40 North degrees latitude.  Arkansas receives a reasonable amount of heat during the year compared to the rest of the United States.  Arkansas’ average temperature during the summer is around 92 degrees Fahrenheit.  The average humidity of the state is around 79% all year long.  Humidity is the amount of water droplets in the air and how saturated the air is with these water droplets.  These heat conditions can cause humans to have heat exhaustion or dehydration only if they exert themselves outdoors requiring them to sweat a lot of body water.  The highest recorded temperature in Arkansas was 120 degrees at Ozark on August 10, 1936.  Maximum temperatures in the state can exceed 100 degrees in July and August (Leeden 78). 

These extreme temperatures and lack of humidity can cause droughts which in turn affect humans, livestock, and crops.  If a drought occurs than humans have little water to perform everyday activities such as bathing, cooking, maintaining hydration, etc.  (Zion Nat. Park 2).  In a drought water is a precious resource that is used on absolute need only, like for crops, humans and livestock to keep them alive and well quenched.  The worst droughts ever recorded in Arkansas were in 1930, 1954, and 1963 because of extreme heat and lack of humidity in those particular years.  Arkansas is the third largest national producer of rice, fourth largest producer of cotton, and fifth largest producer of soybeans.  Crops are needed to maintain proper food supply to cattle and turkeys, which have a national influence for milk and meat (Lydolph 64).  Arkansas’ Tyson company ships chicken and turkey all across the country and are a major source of chicken and turkey products.  Cattle is a major industry in Arkansas also, they produce about 24 percent of southeastern United States beef and other cattle products.  During these severe drought years, these statistics dropped drastically and livestock had little food and water.  In 1930, the cattle population dropped 22%, and the turkey and chicken populations dropped 14%.  In 1954 and 1963, the livestock population only dropped 6 to 7 percent due to the minor conditions of the droughts.  The droughts only lasted about 6 to7 weeks and the effects were light compared to the drought of 1930.  The soils of the lowlands of Arkansas could not produce sufficient amounts of crops to keep the economy running properly.  This affected many farmers and other merchants.  Extreme heat can also cause forests to dry out and eventually create forest fires.  This is part of natures way to thin out forests from time to time and help clean up the environment.  Dryness during drought periods adds to this process.  This affect causes many problems do to prior problems with shortages of water and also property destruction.  Extreme heat conditions happen only a few times a century, but when they arrive they cause severe problems and destruction (Coffey 2).

            Extremely cold weather happens so rarely in Arkansas, that the aftermath is  slightly noticeable.  The coldest recorded temperature was in Pond, Arkansas (which is near Hot Springs, AR) on February 13th, 1905 at 29 degrees Fahrenheit below zero.  The winters are short and only document a few zero temperatures.  Snow is a scarce occurrence in the southern areas of Arkansas and totals from 2 to 12 inches a winter in the northern regions.  The northern regions receive most of the snow because temperatures there are cold enough to freeze the water droplets in the Jet Stream, which is the belt of strong winds that blows weather fronts through North America.  Snow in these southern regions is light and only remains of the ground for a brief time.  If the weather gets cold enough for a good freeze, then crops can be damaged severely.  This results in little productivity of major cash crops and thus creating a slump in Arkansas’ economy (Leeden 93).

            Abnormal precipitation can and does occur in Arkansas.  About 100 days out of the year have measurable precipitation.  Abnormal precipitation would be considered as more than 38 inches a year.  Rainfall is very abundant and is spatially rectified throughout the year for crops and other agricultural production.  The most rainfall occurs from March through May in Arkansas (Green 35).  The averages are between 2 to 3 inches for monthly totals.  The state is also subject to heavy rainfall and storms can produce an excess of 10 inches per storm.  This causes floods mainly on the White, Black and Ouachita Rivers where major natural rivers and lake are formed.  Catastrophic floods are rare but in 1927, the great flood covered one-fifth of the state and spread over the soils of the lowlands.  Every seven years or so, Arkansas’ climate can be affected by El NiÔo and excess rainfall transpires (Erwin 1).

This chart shows how the water off the West coast of Peru warms up extensively from its normal temperature.  This phenomenon is known as El NiÔo.  The dark red regions are extremely warm surface water during November, 1997.  El NiÔo is the result of warming water just off the West Coast of Peru.  The surface of the water raises about 8 degrees Celsius.  El NiÔo was first discovered by fisherman who noticed the ocean was warming and there was an abundance of anchovies.  The name El NiÔo literally means The Christ Child due to its relative closeness to Christmas.  El NiÔo exaggerates seasonal conditions in Northern America.  Thus, in Arkansas, the winter is a little colder and has more precipitation.  During El NiÔo, the trade winds relax in the central and western Pacific.  El NiÔo adversely affects Australia by causing drought and it weakens the trade winds.  El NiÔo affects Arkansas by creating more rain and consequently Arkansas’ agriculture becomes more plentiful and the economy flourishes (COAPS 3). 

            This is a chart of La NiÔa.  Opposite to El NiÔo is La NiÔa, which causes a cool and dry climate for Arkansas.  This climate is an exaggeration of the usual seasonal conditions and causes a lull in Arkansas’ flourishing agricultural production.  La NiÔa precedes El NiÔo and is the cooling of ocean surfaces off the West Coast of Peru.  The water cools and the winds migrate to the Western portion of the Pacific.  This causes rainfall for Australia and Indonesia (COAPS 4). 

            Though these anomalies are extreme and only occur about every seven years, the normal seasonal weather can be extreme at times and can affect Arkansas greatly.  Arkansas is real close to Tornado Alley, which is where most all tornadoes in the world occur.  Tornadoes are the most violent of all weather phenomena.  A funnel cloud forms downward from low-hanging cumulonimbus clouds, that often produce thunderstorms, and a small vortex is created.  Most tornadoes are less than 400 meters in diameter, which yields high wind speeds, perhaps as much as 500 miles per hour.  At the surface of the Earth the vortex of air moves inward toward the center and then upwards.  This affect can lift large objects such as cars and giant portions of houses.  Tornadoes leave paths of destruction behind them having no prejudice as to what it destroys.  A typical United States tornado moves in a northeastward motion, moving at an average of 45 miles per hour (Hanchett 92).  Arkansas is frequently hit by tornadoes causing millions of dollars of destruction and leaving people and animals homeless.  They cost the taxpayers innumerable amounts of money each time they hit and destroy irreplaceable items, such as historical buildings or memorable artifacts in Arkansan’s homes.  Three fourths of tornadoes occur during the months between April and June.  Though these months are when most tornadoes occur, tornadoes can occur anytime of the year, as Central Arkansas recently discovered in January of 1999.  The series of storms and tornadoes destroyed many buildings and left many homeless.  They also cost the state of Arkansas around 64 million dollars for repairs (USGCRP 2). 

            Tornadoes and wind speeds can be outrageous and are definitely something to fear.  The highest recorded wind speeds occur generally in the middle latitudes, between Peru, South America and Mexico, North America.  The highest wind speeds known to man are found in tornadoes; estimated to be around 500 miles per hour.  The highest measured surface wind speeds in Arkansas have been recorded atop Mt. Magazine in the Ozark mountain range at 2,823 feet, where the wind reached a speed of 108 miles per hour.  The lush vegetation of Arkansas and high surface temperatures are conductive to constant wind.  These winds are most prominent in the daylight hours. These winds create a semi-desirable environment for livestock and vegetation in Arkansas (Threwartha 74). 

            All these extreme climate conditions affect human, livestock, and vegetation in everyday life whether we notice them or not.  Some extremes affect Arkansas in a drastic way and can affect how we live, with better or less efficiency.  Over time, we have learned to manipulate the climate with levy’s or drainage areas, but nature is too strong and too overbearing even for humans.  There is still much to learn about climate conditions in Arkansas and hopefully we can better prepare ourselves for those times.  When extreme climates hit Arkansas, we will just have to “weather” the storm.
