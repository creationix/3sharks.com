How did relion diffuse into Arkansas?

B2: Human Geography: Religion 

“The History of Religion in Arkansas”

By Jami Battles, Blake Cook, and Ryan Hoover

Principals of Geography:Geog 1305, Section 2522

Prepared:December 3, 1998

 

How did religion diffuse into Arkansas?

            How did religion come into in Arkansas?  It wasn’t just here when different people came and settled the land.  Different religions were introduced into Arkansas through spatial diffusion; this is the process by which a concept, practice, innovation, or substance spread from its point of origin to new territory (Getis, Getis, &Fellmann 1998: 243).

            The Indians living in Arkansas when the first white men came were members of the Dakota family; this great family of Indians was made up of 12 tribes which came from the East into what is now Arkansas, Missouri, Iowa, Minnesota, Kansas, and Nebraska.  The three main tribes in Arkansas were the Quapaws, Caddo, and Osages, but there is not much information on the religion of any of these groups except the Quapaws (McKnight&Johnson 1955: 27).     

            Like many Indian tribes, the Quapaws believed in a supernatural spirit that permeated everything around them, from rocks, wind, and vegetation to animals and people, and occupied the superior position in their view of life (Davis 1991: 96).  This type of belief that life, or spirits exist in all things is called Animism (Getis, Getis, &Fellmann 1998: 260).  The supernatural spirit  was identified with the sun and called Wah Kon Dah, an name derived for the root, Wa Kan, meaning sacred, mysterious, sacred thing.  In colonial travelers’ accounts, this supernatural force was referred to as the Great Manitou, or Great Spirit (Davis 1991: 96).

            Along with the great forces found in all things, every Quapaw family had a guardian spirit, or manitou, and religious rites were conducted by a group known as Wapinan, the Holy Men, and shamans (Davis 1991: 97).  Therefor, the Quapaw religion, along with many other Indian tribes, was known as Shamanism; it is the religion in which a shaman, through special power, can interpret the spirit world (Getis, Getis, & Fellman 1998: 260).  French writers used the word jongleur, meaning juggler or trickster, for the shaman who in English is called a medicine man.  One writer, Bossu, stated that the jongleurs “performed duties of priests, physicians, and surgeons and want to be regarded as soothsayers.”  They employed sleight-of-hand to mystify and impress spectators when they treated patients with herbal concoctions, drenching, and sweat baths (Davis 1991: 97).

            When the white men came into Arkansas they brought with them Christianity.  Christianity has its basis in the life and teachings of Jesus, a Jewish preacher in the 1st century of the modern era, who was believed to be the messiah promised by God.  Its main belief is salvation for all humankind (Getis, Getis,& Fellmann 1998: 262).  The white men wanted to convert the Indians to Catholicism, a type of Christianity, but the task was difficult, if not impossible.  The first expedition into Arkansas was led by Hernando de Soto.  The king of Spain had appointed him as governor of Cuba and Florida; so, after his expedition into Cuba, De Soto landed in Florida in 1539.  For his first two years, De Soto traveled through Florida, Alabama, Georgia, and Missouri.  He crossed into Arkansas in 1541, where he found the Indian tribes usually friendly and willing to share their food with him, but the Spaniards were often cruel to the natives, causing setbacks in relations with the natives.  De Soto was eager to find gold in Arkansas, and Christianity was an advantage in getting cooperation from the Indians, possibly in getting information on the location of gold.  De Soto had his men erect a cross, told the Indians the story about Jesus, and asked them to worship him.  Some natives believed that De Soto himself was Jesus, or some kind of god.  During the dry summer, De Soto was asked to make it rain; a religious service was held and prayer was offered, and soon it rained, with the Indians believing more than ever that De Soto was a god.  However, De Soto never found gold, and Spanish Catholicism did not spread (McKnight & Johnson 1955: 37-38).

            It was 131 years before any white men came into Arkansas again.  In 1663, the governor of Quebec sent Father Marquette, a missionary of the French Catholic Church, and Louis Joliet, a rich merchant, to determine if there was a water route to the Pacific in the New World.  The explorers were unable to get any information about the Mississippi River or the body of water that it emptied into (McKnight & Johnson 1955: 40-41).  Fortunately, they were able to get some hospitality and information from the Akamsea, a Quapaw tribe, after Father  Marquette showed them the calumet, or peace pipe, that he had received from the Illinois Indians upstream.  The pipe was sacred to the Indians throughout the Mississippi Valley because they looked upon it as the calumet of the sun and offered it to the latter to smoke when they wanted a calm, rain, or fair weather.  Because Father Marquette had the calumet, the Akamsea held him in high regards and indicated to him that they wanted him to return and teach them his faith (Davis 1991: 95-96).

            Then in 1682, La Salle and Henri de Tonti arrived in Arkansas and visited the same people that Marquette and Joliet had visited ten years earlier.  They planted a cross, symbolizing the French Catholic Church, and claimed all of the land for France.  When La Salle died on a later expedition De Tonti went searching for him, and established the first white settlement, the Arkansas Post, in 1686.  De Tonti granted the Catholic Church a large tract of land and paid a priest to serve the Post and his Indian friends nearby.  Father Allouey, the great missionary to the Huron Indians, probably was the first priest to come to Arkansas Post under this arrangement; regular visits were held among the Indians also (McKnight & Johnson 1955: 44). 

            Arkansas Post was a profitable center of trade, and along with the traders and trappers came the priests, eager to preach to the Indians, undergoing suffering and death in the process.  Father Fouccault was received by the Quapaws in 1700, they expected him to establish a permanent mission, but he was killed by two Koroa Indian guides in 1702, supposedly for trying to leave Arkansas (Morris 1991: 88).  Then, Father Paul du Poisson planned to become a permanent resident of Arkansas Post.  He learned the Quapaw language and asked that pictures be sent from France because the Indians loved the pictures of St. Regis (Hall 1941: 89).  Unfortunately, he was murdered in an Indian massacre at Natchez in 1729; the Natchez burst into the chapel he was praying at and cut his head off with a hatchet.  Father Carette, who took up the post  in 1750, left in 1758 due to not having a chapel and the "bad conduct and freedom of language" of his parishioners.  The French never sent a replacement (Morris 1991: 89).

            The French were forced to give all of their Louisiana Territory to Spain in 1762 to keep the English from getting it after the French and Indian War; Spain had a great commitment of furthering Christianity, Catholicism, in the New World.  However, conversion was focused on the white settlers migrating into Arkansas instead of the Indians.  The number of priest were very small until settlements became big enough to support parishes.  After O'Reilly took command, he wrote a memorandum outlining the religious needs of the territory and where he planned to send priests, but he did not even mention Arkansas; it was too remote and too small (Morris 1991: 90).  Arkansas Post Catholics were included in those that the priest of St. Genevieve, Missouri, served after the parish there opened.  However, the 250 mile distance between the settlements and hardships of the travel limited the visits of the priest.  Finally, a Parish of Arcanzas was established in 1796, and its pastor, Pere Pierre Janin, erected a chapel in honor of St. Stephen.  Three years later the pastor left for St. Louis, and the parish was once again dependent upon irregular visits from priests (Davis 1991: 99-101).

            During French control, the only immigration into Arkansas was French from Canada or from lower Louisiana who were devoted to the Catholic Church; however, when the Spanish took control, immigration and diversity increased.  Several Protestant Anglo-American families were allowed to move to Arkansas Posts in 1778.  Protestants were required to convert to Catholicism when they settled in Spanish Territory, but in a few years King Carlos III allowed Protestants to practice their doctrine in private, although their infants would have to be baptized in Catholic faith.  Also, when performing a marriage between a Catholic and Protestant, the priest was not allowed to wear vestements (Davis 1991:101-102).  In 1811, John P. Carnahan, a Cumberland Presbyterian lay preacher, preached the first Protestant sermon at Arkansas Post.  His sermon must have been frustrating since he only spoke English, while most at the post still spoke French.  Eight years later, Timothy Flint, a Congregational missionary and author, conducted Sunday services at the post in French, but he stated that while the people were attentive and polite, the services were not actually taken seriously (Hall 1941: 83-84). 

            Other Christian denominations soon spread throughout Arkansas as the number of settlements and migrants increased.  For example, William Stevenson, a Methodist preacher, established a few Methodist societies along the Red River in 1814.  He became a circuit rider and others soon followed.  The circuit rider created congregations on settlements and recruited local farmers as unpaid preachers.  The Methodists asked only that a member have a personal conversion experience and moral life, in which every man could save himself through good works.  In addition to the Methodists, the Missouri Bethel Baptist Association sent Reverend James Phillip Edwards to organize a Baptist Church, near Little Rock, in 1817.  They believed that preachers should be unpaid farmers or businessman who had been called by God to preach.  Members watched over each other, and charges of public offenses meant expulsion from the church (Tucker 1985: 14-15). 

            Despite efforts made by missionaries over the years to convert the Indians to Christianity, the Quapaws remained attached to their old ways.  During the Quapaw march to the Red River country after giving up their land to the United States in 1824, the Quapaws sacrificed an orphan, carried figures of their manitous, performed ritualistic dances, and shamans treated the sick (Davis 1991: 104).  However, some tribes were more willing to convert.  The Protestants were able to convert some of the Cherokees of the area because they were more willing to adapt to Anglo-American ways.  Overall though, the Indians took Shamanism with them when they left Arkansas. 

            Different types of Christianity spread throughout Arkansas, through the process of diffusion, beginning with Catholicism and a few other denominations.  Although all of the denominations still exist in Arkansas, along with other types of religion, Baptists and Methodists have become the predominant types today.
