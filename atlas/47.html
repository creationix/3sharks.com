What Is El Nino and How Does It Affect Weather in Arkansas?

 

            If you don’t like the weather in Arkansas, wait a minute because it will change.  Arkansas is known for its diversity of weather.  But what causes the weather in Arkansas?  And what affects it?  El Nino, Spanish for the child Jesus, is a phenomenon that affects global weather and thus affects the weather in Arkansas.  This project will cover El Nino and its affect on weather, especially weather in Arkansas.

What Is El Nino?

            What is El Nino?  El Nino is a buildup of water that is warmed up along South America’s west coast.  This warming occurs periodically, ranging from three to eight years.  Normally, cold waters called the Humboldt current reside off the Peruvian coast.  But when this cool current is replaced by warmer waters, plankton levels and fish supply fall.  Fishermen in this area of the Pacific first noticed this decrease in fish back in the 17th century.  The warming trend particularly took place around Christmastime every three to four years (www.coaps.fsu.edu) 

            During El Nino, the trade winds relax in the central and western Pacific leading to a

depression of the thermocline in the eastern Pacific, and an elevation of the thermocline in the

west.  For example, the map showing 110W (figure 1), shows that during 1982-1983, the 17-degree isotherm dropped to nearly 150 meters in depth.  This made the upwelling unable to cool the surface and cut off the rich supply of water to the euphotic zone.  Resulting was a huge decline in primary productivity and a rise in sea surface temperature.  This affects the higher trophic levels of the food chain adversely, which includes commercial fisheries.  One can see in the figure that easterly tradewinds weaken during El Nino.  Rainfall follows the warm water eastward, causing flooding in South America and droughts in Indonesia and Australia.  The displacement to the east of heat in the atmosphere lying over the warmest water causes circulation in the atmosphere of the globe.  This forces change in weather in areas a long way from the tropical Pacific (www.pmel.noaa.gov)

            Between El Nino is a time when cold water peaks.  This period is called La Nina.  These two periods together are known as southern oscillation.  Each of these occurrences can vary in strength with the 1982-83 and the 1997-98 events recorded as the strongest El Ninos on record.  Torrential storms were recorded throughout the southwest United States, while Australia suffered under the worst drought this century (www.pmel.noaa.gov).

            For many years, the phenomena now named El Nino was noticed off the coast of Peru, and its effects were recorded but there was no explanation for the unusual warming of the waters.  The coast waters in that region were normally cool.  This cool water is nutrient rich, supporting high levels of primary productivity, diverse marine ecosystems, and productive fisheries.  Every year during the first couple of months, a warm southward current warmed the waters.  But every few years, this warming started early, as early as December and was far stronger and could last as long as two years.  The biggest problem was the fact that the warm water covered the cooler waters, and that was devastating to the region’s marine life.

 

 

 

Effects of El Nino

            El Nino is a disruption of the ocean-atmosphere system in the tropical Pacific having important consequences for weather around the globe.

            Increased rainfall across the southern states in the United States and in Peru, flooding caused by this rain, and droughts in Australia often associated with fires are just a few consequences of El Nino.

            The impacts of El Nino upon climate in temperate latitudes show up most clearly during wintertime.  For example, most El Nino winters are mild over western Canada and parts of the northern United States, and wet over the southern United States from Texas to Florida.  El Nino affects temperate climates in other seasons as well.  But even during wintertime, El Nino is only one of a number of factors that influence temperate climates.  El Nino years, therefore, are not always marked by “typical” El Nino conditions the way they are in parts of the tropics. 

            El Nino brings about changes in the temperature and precipitation in Arkansas.  During fall months, Arkansas experiences greater amounts of precipitation than in non-El Nino years (see figures 3-6).  The winter and spring months are characterized by more precipitation than usual.  However, the summer months during an El Nino year will be dry and hot.  The Southwest and the South-central regions have recorded more than double the normal precipitation totals over the past 102 years (1895-1997 Climate Prediction Center, NOAA).  With increased precipitation comes many other changes and problems.  Tornadoes are a constant threat in Arkansas and are more frequent and more severe during an El Nino Event.  However, the evidence is not conclusive due to the fact that the temperature is becoming warmer in general.  There was a record heat event the summer of 1998 with temperatures of more than 100 F.  The growing season was devastated due to the excess heat and lack of precipitation.  It seems that El Nino causes large fluctuations swinging back and forth between no rain and excess heat to large rain events, flooding land and crops.  The chicken industry, a significant industry in Arkansas, suffered the summer of 1998 due to extreme heat.  Chicken growers were not prepared for this and thus lost a great majority of their income due to the suffocation of large numbers of chickens.  The inability to predict the arrival and strength of an El Nino event has been one of the most unfortunate aspects of this phenomena.  Significant improvement in the observation and prediction of these events has been made in the past several years, with the array of buoys moored throughout the Pacific Ocean.  These two events, El Nino and La Nina, have caused changes in weather so drastic that lives and property have been lost.  But since there is no way to control these events, the answer is in the predictions.  Arkansas, along with other areas and regions globally, has felt the devastation El Nino can bring.

            Obviously, southern oscillation is not the only factor to factor into predicting weather.  Still, El Nino with its warm waters can really alter temperature, precipitation, and wind patterns from their norm.  It is really amazing to see that a change in water temperatures off the west coast of South America due to a change in direction of prevailing winds can affect the weather of the entire globe.
