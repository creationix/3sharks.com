Article # 18
 
 

"Where do the names and images of college mascots come from?"

B2:  Cultural Attributes:  Mascots

"The Importance and Origins of mascots in the South"

By Laura Lyons, Kevin McClain, Amber McNamar,Brandee Moss, Matt Reese, & Carmen Winslow

Mascots R Us

Principles of Geography:  Geog 1305, Section A

Prepared:  November 21, 2000

 

“Where do the names and images of college mascots come from?  How do they get their ideas on what kind of mascot to have?  Is there a historical, geographical,  or cultural reason?”

 

            The true battles between colleges rage in athletic competition, and no single entity represents a college more than the mascot, whether he’s rallying the troops on the football field or proudly emblazoned on letterhead stationary (Stiefel 1999).  Mascots are chosen in an effort to show pride in heritage.    In the Southern states, like Arkansas, symbols of the Old South and antebellum South are extremely popular.  Examples of this would be the Confederate Soldier, Johnny Reb, who is a common mascot around southern areas (The School Mascot Controversy, Chalk Talk).  Animals common to the area are also used, like the University of Central Arkansas Bear.  Now, for public relations reasons and to avoid conflicts with minorities, colleges are moving to adopting an animal as a mascot rather than mascots that represent, or misrepresent, people (Shea 1993).  However, Native American symbols like Redskins, Braves, Redmen, and Savages are a common theme for mascots throughout the United States, but they are more prominent in the South.  Native American cultural practices, Indian dress, weapons, artifacts, and music are used to boost enthusiasm for the team and the entire school. The Atlanta Braves tomahawk chop makes  for an excited and more unified crowd of fans.  Traditions like mascots, logos, flags, and songs provide a feeling of inclusiveness and pride (The Tomahawk Chop).

            The ten most common mascots in the United States are the Eagles, Tigers, Cougars, Bulldogs, Warriors, Lions, Panthers, Indians, Wildcats, and Bears.  These mascots are chosen typically because they are perceived as savage, powerful, and wild.  They are also a reflection of the area’s heritage and what is regionally common.  Colleges compete for the reputation of machismo and muscle in the criteria of a mascot.  Mascots are also selected precisely because of their coercive and predatory images.  Such attributes of violence, dominance, and power are considered and preferred over compassion, cooperation, and individuality (Segrave 1997).

Most of the students attending the University of Central Arkansas realize that the bear is the schools mascot.  But many students and professors are clueless when it comes to the question of how UCA acquired this animal for a mascot.  First, one must acknowledge both UCA’s history and the history of the state of Arkansas itself.

            In the early nineteen hundreds, the University of Central Arkansas went by a different name.  It was called Arkansas Normal College or Arkansas State Teachers College.  “The Bear, as a symbol, came officially to the normal in 1920” (Worley 33).   In November of that year, Sam A. Huffman, an art student, created a new design for the senior class rings.  He incorporated the bear in the seal and it became official when approved by the board of trustees.  “The Bear was an appropriate symbol for an Arkansas school because the nickname of the state was the Bear State.”  Athletic teams were first called Bears in print April 7, 1921 in a Log Cabin report of a baseball game” (Worley 33).  Writers welcomed and preferred the change of name to Bears over previous names such as “Normalites,” “Pedagogues,” “Teachers,” and “Pea-Pickers.”

            Although the new mascot, the Bear, seemed more convenient, it did have a few drawbacks.  A live bear as a mascot was not very practical, and the stone bear attracted green shades of paint, the rival school, Arkansas Tech’s color during times of competition.  For several years, however, a live bear belonging to L.S. Dunaway was used.  “Big Boy” was paraded around at Estes Field and taken up to Russellville for Tech games (Worley 34).  In 1947, permission of the board was given for a solid, gray bear to stand guard directly in front of the main entrance of the administration building.  The University of Cental Arkansas Alumni Association further states, “Strength and justice are illustrated by the institution’s mascot, the Bear” (http://www.uca.edu/alumni/member.htm).

There are many different universities in the state of Arkansas, and each has it’s own very unique mascot.  Harding University, Arkansas Tech, and Henderson State University are all in the Gulf South Conference with the University of Central Arkansas.  This means that the Bears oppose teams with mascots like the Bisons, Wonders Boys, Golden Suns, and Reddies (Gulf 2000:1).  Each of these mascots have an interesting history. 

According to Scott Goode, the sports information director at Harding University, students attending the university chose the bison as their mascot in 1924.  At that time, it was grammatically acceptable to spell the word with the "s".  It was during this year that Harding University moved from Morrilton to Searcy.  The students most likely chose this mascot because there were still quite a few buffalo in Arkansas at this time.

The teams of Henderson State University are known as "Reddies".  There are many theories about the origin of this name, but it most likely came from the jersey color.  Early teams were referred to as "Red Jackets" and "Red Men" before Nellie Hartsgeld officially named the football team the "Red Jackets."  By 1908, reporters had shortened this name to "Reds."  Eventually, this name turned into the present-day mascot of the "Reddies"  (Home 2000:  1).

According to Larry Smith, the director of media services at Arkansas Tech, the name "Wonder Boys" originated in 1909.  Tech’s football team upset the Jonesboro Aggies, 14-0.  The Aggies had claimed the state championship before this game.  John Tucker, the 17-year-old quarterback, led his team to an awesome victory.  Henry Loesch, who was a sports editor of the Arkansas Gazzette at the time, was the first person to call Tech the "Wonder Boys" because of this game.  This led to the girls’ teams being called the "Wonderettes."  In 1975, the girls decided to vote on a new name.  The female athletes chose the "Golden Suns" as their mascot.

While the Southern states’ school symbolize animal-like figures, it seems that more schools and colleges in the north have mascots that are not animal-like. Northern mascots seem to resemble or represent fighting, warrior-like symbols. Despite these differences there is something similar about northern and southern mascots. It is the fact that while the mascots are different in meaning and figure, they are non-humanistic in outlook. Some southern school mascots are: Kansas Jayhawks, Arkansas Razorbacks, Texas Longhorns, Georgia Bulldogs, Florida Gators, Kentucky Wildcats, and South Carolina Gamecocks. Another example of using animals as mascots are the southern schools that use tigers to represent them. Such schools are the Louisiana State Tigers, Missouri Tigers, and Auburn Tigers. The northern schools seem to focus more on the fighting and war-like figures for mascots. Some examples are: Illinois Fighting Illini, USC's Trojans, Purdue Boilermakers, Michigan State Spartans, Ohio State Buckeyes, and the Notre Dame Fighting Irish. These schools are examples of fighting mascots and are non-animal-like. The Northern schools listed above show that the North has different types of mascots than the southern schools. However, there are exceptions to this rule.  There are also northern schools that have animal mascots, while some southern schools have non-animal figures, but the majority stay true to norm.

Schools around the nation have come up with their mascots in many different ways.  Some school mascots and symbols have a direct relation with the region’s history or tradition, while others may have nothing to do with the history and tradition of that region and are just to rally spirit.  An example of this is the way Notre Dame came about to find their mascot.  They were first called the “Catholics” in the eighteen hundreds, but in the years of the four horsemen they were renamed the “Ramblers.”  Years later, in 1927, Notre Dame officially became know as the “Fighting Irish.”  No one is completely certain on how the school got this nickname, but there are many different stories circulating that try to explain the change.  These stories range anywhere from a teammate using that name as an inspirational pep talk to other teammate before a game, to a rival calling Notre Dame the “Fighting Irish” as an insult.  Another school that got its nickname completely out of the blue is Michigan State.  Their mascot was chosen by a contest to find a new mascot for the school.  This person who came up with “Spartans” did not win the contest because he was not well liked by the rest of the community.  After a few years the school’s president looked over the contest again and decided the name “Spartans” was the most suitable.

The University of Illinois was one school that got its nickname from history and tradition.  The school’s mascot is named an Illini.  It got this nickname from a local Indian chief by the name of Illiniwek who was the leader of a loose confederation of Algonquin tribes.  His name means “they are men,” and former Illinois coach Robert Zuppke believes this is where the “Fighting Illini” got their name.
