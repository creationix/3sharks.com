How does the Mississippi River and its tributaries affect geological formations, soil

 distribution, and soil structure in the bottomlands and terraces of eastern Arkansas?

            The focus of this project will be isolated to a region of Arkansas known as the bottomlands and terraces of eastern Arkansas (See attached map 1b). These terraces begin near Pulaski County and spread eastward to the eastern borders of our state. Small areas such as these are found in the southwestern part of Arkansas near the Red River and in central Arkansas near the Arkansas River. This region is approximately 7,300,000 acres(United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1982: Map). The soil of this area is composed of alluvium. Alluvium is soil material, such as sand, silt, or clay, deposited by flowing water (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1975:63). In eastern Arkansas, alluvium is deposited in formations known as terraces (See attached diagram 1c). Terraces are geological formations above flood plains that are formed by flowing water (Greta Haustein, Sept. 29,1999). They are old alluvial plains, usually flat or with small ridges. Terraces are always near rivers or lakes. Eastern Arkansas exemplifies the phenomena of terrace formation well. This region is a flood plain known as the Mississippi River Delta. The waters of the Mississippi and its tributaries flooded in this area leaving behind terrace formations. The bottomlands and terraces are prime examples of geological land formation by water and soil (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1975:65). The soil is carried in the form of silt and deposited by the rivers.

            The bottomlands and terraces consist of alluvial plains and low terraces. Elevations of these areas range from 100 to 400 feet above sea level. The soils in these areas are a mixture of deep clayey, loamy, or sandy alluvial sediments (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1982:Map). Clay and sand are categorized by size. Clay is the smallest of all soil particles, while sand is the largest of all soil particles. Clay settles slower in water because of its weight; likewise, sand settles the fastest. Loam is nutrient rich sediment.

            The topography or terrain of a region affects the way in which sediment is deposited. “Differences in slope determines the amount of runoff water as well as the degree of erosion and sedimentation that takes place” (Beyrouty, Hanson, Slaton 1996:20). The topography of this area helps  induce the formation of terraces and flood plains. The slope of the land encourages certain formations to form when water is present. Certain conditions have to be just right to produce terraces and flood plains. Due to its location near major rivers these areas have been historically subject to flooding and production of terraces.

            The flooding of the bottomlands makes the land useful for agricultural purposes. If this flooding process is stopped, not only are the natural formations lost but we also lose the replenishment of fertile soil. The Mississippi River dumps nutrient rich sediments on this land during floods. Without floods, farming would not be as fruitful in the bottomlands.

            The soils of the bottomlands and terraces are important in agricultural production. Most of these areas are used for crop land.  “Important crops include cotton, grain sorghum, rice, soybeans, and wheat. A few areas are also used for pasture and hay land” (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1982: Map). Some of these areas are still hardwood forests supporting timber production and wildlife habitat (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1982: Map). These areas are slightly to moderately limited  for urban usage. Many areas are subject to flooding (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1975:11). These areas are limited for urban usage because of the flood rates. When flood plains are inhabited, the government steps in to protect property. In doing this they are preventing soil productivity. Septic tank absorption fields are also limited in some of these areas (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1975:8).The soil in this area  is saturated frequently  and doesn’t hold additional moisture.

            There are several different soil types in the bottomlands and terraces. These differing soil types and locations illustrate the relationship between the formation of certain land forms and the deposited sediments in the area. This depositing of soils by flood waters in specific areas creates our agricultural regions of today. Specific crops grow better in certain areas due to the nutrients that are prevalent there. (See attached chart 1a)

            Erosion is a major problem facing farmlands in the United States such as these found in the Mississippi Delta. “Erosion is the wearing away of the soil by water, wind, and other forces.. Water causes about two-thirds of the erosion on our agricultural land”(United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1987:3). Erosion is not all bad. “River Valleys for example, are fertile because of eons of geologic erosion from the highlands” (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1987:7). The type of erosion we should avoid in the bottomlands and terraces is called accelerated erosion. We cause accelerated erosion by clearing meadows to grow crops and exposing the soil to the forces of nature (United States Department of Agriculture, Soil Conservation Service 1987:7).

            The types of accelerated erosion caused by water are splash erosion, sheet erosion,  rill erosion, concentrated-flow erosion, gully erosion, and mass erosion. Splash erosion happens when the plant life that covers and protects the land is no longer present. Water droplets hit the bare land and displace soil, causing it to be carried by water. Sheet erosion occurs on hillsides when rain fall deposits the soil from the top of the hill at the bottom of the hill. Rill erosion is also prevalent on hillsides. Rill erosion is characterized by small dips, or rills, carved out in the soil. Concentrated flow erosion looks much like to a small water fall or creek when it rains. The soil is carried away by a small source of flowing water. Gully erosion is a larger versions of concentrated flow erosion. The differences between concentrated flow and gully erosion are the resulting formations. Gully erosion forms deep ditch like formations, while concentrated flow forms smaller creek bed like formations (Margolin, Malcom 1975:66)

             We have to be careful and protect these valuable farmlands.  Erosion can cause soil to be less productive. Loss of topsoil can cause soil to have a lower capacity to hold water. Erosion can cause the loss of organic matter and nutrients in soil. Layers with unfavorable characteristics can be worn to the surface because of erosion.

            The bottomlands and terraces are important and productive parts of our state. Our future in agriculture depends on these areas in Arkansas. We need to learn more   about them so we can better protect our resource rich farmland for the future.
