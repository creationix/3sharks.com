Earthquakes? In Arkansas?



It has been said that an earthquake can be felt somewhere in the world every ten minutes (DeNevi, introduction). Even in these technology-rich times, earthquakes are still as difficult to understand as they are to predict. As such, earthquakes are surrounded by many myths. At least in this country, many still believe that earthquakes are limited to the Pacific coast or that they can be caused only by the conversion of two of the earth’s plates.

But earthquakes can also occur well within continental plates along cracks or fractures in the Earth’s crust called faults. These faults often occur in areas of the plate that have weakened due to ancient volcanism (DeNevi, p. 85). One of the major faults in the United States is the New Madrid Fault, located between Arkansas and Tennessee just north of Memphis (see map). The New Madrid Fault was responsible for a series of earthquakes in 1811 whose aftershocks continued for the better part of an entire year. By looking at the New Madrid quakes, as well as looking at earthquakes in general and what causes them, and by looking at the precautions being taken by Arkansas and other states to help prevent earthquake damage, we hope to gain a better understanding of mid-plate earthquakes and how another major earthquake might effect Arkansas.

The New Madrid earthquakes of 1811 and 1812 rank as some of the largest quakes in the history of the United States since being settled by Europeans. Its area of damage was over 600,000 square kilometers and it could be felt in an area of 5 million square kilometers. The area where the worst shaking occurred is two to three times larger than that of the 1964 Alaska earthquake and ten times larger than that of the 1906 San Francisco earthquake. The first two quakes in the New Madrid occurred in Northeast Arkansas. The first occurred on December 16, 1811. Because the area of the epicenter was primarily rural, there was only slight damage to any man-made structures. But the quake could be felt for 2.5 million square kilometers (http://wwwneic.cr.usgs.gov/neis/eqlists/USA/1811-1812.html). Although there was not much damage to homes or others buildings, there was much damage to the landscape. As the ground rose and fell, cracks were opened in the ground and trees were bent until their branches became intertwined. On the steeper hillsides, the quake caused landslides, while lower areas sunk further into the ground and were covered in water from fissures. The quake also caused huge waves in the Mississippi River that collapsed its high banks into the water, washed away entire sandbars and islands, and sent riverboats high onto the shore (http://wwwneic.cr.usgs.gov/neis/eqlists/USA/1811-1812.html). All in all, the effects of the quake could be seen in an area extending from Cairo, Illinois to Memphis, and from Crowley’s Ridge to Chickasaw Bluffs, Tennessee.

Earthquakes are caused by massive shifts in the Earth’s crusts. Certain parts of the crust are under constant stress. When the stress becomes too much, the rock gives way, often far beneath the surface of the Earth. As the Earth’s crust shifts, these shifts release massive amounts of trapped energy, causing the entire globe to vibrate. Any earthquakes are caused by plate tectonics. Where plates separate, molten rock emerges through a rift and forms new, softer crust. Where plates converge, subduction causes one plate to be pushed under the other and cooked down, again into new, softer crust. This softer crust eases in shifting and can lead to earthquakes. For instance, the San Andreas Fault in Northern California represents the area between the Pacific Plate, which is moving toward the northwest, and the North American Plate, which is moving west. It is the sudden movement of one of the plates that had previously been stationary for centuries that caused the San Francisco earthquake of 1906 (DeNevi, p. 85). However, seismic activity is not limited to the areas between major plates. Faults can also occur in the middle of plates, possibly because the integrity has been weakened by ancient volcanic activity. Eventually, the strain within a faults builds up to the point where the fault will collapse, releasing an immense amount of energy (http://www.pbs.org/wnet/savageearth/animations/earthquakes/index.html). A perfect example of a fault within a plate is, of course, the New Madrid Fault. A cross section of the New Madrid region shows that bedrock lies as much as half a mile beneath the surface of much of Eastern Arkansas (see Lowman map).

Though earthquakes are virtually impossible to predict, we can prepare for the damage they inflict. The problem with many older buildings that have not been built to any sort of seismic specifications is that they have been built deep into the Earth so as to be kept completely still. Older architects have depended too highly on concrete on steel to keep structure steady during quakes. In this method, they fight a battle with Mother Nature that they are incapable of winning. The immense forces behind earthquakes basically cause the surface of the Earth to become like water, moving in waves. The newly accepted paradigm for building earthquake-safe structures is that of working with the force of the quake instead of trying to fight it. Modern bridges and buildings are built to, within reason, move with the force of the quake so that only a severe quake can do any serious damage (http://edweb.sdsu.edu/courses/EDTEC572/f96projects/final/disasters/eqs/bildhelp.htm). A shining example of this new thinking comes from perpetual architectural innovator Frank Lloyd Wright, who designed the Imperial Hotel in Japan, a structure that has survived at least 11 earthquakes that registered 8.0 or higher on the Richter scale. The Imperial’s foundation is built above the Earth’s surface, and it’s internal structures such as water and electricity lines were designed to allow a reasonable degree of movement, both factors that work with the energy of an earthquake and have allowed the building to remain standing during even these strong quakes.

Arkansas and the rest of the New Madrid region have been at the forefront of earthquake preparation for the entire United States. A 1992 testimony by then Arkansas Office of Emergency Services Director James Lee Witt before the Congressional Committee on Science, Space and Technology stated that by the year 2000 scientists had predicted up to a 63 percent chance of a major earthquake in the central United States.
Another federal congressional document on reducing earthquake loss cites the New Madrid region as one of the highest-risk seismic activity zones in the country. In fact, charts created by the Arkansas Center for Earthquake Education and Technology at the University of Arkansas at Little Rock show that, among quakes recorded in the Central United States between 1800-1983, the highest concentration of those quakes have occurred in the New Madrid region (http://www.eas.slu.edu/Earthquake_Center/SEISMICITY/cus1800-1983.html). Another chart shows a high frequency of quakes within the region between 1975 and 1995 (http://www.eas.slu.edu/Earthquake_Center/SEISMICITY/newmadrid1975-1995.html). The UALR study also predicts that 100 percent of Faulkner County residents would feel a major New Madrid quake. According to the predictions, a quake registering between 7.0 and 7.9 of the Richter scale would cause 25 percent architectural damage in the county, with a quake of 8.0 to 8.9 causing 50 percent architectural damage (http://quake.ualr.edu/public/estimate/faulkner.htm).

In recent years, many scientists have predicted that the New Madrid Fault is due to cause another major earthquake in the Central United States. After the damage caused by the New Madrid earthquakes of 1811 and 1812, Arkansas is taking no chances in its preparation for any potential quakes in the future. History has shown that the New Madrid Fault can be a devastating natural force, while science has shown that it may be primed to strike again and could be just as devastating. Arkansas: it’s not just for tornados anymore.
