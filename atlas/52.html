How Would Conway and Arkansas be Effected if an Earthquake Ever Occurs on the New Madrid Fault?

In 1990, New Mexico climatoligist, Iben Browning predicted a devastating earthquake would occur in December on the New Madrid Fault line (Markon, 1990, p.6).  Because a huge earthquake had rocked this region early in the nineteenth century, residents took his prediction seriously and began preparing for the projected quake (Allen, 1999, p.1).  They bought extra water, supplies, and additional earthquake insurance.  Schools were dismissed in many districts on the day the quake was predicted.  Discussions concerning what residents could expect to happen were the main topic of interest at the grocery store, their places of business, and the dinner table.  When the predicted quake failed to occur the furor died down and life went back to normal.  Still, questions remain.  What causes an earthquake to happen?  Is the New Madrid Fault a threat to residents of Arkansas?  Is Conway at risk?  What really happened during the earthquakes of 1811-12?  Would the destruction of an earthquake today be significant?  What can citizens do to recover from the earthquake if it does ever occur?  These are all valid and legitimate questions. The information contained in the following report should provide you with answers to these questions.  This information will help you become more informed and better prepared in the event the “Big One” ever does occur.

When one speaks of the New Madrid Fault attention must be given to the series of quakes that rocked the central Mississippi Valley in the winter of 1811-12.  Beginning in the early morning hours of December 16, an earthquake occurred which scientists have estimated would measure an 8.0 on today’s seismological instruments (Tangley, 1999, p. 56).  The destruction that occurred during this quake was great. Riverbanks collapsed and islands in the Mississippi reportedly sank (Penick, 1981, p. 60-63).  There were reports that the river rose dramatically, as much as seven to eight feet, and dropped again just as rapidly.  Disturbances that normally occur on dry land happened in the river.  Fissures opened and closed contributing to the turbulence.  Sand Blows, which on land create deep, circular craters, happened in the river’s bed (Penick, 1976, p. 60-64).  On land, the devastation was extensive. Fissures twenty to thirty feet wide were evident.  Soil and water spewed violently from these fissures (Fuller, 1958, p.47-8). Landslides spilled across the countryside. Forests became fields filled with fallen, split, and twisted trees. Structures were destroyed (Fuller, 1958, p.96-99).

During this period from December 16th through March 8th an engineer from Louisville, Kentucky, Jared Brooks counted 1,874 shocks. Using pendulums to detect horizontal earth waves and a system of springs to detect the vertical shocks, he classified ten of these as very severe, thirty-five as moderately severe, and the remainder as hardly perceptible.  The range of the initial earthquake was so large that it encompassed approximately 1,000,000 square miles (Penick, 1981, p. 6).  The tremors were reportedly felt to the north as far away as Canada, to the east as far as Boston, and to the south New Orleans. This is an area equal to half of the entire United States (Fuller, 1958, p. 17). Without the resources available to us today, the residents of the New Madrid region knew very little about the natural phenomenon that is an earthquake or what causes one to occur.  Residents during this time thought the world was ending when the earthquake started its movement (Penick, 1981, p. 105).

In the period since the New Madrid quake, scientists have provided us with greater


information about earthquakes and how they happen.  We now know that earthquakes have four structures—the focus, epicenter, faults, and waves.  The focus of an earthquake is the point where the Earth’s crust actually moves.  The point on the Earth’s surface directly above the focus is the epicenter.  Faults are caused by stress that is created in the Earth’s crust.  As the crusts move along the plate boundaries, they exert tremendous stress on the rocks.  Despite the rigidity of the rocks, they eventually bend and fold.  When the stress becomes extreme enough, a fracture occurs along the cracks.  These are called faults.  Most earthquakes are caused by the sudden slipping along the fault (Bergman & Renwick, 1999, Ch. 3).  Earthquakes receive their destructive power from the tectonic plates formed in the Earth’s lithosphere.  Because these plates “float” on the top of the asthenosphere there is an area of weakness which gives the plates the ability to move freely (Smith 1986, p. 26, 35).  These floating plates, a jigsaw puzzle of sorts, are approximately 60 miles thick, very rigid, and extremely strong.  Their boundaries are a series of complicated ridges where adjacent plates pull apart from one another.  There are two types of ridges.  Transform Faults are faults which slide past each other and subduction trenches occur when the plates collide and one is pushed under the other and falls into the mantle (Miller, 1983, p. 108).  As these plates interact with each other strain builds up until the rocks can not take any further pressure.  They rupture and leave behind damage that can be devastating (Smith, 1986, p. 26).  But, not all earthquakes are the same.  The New Madrid fault is in the middle of a continental plate, yet earthquakes still occur there.

When earthquakes occur on the New Madrid Fault, it is not because two plates collide.  Rather, it is the result of  “a weakened central part of an underground plate.”  It is a scar beneath the ground that is not visible from above (Franey, 1999, B1).  Often the New Madrid Fault is called a break in the North American plate, a failed rift.  Because the Earth is constructed of large plates that move on the surface, the plates are continually bumping into, pulling apart, or slipping past one another.  This causes the Earth to release energy, which causes shaking to occur.  The New Madrid Fault zone is one of the most active in this country.  In the Rocky Mountains, where the crust is colder and more brittle, less movement occurs.  Thus, the New Madrid Region shakes more and when an earthquake does occur the damage can be quite severe as evidenced by the series of earthquakes in 1811 and 1812 (Shrout, Daniels, Holzman, 1995, p. 1). 


 The damage that occurs from earthquakes is dependent upon the magnitude of the quake.  Until the 1930’s, earthquakes could only be measured by observing the effects they had on the ground, measuring the visible damage, and expressing the power of distance on a scale.  This scale was called an Intensity Scale ( Smith, 1986, p.27).  Although the Intensity Scale is still in use today, modern technology has given scientists another precise instrument.  Today, we also use a Richter Scale to measure the intensity of earthquakes.  Charles Richter, a California scientist, invented this scale and it has proved to be invaluable for measuring quakes.  An earthquake’s magnitude is measured on this scale by examining the energy released and  calculating the amplitude of the wave.  Each point on the scale represents a factor of thirty and ranges from zero upward with each increase creating more damage.  Small magnitude earthquakes, less than four on the Richter Scale, occur often but do not pose a threat to us.  The largest earthquakes ever recorded had a magnitude ranging from  8.5 to 9.5 ( Smith, 1986, p.29).  The effects of this type of earthquake are monumental, killing many people and causing widespread devastation.  The following chart contains the wide range of earthquake magnitudes that can occur as well as the annual average of their occurrence.  As one can see, the very large quakes do not happen with great frequency.

The damage, which can occur when an earthquake happens, varies with the magnitude of the quake and the intensity of the quake.  The Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale shown in figure 2 shows the level of damage that residents of Arkansas could expect should an earthquake occur on the New Madrid fault (UALR, 1998).  An earthquake with a magnitude of 1.0-3.0 and an intensity level of I is typically not felt except under favorable conditions.  A magnitude 3.0-3.9 quake with an intensity level of II will only be felt by a few people, most notably if they reside on the upper floor of a building.  A level III with the same magnitude would produce vibrations similar to that of a passing automobile.  Most people would not realize it was an earthquake.  An earthquake which falls into the 4.0-4.9 range with intensity level IV could  be felt by someone inside their home, but not much by those persons outdoors.  The sensation would be similar to a truck striking a building.  Parked cars would noticeably rock.  A level V with this same magnitude would cause dishes and other objects to break.  Unstable objects would turn over and people sleeping would be wakened.  A 5.0-5.9 magnitude with an intensity of VI would be felt by everyone and would be frightening.  Heavy furniture would move and, in some instances, plaster might fall.  Still, the damage would be minor.  An intensity of VII would result in slight to moderate damage in ordinary structures and very minor damage in buildings of good design and structure.  The damage would be much greater in poorly built structures.  Earthquakes with a 6.0-6.9 magnitude and an intensity level of VIII would result in damage to even the best-designed structures and heavier damage to ordinary buildings.  The poorly constructed buildings would suffer great damage with some buildings collapsing.  Chimneys, walls, and monuments would fall.  The next level, a IX, would result in considerable damage to even the best constructed buildings.  Partial collapse could occur and buildings would shift off their foundations.  An intensity level of X with a magnitude of 7.0 and higher would destroy some of the most well-built structures.  Rails would be bent and most masonry and frame structures would be destroyed.  Within this same magnitude but with an intensity level of XI the damage would be devastating.  There would be few remaining structures standing, bridges would be destroyed, and the rails would be bent at a greater level.  The level XII would cause sight and level to be distorted and objects would be thrown violently into the air.  Total devastation would occur. Scientists today have many different theories about the possibility that another large earthquake could occur in this region.  Geologists have recently completed studies that report very little movement along the fault.  They used satellites to track the smallest of tremors on the fault line between 1991 and 1997.  Their studies show less than two millimeters of movement.  These geologists believe it would take up to 1,000 years for enough stress to be generated to create an earthquake of sizeable magnitude.  Other geologists disagree with this because they believe there was not enough time to gather sufficient data in six years (Tangley, 1999, Doc. 2).  Scientists with a different opinion feel an earthquake of considerable magnitude will possibly occur within the eastern region of the United States in the next few years.  These scientists feel the odds for a sizeable earthquake by the year 2010 is almost 100% (Shrout, Daniels, Holzman, 1995, p.1).

Recent history of the New Madrid quake zone has shown us that numerous quakes have occurred which are measurable on the Richter Scale. The seismograph network at Saint Louis University recorded shocks that were felt by residents in this region as recently as 1968 and 1976 (Penick, 1981, p. 151).  Residents of Arkansas often hear news reports of another small tremor that was detected by seismologists.  Sometimes objects falling off the shelves and the walls alert citizens to the faint rumbling that is occurring deep within the ground beneath them.  Although no earthquakes with devastation have happened since the ones in 1811 and 1812, there is still measurable activity in this area.

If an earthquake does occur, the citizens of Arkansas and Conway residents need to be prepared.  Emergency response teams may not be able to reach all neighborhoods or workplaces immediately.  Individuals might need to be self-sufficient for up to 72 hours.  Emergency response actions should be in place before an earthquake occurs.  At home a supply of food, water, and first-aid supplies could be stored in the event an emergency such as an earthquake should happen.  Everyone could keep tools on hand to aid in the rescue of neighbors, co-workers and family members.  Shovels could aid in digging out anyone who might be buried in the debris.  Crescent wrenches would be useful for turning off gas and water sources.  Flashlights would be useful in the event the electricity is interrupted.  An inventory could be made of the skills and resources available in each neighborhood and office so residents would know where to turn for assistance (  1021htm.).

The earthquakes, which devastated the New Madrid region in the early nineteenth century, will not soon be forgotten.  The small but still detectable tremors that occur today are a constant reminder that this deeply buried fault line is still active.  The plates beneath the Earth’s surface will continue to slip and slide, and the Richter Scale and Modified Mercalli Intensity Scale will continue to be an invaluable tools in the measurement of these movements.  Research compiled by noted geologists and seismologists will keep residents informed of activity.  Although Iben Browning’s predicted earthquake failed to materialize, we should still be thankful for the awareness such a scare created.  Today residents in Arkansas are more aware of the steps they should take to be prepared for the potential damage an earthquake could create.  They are aware of the recovery processes that would need to be utilized after the earthquake.  While there is speculation that sometime in the next decade we could see an earthquake even more devastating than those of 1811-12, there is hope that the anomaly that caused those quakes to happen will never materialize again.
