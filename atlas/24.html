Arkansas History

 

 

            "I believe in the rich heritage of Arkansas and I honor the men and women who created this heritage."  This line, taken from The Arkansas Creed, symbolizes the pride that all Arkansans should have towards our forefathers.  By knowing and understanding the history of Arkansas, people can have a deeper understanding and appreciation for our land and people.  By understanding Arkansas's history, we can appreciate how the first settlers in Arkansas set the path for the rest of us to follow.  To understand this great heritage, one must know how early inhabitants relate to our modern society and how their way of life effects our lives today.

            Among the earliest inhabitants of Arkansas were the Folsome people who wore skins and hunted with darts; the Bluff Dwellers, who lived in rock shelters and caves along the Ozark streams; and the Mound Builders, who used earth mounds as foundations for houses and temples (http://www.sosweb.state.ar.us).  Sometime before the 1500s new groups of Indians began settling in Arkansas.  The Quapaws were the original Arkansas Indians (Williams 1986:  4).  The Quapaws served as the "unofficial greeters of all who traveled the river highways," (Powers 1912:  3).  The Quapaws, or OO-GAQ-PA, were known as the downstream people.  The Algonkian-speaking Indians of the Ohio Valley called them the Arkansas, or south wind (http://www.sosweb.state.ar.us).   

            The first white men to explore the new lands that included Arkansas were a party of Spaniards who had come from Spain to explore Florida and search for gold (Powers 1912:  6).  On June 15, 1541, Hernando De Soto crossed the Mississippi River and saw this "fair and pleasant land" we know as Arkansas (Powers 1912:  6).  (MAP  A)  During their search for gold, De Soto and his men treated the Indians harshly.  The Indians soon learned that if they told the Spaniards they had no gold, they would be left alone (Williams 1986:  9).  After De Soto departed, white man did not set foot in Arkansas for another 131 years (Williams 1986:  9).

            It was not until 1673 that two French men, Father Marquette, a Jesuit missionary, and Louis Joliet, an explorer, explored the Mississippi to the mouth of the Arkansas River (Powers 1912:  8).  (MAP  B)  In 1682, Rene-Robert Cavalier de La Salle claimed the new land for the King of France (http://www.state.ar.us).  After La Salle returned to France, he befriended and enlisted the aid of Henri de Tonti, who left his name in history as the "Father of Arkansas" (Powers 1912:  9).  A number of De  Tonti's men expressed a desire to remain in the new land.  De Tonti's party was pleased with the location and climate and saw an opportunity for profitable trade with the Indians (Powers 1912:  10).  In 1686, ten men were given the authority to establish a fort, founding Arkansas Post, the first permanent European settlement in Arkansas (http://www.state.ar.us).  In March 1721, Benard La Harpe left Arkansas Post to explore the Arkansas River and discovered the site of  Little Rock, the future capitol of the state (Williams 1986:  10).  Arkansas Post was the center of government for Arkansas with a population of 138 in 1768 (Williams 1986:  11).

            The settlement of Arkansas was for many years a slow process.  Many hunters and traders made their way through the territory in search for skins and furs, but few established permanent homes (Powers 1912:  10).  Pioneers gradually migrated into the territory, "building their cabins on the river banks and laying out their farms in the fertile valleys," (Powers 1912:  10).  In the early part of the nineteenth century settlements began to make their appearance.  By 1819, about 14,000 settlers had congregated in Arkansas (Williams 1986:  12).  This was enough for Congress to organize the area as a Territory.  On March 2, 1819, President James Monroe signed a bill making Arkansas a Territory, with Arkansas Post as its capitol (http://www.sosweb.state.ar.us).  The new territory was divided into five counties with the western boarder extending all the way to the 98th Meridian (Williams 1986:  12).  The northern border ran from the mouth of the Little Red River due east to the Mississippi River.  What was to become the northern remainder of the state was still a part of New Madrid County.  (Dougan 1994:  4).  In 1815, Lawrence County was created out of the southern part of New Madrid County with a northern boundary line running north of the current state line (Dougan 1994:  5).  As Arkansas population increased, three additional counties were created in 1818:  Pulaski, Clark, and Hemptstead (Dougan 1994:  5).  (MAP C)

            The native Indians of Arkansas were generally peaceful with the explorers and hunters.  Few of these explorers were murdered, a practice that was common in other parts of the country (Powers 1912:  6).  The white settlers took over the territory gradually and with little resistance from the Indians.  In 1830, Congress passed the Indian Removal Act, forcing all Indians east of the Mississippi River to move to Indian Territory in Oklahoma (Williams 1986:  4).  Because of the sadness of the Indians journey, this event was referred to as the "Trail of Tears" (Williams 1986:  4).  (MAP D)

            Arkansas Post remained the capitol of Arkansas Territory until 1821 when the capitol was moved to Little Rock (http://www.state.arr.us).  Little Rock had many advantages over Arkansas Post.  Arkansas Post was located far from the center of the territory and was in a low, unhealthy location (Powers 1912:  14).  Little Rock, on the other hand, was more centralized being on the Arkansas River and was also a natural crossroad where Indian trials crossed the River (Williams 1986:  12).  Arkansas Territory grew so rapidly that by 1836 it had enough population to petition for statehood (Williams 1986:  14).  On June 15, 1836, Arkansas became the 25th state of the United States with an area of 53,255 square mile with a natural boundary of the Mississippi River to the east (http://www.sosweb.state.ar.us).  (MAP  E)  Also in 1836, the new capitol building of Little Rock was ready for occupancy and served as the capitol until 1911.  (Williams 1986:  15).  Little Rock has been the capitol ever since, "except during 1836-1865 when the Confederate state government was at Washington in Hempstead County,"  (http://www.state.ar.us). 

            The interest the pioneers had for agriculture, and the need for social contacts and amusement made local agricultural societies, festivals, and fairs popular among the inhabitants (Powers 1912:  31).  The crops that the first settlers cultivated are still a vital part of Arkansas’s economy.  For a century after statehood "King Cotton" reigned as the number one crop in Arkansas.  Today, cotton is the 3rd ranking crop in the state (Williams 1986:  17).  In 1904, William H. Fuller, who learned rice cultivation in Louisiana, raised his first successful crop near Hazen (http://www.state.ar.us).  Today, Arkansas ranks first in the nation in rice production (Williams 1986:  17).

            Just as the first settlers established our patterns for agriculture, they also helped to establish our various points of interest.  From the days of the Indian, the mineral springs of Arkansas have been known for their medicinal and cultural powers (Powers 1912:  33).  The Indians believed it was a gift from the Great Spirit (http://www.ci.hotsprings.ar.us).  Stories of the forty boiling springs' beauty spread throughout the county and in 1832 Congress set Hot Springs aside as the first reserve for public recreation (http://www.ci.hotsprings.ar.us).  Today, Hot Springs is one of Arkansas's biggest attractions.  Elegant hotels and casinos have been built to accommodate various travelers such as the notorious Al Capone who visited and used the bathhouses for their therapeutic properties (http://www.ci.hotsprings.ar.us).  Another major tourist attraction that originated in the early years of Arkansas’s existence is the Crater of Diamonds near Murfreesboro.  Diamonds were first found in 1906 by John Huddleston (http://www.state.ar.us).  In 1952 it was turned into a public attraction and in 1972 the state bought it and turned it into a park (http://www.redmoon.net).  To date, over 70,000 diamonds have been found in the park with an estimated four billion dollars worth of diamonds still waiting to be discovered. (http://www.redmoon.net).  The Ozark Mountains, another tourist attraction in Arkansas, was believed to have been inhabited by Indians.  Some historians believe the caves were explored as early as 1000 years ago with skeletal remains of an Indian found dating back to 1030 AD (http://www.alltel.net).  One of the most popular caves, Blanchard Springs, is visited by hundreds of tourists each year who come to see the beautiful crystalline structures that have taken millions of years to create (http://www.alltel.net).  Even the first capitol building in Little Rock is a major tourist attraction today.  Now the original capitol building is called the Old State House and is considered one of the South’s most beautiful structures (Williams 1986:  15).

            Like most things found today, our state government originated with the first settlers.  Once borders were established, a government official was appointed by the President.  The main government official in Arkansas Territory was the governor who served a three-year term.  The second major executive official was the secretary  (Dougan 1994:  12).  Arkansas's first judicial system was made up of three presidential appointed judges with the first legislature made of the governor and these judges (Dougan 1994: 13)

In the same year that Arkansas became a  state, the State Government was organized.  The political system, almost from the beginning, has been comprised of two parties at a time.  At first, the Democrats vs. the Whigs and now the Democrats vs. the Republicans  (http://www.arkdems.org).  There was, however, a party, known as the Know-Nothings, in the 1850s whose main purpose was to keep the immigrants and Catholics out of the government  (http://www/arkdems.org).  The Democratic party has been the strongest political party in Arkansas for most of Arkansas's existence.  "Of our elected Governors, thirty-eight have been Democrats and four Republicans.  The Democrats have controlled the General Assembly since 1873,"  (http://www.state.ar.us).   It is apparent that our present government has not changed that dramatically since the beginning of Arkansas's origin.

            Arkansas has grown from five counties to seventy-five counties with Cleburne County being the last, created in 1883.  These counties divide the state into voting districts.  Throughout the political history of Arkansas, gerrymandering has been used to limit the black vote and thus controlling the democratic majority  (Dougan 1994:  23).

                “Over the past 500 years, Arkansas has grown from vast wilderness to a thriving state with a population of 2.5 million and industries ranging from agriculture to technology to commerce,”  (http://www.sosweb.state.ar.us).  Our forefathers played their part and left us a rich and noble heritage.  It is now our job to continue their gift for generations to come.  Only by knowing and understanding how the early inhabitants of Arkansas relate to our modern society and how their way of life effects our lives today will we be able to appreciate all the wonderful things Arkansas has to offer.
