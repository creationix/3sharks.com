Where Are some Acceptable Bicycle Trails Around Conway, Arkansas?

A17: Physical Geography: Bike Trails

“Bicycle Trails around Conway, Arkansas”

By Jessie Tillman, Mark Crowder, and Megan Heikkila (JAMM)

Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2621

Prepared: October 10, 2000

 

Where Are Some Acceptable Bicycle Trails Around Conway, Arkansas?

 

The helicopter zooms over the mountain, trying desperately to keep Noah Mappa in sight of their curious camera.  The crew can see the trail snaking past hill after hill.  Noah cruises dangerously fast on his Mongoose ten-speed road bike.  The trees impede the vision of the inquisitive camera crew.  On the ground, twigs slap ferociously at Mappa, leaving scratches on his bone-weary face.  Only adrenaline is pushing him to keep on going.  A blind turn looms ahead.  A moment of panic passes through his blood, but quickly subsides.  There is no traffic.  All is well.  The curve approaches, and

Noah Mappa swerves into the shoulder to make the turn more easily.  All of a sudden, the shoulder disappears and is replaced by a concrete wall 42.5 feet tall and 12 feet thick.  Poor Noah never knew what hit him…or rather what he hit.  Noah could have avoided this cataclysmic fate with one simple thing—a map of his bike trail.  To help other cyclists evade a similar demise, we have constructed three bike trails of our own.

We determined our three bicycle paths by finding places near Conway that we thought would be scenic and bike-ride worthy.  We were curious to know if they would be suitable for bicycle routes.  We already knew about one route that was hilly, and we wanted to see if the shoulders were acceptable.  This course, which we later named Kitty Path, is an “out and back” route, meaning that it does not make a circle.  We chose the other two trails, later named Cossman Pass and Lance Armstrong Trail, by looking at an Arkansas map (Arkansas Atlas & Gazetteer 41, Ballard, Greenbrier, and Tyler) and finding a winding road in a place unknown to us.  We determined the classification of these trails with the terms excellent, good, fair, and poor.  Excellent implies that two cyclists can ride on the shoulder safely.  Good states that one cyclist could ride on the shoulder.  Fair means that there is no shoulder but little traffic.  Poor entails that there is no shoulder with high traffic, high-speed traffic, or impossible maneuverability.  In mapping, we considered landforms, grade, shoulders, and visibility around curves (Bennett).  Kitty Path, which is 18.8 miles, is for the inexperienced rider.  Cossman Pass, which is 32.3 miles, is for experienced riders.  Lance Armstrong Trail is 68 miles and is only for professionals.  We have mapped the trails, the topography of each trail, and the shoulders to warn cyclists of what is to come.

            Kitty Path starts at Beaverfork Overlook Dock.  The first half- mile is poor.  There is no shoulder, and the road is semi-steep and curvy.  At .6 miles, the shoulder widens to good, and the road flattens to accommodate one cyclist.  Be careful of the bridge and the steep downhill curve.    The second mile continues with the good shoulder.  The path has a couple of gentle curves.  It does not have a steep slope.  After these curves, the road straightens and stays flat.  At mile three, the shoulder is still good.  The road gradually climbs with a giant downhill curve.  A bridge greets the rider at the bottom of the bend, and then the road is straight again.  At mile four, the shoulder is excellent, with room for two or possibly three bicyclists.  There is an intersection at about 4.5 miles.  At this three-way stop, turn right onto Highway 65.  After this, the road is flat and has room for two cyclists on the shoulder.  About half way through mile six, there is a gentle curve and an uphill climb.  Then it goes downhill, and there is a long curve right after that.  At the end of mile seven, there is a gradual uphill climb and a sharp curve.  Soon after, the road flattens.  At 8.7 miles, the shoulder widens to fit three bicyclists.  In the middle of mile nine, there is a small bridge that can fit two cyclists on the shoulder.  Cyclists come to an intersection at mile 9.5 and can turn around at the FINA Station.  Cyclists should be especially careful of mile 16 coming back.  The road is extra steep uphill with a downhill at the end.  The shoulders are fair to excellent in most places, and cyclists only have to be extremely careful in a few spots.  These two highways make a fantastic bike trail for almost any rider, especially for one who is inexperienced. 

            Cossman Pass begins by the Pennzoil Gas Station next to scenic Toad Suck Park.  This trail goes left from that parking lot on Highway 60 that, with the exception of a few poor bridges, has a good shoulder for one bike.  The trail follows 60 until it intersects with 113, where it goes toward Houston, Arkansas.  This is the safest part of the trip with not much traffic.  After seeing the numerous sites and attractions of Houston, the cyclist may take a break at the abandoned Citgo gas station and pizza place.  After the break, the trail goes right (back the way it came from before the break).  Then, it takes an almost immediate right to go south on Highway 216.  This leg of the trip is only for the more experienced and adventurous riders, and if traffic is low, then the cyclist might actually get out with only minimal scratches and bruises.  The shoulder is practically nonexistent.  The trail turns left on 300 and heads for the exceedingly pleasant town of Pleasant Valley.  The shoulder is almost enough for a bike, but the rider should be extremely wary of any traffic that could smoosh him.  The shoulders in Bigelow increase in width, making it a safe trail to follow back to the Pennzoil station.  The round trip is 32.3 miles long.  It is mostly flat with a few hills.  This course is for those experienced riders who do not mind dodging the occasional car.          

The third route begins at the Pennzoil gas station directly across from Toad Suck Park.  The first leg of the trip is about ten miles to Bigelow.  The shoulder is good, except for a few bridges where the shoulder is poor.  There are a couple of sharp curves, but the terrain is mostly flat.  After Bigelow, the road shoulder basically disappears and becomes poor.  After Pleasant Valley, the trail evolves into a very curvy uphill.  Be careful; there is no shoulder, on the left is a drop-off, and on the right is a cliff face.  The bicyclist then goes down the hill, maneuvering through some extremely tight curves.  At Wye, turn left, and enjoy the scenery going toward Little Italy.  The road has a poor shoulder and is extremely hilly but straight.  After passing Little Italy, it gets curvy again with still many hills.  The trail flattens out and then stays the same past Pinnacle Mountain until Highway 10.  The shoulder of Highway 10 is excellent for its first mile.  Then, it degrades to good.  Just stay on the shoulder, and everything should be great; Highway 10 is a busy highway.  Lake Maumelle is on the right for several miles.  The bicycle trail turns onto 113 and heads back, uphill, to Wye.  After reaching Wye, the trail turns left and goes back through the first leg of the trip back to Toad Suck Park.  The entire round trip is 68 miles.  This trail should only be ridden by professionals willing to put their lives on the line.        

The helicopter zooms over the mountain, trying desperately to keep Gotta Mappa, younger brother to the recently demised Noah Mappa, in sight of their curious camera.  The crew can see the trail snaking past hill after hill.  Gotta cruises dangerously fast on his Mongoose ten-speed road bike.  The trees impede the vision of the inquisitive camera crew.  On the ground, twigs slap ferociously at Mappa, leaving scratches on his bone-weary face.  Only adrenaline is pushing him to keep on going.  A blind turn looms ahead.  A moment of panic passes through his blood, but quickly subsides.  He remembers the map that he studied before he set out on his trek.  Wisely, he swerves to evade the 42.5 feet tall and 12 feet thick, bloodstained, concrete wall.  Gotta Mappa breathes a sigh of relief and continues on his journey to the finish line.
