Boundaries in Conway

 

                                                                   Church of the Holy A+

                Since early times maps have been useful tools.  Not only do they benefit the scientific geographer, they also help the common man.  The technique of setting boundaries to determine what events will take place in a given location has been recorded in use since the early Chinese maps which date around 2000 years before Christ.  Even the ancient Egyptians recognized the value of a visual representation of different boundaries.  Their earliest maps were used to describe property that was flooded annually by the Nile river.  Yet, with today’s complicated boundaries and zones, it often becomes hard to tell what boundary is what.  Located in the southern state of Arkansas sits the quiet city of Conway which  provides a good example of this general confusion of topography.  It is quite an interesting place to study, especially when looking into the many districts it holds. You have probably heard of school districts, police districts, or numerous other districts.  A district is simply a territorial division which is established for administrative purposes.  A district is determined for a variety of reasons and the boundaries used can be highways, railroads, or a number of different characteristics

                There are several different classifications of boundaries.  The first distinction made between types of boundaries is whether they are natural or artificial.  A natural boundary is one that occurs naturally in nature such as a river or a mountain.  Artificial boundaries are created by man.  Such lines usually follow a straight or geometric path.  For the purposes of this essay, we will deal only with artificial boundaries as they abound with enough frequency to support our purposes.  One type of boundary which will be discussed is called a consequent boundary.  A consequent boundary is one that is drawn to accommodate the religious, linguistic, ethnic, or economic differences people may have. 

                Before one is to delve into the deeper aspects of these boundaries, it is useful to have a general knowledge of how the boundaries we are discussing actually work.  Four maps are provided (a, b, c, d).  The first map (a) is fairly simple to understand.  It is a representation of the five school zones in Conway.  Each zone is apportioned by population.  The areas within the blue lines are the areas representing that particular school zone.  The next map (b) is slightly more complicated.  It represents the elementary school attendance zones and sub-zones.  Every area in Conway is assigned a number which ranges from 210 to 285. Numbers which are within a certain area are assigned to a certain school.  The large black lines are zone lines and the smaller black lines are sub-zone lines.  The large red line determines which middle school a child will attend.  Children who live on one side of the line go to Bob Courtway Middle School while children who live on the other side of the line attend Carl Stuart Middle School.  Map (c) shows a few different boundaries.  First, the yellow line shows the boundary line that separates which state representative serves a side of Conway.  Second blue lines show the boundaries for justice of the peace.  Green broken lines represent ward boundaries while red lines show precinct boundaries.  The final map, (d), shows the fire district boundaries.

                Now that we have a basic understanding of what these boundaries are, it is important to determine how they affect us.  What do these maps actually do?  The boundaries determined by Conway School districts affect our children.  Map a affects us by way of representation.  Each of the zones is represented by a school board member.  Elections for this office are held once every 4 years or whenever an existing member leaves the board for whatever reason.  If one has a question or complaint directed towards a Conway school it is vitally important that one understands the boundaries in order to know which school board member to contact.  Map b contains representations of the boundaries that separate which children attend what school.  Understanding these boundaries will allow a person to know which school their child will attend.  Should these lines be redrawn for reasons of population change or other, a child may be forced to attend a different school with strange new teachers and schoolmates.  Another important map, c, shows us several important boundaries.  The first is precinct boundaries, which are very important by the way of representation.  Precinct lines determine where certain groups of people within the lines go to vote.  It is vitally important that these lines accurately represent the population or one group may be over represented while another portion of the population is under represented.  The next lines represented on the map are ward boundaries.  Ward boundaries determine several different city positions such as that of city council.  Once again, these lines must be drawn out so that all areas get equal representation.  Next, the map shows justice of the peace boundaries which it abbreviates JP.  The justice of the peace is an important governing body because it controls much of the money that is apportioned in the county.  Conway has seven justice of the peace members.  The last thing that is represented by map c is the state representative boundaries for Conway.  The state of Arkansas has four state representatives.  It is interesting to know that one of the lines separating the state representatives runs directly through Conway.  This means that people who live in the eastern part of Conway are represented by district 45 while those in the western part of town must contact district 44 for representation.  Our final map, d, represents the fire zones in Conway.  There are five separate fire stations in Conway.  There are  North, East, South, West, and Central stations.  Each station is assigned a particular area in which it will be the first to respond to a fire.

                We have heretofore covered several different maps which each seem to serve a different purpose.  If one is forced to take all of these boundaries in together it is very easy to confuse their purposes.  Often, one system of boundaries has certain characteristics which could benefit other boundary systems.  An example of this can be seen if one looks closely at state representative boundaries.  The supreme court case Thornburg vs. Gingles established a principle in which states have an obligation to draw majority minority districts whenever possible.  This means that district lines should be drawn in such a way that minorities have an effective vote.  However, none of the other political boundaries we have discussed seem to follow the example of this supreme court precedent.  Neither precinct, ward, nor Justice of the Peace boundaries are arranged in such a way to give minorities an advantage.

                The system of boundaries which we have established for use in making our life simpler can at times only serve to confuse us.  Yet, while our boundaries often have certain inadequacies we could not function without them
