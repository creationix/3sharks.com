What is Religion?  When and how did Christianity diffuse from its beginnings into Arkansas and what are the various forms of Christianity that have been and are being practiced in Arkansas?

 

            No simple definition can describe the numerous religions in the world.  Religion is a system of beliefs regarding conduct in accordance with teachings found in sacred writings or declared by authoritative teachers (Bergman 1999: 258).  For many people, religion is an organized system of beliefs, ceremonies, practices, and worship that center on one supreme god, or deity.  For many others, religion involves a number of gods, or deities.  Some people have a religion in which no specific god or gods are worshiped.  Almost all people who follow some form of religion believe that a divine power created the world and influences their lives.

            People practice religion for several reasons.  Many people throughout the world follow religion simply because it is part of the heritage of their culture, tribe, or family.  Religion gives many people a feeling of security because they believe that a divine power watches over them.  These people often ask the power for help or protection.  Numerous people follow a religion because it promises them salvation and either happiness or the chance to improve themselves in a life after death.  For many people, religion brings a sense of individual fulfillment and gives meaning to life.  In addition, religion provides answers to such questions as What is the purpose of life?  What is the final destiny of a person?  What is right and wrong?  and What are one's obligations to other people?  Finally, many people follow a religion to enjoy a sense of kinship with their fellow believers.

            There are thousands of religions in the world.  The eight major ones, in the order of their founding, are Judaism, Hinduism, Buddhism, Confucianism, Taoism, Shinto, Christianity, and Islam (Verkamp, 61).  Hinduism, Taoism, and Shinto developed over many centuries and cannot be traced to personal founders.  The other religions traditionally base its faith on the life or teaching of specific individuals.  They are Abraham and Moses for Judaism; Prince Siddhartha Gautama, who became known as Gautama Buddha, for Buddhism; Confucius for Confucianism, Jesus Christ for Christianity; and Muhammad for Islam (Verkamp, 74).

            The religions that trace their history to individuals follow a general pattern of development.  During the individual's lifetime or soon after his death, a distinctive system of worship ceremonies grew up around him.  This system, called a cult, became the basis of the religion (Crosby, 15).  The heart of the cult is the individual's teachings.  In addition to inspiring worship, the individual represents an ideal way of life that followers try to imitate.

            The eight major religious practices in the world today were either founded or developed their basic form between about 600 B.C. and A.D. 600.   Although all of the eight different religions are found in some form in Arkansas, Christianity is the religion of the majority of Arkansans.

            Christianity, an offshoot of Judaism, is based on the life and teachings of Jesus, a Jewish preacher of the first century A.D. whom his followers believed was the messiah promised by God (Getis 1998: 262).  Christians believe that Jesus is the Son of God and was sent to earth to be the Savior. His teachings was not a rejection of Judaism, but a promise of salvation to all mankind not just a chosen people (Getis 1998, 262). The Jews around 30 A.D crucified Jesus. After Jesus' Crucifixion, a number of his followers spread his teachings throughout the mid-East.  One of the most important of these followers was Saint Paul who preached in many large cities in the eastern Mediterranean such as Rome and Jerusalem. His many letters to early Christian groups make up some of the New Testament (Getis 1998: 263).  On these visits and in his letters Paul stressed that proselytizing is a duty of a Christian. The idea that it was the duty of a Christian to “spread the word of God” helped Christianity grow. The spatial diffusion of Christianity was greatly aided by the Roman Empire’s extensive system of roads and sea lanes that covered most of Europe, the Middle East and northern Africa. The early diffusion of Christianity follows these transportation routes. The Christian church of Egypt was founded in Alexandria in 41 A.D., and Christianity diffused south into today’s Ethiopia by the fourth century. Christian churches were founded in India as early as the second century. To the northeast Armenia became the first Christian nation early in the fourth century (Bergman 1999: 263). In the early 300's, the Roman emperor Constantine the Great became a Christian and favored the spread of Christianity throughout the empire.  By the late 300's, Christianity was widely practiced throughout the empire. Under the rule of the Roman Empire, the church adopted a geographical organization; the priests of the villages were under the rule of the bishops of the cities who were under the rule of the Pope. One reason it took almost 400 years for Christianity to spread throughout the Roman Empire was the friction of distance. It was very expensive and, until the emperor approved of Christianity, very dangerous to travel and spread the teachings of Christ. Also this was before Gutenberg’s movable type printing press so it very expensive to reproduce copies of the Bible.

             During the Middle Ages, Christian missionaries converted many European barbarian tribes, which led to the Christian church's dominant influence on European life for centuries.  For many years, a split had been developing between Christians in Western Europe and those in Eastern Europe and western Asia.  The spilt finally occurred in the 1000's.  The churches in Greece, Russia, and other parts of Eastern Europe and western Asia became known as the Eastern Orthodox Churches.  The church in Western Europe became known as the Roman Catholic Church.

            In the 1500's a religious movement called the Protestant Reformation divided western Christianity into several bodies.  Most southern Europeans remained Roman Catholics.  A great number of northern Europeans, known as Protestants, formed new churches.  The largest included the Baptists, Congregationalists, Episcopal, Lutheran, Methodist, and Presbyterian churches.

            When Europeans began settling North America in the 16th century they brought Christianity with them. The early explorers motto was “Gold, God and Glory” (Cossman 2000: lecture). This desire for converts was aided by the printing press that made it easier and cheaper to spread copies of the Bible. The lack of roads and language barriers made converting the natives harder. The early explorers often forced the natives to convert giving then the choice the convert or die. Because the Catholic Spanish conquered Central and South America it is today mainly Catholic. The Protestant English were the major settlers of present day USA and Canada and therefore the countries are mainly Protestant. It took much less time for Christianity to diffuse across America that it did Europe. One reason is because by the time the Europeans settled the Americas Christianity was an established religion that they brought over with them. With each town established one off the first buildings built was a church so as Europeans spread across America so did Christianity.

            Within Christianity, a movement called charismatic Christianity has attracted million of followers.  The movement began with the founding of Pentecostalism in the United States in 1901.  Pentecostalism is a highly emotional form of religious worship and behavior that emphasizes praying.  Christian fundamentalism is a conservative religious movement that has had great social and political influence in the United States.  Christianity fundamentalist leaders include such television evangelists as Billy Graham and Pat Robertson.

Now that we have discussed religion and the predominate forms of religion in the United States we will now move on to the background of religion in Arkansas.

                        Christianity was introduced to Indians living in Arkansas thought the European explorers. Most of the explorers arriving in Arkansas brought Christianity with them.  Despite effort put forth by missionaries over the years to convert Indians to Christianity, the Quapaws remained loyal to their beliefs and way of life.  Protestants were able to convert some of the Cherokees of the area because of their willing to adapt to the Anglo-American ways.  When the Indians were ultimately forced out of Arkansas they took their beliefs with them.

            In 1541 Hermando De Soto tried to convert the remaining Indians to Catholicism but ultimately was unsuccessful.  In 1663, 131 years later, Father Marquette and Louis Joliet set out to determine if there was a water route to the Pacific in the New World.  Upon arrival in Arkansas they were able to gain the trust of the Quapaws due to a calumet, or peace pipe that was sacred to the Indians, given to them by the Illinois Indians upstream (McKnight 1955: 41).  The Indians became interested in their beliefs of the French Catholic Church and welcomed their teachings. A cross symbolizing the French Catholic Church was planted in Arkansas claiming the land for France in 1682 by La Salle and Hernri de Tonti.  In 1686 De Tonti established the first white settlement, The Arkansas Post.  Father Allouey was the first priest at the Arkansas Post, which was granted land for the Catholic Church by De Tonti.   As a center for trade, the Arkansas Post attracted many traders, trappers, and priest eager to preach to the Indians.

            Spain took control in 1762 to avoid allowing the English to gain control after the French and Indian War.  Focusing now on the white settlers in Arkansas rather than the Indians, Spain had a commitment in furthering Christianity and specifically Catholicism.  In 1778, several Protestant Anglo American families moved to Arkansas Posts and were required to convert to Catholicism.  This continued until King Carlos allowed them to practice their beliefs in private.  In 1811, the first Protestant sermon took place at the Arkansas Post by John Carnahan.  As migrants increased throughout Arkansas other Christian denominations soon spread. William Stevenson established a few Methodist societies along the Red River in 1814.  He became a circuit rider and others soon followed.  The circuit rider created congregations on settlements and recruited local farmers as preachers. In addition to the Methodists, the Missouri Bethel Baptist Association sent Reverend James Phillip Edwards to organize a Baptist Church, near Little Rock, in 1817 (Tucker 1985: 14-15). 

            Through diffusion different types of Christianity have spread throughout Arkansas.  Arkansas, which started with Native American Shamanism, now has various forms of Christianity present.  Today Baptist and Methodist are the dominant forms of religions practiced in Arkansas.

            As technology advances the friction of distance decreases. With the advent of cars and paved roads it is easier and faster to move around the state and as a result it is easier to move people and ideas. Early in the state’s history when it was difficult to travel only the main denominations of Christianity (Catholic, Methodist, and Baptist) were practiced but now because it is easier to bring fringe groups together smaller, newer denominations are represented in the state. With the advent of the Internet it is now possible to instantly gain access to almost any kind of religion so you can practice a religion even if there is not a physical church in the state.
