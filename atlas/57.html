What is an earthquake and its relation to Arkansas?

A2: Physical Geography: Earthquakes
"Earthquakes in Arkansas"
By Terra Barker, Courtney Crane, Amy Gannaway, Sommer Martinez, and Tia Temple (Foxy Faulkner Fault Line Females)
Principles of Geography: Geog 1305, Section 2518

"What is an earthquake and its relation to Arkansas?

Many people believe that earthquakes are scarce throughout Arkansas. However, Arkansas has a very active seismic zone and has been touched by earthquake activity throughout the years (See Figure 1a; 1b). There have been several earthquakes documented in Arkansas from 1811 until 1988. Causes of earthquakes, measurement of earthquakes, and history of earthquakes in Arkansas are all very important in understanding what an earthquake is and why they occur in Arkansas.

The crust of the Earth is floating on the mantle, which is moving melted rock. The movements of the crust over many years has cracked it into many pieces called plates. The plates then float about on the mantle slowly. Sometimes they run into or pull away from each other, causing strains in the rocks along the edges of the plates. The plates that collide with each other are what causes most earthquakes, although they can occur anywhere that rocks are under stress (Simon, 1991). According to Shedlock and Pakiser, the crust bends first, then when the stress exceeds the strength of the rocks, the crust breaks and "snaps" into a new position. It is during this process that seismic waves are generated. They are vibrations that travel outward from the source of the earthquake along the surface (surface waves) and through the Earth (body waves). (Shedlock and Pakiser, http://pubs.usgs.gov/gip/earthq1/ ). Depending on the material through which the waves are moving, the speed at which they move varies. Surface waves usually have the strongest vibrations and probably cause most of the damage done by earthquakes. Although some of these vibrations are at a high enough frequency to be heard, others are of very low frequency and are not audible. The vibrations cause the entire planet to quiver, and sometimes ring like a bell (Shedlock and Pakiser, http://pubs.usgs.gov/gip/earthq1/ ).

"A fault is a fracture in the Earth's crust along which two blocks of the crust have slipped with respect to each other" (Shedlock and Pakiser, http://pubs.usgs.gov/gip/earthq1/). For many years earthquakes were believed to be the cause of occurring faults in the earth. In 1891, Professor B. Koto wrote, "It can be confidently asserted that the sudden faulting was the actual cause (and not the effect) of the earthquake." People did not agree with this for some time because the victims of the earthquakes could not see the difference between the cause and effect of the earthquake. The result was the same either way (Halacy, 1974).

Shedlock and Pakiser explain that there are three types of faults: normal faults, thrust (reverse) faults, and strike-slip (lateral) faults (See Figure 2a; 2b; 2c) . "Normal faults occur in response to pulling or tension; the overlying block moves down the dip of the fault plane. Thrust (reverse) faults occur in response to squeezing or compression; the overlying block moves up the dip of the fault plane. Strike-slip (lateral) faults occur in response to either type of stress; the blocks move horizontally past one another. Most faulting along spreading zones is normal, along subduction zones is thrust, and along transform faults is strike-slip" (Shedlock and Pakiser, http://pubs.usgs.gov/gip/earthq1/).

According to Halacy, the Earth trembles sufficiently to produce a shock every thirty seconds on the average. About a million earthquakes rock our planet each year. There have already been over a million deaths this century, along with great suffering and damage, caused by the destructive force of great blocks of the Earth's crust on a rampage (Halacy, 1974).

Measuring earthquakes is complicated because the size cannot be estimated by the damage to buildings or the number of people killed. For this reason, scientists use seismographs, which are instruments used to measure earthquake shocks (Simon, 1991). Scientists also use the Richter scale, developed by Charles F. Richter in 1935. It is a mathematical device used to compare the size of earthquakes. “The magnitude of an earthquake is determined from the logarithm of the amplitude of waves recorded by seismographs” ( USGS, http://gldss7.cr.usgs.gov/neis/general/handouts/richter.html). When using the Richter Scale, magnitude is measured and expressed in whole numbers and decimal fractions. The higher the magnitude, the stronger the earthquake. A 6.9 earthquake does more damage than a 5.4 earthquake. Each whole number increase is a tenfold increase in amplitude and a release of about 31 times more energy than the preceding number (USGS, http://gldss7.cr.usgs.gov/neis/general/handouts/richter.html). Some earthquakes are very small and may not be felt by people. They can only be recorded by local seismographs, and usually have a reading of 2.0 or less. These are called microearthquakes. An earthquake with a magnitude of 4.5 of higher are strong enough to be recorded all over the world by sensitive seismographs. The largest earthquakes recorded have had a magnitude in the 8.8 to 8.9 range (USGS, http://gldss7.cr.usgs.gov/neis/general/handouts/richter.html). When the Richter Scale was first invented, Charles F Richter himself stated, “ I did the work to provide a purely instrumental scale for rough separation of large, medium and small shocks” (Bolt, 1988). However, it has grown since that time. The method describes an earthquake by using only one number. In order for this to be efficient, the method has had to be extended to apply to a number of different seismographs throughout the world. There are now a variety of magnitude scales based on different formulas for the distance the earthquake travels from its center. The Richter Scale measures the amplitude. Once it is read, scientists, engineers, and technicians recognize the number as a measure of the size of the earthquake and how severe it is. Then, magnitudes of previous earthquakes are used to determine what the greatest acceleration of the waves may be at a certain place so that engineers may design a structure in that particular place to withstand an earthquake (Bolt,1988). In 1811, Arkansas was hit by the New Madrid Earthquakes. The New Madrid Fault is a primary fault 2 to 20 miles below land surface (See Figure 3a; 3b). It extends 150 miles southward from Cairo, Illinois, through New Madrid and Caruthersville, Missouri, and down through Batesville to Marked Tree, Arkansas. It dips into Kentucky, near Fulton, and extends southeast to Dyersburg, Tennessee. The New Madrid Earthquakes caused rivers to run backwards, snapped cypress forests into splinters, and even made towns disappear (Fuller, 1984). Also, Lake Catherine, a forty mile long and a half mile wide Arkansas lake, was formed by these quakes (USGS, http://wwwneic.cr.usgs.gov/neis/states/arkansas/arkansas_history.html). On December 16,1811, just west of Blytheville, Arkansas, the ground rumbled and cracked, causing one of the most disastrous earthquakes that is known (See Figure 4). The ground rose and fell as the earth waved, opening the soil in the deep cracks as the surface was bent. During December 16 and 17, 1811, shocks continued at short intervals, but gradually diminished in intensity. The earthquakes epicentral zone encompassed southeastern Missouri and northeastern Arkansas. This region was unlike any other part of the territory. Taken as a whole, the area was characterized by a series of lowland beds divided from one another by ridges running in a southern direction. Some of this bottomland was poorly drained or not drained at all, but these areas were not swamps (Mueller, 1972). Crowley’s Ridge is the largest of the ridges that were formed from the bottomland (See Figure 5). It arose at a point 35 miles west of the Ohio junction and stretched one hundred miles further into Arkansas. Of all the phenomena of the earthquake, none filled people with more fascinated horror than fissuring. This was due to the scary thought of being sucked into the earth and being buried alive. There are two kinds of fissures in the earthquake zone, one due to the down-faulting of narrow strips of earth, and the other to the cracking of the soil by tension as rocks begin to cool. The first caused wide depressions, but not the gaping chasms of the narratives. Those were produced by the second type and produced the greatest alarm (Mueller, 1972). Liquefaction may also occur. It takes place when sediments at or near the ground loose strength due to strong ground shaking (MCEER, http://nceer.eng.buffalo.edu/faqs/liq.html). For a full year from December 16, 1811, small shocks occurred at intervals of a few days, but as there were no other shocks, the people became accustomed to the vibrations, and gave little attention to them (Mueller, 1972). The New Madrid Fault Zone averages more than 200 earthquakes per year. Of these earthquakes, 8 to 10 are large enough to be felt. There are approximately 46 earthquakes in the 2.0 range and 7 in the 3.0 range per year. The possibility of a great earthquake happening soon in this area is small. Scientists believe that catastrophic events like those of 1811 happen in the New Madrid region every 550 to 1200 years. This means there is a 0.3% to 1.0% probability of an event greater than magnitude 8.0 within the next 15 years. Even though the chances are small, there have been other damaging earthquakes in the area since 1811 and 1812 (USGS, http://www.neic.cr.usgs.gov/iso.html). Another earthquake occurred in December of 1883 near Melbourne, which is about 95 miles northeast of Little Rock (See Figure 6). Rockslides occurred and buildings shook. In March of 1911, a shock struck Pine Bluff. It excited many citizens who ran in the street in panic. The record notes, “Glasses were shaken from counters in confectionery stores, and dishes were broken in many kitchens” (USGS, http://wwwneic.cr.usgs.gov/neis/states/arkansas/arkansas_history.html). In 1928, Arkansas was struck by 2 shocks. In November, a shock that appeared to come from the west struck Black Rock, Arkansas (See Figure 7). Again in December, Black Rock was hit with an earthquake that “caused the windows to rattle” (Jackson, 1979). In 1937 and 1938, very light tremors were noted in the northeastern part of the state. The tremors were felt over 25,000 and 90,000 square miles of Arkansas and several surrounding states. Luckily, neither one of those earthquakes caused damage. In January of 1955, a quake occurred in northeastern Arkansas near the Missouri-Tennessee border. This early morning earthquake affected some property in the surrounding states, and it awoke hundreds of residents (USGS, http://wwwneic.cr.usgs.gov/neis/states/arkansas/arkansas_history.html). In May of 1960, another quake struck Pine Bluff, Arkansas (See Figure 8) . This tremor was felt by many. Houses shook and bottles rattled, and a nearby building shook for 2 seconds (Jackson, 1979). A tremor centered about 19 miles of northwest of Little Rock in January of 1969. This tremor caused plaster to crack and furniture to be moved in some homes. Residents in Missouri and Tennessee also took note of this shock (USGS, http://wwwneic.cr.usgs.gov/neis/states/arkansas/arkansas_history.html). In December of 1988, a small earthquake struck northwest Arkansas. The quake occurred about 8.98 km south of Blytheville and it had a 2.4 on the Richter Scale (UALR, http://quake.ualr.edu/news/index.htm). Anywhere rocks are under pressure, an earthquake can occur. The Richter Scale helps measure these earthquakes, and in some cases may be helpful in predicting future quakes. Arkansas has had several earthquakes in the past couple of centuries, and by looking at the very active seismic zone, there may be more to come. Still, many people believe you have to be in California to catch the quake action. However, as evidence proves, Arkansans do get a taste every once in a while!
