How do elevation and regional differences affect temperature and precipitation?

 

            To illustrate how elevation and regional differences affect temperature and precipitation, four points have been chosen.  Conway, Fort Smith, Jonesboro, and Texarkana are four cities throughout Arkansas that vary in elevation.  Each belong to a different region within the state.  These points lie within the regions of the Ouachita Mountains, the Arkansas River Valley, Crowley’s Ridge, and the Gulf Coastal Plain. (See Appendix A)

            Important in geography, the concept of regions has many useful applications.  An endless number of descriptions of the earth’s surface may be identified through regions.  Location of environmental features or physical elements can also be identified by regions.  These natural regions correspond with a particular type of topography.

            The Ouachita Mountains stretch across west central Arkansas.  Different from other mountains in Arkansas, the Ouachita Mountains are folded with long east to west running ridges.  Relatively wide valleys-20 miles or more in width-separate these ridges(Stroud 1981: 20).  A stream or river runs through each valley.  The Ouachita Mountains are subdivided into four regions:  the Arkansas Valley, Fourche Mountains, the Central Ouachita Mountains, and the Athens Plateau.  Conway, with an elevation of 309 feet, is located within the Athens Plateau.  This region is not characterized by the mountains the Ouachita Mountain region is named for, with few elevations above 500 feet. The Ouachita Mountains are bordered on the north by the Arkansas River Valley, on the east by the Mississippi Alluvial Plain, and on the south by the Gulf Coastal Plain.

            The Arkansas River Valley is characterized by long, flat-top mountains and broad bottomlands.  The Arkansas and Petit Jean rivers run through the major bottomlands.  Through the central part of the state, a broad area-almost 40 miles wide-has been carved by the Arkansas River(Stroud 1981: 19).  Since the Petit Jean River is sufficiently decreased in force compared to the Arkansas River, it has not worn down the ridges as rapidly.  As a result, the Petit Jean Valley is much more narrow and, at its eastern end, only a few miles wide.  The Arkansas River Valley is a transitional region that separates the Ouachita Mountains and the Ozark Plateau.  Fort Smith, with an elevation of 458 feet, is located in the Arkansas River Valley region.

            Extending from Southern Missouri to Helena, through east Arkansas, for about 200 miles, is Crowley’s Ridge.  The hills of Crowley’s Ridge range in width from 1 to 12 miles.  Surrounded by the flat flood plains of the Mississippi Alluvial Plain, Crowley’s Ridge rises about 250 feet and can be easily seen.  Jonesboro, with an elevation of 345 feet, is located in this region.

            Elevational differences within regions contribute to temperature fluctuations and the amount of precipitation.  When warm, moist air from the Gulf rises above colder, drier air, rainfall occurs and produces much of Arkansas precipitation.  Ranging from a high of 58 inches per year in small areas of the Ouachita Mountains, and a low of 40 inches per year near Fort Smith, precipitation totals vary throughout Arkansas (Stroud 1981: 6).  Since air is forced to rise over the mountainous terrain, the Ouachita mountains are more humid than surrounding areas.  The  capacity to hold water for warmer air is higher than cooler air, and as the warm, moist air rises it cools and precipitation occurs.

            Temperature, or the amount of heat in the air, decreases as elevation increases.  In the Ozark and Ouachita Mountains, elevation is largely responsible for the cooler temperatures.  The temperature is subject to the lapse rate, or a decrease of 3.5 degrees Fahrenheit for every 1000 feet about sea level.  Due to elevation alone, temperatures may vary by nine degrees Fahrenheit in that range.  However, because of latitude and air masses, they do not.

            Latitude is the most important factor that influences temperature.  As distance from the equator increases, temperatures decreases because of the angle of the sun’s rays and the length of daylight.  The amount of potential energy taken from the sun to heat the ground is controlled by these factors, which vary with latitude.  Arkansas is found between 33 degrees and 36 degrees 30 minutes north latitude (Smith 1989: 35).

            Air masses are the second most important factors that influence temperature.  Temperatures is Arkansas are affected by air masses from Canada in the winter and the Gulf of Mexico in the summer.  Air masses from the arctic region are responsible for very cold temperatures, and air masses from the southwestern United States are responsible for hot temperatures.

            Within Arkansas, temperatures vary from north to south.  Temperatures are mild and rarely fall below zero degrees Fahrenheit during the short winter, while daily highs sometimes exceed 100 degrees Fahrenheit in the long, hot summers.  Because warm, moist air is dominant, intense heat from the summer sun produces rainfall.  Winters in Arkansas are met with cold, dry air from Canada which collides with warmer, more humid air and causes precipitation.  Most of Arkansas’ precipitation occurs along these fronts.

            Arkansas precipitation usually falls in the form of rain; however, snow, sleet, and hail may also be seen.  From the northwest corner to the southeast corner across Arkansas, rainfall amounts increase, in general.  During the months of March, April, and May the greatest amount of rainfall is seen.  The driest months are July, August, September, and October.

            Due to the elevation of Fort Smith, the amount of precipitation was expected to be the highest and the temperature the lowest of the four cities observed.  The temperature was the lowest, but the amount of precipitation was not the highest.  Following Fort Smith in elevation, Texarkana met the standards with the second lowest temperature and the second highest amount of precipitation.  Lower than Texarkana, but higher than Conway, Jonesboro unexpectedly had the highest amount of precipitation.  The temperature of Jonesboro was the third lowest.  Conway, at the lowest elevation, had the lowest amount of precipitation and the highest temperature.  These figures are illustrated in Table 1.1
