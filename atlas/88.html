How have the political boundaries of Arkansas changed over time, who changed them and why?

“I was born in a kingdom, raised in an empire, reached manhood in a territory, I am now a citizen of a state, and never been 100 miles from where I live,” spoke John Patterson, the first child born in Arkansas from European descent. This quote briefly describes part of Arkansas’s past. However, people lived in Arkansas long before John Patterson was born. Before a single white man entered onto what is now Arkansas.

In 1673, Jacques Marquette and Louis Joliet, who were French Catholic missionaries, came down the Mississippi River into the territory. The land was not in anyone’s possession until 1682, when Chevalier de LaSalle came from France, claimed all of the areas drained by the Mississippi, and named it Louisiana in honor of the French King (http://www.educationplanet.com). This Louisiana area was ceded to Spain in 1763. When Thomas Jefferson was elected president in 1800, the French had once again gained control of the Mississippi Valley, and this deeply worried him (Arkansas: The World Around Us). Jefferson knew that if the French closed the Mississippi River to Americans it would be next to impossible for Americans to trade or even settle, thus making war unavoidable. He knew that the only way was to cut all ties that France had to the area by offering to buy the land. At the time, this area included the entire western half of the Mississippi Valley, from New Orleans up to Canada. It also included the land of present day Arkansas (http://www.ArkansasHistory.com). Three years later, the United States acquired the entire area through the Louisiana Purchase. In two years, the Louisiana Territory consisted of three different districts: all of the lands east of the Arkansas River were Indian lands, all of the lands above and west of the Arkansas River were the New Madrid and Arkansas districts. In 1812, Arkansas became part of the newly declared Missouri Territory, but only one year later, Arkansas County was established (http://www.sosweb.state.ar.us/newcomer.html). Missouri’s people though that their state would be too big if they included Arkansas to it, so Arkansas became its own separate territory in 1819 (Ferguson: pg. 26).

The new territory was divided into five different counties: Arkansas, Clark, Hempstead, Lawrence, and Pulaski (See Figure 1).















One year later, Miller County was formed; however, Miller County was established south of the Red River, which actually constituted as part of what is now Texas (Ferguson: pg 36). On October 18, 1820, a treaty was signed to give the Choctaw two of the new territory’s seven counties. In 1824, the Quapaw signed away the rest of their land, and in 1823 the Cherokee land was “confined” to the north of the Arkansas River. By 1828, the boundary was pushed west, placing it just north of Fort Smith (Ashmore: 33). St. Francis County was later established in 1827. By1830, Arkansas had twenty-two established counties. Miller County was no longer included though; it was included in Texas for the 1830 census (http://www.arcounties.org/. html). In 1836, Arkansas was officially declared as a state. Four years later, in 1840, there were thirty-eight established counties in Arkansas (See Figure 2).












That number changed to fifty-one counties through the next ten years. In 1861, Arkansas ceded from the Union with fifty-five established counties (http://www.deltaculturalcenter.com). By the year 1890, all seventy-five counties that Arkansas holds today had been established (see Appendix A for listing).

County lines are not the only political boundaries that have changed over time. “The richness of the state’s history is partly due to the diversity of its landscape” (Foti: pg. 1). There are six major natural geographic divisions of Arkansas. They are the Ozark Mountains, the Ouachita Mountains, the Arkansas River Valley, the Coastal plain, the Delta, and Crowley’s Ridge (Foti. pg1) (see Figure 3).

Figure 3










In the beginning, long before Jacques Marquette and Louis Joliet were here, Arkansas was under the ocean, at the beginning of the Paleozoic Era. The waters were full of living things in the place that became northern Arkansas (Foti, Hanson: pg.21). A common rock found in Northern Arkansas today is known as limestone. The creature’s hard shells formed limestone as they died and fell to the bottom. (pg.21) During the Tertiary Period of the Cenozoic Era, about 50 million years ago the Gulf of Mexico began to withdraw from Arkansas (Foti, Hanson Map pg. 21) (See Figure 4).

Figure 4








When the gulf withdrew, its floor was exposed and its beaches were left behind. Sand and gravel left by the gulf are common in Southern Arkansas today (Foti, Hanson pg. 27) (See Figure 5).

Figure 5






The geography of Arkansas can be divided into two regions: the Uplands and the Lowlands (Foti, Hanson: pg. 31) (See Figure 6).

Figure 6







Farmlands in the uplands have never been good; however, the lowlands are rich and fertile providing the state with wonderful crops. The rich lands of south and east Arkansas were admirably suited for growing cotton (Powers pg.31). Pioneers first settled on theses lands and began their culture.

The Ozark Mountain division occupies the northwestern corner of the state (Foti pg.1). These mountains are more than two thousand feet higher than the lowlands of the southwestern half of the state (Foti, Hanson pg.39). High plateaus are the result of the rock being pushed straight up thousands of feet. Because they were formed this way, the mountains often have fairly flat tops, all at about the same height (See Figure 7).

Figure 7







Long, narrow ridges of folded rock characterize the Ouachitas, another mountainous region south of the Ozarks (Foti pg.1). Hot Springs, Mount Ida, Mena, and part of Little Rock are major cities in the Quachitas (Foti pg.51) (See Figure 8). The ridges and many of the valleys are large and fertile enough for large-scale agriculture. Fast growing forests of oak and pine are capable of supporting thriving timber industry (Foti. Pg1).

Figure 8






The Ouachitas, along with the Ozark Mountains, are the boundaries for the Arkansas River Valley. The River that lies between the two mountain regions was an early path of communications. The Arkansas River flows through the western part of the valley with wide bottomlands supplying deep fertile soil along the river, which soon became a necessity due to the cash-crop agriculture that was important to the valley. Bison and elk were common on the prairies of the valley, at the time of settlement (Foti pg.63) (See Figure 9).

Figure 9



In Southern Arkansas, the Coastal Plain division is an area of rolling, pine-covered hills (Foti pg.2) (See Figure 10).

Figure 10






The first settlers of the Coastal Plain were the French, who left their names on places such as L’Aigle Creek, Terre Rouge Creek, and Smackover (Foti, Hanson pg.74). The pine forest of the rolling land provided a valuable resource to the northern timber industry (Foti pg.2).

Next to the Coastal Plain lies the Delta region. Hazen, Stuttgart, McGehee, Lake Village, Clarendon, Brinkley, and Blytheville are the major cities in the Delta of Arkansas. Its geography deep below the surface is the same as that of the Coastal Plain (Foti, Hanson pg.81) (See Figure 11).

Figure 11







The ocean-bottom sands and gravels of the coastal plain have been swept away. Sand, silt, and clay have been deposited in river bottoms (Foti pg.2). The rivers giving shape to the Delta were the Arkansas, Mississippi and White (Foti, Hanson pg.81). The delta is a wildlife paradise due to water and productive soils. Fish are abundant. So are the birds, like herons, that feed on them (Foti, Hanson pg.84). Other water birds thrive there as well. Bear were once common in this area. Settlers used Bear oil for cooking. It was also one of the first exports from Arkansas in the eighteen century. Arkansas was known as the Bear state until hunters killed almost all of them.

The Crowley’s Ridge division is the exception to the character of eastern Arkansas (Foti, pg.2). The geologic history of the ridge is the cause of most of the differences (Foti, Hanson pg.91) (See Figure 12).

Figure 12






It is a two to three hundred foot high ridge made of the dust deposited by ancient winds (Foti pg.2). The rivers left old ocean-bottom material, which left a long narrow ridge between them about 150 miles long and up to 5 miles wide (Foti, Hanson pg.92). The forest is much like Northwestern Arkansas. The tulip tree is common but is native nowhere else in Arkansas (Foti pg 94). The Delta’s people depend on the fields for cotton, soybeans, and rice.

These are the major natural divisions of our state. Each division represents a unified system whose representative components are geography, topography, soil, plants and animals (Foti pg.2).

To those who live in the Natural State, it is unimaginable to see Arkansas as any other way than present. However, Arkansas has not always been how it is today. “The land is old, older than the hill, and older than the mind of man can determine” (Powers: pg. 1). From the Indian tribes who first inhabited the area, to the men who counties have been named after, many people have lived here, and many changes have occurred. From the time when Arkansas’s magnificent landscaped were underwater, to the six well-defined regions of today, numerous differences have taken place. However, no matter the changes, Arkansas has stood the test of time.Appendix A
